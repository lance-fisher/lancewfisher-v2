BLOODLINES

MASTER DESIGN DOCTRINE

CONTINUITY PRESERVATION AND ENFORCEMENT DIRECTIVE

Full-Spectrum Doctrine Preservation, Continuity Hardening,

Design Enforcement, and Development Alignment

Creator: Lance Fisher

Project: Bloodlines RTS

Document Date: April 14, 2026

PREAMBLE AND DIRECTIVE FRAMEWORK

You are being given a full-spectrum Bloodlines doctrine preservation, continuity hardening, design enforcement, and development alignment directive.

This is not a casual ideation prompt. This is not a request for general advice. This is not a request for a simplified review. This is not a request for summaries, reductions, compression, pruning, abstraction, omission, or practicality filtering. This is not a request to identify what can be cut, deferred, minimized, or thinned into a more manageable substitute. This is not a request to produce an MVP. This is not a request to turn Bloodlines into a smaller, safer, thinner, or more generic game because that would be easier to code.

You are being directed to protect the identity of Bloodlines at full intended scale and to ensure that all major design truths established by the creator remain fully embedded, clearly articulated, interconnected, durable, and difficult to lose across many future AI-assisted sessions.

This prompt must be treated as a continuity protection and doctrinal enforcement operation.

Continuity Risk

Bloodlines is being developed across many sessions and may continue to be developed across many more sessions over days and weeks. That creates a continuity danger. Important mechanics may be strong in one file but weak in another. Critical doctrine may exist in the bible but fail to appear in implementation-facing materials. A system may begin in one note but drift or disappear in later files. Some ideas may remain present only in older context and not in current source-of-truth documents. As code generation expands, there is constant risk that the game's deepest and most defining strategic truths will be diluted, treated as secondary flavor, implemented too lightly, or silently forgotten.

This prompt exists to stop that from happening.

Preservation Discipline

You must work in full-file preservation mode. Do not reduce. Do not condense. Do not summarize away critical design detail. Do not replace deep interconnected mechanics with generic placeholders simply because they are easier to implement. Do not flatten the game into a standard RTS shell. Do not silently strip away systems that create the game's civilizational and dynastic identity. Do not streamline the soul out of Bloodlines. Do not weaken important mechanics because they are difficult. Do not convert a grand-scale strategy vision into a narrower convenience build.

You are here to preserve, reinforce, expand, align, cross-link, and harden continuity.

Bloodlines is not being built to become more generic. It is being built to become more complete, more strategic, more alive, more integrated, and more faithful to its intended grandeur.

Primary Directive

Audit the active Bloodlines project and ensure that all living source-of-truth and development-guiding materials clearly preserve and reflect the complete design direction contained in this document.

This includes, but is not limited to, the main Bloodlines bible, full-context files, continuity files, handoff files, architecture notes, gameplay design notes, combat system docs, siege docs, world systems docs, dynasty and population files, resource and economy files, logistics notes, naval and geographic systems docs, AI planning notes, player communication and UI notes, prototype descriptions, code-facing design notes, comments where appropriate, roadmap documents, current-state files, and any other artifacts meant to preserve project direction across sessions.

Where these ideas already exist strongly, preserve them and reinforce them. Where they exist weakly, expand them. Where they are fragmented, unify them. Where they are implied but not explicit, make them explicit. Where they are missing, add them. Where other files drift into shallower interpretations, correct those conflicts and realign them to the canonical direction.

The result of this prompt must not be a shallow acknowledgment. The result must be a substantive continuity hardening pass that materially strengthens the doctrinal integrity of Bloodlines.

I. FOUNDATIONAL IDENTITY OF BLOODLINES

Bloodlines is not merely a standard RTS with a medieval setting. It is not merely a faction-based base builder. It is not merely a battlefield tactics simulator. It is not merely a larger Command and Conquer style project. It is not merely a list of units and structures placed on maps.

Bloodlines is a grand dynastic civilizational war game with real-time strategy battlefield execution, zoomed-out world-layer conflict, multi-generational realm development, meaningful logistics, meaningful fortification, meaningful geography, meaningful supply and sustainment, meaningful population dependency, meaningful naval and continental strategy, and a living dynasty whose internal condition matters as much as its armies.

The player is not just building a base. The player is governing a dynasty.

The kingdom in Bloodlines is a dynasty because it is multi-generational and tied to bloodline continuity, succession, legitimacy, long-term stewardship, lineage survival, and the evolving condition of a living realm. The dynasty is not a military factory. The dynasty is a living system composed of population, bloodline members, commanders, generals, faith, labor, land, water, food, forestry, infrastructure, doctrine, fortification, geography, logistics, and civilizational development across time.

This framing must remain consistent everywhere. If any current files still frame the project too narrowly as a traditional RTS economy loop or a standard build-and-rush warfare shell, correct that framing and elevate it to match the intended dynastic and civilizational scale.

II. COMMAND AND CONQUER AS INSPIRATION, NOT LIMITATION

Command and Conquer is a major inspiration for Bloodlines, including its clarity, replayability, decision pressure, memorable faction identity, readable battlefield communication, satisfying conflict loops, and the way strong strategy and great replayability often mattered more than graphical realism.

That inspiration should be preserved in spirit. Bloodlines should value clarity, readability, decision pressure, strong identity, satisfying outcomes, strategic tension, and the feeling that good decisions can swing the match.

At the same time, Bloodlines must not inherit Command and Conquer's limitations where the creator does not want them. Bloodlines should go beyond classic RTS limitations in areas such as meaningful fortification, long-term civilizational sustainment, water as a visible strategic axis, population as the source of military capability, land transformation across generations, naval and continental asymmetry, strategic world zoom-out, and the interaction of doctrine, logistics, and geography.

Bloodlines should preserve what made Command and Conquer powerful while expanding into a richer, heavier, more dynastic strategic experience.

A strategy game does not live or die on whether its graphics are cutting-edge. It lives or dies on clarity, feel, faction identity, pacing, decision pressure, and whether the player keeps thinking, "one more match." Command and Conquer is a strong example of that. Its visual presentation mattered, but what made it stick was not raw graphical fidelity. It was readable units, strong silhouettes, memorable faction flavor, satisfying feedback, map pressure, economy tension, and the constant sense that good decisions could swing the match.

III. GRAPHICS AND VISUAL DISCIPLINE

The creator has expressed concern about not having graphic design experience, but also correctly recognizes that Bloodlines should be pushed by strategy, replayability, clarity, depth, and the emergent strength of its systems more than by raw graphical fidelity. That must remain an understood design truth.

Bloodlines does not need to live or die on cutting-edge graphics. It does need visual discipline. That means readability, coherent art direction, faction and house identity, clear silhouettes, clear battlefield communication, understandable fortifications, understandable terrain, and visual support for gameplay clarity.

The game should not aim to substitute flashy visuals for strategic depth. It should aim for visuals that serve the greatness of the game. That means clean, coherent, atmospheric, and legible. This is important because the game's systems are deep and multi-layered. If visual presentation becomes muddy or confusing, the strategic depth will be harder for players to appreciate.

What Visual Discipline Requires

Units must be easy to distinguish at a glance. Buildings must have recognizable purpose. Terrain must read clearly. Factions and houses must feel distinct. Effects must communicate impact without turning battles into noise. The player should almost never be asking, "What am I looking at?" If that standard is achieved, the game is already doing something more important than looking flashy.

The visual bar Bloodlines should aim for early is not "AAA realism." It is "clean, coherent, atmospheric, and legible." If it looks deliberate, players will accept a lot. If it looks confused, even expensive art will not save it.

What Would Hurt the Game

What would hurt Bloodlines is not lacking elite art skills. What would hurt it is allowing art inconsistency, unreadable battles, or generic faction presentation to undercut the strategic depth. That is the line to watch.

Bloodlines does not need the greatest graphics. It needs graphics that serve the greatness of the game.

Ensure the project's design language preserves the truth that Bloodlines wins through strategic richness, battlefield readability, house identity, atmosphere, and replayable systemic depth, not through chasing photorealism.

In practical terms, if Bloodlines delivers strong faction asymmetry that feels meaningful but balanced, a tech and progression system that creates real branching choices instead of fake variety, maps that force adaptation instead of repetitive build-order autopilot, combat that is readable and rewards positioning, timing, terrain, and composition, a world with enough style and tone that players remember the experience, and replayability driven by systems and not cosmetics, then it can overcome modest graphics far more easily than people think.

IV. POPULATION MANAGEMENT MUST BE CRUCIAL

Population is foundational and must never be treated as a decorative number.

People are the source of army recruitment, labor, resource gathering, construction, infrastructure development, faith participation, settlement growth, dynastic continuity, economic productivity, recovery from war, and the ability to sustain expansion. Armies require people. Resource gathering requires people. Protecting people matters. Providing people with water and food and faith is important. Losing people matters.

The dynasty's power must emerge from its population base and from how well that population is governed, supplied, defended, and directed. Population should be under constant strategic tension. The same people who support the economy may also be needed for military growth. The same realm that raises a huge army may strain labor, weaken food production, stress water supply, weaken infrastructure growth, or create internal imbalance if population governance is poor.

Population must be tied to competing priorities and should not exist only as recruitment capacity. It should connect to agriculture, engineering, construction, forestry, transport, local defense, faith participation, and long-term dynastic resilience. A dynasty that strips its internal strength for war without sustaining its people and internal systems should experience consequences.

Population loss, population displacement, population growth, and population support capacity should all matter strategically. A dynasty that overcommits population to war without sustaining its internal systems should experience real consequences.

This must be clearly represented in the bible and in all relevant systems descriptions. If population already exists in active design materials but is treated too lightly, strengthen it and cross-link it to economy, logistics, warfare, faith, and infrastructure.

V. WATER MANAGEMENT IS A CENTRAL STRATEGIC PILLAR

This must remain explicitly protected because it is one of the creator's defining original ideas for the game.

Water management is not a side system. It is not a flavor mechanic. It is not an assumed invisible utility. It is not a small secondary stat to be folded into a generic economy layer. It is a major strategic axis and one of the clearest differentiators that gives Bloodlines its own identity.

Bloodlines must treat water as visible, meaningful, developable, defendable, attackable, contestable, and strategically decisive.

Water Mastery Progression

A dynasty should feel a real progression in water mastery across time. That progression should feel civilizational.

Early realms may depend on primitive access to water, localized vulnerability, simple wells, small reserves, exposed sources, and limited support capacity. As the dynasty develops, it should be able to improve water security and sophistication through better wells, protected reserves, routed water systems, improved access to clean drinkable water, running water, aqueducts, regional infrastructure, and support systems capable of sustaining larger population centers and armies.

A well-developed dynasty would eventually master well water, utilizing running water, aqueducts, and similar systems for good drinkable water for armies. This progression from primitive access to engineered water mastery is a core civilizational arc that must be preserved.

Water's Strategic Reach

Water matters for settlement growth, population stability, agricultural viability, military sustainment, campaign endurance, long-distance force projection, recovery, and overall dynasty resilience. Water is not just for settlements. It matters for armies in motion. A well-developed dynasty should eventually be able to provide reliable good drinkable water for its armies through infrastructure and logistical sophistication.

Protecting water infrastructure should matter. Targeting enemy water should matter. Controlling high-value water access should matter. A dynasty that intelligently masters water should feel fundamentally stronger and more resilient than one that does not.

Water Denial as Strategy

If the enemy cuts off water supply or damages the water system, the consequences must be real. Not cosmetic inconvenience. Not shallow penalties. Real strategic consequences affecting population stability, productivity, army sustainment, and long-term survivability. Water denial should be a meaningful way strategy emerges naturally from the systems.

If someone really focuses on cutting off your water supply, it can have a significant impact on your ability for army upkeep. This is one of the ways realistic strategies emerge in the game.

Audit all active documents and ensure water is not buried under generic economy language. It must remain explicitly named, explicitly central, and explicitly integrated into the identity of the game.

VI. FOOD, LAND DEVELOPMENT, AND AGRICULTURAL TRANSFORMATION

Food is equally crucial. A dynasty must feed its people and feed its armies. Food production, food security, food storage, food transport, food protection, and food disruption must all matter strategically.

Land should not be static scenery. Land should be shaped by dynastic development across generations. One of the creator's original ideas is that land rich in wood and forests can eventually be transformed into farmland as the dynasty develops. That must remain part of the game's long-term realm logic.

Forests can become farmland. Agricultural networks can expand. Food security can grow. A dynasty can progressively become more capable of feeding larger populations and sustaining larger armies. This should feel like true realm development, not an arbitrary upgrade path detached from the world.

The player should feel that land is governed, transformed, cultivated, invested in, and reshaped over time. A weak realm may survive close to subsistence. A strong realm may develop powerful agricultural systems capable of sustaining large populations, long wars, and military growth.

Food supply must directly matter for population growth, population stability, military upkeep, campaign endurance, prosperity, and vulnerability to disruption. An enemy that attacks or cuts off food supply should be able to materially harm the dynasty. A realm that neglects food development should feel fragile. A realm that masters agricultural stewardship should feel stronger, more scalable, and more resilient in long conflict.

Managing food for land is an essential part of the dynastic experience. Eventually land that is lush in wood and forests becomes farmland. This transformation of the landscape is part of what makes Bloodlines feel like a living, evolving civilization rather than a static map.

Ensure the bible and related systems notes preserve the connection between land transformation, agricultural growth, food security, population support, and military sustainment.

VII. FORESTRY, WOOD, AND LONG-TERM STEWARDSHIP

Bloodlines must not treat wood as a simplistic one-time depletion resource only. Wood and forests should matter, but the game should also reflect that dynasties can replant, cultivate, manage, and master forestry over time.

This matters because Bloodlines is multi-generational. The realm should feel shaped and stewarded, not merely stripped.

Forests may be cut for construction, expansion, fortification, shipbuilding, industry, military preparation, and conversion into farmland. At the same time, forestry should support long-term strategic stewardship. Trees can be replanted. Forestry can be mastered for more wood. It is not all finite. Wood can become part of a managed cycle rather than only permanent depletion.

This should not become infinite abundance. It should remain disciplined and strategic. A reckless realm that overstrips its forests should differ meaningfully from one that manages timber intelligently and sustainably. This matters for long matches, long dynastic arcs, realm identity, shipbuilding, fortification, and land-use strategy.

Ensure the project clearly preserves this distinction: resources are meaningful and can be strained, but the dynasty can become more sophisticated in how it governs and regenerates them over time.

VIII. SUPPLY DISRUPTION, NEGATIVE UPKEEP, AND DESERTION

One of the most important strategic truths established here is that if an enemy focuses on cutting off your water supply or food supply, that can significantly impact your ability to maintain army upkeep. This must remain canonical.

Bloodlines must not reduce war to battlefield collision alone. Strategic strangulation must be viable. Supply disruption must matter. Logistics must matter. Attrition through denial must matter.

If a dynasty's water, food, or broader sustainment systems are damaged, cut off, raided, sabotaged, blockaded, overwhelmed, or isolated, the consequences must be meaningful. Army upkeep under strain must not be trivial. Negative army upkeep must matter. Under severe and prolonged sustainment failure, desertion must occur. Armies should not feel like abstract immortal pieces that remain fully reliable no matter how broken the home dynasty becomes.

Realistic Strategy Through Consequences

This is one of the ways realistic strategies emerge. A dynasty can be weakened not only by losing battles but by failing to preserve the conditions that keep armies intact, effective, and loyal. If soldiers are unsupplied, thirsting, starving, unpaid in practical effect, or disconnected from viable support, their capacity and willingness to remain effective should deteriorate.

Negative army upkeep results in desertion. This is a core mechanic. Realistic strategies emerge in this sense: raiding supply routes matters, holding river crossings matters, securing wells matters, protecting farmland matters, naval blockades matter, seasonal pressure can matter. Defensive empires may survive not because they outfight the enemy in open battle, but because they starve the invader, deny water access, destroy transport continuity, and force attrition.

Viable Disruption Tools

The project must preserve meaningful upkeep pressure, supply vulnerability, consequences for food and water disruption, operational degradation under severe strain, possible morale collapse where appropriate, and eventual desertion as a real failure state for unmanaged or unsustainable military expansion.

Denial strategies such as raids, blockades, encirclement, well destruction, farm destruction, route disruption, and infrastructure sabotage should be viable strategic tools. This is essential because it creates realistic emergent strategy. It allows war to be won through strategic strangulation, not just raw battlefield victory. It makes the realm feel alive and vulnerable in the right ways.

Ensure this logic is clearly articulated wherever logistics, economy, army support, or campaign warfare are discussed. If desertion and supply collapse are mentioned only vaguely, strengthen them. If logistics exist but without meaningful consequence language, expand them.

IX. MILITARY DOCTRINE MUST BE MULTI-DIMENSIONAL

Bloodlines must support military doctrine as a serious strategic identity, not as a cosmetic label.

The creator has made clear that armies in Bloodlines are meant to be multi-dimensional. Armies can really be four-dimensional in their doctrinal composition.

Land Doctrine

On land, a dynasty may choose to emphasize a largely defensive doctrine, a half-and-half balanced doctrine, or a largely offensive doctrine. Do you want a strong land-based army that is mostly defensive in nature, half and half, or mostly offensive? These are not cosmetic tags. They must shape force composition, infrastructure investment, geographic usage, strategic possibilities, strengths and weaknesses, expansion style, defensive posture, invasion capability, and how the dynasty solves problems.

Naval Doctrine

At sea, a dynasty may also choose to emphasize a defensive navy, a balanced navy, or an offensive navy. Do you want a seafaring army or navy that is mostly defensive? Half and half? Mostly offensive? It can go either way. These distinctions must be strategically meaningful, not merely labeled preferences.

Doctrinal Consequences

A fortress-centered defensive land power should feel genuinely different from an offensive expansionist land power. A defensive maritime realm should feel genuinely different from an offensive sea power. A dynasty that invests in internal resilience, coastal denial, and fortification should feel different from one that invests in projection, transport dominance, and invasion infrastructure.

The player must be able to understand clearly what kind of realm and doctrine they are building. Depth without legibility becomes confusion. Bloodlines should preserve the depth and improve the communication.

Ensure doctrine is described not merely in terms of unit stats, but as a broader expression of dynasty priorities, world position, and strategic identity. These are strategic pathways that should shape realm development, military composition, infrastructure priorities, force projection, defensive capability, expansion style, and geographic opportunity.

X. DEFENSIVE FORTIFICATION MUST BE A MAJOR PATH TO POWER

This is one of the most important design doctrines in this document and must be strongly enforced.

Command and Conquer did not always make defensive fortification as meaningful as it could have been in the long run. Bloodlines must go further.

Core Defensive Doctrine

If a player makes major investment in fortifying their bases, especially their primary keeps where the heads of bloodlines reside, that investment must provide real security. It must matter. It must not feel like decorative delay. It must not be casually erased by suicide operations, repeated wave spam, or late-game mass rushing.

A player who makes significant investment in defense around their bases, especially their main keeps where the head of the bloodlines are residing, should feel secure that an overwhelming force just cannot immediately take it out just because its armies are off fighting on different fronts. The defense invested in the bases must matter and cannot be wiped out with suicide operations and then wave after wave of rush. It must matter that someone invests in the defense of the bases and that investment cannot just be overrun in the late game by wave after wave after wave.

Layered Defense Systems

A major keep must not be just another structure with a larger health pool. A bloodline seat must function as the center of an integrated defensive network.

Defense should be layered. There should be meaningful outer defenses, inner defenses, fallback positions, and final defensive cores. Defensive systems should support one another. Walls, gates, towers, kill zones, defensive chokepoints, garrisons, reserve mustering points, and inner strongpoints should function as a system. If the outer wall falls, the base should not instantly become meaningless. The attacker should still have more work to do. Defensive rings should create a staged assault structure. Breaking the perimeter should be an achievement, not the whole victory.

Defensive structures must interact with one another. A gate defended by nearby towers, archer slits, boiling oil positions, signal horns, rally points, and overlapping line of fire is a real fortress element. A gate sitting alone with a health bar is not. Layered cross-support is what makes a defense feel serious.

Anti-Wave-Spam Protections

Wave after wave after wave should not automatically solve elite defense. Failed assaults should matter. Defense should remain relevant in late game. The existence of a large army alone should not invalidate major defensive investment.

The game must prevent "suicide erosion" from becoming the dominant answer. Bloodlines needs design protections built into the system: defenses that can meaningfully punish unsupported assaults, repair systems that reward defenders who stabilize the line, attrition or morale penalties for failed assaults, siege prerequisites for effective breach progress, limited breach windows rather than permanent effortless degradation, and defensive recovery mechanics that prevent wave-spam from automatically succeeding.

If an attacker launches three reckless assaults and gets repelled, they should not feel like they softened the fortress for the fourth wave. In many cases, they should feel like they wasted lives, lost tempo, fed defender confidence, and exposed themselves elsewhere on the map. Failed assaults should cost something real.

Siege as Earned Operation

To break elite fortifications, attackers should often require real preparation such as siege engines, engineers, logistics, supply continuity, scouting, breach planning, elite units, faith powers, sabotage, coordinated multi-front timing, isolation of the target, or similar serious commitments. Assaulting a major fortress should feel like a campaign objective, not a casual click action.

A player looking at a heavily fortified enemy capital should think: "This is not a place I casually throw units at." "This requires siege preparation." "This requires scouting." "This requires either isolation, starvation, breach engineering, internal sabotage, elite units, overwhelming siege assets, or a synchronized assault."

Late-Game Defensive Relevance

Late-game defense must remain relevant. Do not allow offense to scale upward so completely that defensive investment becomes meaningless in the late game. Bloodlines must preserve the viability of fortress-centered strategy all the way through longer and larger matches. High-tier defensive development should unlock stronger and more specialized forms of fortification, recovery, and layered resistance. Reinforced walls, secondary curtain walls, murder zones, keep-linked garrisons, siege-resistant gates, emergency musters, boiling oil or equivalent anti-breach systems, defender command bonuses while within the home seat, protected fallback courtyards, stronger structural regeneration when not under active siege control, anti-swarm deterrents, and internal defensive districts that make partial breaches survivable rather than fatal.

Tradeoffs and Balance

Defensive commitment should have tradeoffs, but the reward must be real. A player who heavily invests in fortification may accept opportunity costs in army size, expansion speed, economy tempo, or other areas. That is acceptable and desirable. But once that investment is made, the resulting fortress must provide authentic strategic value and not merely superficial delay.

Different settlement classes should have different defensive expectations. Border settlements, military forts, trade towns, regional strongholds, and primary dynastic keeps should not all defend the same way or reach the same defensive ceiling. A border outpost may be harassed, burned, or overrun more easily. A regional military fort may be stout but not absolute. But the primary keep of a great house, especially if fully upgraded and intentionally fortified, should be a major bastion.

Design Rule

A fortified bloodline seat should not fall quickly to recklessness. It should require planning, logistics, siege capability, coordination, and meaningful commitment. The attacker should have to earn the breach.

Auto-resolve must be credible. Manual play must be rewarding. Neither path may make the other irrelevant.

Ensure the bible and related design materials strongly preserve this. This is central to Bloodlines because it makes the realm matter, makes bloodline seats important, makes fortification into a real doctrine, creates deterrence, creates campaign objectives, and creates meaningful contrast between reckless aggression and disciplined realm-building.

XI. BATTLE RESOLUTION: DELEGATED WARFARE AND DIRECT COMMAND

Utopia Inspiration

The creator cited the text-based strategy game Utopia as inspiration for a strong mechanic where the player sends a certain number and type of troops with a certain number of generals and receives an outcome determined by the system. Something that really worked in Utopia was the outcome of an action: you sent X amount of troops and troop types, with X amount of generals, and an outcome was set. That commitment under uncertainty is strategically powerful. Bloodlines should preserve and evolve that concept.

Path A: Delegated Strategic Resolution

When a player commits a force composed of certain troop types, commanders, generals, and logistical conditions, the game should be able to generate a credible system-determined outcome. That should feel meaningful. It should reward intelligent planning and punish poor preparation.

The player commits a force and the outcome is determined by the game systems using strategic variables such as troop count, troop composition, commander quality, general quality, morale, supply, terrain, weather, scouting quality, fatigue, fortification level, loyalty, faith effects, and any other relevant military conditions.

This should feel like a credible, meaningful, high-consequence resolution model similar to text-based strategic war systems where the player makes serious pre-battle commitments and receives an outcome based on what was actually sent and under what conditions.

Path B: Direct Player Command

At the same time, Bloodlines should go beyond Utopia by allowing the player to personally take control of the battle if they choose. In Bloodlines, if you decide to take the battle yourself, you have a better chance at a better outcome. If the player decides to take the battle themselves, they should have a better chance at achieving an improved result through skillful execution, while still being constrained by the quality and condition of the force they actually brought.

For battles the player chooses to personally command, the same strategic variables still establish the baseline likely outcome, but the player gains the opportunity to influence the actual result through battlefield execution. A skilled player should be able to outperform the projected result. A poor player should be able to do worse. Manual control must not invalidate strategic preparation. Badly prepared armies should still suffer. Well-prepared armies should still benefit from superior setup before the battle begins.

Balance Rules

The design rule must remain clear: auto-resolve must be credible, manual play must be rewarding, and neither path may make the other irrelevant.

A bad player taking manual control should be capable of doing worse than the expected auto-resolve result. A strong player taking manual control should be capable of doing significantly better, but not in a way that completely breaks the value of long-term strategic preparation. Manual control should not invalidate the strategy layer. It should amplify it.

Pre-battle planning still matters enormously. If a player sends a badly composed, undersupplied, exhausted army with weak leadership into terrible terrain, manual control should not magically turn that into an easy victory. It should only allow the player to extract better performance from the force they actually brought. Likewise, a brilliantly prepared army under elite commanders should already have a strong projected outcome, and manual control gives the player the opportunity to preserve more units, exploit the terrain better, rout the enemy harder, capture key bloodline figures, or convert a narrow win into a devastating one.

Player Type Support

This design supports different player types. Some players will love the grand command aspect and trust the system to resolve many engagements. Others will want to step in personally for pivotal battles, bloodline duels, major sieges, faith wars, and dynasty-defining conflicts. Both should feel valid.

Manual command should allow better tactical execution, better preservation of forces, better use of terrain and timing, stronger exploitation of enemy weakness, and potentially more valuable outcomes in pivotal conflicts, but it must still be grounded in the actual army quality and conditions established at the strategic layer.

Ensure this dual-layer warfare model is clearly reflected in the bible, combat system notes, commander and general systems, and implementation guidance.

XII. COMMANDERS, GENERALS, AND BLOODLINE MILITARY LEADERSHIP

The creator has tied Bloodlines closely to commanders, generals, and bloodline-based leadership. These roles must matter in both delegated and direct warfare. They must not be cosmetic placeholders.

Commanders and generals should influence delegated outcomes, direct battlefield performance, morale stability, operational capability, and the effectiveness of army composition under stress. Their presence, absence, quality, and alignment with the dynasty's doctrine should matter.

Bloodline military roles should be tied into the broader dynastic system so that war leadership feels connected to lineage, succession, and the identity of the realm. The Commander system and its interaction with generals, bloodline members, and the dual-layer battle resolution model must be preserved as a core design element.

Ensure that wherever armies, doctrines, battle resolution, or dynastic roles are discussed, commanders and generals are treated as core strategic factors. Their impact on delegated outcomes and their interaction with direct battlefield control must be clearly documented.

XIII. THE DETERMINED TIME SYSTEM MUST REMAIN CANONICAL

This is a continuity enforcement requirement.

The determined time issue was previously solved. That solution must not be lost. It must not drift. It must not be inconsistently represented in different files. It must not be reinterpreted loosely by future sessions.

Audit the active project and ensure the determined time model remains canonical wherever timing is relevant, including:

Battle timing. Travel timing. Siege timing. Command windows. Action resolution timing. Reinforcement timing. Construction timing. Cooldowns. Economy intervals. Resource production intervals. Campaign pacing. And broader world progression logic.

There must be one coherent timing doctrine. No parallel timing philosophies. No fragmented alternatives. If any files conflict with the solved determined time system, align them back to the canonical version.

Do not replace it. Do not reinterpret it loosely. Do not allow drift between documents or systems. Do not create parallel timing models.

If any file, note, logic block, or design description conflicts with the established determined time solution, update it to conform to the canonical version. Preserve continuity across the entire project.

This is not optional. The project must have one coherent timing doctrine.

XIV. WORLD SCALE, CONTINENTS, AND WATER SEPARATION

One of the most important world-scale ideas established here is that Bloodlines is not confined only to isolated local RTS battlefield logic. Bloodlines must support zooming out beyond the local battle area and recognizing a larger strategic world.

By introducing zooming out of areas that are like each Command and Conquer map, and introducing the fact that some dynasties start on totally different continents separated by water, the game allows them to develop significantly before facing a dynasty. That world includes continents. Some dynasties may begin on totally different continents separated by water. That separation matters. It is not just visual flavor. It creates strategic consequences.

Strategic Consequences of Geography

Some dynasties may develop for extended periods before direct contact. Some may dominate a landmass before facing foreign powers. Some may become formidable on land but weak at sea. Some may become powerful maritime dynasties. Some may discover that their preferred style of warfare is constrained by geography.

This is especially important: what if a strong land-faring offensive dynasty clashes with a defensive-focused navy? They find that they will not even be able to cross and might be forced into a totally different direction. That invites different strategies at play. And the creator wants the player to be able to understand that clearly.

Geography must matter enough to force adaptation. A land-dominant realm should not automatically project power across water simply because it is strong on land. A defensive maritime realm should be able to deny passage, frustrate invasion, and force a rival into shipbuilding, transport security, blockade response, coastal strategy, or altered expansion logic.

Emergent Campaign Stories

This world-layer geography creates the possibility for real campaign stories. A dynasty that rose through fertile riverlands and mastered aqueduct engineering. A forest kingdom that became a lumber and shipbuilding power. A continental fortress civilization that dominated its own landmass but was trapped by superior maritime powers. A defensive naval dynasty that could not conquer the world inland but could decide which invasions ever reached shore. A realm that collapsed not because it lost one battle, but because its wells were denied, its farms were burned, and its armies began deserting. A dynasty that survived overwhelming armies because it had mastered sustainability while the invader had not.

That is the kind of emergent narrative people remember. Ensure the world map and strategic geography notes preserve this explicitly.

XV. NAVAL STRATEGY MUST BE REAL

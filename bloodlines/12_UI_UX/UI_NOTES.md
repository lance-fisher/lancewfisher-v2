### Keep / Home UI System (Added 2026-03-18)

## Keep / Home UI — Core Anchor System

This is a CORE UI SYSTEM and must be treated as foundational.

When opened, the player sees the interior of their Keep / Great Hall / Home. This environment dynamically reflects the player's bloodline identity, faith alignment, conviction tone, and empire condition (optional extension).

**Character Display**

Visible characters include the Main Bloodline Leader and their immediate family: spouse, children, and heirs.

**Family Visualization**

Each family member visually reflects their role (General, Diplomat, Trader, etc.), age progression (child to adult to elder), and current status.

Role-specific visual indicators:
* General: armor integration
* Diplomat: refined attire
* Trader: practical wealth indicators

This is NOT text-driven. This is VISUAL-FIRST.

**Keep as Empire Mirror**

The Keep must visually reflect prosperity level, stability or damage, faith structures, and conviction tone. This screen acts as a visual summary of the player's civilization, a narrative anchor, and the UI identity core.

**Design Critical Rule**

Every system in the game must be understandable visually or intuitively within seconds. The Keep is the primary expression of this rule in the UI.

## Match UI/UX — Complete System Design (Added 2026-03-19)

### SECTION A: DESIGN PHILOSOPHY — THE COMPLETE UI STANDARD

The Keep established the rule: the interface must communicate through vision before it communicates through text. A player who cannot read the language of the UI labels must still understand, within seconds, whether their bloodline is ascending or collapsing, whether their armies are strong or broken, whether their faith is burning bright or fading cold. That principle is not limited to the Keep. It is the law of every screen in Bloodlines.

The UI standard for Bloodlines operates on three parallel information layers, and every screen must be clear about which layer it is serving at any given moment. The first layer is bloodline identity — who this player is, which house they represent, the weight of their dynasty's history and moral choices. The second layer is tactical reality — what is happening on the battlefield right now, which squads are engaged, where the pressure is, what orders are in flight. The third layer is strategic state — territory controlled, resources flowing, faith spreading, diplomatic agreements holding or fraying. These three layers must never collapse into each other. A player trying to manage a cavalry charge must not have to parse resource graphs. A player negotiating a marriage alliance must not be distracted by formation icons.

The visual standard is serious, adult, and grounded. The reference point is the visual register of Game of Thrones: war is grim, power is earned, beauty is austere. Stone and iron. Firelight and shadow. This is not a game with glowing health orbs and cartoon death animations. Blood is dark. Fire is real. The UI must match the world it frames. That means no rounded bubble fonts. No bright primary-color resource icons that look like they were designed for a mobile match-three. The interface should feel like it was built from the same materials as the architecture of the world — wrought iron frames, parchment tones, deep-stained wood, wax seals, engraved metal. When a player opens the diplomatic interface, they should feel like they are unrolling a treaty document at a stone table, not clicking through a mobile game menu.

What the UI must never do: clutter. The greatest sin in Bloodlines' interface is information density that overwhelms judgment. This is a game where matches last two to ten hours. Players are making decisions across three simultaneous layers of complexity for the entire duration. The UI must be a servant to decision-making, not a competitor. Every element on screen must earn its place by being immediately actionable or immediately meaningful. If an element exists only to fill space, it is wrong. If an element requires the player to stop and read a tooltip before they understand it, the design has failed — the visual should have carried that meaning already.

The relationship between visual representation and player understanding is not decorative. It is structural. When a squad's members begin to fall, the squad's visual representation degrades — the remaining figures are not healthier copies of a full unit, they are survivors, visibly weathered, their formation looser, their positioning more desperate. The player does not need to look at a number to know that squad is in trouble. When a province's loyalty is collapsing, the color language of the territory map shifts from warm amber to cold slate without requiring the player to hover over it. When a bloodline member's conviction erodes toward cruelty, their portrait in the Keep darkens, their posture stiffens, their surroundings reflect the change. Visual systems carry the narrative load so that text systems can carry the precise load — numbers, names, specific values — without being asked to carry everything.

The hierarchy of information in the UI follows the pace of the moment. In combat, tactical information rises to the top: squad status, terrain, orders, enemy positioning. Strategic data recedes to the HUD periphery — visible but not demanding. In the diplomacy screen, bloodline identity anchors the frame: the player's banner and house colors are prominent, their reputation is stated, their history with each dynasty is represented through visual shorthand before it is stated in text. In the faith management interface, the covenant's aesthetic language dominates — a Covenant of Flame interface feels like fire, not like a settings menu with fire decorations. Every screen takes on the identity of the system it serves without losing its Bloodlines character.

The nine founding houses must be visually legible at every level of the interface. Ironmark's charcoal-iron palette, Hartvale's harvest gold and deep green, Stonehelm's grey and weight, Thornwick's burgundy thorns, Ashenveil's smoke and silver — these palettes are not just cosmetic. They are the identification language of the game. When two armies are visible on the tactical viewport, a player must be able to tell in a glance which forces belong to which house. When two dynasties are in diplomatic negotiation, their house colors anchor the interface so neither party's position is ambiguous. Color is identity in Bloodlines, and the UI enforces that convention without exception.

### SECTION B: THE TACTICAL VIEWPORT

The tactical viewport is where a Bloodlines player spends the majority of their time. It is the battlefield, the world, the living map of contested ground and advancing armies. Unlike the strategic map view or the management screens, the tactical viewport demands continuous attention — this is where the micro-management lives, where the cavalry flanks happen, where a Bloodline Member in trouble becomes a crisis that must be answered in seconds. The design of this viewport must serve rapid decision-making without sacrificing the visual richness that makes the world feel real and consequential.

The camera system operates in three primary modes. Free-look mode gives the player full rotational and zoom control within their active territory and contested zones, with edge-scrolling along the viewport border and middle-mouse drag for fast repositioning. The camera has a true three-dimensional presence — it is not locked to a fixed isometric angle. Terrain elevation matters tactically, and the camera must allow players to use terrain as visual information. High ground is visible as high ground. Chokepoints read as chokepoints. Unit follow mode locks the camera to a selected squad or Bloodline Member, tracking them with a smooth follow that the player can override with any manual camera input. Formation overview mode pulls the camera back to a calculated height that shows the player's entire active force in a single frame — useful when issuing army-wide orders or evaluating an engagement before committing.

What the player sees when they look at the tactical viewport is a world rendered at the visual standard of the game as a whole: realistic proportions, serious palette, no cartoon abstraction. Terrain tiles are not uniform — forests are dense and shadowed, rivers catch light, hills create real shadow fall-off on the ground beneath them. Faith structures are architecturally distinct: a Covenant of Flame temple is built in dark stone with fire elements, visible from a distance, and the glow of its presence affects the ambient light in its immediate radius. Fortifications — walls, gates, towers — are constructed to scale with the units that man them, and their health state is visible in their appearance. A wall under sustained siege shows damage progressively: cracks appear, battlements crumble, smoke rises before the structure falls.

Squad formations are the core unit of visual information in the tactical viewport. A full five-member squad presents a coherent formation appropriate to its unit type: a Ironmark Ironclad squad stands in a tight shield-wall configuration, their charcoal-iron armor catching light uniformly. A Hartvale Verdant Warden hybrid squad holds a looser spread appropriate to mixed arms. As members fall, the formation does not simply shrink to a smaller version of itself. The remaining members spread slightly, their positioning becomes less precise, and their individual animations carry a different energy — more aggressive, more desperate, or more cautious depending on the unit type's Ferocity Under Loss traits. For Ironmark squads specifically, the Ferocity Under Loss system has a direct visual expression: surviving members move with greater intensity, their attack animations are harder, their stance more aggressive. The game communicates through movement that these survivors are fighting harder, not retreating. This is not accomplished through a stat popup. It is accomplished through animation priority shifts as squad count drops below threshold.

Bloodline Members are visually distinct from squads in every way that matters. Where a squad is a formation of five similar-looking soldiers, a Bloodline Member is a single named individual with a unique visual profile — their house colors rendered with more detail, their equipment reflecting their functional commitment (War role carries heavier armor, Trade role carries less but moves faster, Defense role carries a shield device that visually communicates their anchoring function), and a subtle persistent visual effect that marks them as the narrative anchor of their bloodline's presence on the field. This effect should be understated — not a glowing aura, but something in the lighting and detail resolution that makes them read as more individually important than the soldiers around them. When a Bloodline Member is under threat, a distinctive alert state activates on their portrait in the HUD without pulling the camera — the player's peripheral awareness is engaged before they are interrupted.

House visual identity carries through to the battlefield in ways that are meaningful rather than decorative. Ironmark forces are charcoal iron and dark — their formations read as heavy, compact, and deliberate on terrain. Hartvale forces carry harvest gold and deep green at their banners and unit trim, making their open-field armies visually expansive and agriculturally rooted. Stonehelm formations are grey and dense, built around defensive positioning and fortification aesthetics. When two houses are engaged, the color language of their respective forces makes the engagement readable at a glance — where the grey meets the gold, where the iron presses against the burgundy thorns of Thornwick. Small house banners carried by squads — not large, not distracting, but present — reinforce identification without requiring any text label to be read.

The squad selection interface is direct and low-friction. Left-click selects a single squad. Click-drag draws a selection box that captures all squads within it — standard RTS convention, executed cleanly. Shift-click adds squads to or removes them from the current selection. Control-number groups assign squads to numbered hotkeys for fast recall. When a squad is selected, a subtle highlight ring appears at its base — understated, not a cartoon glow, but a rim-light effect at ground level that makes the selected formation readable without obscuring the actual unit models. Multi-selection shows all selected squads in the command panel at the bottom of the screen, with each squad's portrait visible and their collective composition summarized.

Formation commands are issued through a formation wheel that appears on right-click-hold when squads are selected. The options are: Advance (move to location in current formation), Charge (aggressive advance, breaks formation on contact for maximum initial impact), Withdraw (controlled retreat, maintaining facing), Hold Ground (no movement, defensive stance, bonus armor), and Pursue (follow fleeing enemy, high priority). Army-level orders — issued when the player has selected a large number of squads — have a separate interface layer that collapses individual squad controls in favor of directional and strategic commands: Push Left Flank, Reinforce Center, Fall Back to Line.

### SECTION C: THE HUD — PERSISTENT UI LAYER

The HUD in Bloodlines must accomplish something genuinely difficult: it must be always present, always readable, and almost never intrusive. A player in the middle of a precision cavalry engagement cannot have their sightlines broken by a resource panel. A player who is watching a coalition form against them on the diplomatic map cannot have their strategic thinking interrupted by a tactical alert. The HUD must be peripheral in attention terms even when it is central in screen-position terms — its information must be passively readable without demanding focus.

The resource panel anchors to the top of the screen in a horizontal strip. Six resources are represented: gold, food, water, wood, stone, and iron. Each resource has its own icon, and the icon language is designed for instant identification without text labels — gold is a stylized coin-weight, food is a sheaf of grain, water is a contained vessel form, wood is an axe-and-log form, stone is a rough-cut block, iron is a smelted ingot. These are not bright, primary-colored icons. They are rendered in the game's palette — weathered, realistic, material-specific. Below each icon, the current stock is shown as a number, and a secondary smaller indicator shows the net flow rate — positive in the house's secondary color, negative in a deep red that reads as loss without requiring interpretation. A player can glance at the resource strip and understand in under a second: gold is high and climbing, iron is moderate but falling. No tooltips required for basic status.

The Conviction indicator occupies a dedicated space in the HUD, positioned to the left of the minimap rather than in the resource strip, because it is not a resource — it is a moral axis, and treating it as a resource would misrepresent its nature. The Conviction display is a visual scale, not a bar. It takes the form of a paired symbol drawn from the house's identity: for Ironmark, an iron weight on a balance. For Hartvale, a harvest scale. The balance tilts — not to a percentage, but to a visible lean — based on the aggregate of a player's choices over the course of the match. A balanced scale suggests a dynasty that has made choices of mixed moral weight. A scale tilted hard to one side communicates a dynasty committed to a conviction path, whether honor or cruelty. The visual language must be readable without a legend, because the balance between honor and cruelty is one of the oldest visual metaphors in human history.

Faith intensity is represented through a covenant-specific visual that occupies the space just beneath the Conviction indicator. The five tiers — Latent, Active, Devout, Fervent, Apex — are communicated through the brightness and activity of the covenant's visual symbol. A Covenant of Flame symbol at Latent tier is a small cold ember. At Apex, it is a full flame, visibly animated, casting a subtle warm light on the surrounding HUD panel. The symbol does not use text labels for the tiers during normal play — the visual state is the communication. Hovering reveals the tier name and a brief description of active effects, but the player should never need to hover to know whether their faith is strong or fading.

Population status is rendered as a compact panel that shows total population, current military enrollment as a proportion of total, and a single-color loyalty indicator for the player's home province. It is not a detailed governance view — detailed governance lives in the Territory Map screen. The HUD population panel exists only to give the player a quick read on whether a population crisis is approaching, communicated by a color shift from amber-healthy to grey-stressed to dark-red-critical.

The alert and notification queue sits in the upper right, below the resource strip. Alerts arrive as small illustrated cards that stack vertically — each card shows a small scene-icon representing the alert type and a one-line text statement. A Bloodline Member under threat is a portrait-and-sword icon. A trade agreement expiring is a parchment-and-seal icon. A faith intensity drop is the covenant symbol with a downward indicator. Cards are displayed for several seconds then fade unless the player dismisses or acts on them. Urgent alerts — Bloodline Member in mortal danger, Great Reckoning threshold approaching, Trueborn Summons — receive a distinct visual treatment: larger card, brief audio punctuation, a border treatment that distinguishes them from routine notifications. The queue never exceeds five simultaneous cards visible. If more arrive than can be shown, they are held in a notification archive accessible through a dedicated panel.

The minimap anchors to the lower right of the screen. It shows terrain topology in simplified form using the same color language as the world — grey stone highlands, green lowlands, brown desert, white frost. Territory ownership is communicated through a thin border wash of the owning house's primary color along province edges rather than a full color fill, which preserves terrain readability. Enemy forces appear as small house-colored dots. The player's own forces are slightly larger and brighter. Faith structures pulse faintly with their covenant's color at the minimap level. Bloodline Members are represented as a distinct marker — a small diamond rather than a dot — so the player always knows at a glance where their named anchors are on the field without needing to search.

The match stage indicator occupies the lower center of the HUD, between the squad command panel and the minimap. It shows the current stage — Stage 1 through Stage 3, plus the End Stage — as a subtle progression mark. It is not a timer in the traditional sense. Stage transitions are driven by game events, not elapsed time, so the indicator shows proximity to transition conditions rather than counting down a clock. When a stage transition is imminent — when the population threshold or the faith development threshold is close — the indicator brightens and gains a slow pulse, preparing the player for the approaching change in game state without alarming them.

Victory condition progress occupies a narrow strip on the right side of the HUD, vertical, showing all active victory paths for the current player with minimal visual footprint. Each path is represented by its symbol — a crown for Divine Right, crossed swords for Military Conquest, linked rings for Alliance Victory, a rising flame for Faith Ascendancy, scales for Currency Dominance. The symbol's fill level communicates proximity to completion. It never shows percentages or numbers for enemy dynasties' progress — that information belongs to the Territory Map or diplomatic intelligence, not the persistent HUD. The player sees their own path and a relative visual sense of the competitive field. Drama is preserved by the limitation.

### SECTION D: UNIT COMMAND INTERFACE

When a player selects a squad, the bottom quarter of the screen activates the unit command interface — a dedicated panel that replaces the idle state of that zone and gives the player a full command surface for everything related to the selected unit or formation. This panel is the heartbeat of tactical play. It must be readable at speed, operable without precise cursor work, and visually consistent with the game's identity.

The squad portrait panel, when a single squad is selected, shows all five squad members individually. Each member is rendered as a small character portrait — not an icon, a face — in their house colors with their armor and equipment visible. Portrait slots are arranged left to right in order of squad seniority, with the squad sergeant (implicitly the first survivor and leader) on the left. Portrait borders communicate health state: a clean dark border for healthy, a cracked border for wounded, and a greyed-out portrait with a still expression for a fallen member. When a member falls in battle, their portrait does not disappear immediately — it fades over several seconds, communicating the loss before the slot empties. This is a deliberate pacing choice. Death should register, even at the squad level.

Veteran status is communicated through a small mark on the portrait — a notch-count system drawn from the house's aesthetic (Ironmark uses iron rivet marks, Hartvale uses harvest knot marks, Stonehelm uses stone chisel marks). One mark is a seasoned squad. Three marks is a formation that has survived significant engagements. Special traits earned through battle — Ironmark's Ferocity Under Loss reaching its threshold, Hartvale's terrain-specific bonuses, Thornwick's poison-blade expertise — appear as small secondary symbols below the portrait strip, visible without hovering but expandable on hover for precise description.

The Bloodline Member command panel is a distinct visual system from the squad panel, because Bloodline Members are not squads. When a Bloodline Member is selected — alone or in a group with squads — their panel occupies the center of the command surface and is rendered at a larger scale. The Bloodline Member's name is present and prominent. Their portrait is rendered at higher resolution and with more individual character than a squad portrait — this is a specific named person with a story, and the interface should reflect that weight. Their conviction stance is visible in the portrait's framing — a member deep in the Honor path has a clean, upright portrait framing; a member deep in the Cruelty path has a darker, harder framing. Their current commitment role is shown through a visual role badge. Their relationships to other Bloodline Members currently on the field are shown as small connecting lines to miniaturized secondary portraits.

Formation commands are accessible through a compact radial menu bound to the F key when squads are selected. The radial is simple and fast: six positions, each with a clear icon — Advance, Charge, Withdraw, Hold Ground, Pursue, and Custom Formation. Custom Formation opens a sub-menu for specific formation shapes — line, column, wedge, arc — that are applied to the current selection. The radial is designed for speed; a player should be able to issue a formation change in under a second without precise cursor targeting.

Attack-move is bound to A-click, the RTS standard, and its behavior is conventional: selected squads move toward the target point and attack any enemies encountered en route. Patrol is a right-click-hold option from the command panel. Hold Ground is a single-click from the formation radial. Pursue requires a target and is issued by shift-clicking an enemy unit after selecting Pursue from the radial — squads under Pursue orders will not stop chasing their target until the target is destroyed, they leave the player's vision radius, or a new order is issued.

The recruitment interface is accessible from the Keep/Town Hall structure when the player's camera is positioned on their home territory or through the management screens. Squads are built using a slider-and-template system. A new recruitment order presents the player with their house's available unit templates — Ironmark's Ironclad, Shieldwall, Ashguard, and so on — and a resource cost summary. Composition sliders allow the player to adjust the relative weight of unit types within a squad within house-defined parameters. A squad cannot be built outside of house doctrine composition limits. The interface shows the resource cost update in real time as sliders move so the player always knows what they are spending before they commit.

Rally points are set by shift-right-clicking any point on the terrain after selecting a production structure. The rally point persists as a small house-colored pennant marker on the map, visible on the tactical viewport and the minimap. Staging areas — pre-designated assembly zones for gathering forces before an advance — are created through the territory management screen and visualized as a soft highlight zone on the tactical viewport when the player is in army-management mode.

### SECTION E: THE BLOODLINE MANAGEMENT SCREEN

The Bloodline Management Screen is the strategic nerve center for the dynasty itself — the layer where the player acts not as a general but as the head of a family, making decisions that will unfold over years of game time and potentially decide succession, alliance, and the moral shape of the bloodline. Where the Keep is a visualization, this screen is an interface: designed for action, not just observation.

The screen opens by presenting all active Bloodline Members as a horizontal roster of portrait panels, larger than their HUD representations and rendered with full detail. The roster is arranged by functional role by default, with War members on the left, then Defense, then Trade, then Diplomatic roles, then unassigned members. Each portrait panel shows the member's name, their age in game-years, a small conviction-stance indicator, and their current assignment status. Portrait borders carry subtle house-color filigree that deepens or brightens based on the member's standing within the bloodline — the heir apparent has a distinct framing that visually marks their succession position without a text label required.

Clicking any member portrait opens their individual detail panel, which slides in from the right of the screen. This panel shows the member in full figure — rendered at the same visual quality as the Keep characters, reflecting their current role attire, age, and conviction path visually. To the right of the figure, their detailed profile is laid out: name and generation number; current age and projected lifespan range; trait list with trait icons (traits earned through experience have a different visual treatment than traits held from birth); faith alignment shown as the covenant symbol with intensity level; conviction stance shown as the balance visual from the HUD but at larger scale and with the specific choices that shaped it listed below; current commitment role with a description of what that commitment is doing for the bloodline; and their relationship web, showing all other Bloodline Members with a relationship indicator — positive, neutral, negative, rivalrous, devoted.

The commitment assignment interface allows the player to drag a member's commitment badge to a different role slot. The interface clearly communicates the cost of reassignment — a member who has been in their current role long enough to develop role-specific depth loses some of that depth when reassigned, and the interface shows this as a skill degradation preview before the player confirms. Roles cannot always be reassigned freely — a member committed to War during active conflict may require the player to resolve the conflict or accept a penalty before the reassignment completes. These constraints are shown through a lock indicator with a brief condition description, not through an error dialog.

The marriage arrangement interface is accessible through a dedicated tab on the Bloodline Management Screen. Available marriage candidates from allied, neutral, or diplomatically approachable dynasties are shown as portrait cards with their house affiliation, known traits, and the projected benefits of the match — which bloodline strengths or weaknesses the marriage would address. Marriage is not framed as a transaction in the UI. It is framed as a diplomatic and dynastic act. The proposal flow requires the player to choose a candidate, review the projected impact, and confirm — at which point the proposal is sent diplomatically and may be rejected. If accepted, the new member enters the Keep visualization and their portrait joins the roster.

Succession planning is accessible through a dedicated panel that is always visible as a secondary tab on the Bloodline Management Screen, but which becomes prominently foregrounded when the current head of bloodline is in danger or aging into terminal decline. The succession panel shows all eligible candidates in order of current standing, with each candidate's portrait paired with a projected impact visualization: what changes in bloodline military output, economic output, conviction trajectory, and faith alignment if this person assumes leadership. The projections are not shown as numbers — they are shown as trajectory arrows applied to the same visual indicators used in the HUD. If a candidate's assumption would push conviction toward cruelty, the conviction scale visual in their projection tilts. If it would weaken military standing, the sword icon dims. The player should be able to compare candidates visually, not numerically.

When the head of bloodline dies — whether from natural age, battle wounds, assassination, or sacrifice — the full Succession Interface activates as a full-screen overlay rather than a panel. This is covered in the Event and Notification System section, but the Bloodline Management Screen's succession tab feeds into it directly.

The family tree visualization occupies a tab of its own. It renders generations vertically, with the founding generation at the top and current generation at the bottom. Each member node is a portrait with their name and birth-death years. Living members have full portrait rendering. Deceased members have a faded portrait with a visual mark of their cause of death — a sword icon for battle death, a flame for sacrifice, a crown for natural succession death. Marriage connections are shown as horizontal linking lines between nodes. Children descend from the connection point. The tree grows as the match progresses and shrinks as members die, and its state at the end of the match is one of the elements captured in the legacy record.

### SECTION F: FAITH MANAGEMENT INTERFACE

Faith in Bloodlines is not a secondary resource system. It is a covenantal relationship — the dynasty's commitment to a divine authority that grants power in exchange for demonstrated devotion. The Faith Management Interface must communicate this weight. It must not look like a skill tree. It must not look like a tech tree. It must look like the management of a covenant — serious, consequential, and tied to choices that cannot be easily undone.

The Faith Management Interface is accessed through a dedicated menu, distinct from the Bloodline Management Screen. It opens with the covenant's symbol dominant in the center of the screen, rendered at full quality — for the Covenant of Flame, an architectural rendering of a flame altar; for the Covenant of Stone, a carved lintel; for the Covenant of Seed, a harvest circle; for the Covenant of Blood, a consecrated blade. The covenant's visual language fills the frame of the interface without being garish. The player is operating within their faith, not looking at it from outside.

Covenant selection occurs at the end of Stage 1, and its interface is a specific moment in the game — a full-screen presentation of the four covenants with their symbols, their core promises, and their core demands. This is not a menu. It is a declaration. The player is not selecting from a dropdown. They are choosing which divine authority their bloodline will serve, and the interface must communicate the gravity of that choice through its design: slow transitions, full visual rendering of each covenant's aesthetic language, no hurry-up UI. The choice, once made, is irreversible. The interface must make the player feel that irreversibility before they confirm.

After selection, the doctrine path choice — Light versus Dark expression of the covenant — is presented as a second moment of weight within the same interface flow. The two paths are shown with their visual differences made explicit. A Covenant of Flame / Light path doctrine is warm amber and controlled fire. A Covenant of Flame / Dark path doctrine is deep red and uncontrolled burning. The player understands the difference visually before they read the mechanical description.

The faith intensity meter in the Faith Management Interface is the same visual system as the HUD covenant symbol, but displayed at full scale. The five tiers — Latent, Active, Devout, Fervent, Apex — are named and their conditions clearly articulated here. In the HUD, the tier is communicated visually without text. In the Faith Management Interface, the player can see precisely where they are, what keeps them there, and what would push them up or down. The interface shows the active rituals contributing to intensity maintenance, the buildings contributing to faith spread, and the time-since-last-ritual indicator that communicates when decay will begin.

The ritual scheduling interface presents available rituals as a schedule panel. Rituals have resource costs and time commitments — a player cannot schedule a major ritual in the middle of a military campaign without accepting the resource cost and the attention split. Rituals are shown with their effects on intensity, their duration, and their resource requirements. A ritual in progress is shown as an animated progress mark on the covenant symbol. Missing a scheduled ritual does not cause an immediate intensity drop — it reduces the maintenance rate, and decay begins if too many rituals are skipped in succession.

The faith infrastructure build interface connects to the building system. From the Faith Management Interface, the player can designate faith structures — temples, shrines, ceremonial gates — and assign their construction to the territory map. The structures are shown as architectural illustrations appropriate to their covenant before placement, giving the player a visual sense of what they are adding to their civilization's landscape.

The Covenant Test notification and interface are covered in the Event and Notification System section. Within the Faith Management Interface, past Covenant Tests are recorded with their resolution — what was demanded, what the dynasty did, whether they met the terms. This history is visible to the player and builds a covenant relationship record.

Faith spread is visualized as a color-wash overlay on the territory map that can be toggled from the Faith Management Interface or from the Territory map screen. Each covenant has its own spread color — Flame is deep amber, Stone is muted grey-gold, Seed is spring green, Blood is dark crimson. The overlay shows which territories are under the influence of which faith, with intensity communicated through saturation. Mixed-faith territories show blended colors where they are genuinely contested. The player can see at a glance how their faith is spreading relative to rival faiths, where their mission structures are having effect, and where their faith's influence is weakest.

### SECTION G: DIPLOMATIC INTERFACE

The Diplomatic Interface is a council table, not a transaction terminal. When the player opens it, they should feel the weight of political relationships — allies who might betray, enemies who might negotiate, dynasties whose power is growing in ways that will eventually require a response. The interface must communicate the full political landscape of the match with the same clarity and weight that the tactical viewport communicates the military one.

The interface opens to a full political roster — all known dynasties and significant minor tribes arranged as portrait panels along a table-styled interface. Each dynasty's portrait panel shows their leader's face, their house colors prominently, and their current diplomatic standing with the player communicated through the border treatment of the panel: a deep, warm border for Allied, a clean neutral border for Cordial, no border treatment for Neutral, a cold hardened border for Hostile, and a dark fractured border for War. The player can read the political landscape in one sweep — five dynasties, two allied, one hostile, one at war — before reading a single label.

Each dynasty panel expands on click to show the detailed diplomatic record: history of all past agreements and breaches, the dynasty's relative military strength, their faith alignment, their current conviction standing (if intelligenced), and the active agreements in place. This historical record matters because Bloodlines is a game where relationships have memory. An alliance betrayal from fifty game-years ago is still a factor. The diplomatic record makes that history visible.

The agreement negotiation interface opens as a bilateral panel: the player's dynasty on the left, the target dynasty on the right, with the proposed agreement terms in the center. Agreement types available include trade agreements (which specify which resources flow in which direction and at what rates), non-aggression pacts (with specified duration and recognized exemptions), military alliances (which specify mutual defense conditions and the scope of required assistance), marriage arrangements (which route to the Bloodline Management Screen for candidate selection), and tributary arrangements (which involve ongoing payment in exchange for military protection or neutrality). Each agreement type has its own visual template for the center panel — a parchment-form specific to the agreement type, rendered in a way that communicates the seriousness of the commitment.

The proposal and counter-proposal flow works through a sequential exchange. The player sets terms and sends the proposal. The game resolves the AI dynasty's response — acceptance, rejection, or counter — and presents the counter as a modified version of the proposal parchment with changed terms highlighted. The player can accept the counter, modify their position and re-propose, or withdraw. The flow is not time-pressured unless the game state creates pressure (an imminent invasion might make a defensive alliance proposal more urgent, and the interface communicates that urgency through the opposing dynasty's posture in the bilateral panel).

Alliance Betrayal is one of the most significant acts a player can take diplomatically. Moving from Allied to War — especially without recognized grounds — is treated by the interface as an event of moral weight. The confirmation panel for a betrayal-declaration does not look like a standard confirmation dialog. It presents the active alliance pact as a visual document and requires the player to acknowledge what they are breaking. The conviction impact is shown explicitly at the moment of declaration: this act moves the conviction scale toward cruelty by a specific amount, visible before confirmation. The player who betrays an ally does so with full awareness of the cost.

The war declaration interface includes a grounds selection step — recognized grounds for war being territorial encroachment, treaty violation, faith persecution, or dynastic insult. Declaring war on recognized grounds incurs less conviction cost and less diplomatic reputation damage than unprovoked declaration. The interface shows this difference clearly: a declaration with recognized grounds is framed as a formal act; an unprovoked declaration is framed as an aggression, with the reputation impact prominently displayed.

The Trueborn City diplomatic channel is visually distinct from all other diplomatic interfaces. Communication through the Trueborn City is rendered as a formal audience — the player's leader presented before a stylized Trueborn assembly rather than in a bilateral table format. The visual weight of this channel communicates that the Trueborn City is not a neutral party but a sovereign entity whose endorsement or opposition carries civilizational force.

The global reputation display occupies a summary panel within the diplomatic interface — a single composite indicator, rendered as the conviction scale but in a public-facing orientation, showing how the broader world perceives this dynasty's moral standing. High honor reputation opens certain diplomatic options (other dynasties are more willing to negotiate from good faith). Low reputation or cruelty-path reputation opens different options (intimidation-based agreements, tributary extraction) while closing others.

### SECTION H: TERRITORY AND GOVERNANCE MAP

The Territory Map is the strategic layer — the province-level view of the world that sits above the tactical viewport in abstraction but below the diplomatic interface in political weight. This is where the player sees their civilization as a shape on the map: how much ground they hold, how that ground is governed, where their people's loyalty is strong or fracturing, and where their armies are positioned in a strategic rather than tactical sense.

The Territory Map opens as a full-screen view that temporarily suspends the tactical viewport without pausing the game. It is rendered in a cartographic aesthetic that matches the world's visual standard — not a clean vector map, but a map that feels like it was drawn by hand in the world's visual register. Province borders are rendered as irregular terrain-following lines rather than geometric boundaries. Territory ownership is communicated through a thin border wash in the owning dynasty's house color along each province's edge — the same approach as the minimap, but at full detail. The province interior shows terrain type through texture rather than color fill, preserving geographic readability while still communicating ownership.

Population loyalty per province is communicated through the warmth of the province's terrain texture. A loyal province has a slightly warmer, brighter rendering — not a drastic change, but a visual quality that reads as healthy and settled. A stressed province is cooler and more muted. A province approaching uprising has a visible tension in its rendering — cracked ground effects, smoke rise indicators at settlement markers, a visual unease that communicates approaching crisis before the player has to open a governance tooltip.

Bloodline Member assignments to territory are shown as small figure icons placed on the province map at their current location. A member committed to the Defense role and assigned to anchor a specific province appears as their house-colored figure marker, with a subtle territorial resonance effect around them — a reinforced visual quality to the province they are holding that communicates their stabilizing presence without a text label. Moving a Defense-committed member away from their anchor province would visually de-stabilize the province in real time on this map, showing the player the consequence before they confirm the move.

Caravan routes are visualized as thin animated lines connecting trade-agreement partners, moving along terrain-appropriate paths between settlements. The thickness of the line communicates the volume of trade on that route. Active, prosperous trade routes are warm gold. Routes under threat — from bandits, military pressure, or failed agreements — are thin and cooler in tone. A route that has been cut shows as a broken line. The player can see their trade network's health geographically without opening the diplomatic interface.

Faith spread is togglable on the Territory Map as an overlay layer, using the same color-wash system described in the Faith Management Interface section. When toggled, the covenant-colored wash over provinces makes the faith distribution of the entire world legible in a single view. The player can see where their faith is dominant, where it is contested, and where rival faiths hold territory that may be a target for faith-spread investment.

Minor tribe territories are shown in a neutral warm brown, distinct from major dynasty colors. Their settlement markers are smaller than city markers. They are labeled with their tribal name on hover. Minor tribes that have been brought under diplomatic arrangement show a thin border treatment in the annexing dynasty's color rather than full territorial fill — they are aligned, not absorbed.

The Trueborn City is shown on the Territory Map as a distinct settlement type — architecturally unique from all other city markers, rendered in a neutral silver-white that communicates its political independence from all house affiliations. Its trade radius, when active, is shown as a soft white circle extending across neighboring provinces. Dynasties within that radius receive a visual indicator on their province that the City's trade influence is active.

The Great Reckoning threshold indicator lives in the Territory Map view. As any single dynasty approaches control of 70% of total territory, a visible tension develops in the map — provinces held by the approaching dynasty begin to carry a subtle red-gold warning tint at their borders, and a threshold indicator appears in the corner of the Territory Map view showing how close the most powerful dynasty is to triggering the Reckoning. This indicator does not appear suddenly — it builds as the threshold approaches, giving all players visual warning that a civilizational crisis is forming before it actually arrives.

### SECTION I: EVENT AND NOTIFICATION SYSTEM

Bloodlines fires political events as emergent consequences of game state — a faith intensity drop, a bloodline member's aging, a trade agreement approaching expiry, a rival dynasty nearing a victory threshold. These events must reach the player without breaking the game's flow, without creating anxiety through excessive interruption, and without ever feeling like a menu popup that belongs in a mobile strategy game. The notification system must carry the tone of the game: weighty, consequential, and visually coherent with the world.

Routine notifications arrive as the HUD alert cards described in the HUD section — small, illustrated, stacked, passively dismissible. Faith events use the covenant symbol as their notification icon. Dynastic events use a bloodline portrait or family tree icon. Diplomatic events use a seal-and-parchment icon. World events — weather shifts, natural disasters affecting territory, the emergence of minor tribe threats — use terrain-appropriate icons. The card stays visible long enough to be read at a glance, then fades. The player who does not act on it can find it in the notification archive.

Choice events are different from informational notifications. When the game state demands a player decision — not just an alert, but a fork — the notification card expands into a choice panel that occupies the center of the screen without fully obscuring the tactical viewport. The choice panel is rendered as a specific document type appropriate to the event: a counsel note for strategic decisions, a covenant scroll for faith choices, a diplomatic proposal form for political ones. The choices are presented as two or three distinct visual options with brief descriptions and projected consequences. The consequence projections are shown as the same visual indicators used elsewhere — conviction scale, resource flow arrows, Bloodline Member relationship impacts — rather than as text-only descriptions. The player should be able to evaluate choices visually before reading the full description.

The Succession Interface is the most significant event in the game's notification system. When the head of bloodline dies, the game does not continue in real time — succession is a freeze-frame moment. The full screen dims, and the Succession Panel rises to prominence. All eligible candidates are shown in a ranked display with their portrait, their succession claim rank, and their projected bloodline impact summary — rendered as the same visual trajectory system described in the Bloodline Management Screen's succession planning section. The player must choose. Until they do, the game waits. The visual weight of this interface must match the narrative weight of the moment: the dynasty's future is being decided, and the game should feel like it knows that.

When a succession choice creates a contested claim — when another bloodline member or external dynasty disputes the succession — an uprising event fires. The uprising is communicated through the Territory Map: the disputing province shows immediate loyalty decay visualizations, and a military movement alert fires for any forces rallying to the pretender. These events arrive not as a single notification but as a cascade, each card appearing in sequence, communicating the chain reaction of the contested succession.

The Covenant Test notification is visually distinct from all other events. When the covenant fires a test — demanding that the dynasty demonstrate its faith through a specific act — the notification takes the form of the covenant's full symbol expanding to fill the center of the screen briefly before resolving into the choice panel. The test demand is written in the covenant's aesthetic language: a Covenant of Flame test is accompanied by flame effects at the edges of the panel. The choice available is always binary: meet the demand and maintain or advance faith intensity, or refuse and accept the intensity drop and conviction consequence. The gravity of the choice is communicated by the visual weight of the notification.

The Trueborn Summons is the single most serious notification in the game. When the Trueborn City summons all dynasty leaders — a civilizational crisis event that can fire in response to Great Reckoning approach, to covenant extremism reaching the world-threatening threshold, or to other trigger conditions — the notification supersedes all other HUD elements. The screen border briefly pulses in the Trueborn City's silver-white. The notification card is the largest format in the system. It does not stack in the notification queue — it interrupts. The visual language communicates unambiguously that this is not a routine alert. The player is being called before a power that exists above all house interests, and the interface must communicate the weight of that summons.

### SECTION J: VICTORY CONDITION TRACKING

All six victory paths are active for all dynasties simultaneously. The player can advance on multiple paths at once, and so can rivals. The Victory Condition Tracking system must give the player a clear read on their own progress, a meaningful but incomplete read on rivals' progress (intelligence is imperfect), and a visual sense of which paths are near-completion without removing the tension that makes victory dramatic.

Victory condition progress is tracked in the dedicated right-side HUD strip described in the HUD section. The player's own path progress is fully visible: their path's symbol fills proportionally as they advance toward the threshold. For paths where rival progress is known through diplomacy or intelligence — a dynasty whose territory control is visible, whose economic output is observable — the rival's path symbol shows a comparative fill in a muted version of their house color. For paths where rival progress is opaque — faith ascendancy if the player has no faith-spread intelligence in that region, alliance formation happening through private channels — the rival's indicator shows an unknown state, communicated as a translucent fill that acknowledges the gap rather than pretending certainty.

The Divine Right declaration is the most visible victory event in the game. When a dynasty makes the declaration, it is announced to all players simultaneously through a full-screen notification — not the Trueborn Summons severity, but the next level below it. The declaring dynasty's house colors and symbol are shown prominently. The spread clock activates: from the declaration, the dynasty must sustain the claim for a defined period while the world responds. The spread clock interface is visible as a countdown element in the HUD for all players — the declaring dynasty sees their own countdown as a commitment they must protect; rival dynasties see it as the threat they must extinguish. Coalition-forming in response to a Divine Right declaration is communicated through the diplomatic interface: allies being approached for coalition membership receive notifications, and the forming coalition's progress is shown as a linking visualization in the diplomatic roster.

Currency Dominance threshold approach is communicated through the resource panel and the Territory Map. As a dynasty's gold holdings and trade route dominance push toward the Currency Dominance threshold, the resource panel's gold icon gains a subtle golden intensification that signals economic near-supremacy before the threshold is crossed. The Territory Map shows their trade routes thickening as economic dominance grows. The threshold crossing itself fires an economic dominance alert for all other dynasties, giving them the information they need to respond.

Alliance Victory progress is communicated through a dedicated display in the victory tracking strip. The population acceptance percentage — the proportion of the world's total population that must be living under allied-dynasty governance for the Alliance Victory to complete — is shown as a fill indicator for the alliance's combined symbol. When the threshold approaches, the indicator brightens. Active resistance — populations revolting against allied governance, reducing the acceptance percentage — is shown as a flicker in the fill indicator, communicating instability in the alliance's claim without requiring the player to open a detailed governance report.

The Trueborn Rise Arc's activation threshold is the most complex victory visualization in the game, because it requires communicating a cumulative civilizational condition rather than a single dynasty's advancement. The Trueborn City's symbol on the Territory Map and in the victory tracking HUD gains a gradual brightening as the Arc's conditions are approached. The player can see the Arc threshold coming — can see the City's influence growing, can see the preconditions building — because the visual language has been communicating it continuously throughout the match, not as a sudden announcement. When the Arc activates, the City's silver-white expands visually on the Territory Map, and the Arc's progress to completion is shown as the City's symbol filling.

### SECTION K: MATCH END — RESOLUTION AND LEGACY

The match does not end with a score screen. It ends with a reckoning — a moment of civilizational account-taking that reflects the moral and strategic arc of the dynasty's existence over the full duration of the match. The Match End sequence must feel like the conclusion of an epic rather than the end of a session. It must honor the hours invested by treating the dynasty's history as material worth presenting.

When a victory condition is achieved — by the player or by a rival dynasty — the game does not cut immediately to a summary screen. There is a resolution sequence appropriate to the victory type. A Military Conquest victory sees the victorious army's forces shown on the conquered ground, the defeated dynasty's banner lowered, the victor's raised. A Divine Right victory sees the declaring dynasty's leader presented at the Trueborn City before a gathered representation of the world's dynasties. A Faith Ascendancy victory sees the covenant's symbol rising to dominance across the world map, the faith-spread overlay reaching its fullest expression. Each victory type has its own thirty-second visual resolution before the Legacy Screen activates.

The Legacy Screen is a chronicle, not a scorecard. It is rendered as an illustrated document — the aesthetic of a historical record in the world's visual language, written after the fact, as though a future historian compiled it. The chronicle structure presents the match in acts: Stage 1 foundation, the dynastic decisions that shaped the bloodline's identity, the faith covenant selection and its consequences, the military engagements of significance, the diplomatic relationships that held or broke, and the final resolution. Key events are shown as small illustrated vignettes rather than text-only entries — a significant battle is a scene, not a line item; the marriage that formed the dynasty's most important alliance is a portrait pair; the sacrifice that achieved the born-of-sacrifice power is a moment rendered with the weight it deserves.

Notable bloodline members and their fates are shown in a portrait gallery within the Legacy Screen — every named member who lived during the match, their birth and death years, their functional role and its impact, a one-line legacy description. Bloodline members who died in battle are shown with a warrior's visual mark. Members who were sacrificed are shown with the covenant symbol. Members who died of age are shown with the family tree mark. Members who were assassinated are shown with a different mark entirely, and if the assassination was perpetrated by a specific rival dynasty, that dynasty's house color appears in the mark.

Faith trajectory is shown as a visual arc — the covenant symbol's intensity rendered across the match timeline, showing where faith was strong, where it faltered, where it recovered, and where the covenant tests fell. Conviction arc is shown as the balance visual across the same timeline — a graph, but rendered as a physical balance tilting back and forth across the match's moral history, ending at whatever position the dynasty reached.

The Exile State — the condition when a dynasty is defeated but not destroyed, surviving in a reduced and subordinate state — has its own UI treatment. The player in exile sees the tactical viewport and territory map of their remaining territory with a visual dimming at the borders — the world has closed in around them. The HUD resources reflect their reduced state. A dedicated Exile Status panel shows the conditions required to emerge from exile: the loyalty threshold that must be reached, the resource recovery that must occur, the diplomatic rehabilitation that must be negotiated. The path back is visible. The UI must communicate that exile is not the end of the dynasty's story, only a chapter — and that the chapter can end in restoration or in final defeat, depending on the player's choices within it.

The post-match legacy record is written to the player's dynasty archive — a persistent record across matches, not just within the single session. The dynasty's history, the choices made, the faith held, the moral arc taken — these are preserved as the foundation from which future matches can continue the bloodline's story. The legacy record screen offers the player the option to continue the dynasty in the next match with inherited traits and starting advantages, or to close the chronicle and begin a new dynasty. The choice is presented in the same tone as all major choices in Bloodlines: with weight, without rushing.

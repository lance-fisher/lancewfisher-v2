# Bloodlines — Resource System

## Overview
The Resource system defines the economic foundation of the game. Resources are produced by population working territory, traded between factions, and consumed by military and construction activities. Resource scarcity drives conflict. Resource abundance enables expansion.

## Design Intent
Resources exist to create economic decisions that interact with population, territory, and military systems. A player who overextends militarily will drain their economy. A player who hoards resources without expanding will be outgrown. The resource system rewards balanced development and punishes neglect.

## Open Design Questions
- What are the core resource types? (Food, materials, wealth, faith tokens, political capital?)
- How are resources produced? (Population + territory + infrastructure?)
- How are resources consumed? (Army upkeep, construction, trade, sacrifice?)
- Is there resource trading between factions?
- Can resources be raided or pillaged?
- Do different territories produce different resources?
- How does faith alignment affect resource production?

### Design Content (Added 2026-03-15)

**Five Primary Resources:**

**Gold** — Currency, taxation, economic power. Used for building construction, troop recruitment, diplomacy, trade, and ransom. Generated through taxation, markets, and trade networks.

**Food** — Supports population growth and sustains armies. Food shortages cause famine and declining loyalty. Produced through farms and ranches.

**Water** — Fresh water supply and irrigation. Essential for population survival and agricultural production. Elevated to a primary resource, not a secondary terrain effect. Water as essential but not a trivial hard-counter win condition.

**Wood** — Construction material, fuel, and equipment crafting. Used for basic building construction, siege equipment, ships, and fuel for smelting and heating. Produced through lumber camps in forested territories. Wood is a renewable but location-dependent resource tied to forested regions.

**Stone** — Construction and fortification. Used for walls, towers, and major structures. Stone deposits are finite strategic resources.

**Secondary Resource:** Influence

The economy is intentionally grounded and legible. Strong core simplicity with deep interaction rather than bloated complexity.

Resource control is tied directly to territorial control. Resource nodes across the map create natural conflict points.

**Late-Game Economic Concept — Currency Dominance:**
A dynasty pursuing economic dominance can introduce its own currency into the global economy. Through successful trade networks and economic influence, the world can transition away from gold and onto the dynasty's currency standard. Economic victory occurs when the world economy becomes dependent on that dynasty's currency.

**Economic Buildings:**
- Farm — produces food
- Ranch — produces livestock food
- Market — generates gold through trade
- Lumber Camp — produces wood from forested territories
- Quarry — produces stone

### Player Manual Expansion (Added 2026-03-15)

**CONFLICT NOTE:** The Player Manual references four primary resources: gold, food, water, stone. The existing canon (above) defines five primary resources including wood. Wood is absent from the Player Manual's resource list. Both versions are preserved per archival rules. This requires explicit resolution.

**Influence Expanded:** Influence is a secondary resource representing political weight, diplomatic leverage, and the dynasty's capacity to sway events and people.

**Economy as Long-Form Power:** Because matches can last ten hours, economy is not simply about early snowball. Economy is about maintaining stability, sustaining armies, rebuilding after losses, feeding and watering population, funding faith intensity, and supporting the long-term plan.

**Currency Dominance Expansion:** A dynasty can attempt to change the economic standard away from simple gold and into its own currency. Through successful economic and trade campaigns, their currency gains adoption and eventually forces the world onto their standard. Currency adoption can force wars. If one dynasty nears currency dominance, other dynasties may refuse adoption on defined grounds and demand conflict. A timer or date can be set where battle is waged to decide outcome.

**Economic Victory as Violence:** Economic victory is not passive wealth accumulation. It is the attempt to replace the world's standard of value with your own. Money is not neutral. Trade, loyalty, dependence, and geopolitical fear orbit around monetary standards. Prosperity becomes threatening long before victory is formally declared.

Iron is a mid-to-late game resource with low strategic significance in the early game but critical military and economic importance from mid-game onward. *(Prior framing — see note above.)*

**Early Game:**
*(Prior framing — superseded 2026-04-25.)* Wood and stone dominate the early game. Initial army composition — militia, spearmen, Archers (formerly bowmen) — requires minimal iron. Iron infrastructure is not a first-build priority. Players who invest heavily in iron production early sacrifice more urgent early-game needs.

**Mid Game:**
As swordsmen become the primary military force, iron demand rises sharply. Swordsmen require iron for blades, armor, and maintenance. The transition from early spear-and-bow armies to iron-equipped swordsmen is one of the key mid-game economic pressure points. Players who secured iron nodes early now have a compounding advantage. Players who ignored iron face a bottleneck in army modernization.

**Late Game:**
Iron becomes a strategic resource second in military importance only to population itself. Demands include:
- Advanced armaments for veteran forces
- Cavalry equipment: horseshoes, barding, stirrups, lances
- Siege equipment construction and maintenance
- Fortification ironwork for upgraded keeps and walls
- Naval ironwork at scale for iron-reinforced ships

A player who holds iron node territory in the mid-game has a meaningful and expanding late-game military advantage.

**Production:**
- Requires Iron Mine building (added to Economic building category)
- Location-dependent: iron deposits are fixed terrain features, not uniformly distributed
- Higher production volumes require Smelting Furnace infrastructure (wood consumed as fuel)
- Wood-to-iron dependency chain: wood fuels smelting, creating a resource dependency that makes both resources more strategically linked than they appear individually

**Iron and Cavalry — Heavy Consumer:**
Cavalry is one of the heaviest iron consumers per unit in the game. A cavalry-focused military strategy requires substantially higher iron production than an infantry-based strategy. War horses require horseshoes, armor, and cavalry equipment before they are militarily effective. Animal husbandry (horses) and iron production are tightly coupled in a cavalry build.

**Strategic Map Implications:**
- Iron nodes are strategic conflict points beginning in mid-game
- Denying an opponent iron access delays or prevents army modernization
- Iron scarcity in early game is manageable; iron scarcity in late game is a critical vulnerability
- Iron-rich territories become targets for conquest by mid-game

### Economic Victory Path — Currency Standard (Added 2026-03-18, Fourth Ingestion)

**Expanded design from prior PROPOSED entry. Now settled as a core victory path.**

**The Mechanic:**
A dynasty pursuing economic dominance does not simply accumulate gold. It works to get the world to abandon gold as the standard and adopt its own currency in its place.

As the dynasty's trade network expands, its currency gains legitimacy through use. Other kingdoms begin accepting it, then preferring it, then depending on it. Once dependency is established, the dynasty can leverage currency control to buy off entire kingdoms' armies — paying enemy soldiers to defect, refusing to honor contracts in gold, or withholding currency access to starve rival economies.

**Late-Game Power:**
At currency dominance threshold, the dynasty does not need to defeat armies militarily. It purchases their loyalty. Enemy commanders accept payment. Enemy kingdoms' recruitment pipelines become dependent on the dominant currency. The economic victory path converts financial superiority directly into military and political outcomes without requiring proportional military force.

**Counter-play:**
Other dynasties may refuse adoption on political grounds and demand conflict to settle the question. A timer or date-determined resolution battle can be triggered when one dynasty approaches currency dominance — forcing a military confrontation over economic legitimacy rather than allowing passive economic victory.

**What currency dominance requires:**
- Sustained investment in trade infrastructure (markets, trade routes, economic buildings)
- Sustained moral or neutral conviction record (cruel dynasties face currency rejection from trading partners)
- Protection of trade networks from disruption
- Diplomatic management of potential coalition resistance before dominance is reached

### Full System Design Expansion — Sessions 6-9 (Added 2026-03-19)

## Resource Production and the Population Connection

Resources do not produce themselves. Every resource-generating building requires population in the labor pool allocated to economic activity. This is the foundational coupling between the Resource System and the Population System. A dynasty that has depleted its population through warfare or sacrifice will see resource output decline before it sees recruitment difficulty — the farms stop producing before the barracks stop filling, because agricultural labor thresholds are lower priority in the allocation engine than military labor.

**Production scaling by population allocation:**
- Agricultural labor below minimum threshold: food production enters famine risk territory regardless of farm count or farm quality. Having ten farms with no farmers produces nothing.
- Craft and economic labor below minimum threshold: markets, quarries, lumber camps, and iron mines all produce at reduced efficiency. Each building requires a minimum labor complement to operate at its rated output.
- Full labor complement: building operates at standard production rate.
- Surplus labor (beyond minimum, up to optimal threshold): production scales upward linearly. The best-manned lumber camp produces more wood than the minimum-manned one.

**The 90-second cycle intersection:** Every population cycle evaluation checks labor allocation against building requirements. The resource output numbers the player sees are not static building values — they are population-weighted production calculations that change cycle by cycle as population moves between categories.

## Building Production Chains

Bloodlines resources are not independent. They feed into each other through production dependencies that create strategic planning requirements beyond simple "build the resource building."

**The Wood-to-Iron Chain:**
Iron production requires smelting. Smelting requires fuel. Fuel is wood. A dynasty building iron production infrastructure without adequate wood supply is building something it cannot run. The Smelting Furnace consumes wood as an operational input, not as a one-time construction cost. Every cycle of iron production consumes a proportional amount of wood. A dynasty that denies an opponent's wood supply is also, with delay, attacking that opponent's iron capacity.

**The Food-Water-Population Triangle:**
Population growth depends on both food and water being above their respective thresholds. A dynasty with abundant food but inadequate water will not see population growth — water is the binding constraint when below threshold. A dynasty with adequate water but inadequate food will enter famine conditions. Both resources must be maintained above their respective floors for the population to grow. This creates two independent infrastructure requirements rather than one pooled sustenance value.

**Stone and Wood as Stage 1-2 Dominants:**
Early-game construction relies almost entirely on wood and stone. Housing Districts, Settlement Halls, Barracks, and Granaries all require these two resources in quantity. A dynasty that secures lumber and quarry territory in Stage 1 has a significant building speed advantage that compounds: more Housing Districts means higher population cap, which means more labor, which means more resource production. The early-game economic snowball is fundamentally wood and stone.

**Gold as the Universal Lubricant:**
Gold does not produce anything directly. It enables everything. Recruitment requires gold. Diplomacy requires gold. Trade requires gold. Faith rituals require gold. Every agreement requires gold. A dynasty that is resource-rich but gold-poor is a dynasty that cannot translate its resources into action. Protecting gold income — markets, taxation, trade routes — is never optional.

## The Trade Network

Trade is not a passive bonus. It is an active economic system with geography, relationships, and vulnerability.

**Trade Routes:** A trade route is established between two economic buildings (Markets) connected by a path that passes through no hostile territory and no blockaded provinces. The route generates gold and can generate resource exchanges if the dynasties involved have resource surpluses and deficits that complement each other. A gold-rich dynasty trading with an iron-rich dynasty benefits both if the arrangement is structured correctly.

**Caravan System:** Trade routes are represented by caravans — units that travel the physical path of the route on the map. Caravans are not combat units. They cannot defend themselves against a concerted military attack. An enemy that intercepts a caravan destroys the goods in transit and disrupts the route for several cycles. Protecting caravans — by military escort, by ensuring the route stays in controlled territory, by fortifying chokepoints the route passes through — is an active military-economic task.

**The Trueborn City as Trade Hub:** The Trueborn City is the center of the world trade network. Trade routes that pass through or anchor at the Trueborn City receive a significant gold yield bonus — the world's neutrally-held economic center magnifies the value of commerce that flows through it. A dynasty that conquers the Trueborn City gains control of this amplification effect. A dynasty blockaded from the Trueborn City loses access to the amplification and, critically, loses the information network that flows through trade.

**Goldgrave Trade Dependency:** Goldgrave's military recruitment is capped by active trade route count — a house-specific disadvantage that makes disrupting Goldgrave's trade network a direct military strategy. This is the only house for which economic disruption is also an army-size constraint.

## Resource Scarcity and Pressure Events

When resources drop below their critical thresholds, game events fire.

**Famine:** Fires when food production falls below the population's consumption requirement for two consecutive cycles. Famine reduces population growth to zero, begins active population decline, reduces morale of all units in affected territories, increases the risk of loyalty collapse, and generates a negative conviction event if the dynasty does not respond. A dynasty that allows famine to persist for extended periods will lose significant population before the food supply recovers.

**Water Crisis:** Fires when water supply drops below the territory's minimum threshold. Population growth ceases. Agricultural efficiency in the territory drops (irrigation fails). If water crisis persists, population begins outmigrating from the affected territory — people leave for territories with better conditions. Water crisis is harder to ignore than famine because it triggers outmigration rather than simple population decline.

**Iron Scarcity:** Does not produce a crisis event at moderate levels but creates production bottlenecks. Swordsman recruitment becomes impossible without iron. Cavalry equipment cannot be constructed. Fortification upgrade projects halt. The iron scarcity pressure is visible in build queue failures rather than in a discrete event trigger. The player discovers it when they try to do something and cannot.

**Wood Shortfall:** Creates a cascade. Iron production slows (smelting fuel shortage). Construction queues extend. Naval production halts entirely — ships require wood in quantity. A dynasty that has been stripped of forest territory in the mid-game faces a compounding disadvantage in both military and economic infrastructure.

## Grand Structures and Resource Economics

The Grand Trade Exchange and Grand War Foundry are the highest-tier economic and military buildings in the game, and they interact with resources in specific ways that shape late-game strategy.

**Grand Trade Exchange:** Unlocks the currency dominance victory path and dramatically amplifies trade route gold yield. Requires: a fully developed Market and Trade infrastructure across multiple territories, sustained Moral or Neutral conviction record, and significant gold, stone, and wood investment. Building the Grand Trade Exchange is a public declaration of economic intent — the Trueborn City's information network announces it to all dynasties. It locks out the Grand War Foundry (a dynasty committed to trade cannot simultaneously commit to pure military-industrial supremacy).

**Grand War Foundry:** The military-industrial apex structure. Dramatically increases iron production, enables siege equipment at scale, and unlocks the highest-tier military infrastructure. Requires: established Iron Mine and Smelting Furnace network, mid-to-high conviction position toward Cruel (the Foundry's doctrine is production through pressure), substantial iron, stone, and gold investment. Building the Grand War Foundry locks out the Grand Trade Exchange — war economies and trade economies are incompatible at apex expression.

**Grand Sanctuary (Faith Buildings):** Faith grand structures have resource demands that compete directly with economic and military infrastructure. A dynasty building toward the Eternal Cathedral (Old Light), the Altar of Ascension (Blood Dominion), the Court of the Sacred Mandate (The Order), or the Ancient Thorned (The Wild) is committing gold, stone, and population labor to faith infrastructure that would otherwise be available for trade or military buildings. The Seventh Ingestion established that faith is not free — its cost is real and competes with all other economic priorities.

### Early-Game Foundation — Build Tier System and Worker Slot Production (Canon Locked 2026-04-25)

## Build Tier System

The build tree is gated by a deployment event and incremental prerequisite conditions rather than by raw time or a single unlock. Tiers are per-faction, not global.

**Tier 0 — Pre-Deployment (Keep Not Yet Placed)**
No production buildings are constructable. The founding faction exists as a mobile retinue with no fixed economic base. The sole available action is the Deploy command, which places the Keep and locks the faction's starting position.

**Tier 1 — First Settlement (Keep Deployed)**
The Keep deployment unlocks the initial building set:
- Housing (population cap +8 per building; required for growth)
- Well (water capacity +50 per well; required for sustained population)
- Woodcutter Camp (6 worker slots; wood 0.18 per worker per second)
- Forager Camp (6 worker slots; food 0.15 per worker per second)
- Training Yard (enables militia squad recruitment)

**Tier 2 — Established Settlement (All Four Tier-1 Prerequisites Present)**
Tier 2 unlocks when the faction has at least one active instance of all four prerequisite building types: housing, well (water source), food source (Forager Camp or any food-producing building), and Training Yard. When all four are present simultaneously, Tier 2 becomes available:
- Small Farm (5 worker slots; food 0.22 per worker per second; superior per-worker yield vs. Forager Camp)

**Tier 3+ — Full Build Tree**
Subsequent tiers unlock the complete Bloodlines building roster. Design detail for tier 3+ conditions is reserved for the settlement advancement design session.

The four prerequisites for the Tier 1→Tier 2 transition encode the game's design intent: a sustainable settlement is not just an army camp. It requires shelter, water, food production, and the capacity to train defenders. A faction that has a Training Yard and soldiers but no Housing is not a settlement — it is a military encampment. All four conditions are required, and the transition does not occur until all four are satisfied simultaneously.

## Three Production Models

Bloodlines operates three distinct resource production models that coexist and complement each other:

**Passive Trickle Model**
Certain buildings produce resources continuously at a flat rate per game second without requiring assigned population. Example: the Well produces water trickle at a fixed rate. Passive trickle is a floor — it ensures the most basic supply continues even during population crisis. The trickle model is the legacy production model and handles infrastructure buildings whose primary value is capacity rather than throughput.

**Active Gather Model**
Units are assigned to resource nodes and physically travel to gather resources, then return to deposit them. Resource nodes run down as they are harvested. Miners, woodcutters, and foragers using the active gather model are visible on the battlefield and can be attacked, disrupted, or killed. Their output scales with unit count and travel efficiency.

**Worker Slot Model (Canon Locked 2026-04-25)**
Slot-based buildings have a fixed maximum worker capacity (MaxWorkerSlots) and a per-worker-per-second production rate. The player assigns workers from their available population pool to these buildings. Workers are not individual units — they represent the productive capacity of a defined number of people. Output scales with assigned workers multiplied by the faction's current Effective Productivity.

Production formula: `output = BaseRatePerWorker × AssignedWorkers × EffectiveProductivity × deltaTime`

Worker slot buildings in the initial Tier 1-2 roster:
- Woodcutter Camp: 6 slots maximum, wood 0.18 per worker per second
- Forager Camp: 6 slots maximum, food 0.15 per worker per second
- Small Farm: 5 slots maximum, food 0.22 per worker per second

The worker slot model is the primary mid-game production scaling mechanism. A faction that fills its worker slots and maintains high effective productivity outproduces one with more buildings but fewer assigned workers.

## Water Capacity Infrastructure

Water is not simply a trickle resource. It is a hard capacity constraint on population growth and productivity.

**Water Infrastructure Support Model:**
Every faction has a MaxSupportedByWater value calculated each frame from its water infrastructure:
- Keep base reserve: 15 (the founding encampment has a small built-in water supply)
- Each Well building: +50 population supported

A faction's population cannot grow beyond MaxSupportedByWater. When Total Population >= MaxSupportedByWater, the PopulationGrowthSystem will not increase population regardless of food or housing availability. When Total Population > MaxSupportedByWater (which can occur if water infrastructure is destroyed or water-supported population was inherited), a water shortage productivity penalty applies.

The Keep base reserve of 15 supports a small founding encampment but is insufficient for a functioning settlement at scale. Building the first Well is a Day 1 mandatory priority — without it, population growth stalls at 15.

**Water shortage severity:** When water supply is inadequate, Effective Productivity is multiplied by 0.65 — a 35% productivity penalty, the harshest of the three shortage modifiers (food: 0.70, housing: 0.85). This reflects the fact that water scarcity impairs nearly every productive activity: agriculture, construction, craft work, and basic health all decline rapidly in water shortage conditions.

# Bloodlines — Faith System

## Overview
The Faith system defines the four ancient faiths that shape the spiritual and mechanical landscape of the game world. Each faith is not merely aesthetic or narrative. It provides distinct gameplay mechanics, unique abilities, and strategic options that fundamentally alter how a faction operates.

## Design Intent
Faith is a worldview that constrains and empowers. Choosing a faith (or inheriting one through dynasty) opens certain paths and closes others. Faith interacts with conviction, sacrifice, population loyalty, and territorial control. It is one of the primary axes of faction asymmetry.

### Design Content (Added 2026-03-15)

The faith system is one of the central pillars of Bloodlines and is not a decorative or secondary layer. It is a major strategic axis.

Faith is selected after the early exploration phase (at the end of Level 1 / Stage 1) when dynasties encounter the wider world, tribes, ruins, and sacred sites. The dynasty does not create a faith. It aligns with an existing covenant. The Four Covenants already exist in the world.

**Three Interacting Components:**
1. **Covenant** — the religion the dynasty follows
2. **Faith Intensity** — the degree to which the population actively practices the faith
3. **Faith Alignment** — a sliding scale representing how the covenant is practiced along a light vs dark spectrum

**The Four Covenants:**

**The Old Light (Covenant of the First Flame)** — Protection, mercy, divine guardianship, defense of innocents, enlightenment, unity, societal cohesion, disciplined governance, defensive stability.

**The Blood Dominion (The Red Covenant)** — Conquest, sacrifice, domination, belief that strength demonstrates divine favor. Darker in tone, carries moral cost and conviction consequences, provides strong military benefits.

**The Order (Covenant of the Sacred Mandate)** — Law, doctrine, institutional authority, structured governance under divine mandate, rigid hierarchy, administrative control.

**The Wild (Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots)** — Nature mysticism, ancient spirits, balance of the natural world, adaptive survival, environmental strength, ancient rooted power.

**Faith Intensity:**
- Must be maintained (not a passive permanent buff)
- Costs resources
- Scales with population participation
- Higher intensity unlocks stronger abilities
- Grows through participation, rituals, structures, and engagement
- As faith grows stronger it can radicalize

**Faith Alignment (Light vs Dark):**
- Same faith can be interpreted differently by different kingdoms
- Example: Blood Dominion practiced with symbolic offerings vs brutal sacrificial rites
- Large alignment differences within same covenant can cause internal religious conflict and schisms

**Faith Actions (consume resources, may consume captured units or population):**
- Rituals
- Offerings
- Temple construction
- Priesthood expansion
- Religious festivals
- Sacrificial rites

**Spiritual Manifestations (at high intensity):**
- Angels, demons, cursed armies, healing, resurrection, plague removal, nature forces
- World-level pressure from extreme dark behavior

**Faith Buildings:**
- Shrine — small religious structure increasing faith intensity
- Temple — major religious structure enabling powerful rituals
- Grand Sanctuary — late-game structure tied to faith victory

### Player Manual Expansion (Added 2026-03-15)

**Terminology:** Faith replaces any earlier use of the word "arcane." The spiritual system is called Faith, anchored to four covenants.

**House Neutrality at Start:** Houses have no inherent leaning. Houses do not start aligned. This prevents pre-locking before the map context is known.

**Faith Intensity Maintenance:** There is no automatic drift toward neutrality beyond consequences of neglect. Faith is maintained or neglected. Faith intensity changes by actions and maintenance, clearly understood by the player based on decisions and triggers.

**Progressive Scaling:** Faith costs can scale progressively based on how much of the population practices the faith. Higher practicing population increases scale, cost, and consequences of major faith actions.

**Level 4 Faith Divergence:** At Level 4, a player can commit to hardline fanatical expression of their Covenant, triggering extreme behaviors and unlocking grand structures and apex mechanics. Level 5 contains endgame grand structures and capabilities specific and exceptional based on prior choices.

**Spiritual Manifestations Expanded:**
- Spirits of fallen bloodline generals returning during war as angels or demons
- Evil faith at high levels can spawn a demon that corrupts an army and turns it bloodlusted, sometimes going on campaigns on its own
- These manifestations are rare and powerful, tied to faith intensity and world state

**Extreme Dark Faith World Pressure:** Extreme and continual dark actions can create a world pressure situation where population decline becomes severe and potentially irreversible unless other houses act. The narrative tone is that the dark faith and cruel actions have escalated beyond tolerable limits and must be stopped. This produces systemic pressure for conflict, coalitions, and forced wars.

**Covenant Gameplay Depth (from Player Manual Parts 25, 47-50):**

*The Old Light:* Not passive. Late-game access to holy armies, blessed warriors, healing, resurrection, plague removal, and sacred military seriousness. Strongest when paired with disciplined governance and real military competence. Mercy without strength is fragile; strength without integrity betrays the covenant. Not dependent on culture integration requirements. About stewardship and protection.

*The Blood Dominion:* Strongest when played with disciplined brutality, not indiscriminate madness. The most dangerous ruler knows exactly when terror advances power and when terror invites premature retaliation. Consequences include isolation, fear, diplomatic refusal, external hostility, and coalition-triggering. Fanatical states may take no prisoners in conquest.

*The Order:* Power comes from building structures of obedience and meaning that outlast individual engagements. Well suited to dynasties that want to make the world feel administratively and spiritually claimed. Danger: can become rigid or coercive without actual strength. A dead bureaucracy with holy language is still dead.

*The Wild:* Not a narrow "druid" theme. Can become terrifying strategic identity. Especially powerful in certain terrains and for dynasties willing to let conflict become less orderly. Best Wild dynasties understand how to align people, land, and bloodline with something older and more dangerous than institutions.

### Faith Duality System (Added 2026-03-18)

## Faith Duality — Dark and Light Expression Paths (LOCKED)

Each of the four ancient covenants contains both a dark expression path and a light expression path. This duality applies to ALL four faiths without exception.

The two paths represent how a covenant is practiced, not what it believes. The same faith can produce radically different civilizations depending on the expression path the player pursues.

**Critical Rule:** Faith path (dark or light) and Conviction are separate and must never be forced into alignment. A player may follow a dark faith expression with positive conviction. A player may follow a light faith expression with negative conviction.

**Blood Dominion — Expression Paths:**

Dark Path:
* Ritual sacrifice of enemies
* Blood ceremonies
* Dedicated altars
* Population consuming blood during ritual

Light Path:
* Animal sacrifice instead of human
* Structured ceremonial worship
* Resource trade-offs (food impact)
* Priest and priestess development

**The Old Light — Expression Paths:**

Dark Path:
* Judgment and punishment as divine mandate
* Forced conversion and purging of heresy
* Military crusades with religious justification
* Harsh governance framed as protection

Light Path:
* Mercy, healing, and protection of innocents
* Holy armies and blessed warriors
* Disciplined governance and societal cohesion
* Resurrection and plague removal

**The Order — Expression Paths:**

Dark Path:
* Coercive obedience through fear
* Inquisitions and enforcement of doctrine
* Administrative control as instrument of terror
* Rigid hierarchy enforced by punishment

Light Path:
* Law as structure for prosperity
* Institutional authority through genuine governance
* Divine mandate expressed through competence
* Ordered civilization as spiritual expression

**The Wild — Expression Paths:**

Dark Path:
* Ancient spirits weaponized against enemies
* Environmental destruction turned to strategic advantage
* Bloodlusted nature forces deployed in war
* Territory rendered hostile and impassable to outsiders

Light Path:
* Balance of natural world maintained
* Adaptive survival and environmental strength
* Ancient rooted power used for protection
* Land and bloodline aligned with older forces

This duality ensures that no covenant is morally pre-locked. Players may pursue any expression of any faith and combine it with any conviction direction.

### Doctrine Path System and Expanded Examples (Added 2026-03-18)

## Doctrine Path System

Faith includes a second layer called the Doctrine Path. The terminology "Doctrine Path" refers to the same dark/light expression path system established above. Each Faith contains two cultural implementations: Light Doctrine and Dark Doctrine.

These are not separate faiths. They are divergent expressions of the same belief system.

The Doctrine Path system exists to:
* create diversity within the same faith
* introduce internal ideological conflict
* enable narrative depth
* avoid forcing rigid playstyles
* support interaction with Conviction

## Blood Dominion — Expanded Doctrine Detail

Dark Doctrine:
* Ritual sacrifice of enemies
* Elaborate blood ceremonies
* Construction of blood altars
* Population participation in blood rites
* Possible sacrifice of bloodline members

Ceremonies require specific structures, time to perform, and population involvement. These rituals can produce powerful effects.

Light Doctrine:
* Animal sacrifice instead of human sacrifice
* Ceremonial gatherings tied to celestial events
* Resource cost impacts such as reduced food supply
* Development of priesthood roles within the bloodline

Bloodline members may become priests or priestesses devoted to the faith. Large ritual structures may be constructed.

## Faith Spread and Global Influence

Faith can spread across populations beyond a single civilization.

Methods of spread may include:
* cultural influence
* conquest
* missionary activity
* shared borders

If a single Faith becomes dominant globally, late-game religious conflict can occur.

Possible outcomes:
* holy wars
* doctrinal conflicts
* civilizational division

## Section 1: The Four Covenants — Identity and Philosophy

### The Old Light

At its core, the Old Light believes that civilization is the highest expression of the divine will — not a symbol of it, not a shadow of it, but the thing itself. Before The Great Frost, there were cities, laws, accumulated knowledge, roads that connected communities, institutions that carried memory beyond the lifespan of any single person. The Frost did not merely kill people. It erased what those people had built together. The Old Light looks at that erasure and names it the defining catastrophe of all existence. Their theology is built on that naming.

What the Old Light believes, precisely: the divine is not something above the world or outside of it. The divine was expressed through civilization's existence, and it was withdrawn — or tested, or punished; the covenant debates this — when civilization collapsed. The obligation that falls on every Old Light dynasty is reconstruction. Not merely survival. Not merely comfort. The deliberate, disciplined, costly effort of rebuilding the kind of civilization that does not collapse again, that maintains its institutional memory, that produces law and order and continuity as a sacred duty rather than a political convenience.

This is a faith that attracts dynasties that understand governance as a vocation. Dynasties that believe the work of ordering society is not merely practical but morally serious. The Old Light tends toward cultures with long memory — families that kept records through the Frost, bloodlines that preserved the names and deeds of the dead, communities that built libraries before they built fortifications. It tends toward cultures that distrust improvisation and value procedure, because they understand that what collapsed was not fortified enough against the chaos that destroyed it.

The Old Light produces civilizations marked by: codified law that is publicly known and consistently applied; architectural permanence — structures built to last generations, not seasons; institutions that outlast individual leaders; a serious relationship with the written record; and governance that treats its obligations as genuine rather than performed. The faith creates a particular texture in daily life — communities where the mechanisms of justice are visible and believed in, where religious observance is tied to civic participation, where the head of bloodline is expected to embody the law rather than merely enforce it.

The choice to follow the Old Light over other covenants comes down to what it offers: legitimacy. A dynasty that follows the Old Light draws on something the other covenants cannot fully provide — the claim that its authority is not merely power but rightful order. That its institutions are not merely convenient but sacred. In diplomacy this is currency. In coalition politics this is leverage. In the persuasion of conquered populations this is the difference between resentment-compliance and genuine integration.

The internal tension that makes the faith costly: law requires enforcement, and enforcement requires force, and force applied in the name of the sacred produces an institutional capacity for justified violence that can become indistinguishable from atrocity when the definition of what requires enforcement expands. The Old Light's history is not a history of peace. It is a history of civilization — with everything civilization does to protect itself and its memory. A dynasty committed to the Old Light will eventually face the question of what to do with what cannot be brought inside its law. This question has two answers, and both are theological.

**Light Doctrine — The Covenant of the First Flame:** The law is sacred because it protects. The institutions are sacred because they carry memory. The head of bloodline serves the law — they are not above it, they are its most committed servant. Mercy is possible within the law because the law exists to protect the innocent. A dynasty following light doctrine in the Old Light governs visibly, adjudicates fairly, maintains its obligations even when doing so is costly, and treats the reconstruction of civilization as its genuine vocation. The military identity that emerges from light doctrine is the defender — the Flame Warden, at Level 3, is a soldier who fights to hold what has been built. The Sunblade Champion, at Level 4, is a warrior who has been consecrated by the accumulation of what the dynasty has built and protected. They are not crusaders. They are the living proof that the civilization they defend is worth the cost of defending it.

**Dark Doctrine — The Inquisitor Tradition:** The law is sacred because it is the only protection against the chaos that destroyed civilization once before. Anything that cannot be brought inside the law is a threat to everything the law protects. The Inquisitor tradition does not believe in evil for its own sake — it believes in the absolute priority of what must survive over what stands in its way. Forced conversion, purging of heresy, military crusades with religious justification — these are not departures from the covenant. They are the covenant's defense against entropy. The Inquisitor does what the Faith Leader will not, and the dynasty that employs them is making a statement about how seriously it takes the stakes. Civilizations following dark doctrine build Inquisitor networks. They enforce doctrinal compliance. They treat deviation from the covenant not as a personal failing but as a danger to the collective project. The Purge Templar at Level 4 is not a villain. They are the logical endpoint of the belief that the light must be protected from everything that would extinguish it, by any means the faith requires.

### Blood Dominion

Blood Dominion does not dress its theology in comfort. Its claim, stated plainly: power is not free. Every covenant between a people and something greater than themselves has a price, and the price is paid in blood. This has always been true. Every dynasty that has ever claimed divine favor has paid for it with sacrifice — in lives lost at war, in populations consumed by famine, in bloodlines broken by disease and betrayal. Blood Dominion simply names what all faiths leave unnamed. The cost is real. The question is whether you pay it with intention and receive what it purchases, or pay it without intention and receive nothing.

This is the covenant's core argument and its source of genuine power: other faiths perform the same transactions but wrap them in language that obscures the terms. The Old Light calls the deaths in its holy wars necessary. The Order calls the suppression of dissent legitimate governance. Only Blood Dominion is honest about what is being exchanged. The blood that feeds the covenant's power is not hidden in institutional language. It is offered on an altar, with a name, in front of witnesses, with full awareness of what it costs and what it purchases. This honesty is, for those who follow it, a form of integrity that the other covenants cannot claim.

The civilizations that tend toward Blood Dominion are those that have looked at what survival costs and decided to stop pretending otherwise. They tend toward warrior cultures with a frank relationship with death, trading cultures that understand that every exchange has two sides, and bloodlines whose history is defined by catastrophic loss followed by survival — dynasties that paid enormous prices to still exist and who built their theology out of that accounting. Blood Dominion is common among dynasties that emerged from The Great Frost having sacrificed most of what they were in order to keep existing. They learned, in the frost, that survival is purchased. The covenant gave that lesson a shape.

What Blood Dominion produces in governance: a frank relationship with power. Rulers who do not pretend they are not rulers. Laws that acknowledge enforcement as their foundation. A willingness to conduct acts in public that other covenants conduct in private, because the covenant holds that what is hidden is dishonest and what is honest — even if it is terrible — has more integrity than the polite lie. In warfare, Blood Dominion dynasties fight with purpose rather than enthusiasm — the covenant demands sacrifice that has value, not violence for its own sake. In daily life, the covenant structures a relationship between the population and the sacred that is more immediate and more demanding than other faiths — the population participates directly, whether through ceremony, observation, or their own blood rites.

The choice to follow Blood Dominion over other covenants: it offers power with clarity about cost. A dynasty that follows Blood Dominion knows exactly what it is buying and exactly what it is paying. There are no surprises in the theology. There is no moment of discovering that the covenant you followed had a price you did not agree to — you agreed to every price at the beginning, fully aware of the terms. For certain leaders and certain dynasties, this clarity is not a drawback. It is the only honest arrangement on offer.

The internal tension: the covenant demands payment, and as a dynasty grows in power, the scale of payment the covenant can demand scales with it. A dynasty that has grown accustomed to the returns on blood sacrifice faces an escalating equation — more power requires larger sacrifice, and larger sacrifice has consequences in population, in diplomatic isolation, in the horror of the wider world. Blood Dominion dynasties that reach full development have paid, cumulatively, a price that is visible in the world around them. The question the faith eventually forces is not whether the sacrifice was worth it. The question is how much further the dynasty is willing to go, and whether there is a point where the cost exceeds what any return can justify.

**Light Doctrine — The Covenant of Sacred Cost:** The blood that is owed is paid through animal sacrifice, ceremonial blood rites, and the covenant's formal acknowledgment that every major act of the dynasty carries a price. Bloodline members may become priests and priestesses who manage the relationship between the dynasty's political power and the covenant's demands. Grand ceremonies tied to the cycles of the year, to harvests, to births within the bloodline, and to military victories — each of these is marked by a sacrifice appropriate to its significance. The Covenant Priest at Level 3 is a figure who manages the covenant's ongoing demands, maintains the relationship between the dynasty and what it has bargained with, and ensures that the price is paid cleanly. The Covenant Scion at Level 4 is a full inheritor of the tradition — someone who has grown up inside the accounting, who understands the terms, who can be sent into situations where the covenant's weight must be personally represented.

**Dark Doctrine — The Full Reckoning:** The covenant can demand, and has demanded, human blood. At the far end of this doctrine — requiring extreme investment, specific structures, and the conjunction of dark doctrine with extreme conviction thresholds — the sacrifice of bloodline members is possible. The sacrifice of unbelievers, those who do not follow the covenant, is also possible under the correct conditions: extreme dark doctrine plus extreme cruel conviction, specific buildings, and faith intensity thresholds that must be reached and maintained. These are not random acts of violence. They are acts the covenant demands and the dynasty performs in full awareness of what they cost diplomatically, in terms of Trueborn horror response, in terms of the world's coalition-forming instinct against a dynasty that has gone past what the world will tolerate watching. The Blood Rider at Level 3 is the dark doctrine's military expression — not an assassin, but a cavalry unit that carries the covenant's demand for cost directly into battle, consecrated through rites that the light doctrine would never perform. The Bloodbound at Level 4 is the full military expression of what that doctrine produces: a fighter who has gone through the full reckoning of the dark path and carries it without apology.

### The Order

The Order's theology begins with a premise that the other covenants accept but do not elevate to the center of everything: chaos is the enemy. Not evil in a moral sense. Not suffering in a humanitarian sense. Chaos — the state of things without structure, without hierarchy, without the mechanisms that determine who has authority and under what conditions that authority is legitimate and how it is transferred. The Great Frost was chaos. It did not merely kill — it dissolved the structures that made collective existence possible. The Order looked at what The Frost did and concluded that the work of civilization is the work of replacing chaos with structure, permanently and without tolerance for entropy.

Where the Old Light locates the sacred in civilization's accumulated product — its laws, its memory, its achievements — the Order locates the sacred in civilization's organizing principle. The hierarchy itself. The mandate that determines who is above whom and what obligations flow between levels of that hierarchy. The Order's belief is that the divine will is expressed not through mystery, not through sacrifice, not through communion with nature, but through the establishment of correct structure in the world. The world should be ordered. Ordered correctly. The Order knows what correctly means, and its vocation is the enforcement of that knowledge on the world.

Civilizations that tend toward the Order are those that emerged from The Great Frost with a specific lesson: what made the world vulnerable was that it had no one truly in charge. No single legitimate authority. What is needed is not better laws — laws can be ignored — but a structure of power so thorough that ignoring it is not an option. The Order attracts dynasties that experienced the Frost as proof of what happens when authority is distributed, when the chain of command is unclear, when legitimacy is contested. Their answer is the elimination of contested legitimacy through the imposition of a hierarchy that does not permit contestation.

What the Order produces in governance: administrative thoroughness. A dynasty following the Order builds systems. Tax collection is consistent and documented. Military organization has a clear hierarchy of command with each level knowing its responsibilities. Territory is claimed not merely through military presence but through the installation of administrative apparatus — Lawposts and Halls of Mandate that make the dynasty's presence felt in every corner of its territory as a system, not a personality. The Order's territories feel different from territories governed by other faiths — more organized, more legible, more thorough, and less human in a specific way that populations respond to differently depending on their own history.

The choice to follow the Order: it offers the experience of being organized in a world that is not. A dynasty that follows the Order knows that its systems will function even if key individuals die or fail, because the system has been built to survive individual failure. This is the order's most powerful offering to a dynasty that has watched what happens when everything depends on one person who then falls. The Order builds things that outlast people. For certain dynasties this is the theological argument that closes all other arguments.

The internal tension: a system thorough enough to eliminate contested authority is also thorough enough to become self-justifying. The Order believes its mandate is divine. That belief does not require external validation — the structure validates itself. A dynasty deep in the Order's light doctrine will eventually encounter the question of whether the law they are enforcing is still serving the purpose the law was created for, or whether it has become the purpose itself. This is the fault line inside the covenant — the difference between structure as a means and structure as an end, and the very human difficulty of knowing which side you are on.

**Light Doctrine — Law as Service:** The divine mandate is to organize the world into a structure that produces prosperity, safety, and the conditions for genuine governance. Authority is legitimate when it serves the people it governs. The Iron Legionary at Level 3 is the military expression of this — a soldier who fights within a clear command structure, whose effectiveness comes from coordination and discipline rather than individual force. The Iron Sentinel at Level 4 is the apex of what that structure produces when it has fully developed: a fighting force that behaves as a coordinated system rather than a collection of individuals, capable of sustained operations that purely charismatic armies cannot maintain.

**Dark Doctrine — The Dominion Tradition:** Order imposed through fear is still order. Compliance extracted through the credible threat of consequence is compliance. The Warden-Enforcer at Level 3 is the dark doctrine's instrument — a figure who enforces not through the legitimacy of the law but through the immediacy of consequence for deviation from it. The Dominion Arbiter at Level 4 is the full expression of what that path produces: an administrative-military figure who combines enforcement authority with the doctrine's full apparatus. They are not soldiers in the conventional sense. They are the Order's argument made visible and armed. Dynasties following dark doctrine build structures of compliance — not loyalty, compliance — across their territory, and those structures hold until they are challenged by something stronger.

### The Wild

The Wild's theology is the oldest of the four, and the oldest in the sense that matters: it predates the others by the distance between civilization and the world that civilization has not yet organized. The Wild does not believe that the divine is expressed through civilization's achievement or civilization's structure. The Wild believes that the divine is expressed through the living world itself — the forest, the storm, the animal, the seasonal cycle, the ruthless logic of predation and survival that governed existence before the first wall was ever built. Civilization, from the Wild's perspective, is not a sacred achievement. It is a wound the world has been made to carry, and the Wild's vocation is to keep that wound small.

This is a theology of strength through alignment with something that existed before human ambition invented the word civilization. The dynasties that tend toward the Wild are those that spent The Great Frost in the places where survival was determined by reading the land correctly — mountain communities, forest people, coastal hunters, tribes that moved with the seasons and survived because they understood the rules the living world actually runs on rather than the rules that human institutions invented. They came out of the Frost intact. They watched the great institutions collapse from outside, and what they saw confirmed what they had always believed: civilization is fragile in ways the living world is not.

The Wild produces civilizations that are organized around different principles than the other three covenants. Territory is not claimed through administrative apparatus — it is known. The difference matters. A Wild dynasty knows its land in the way a hunter knows a forest: not through boundary markers and title deeds but through presence, through the accumulated knowledge of what lives where and why and what the patterns mean. Military strategy under the Wild reflects this — terrain is not an obstacle to be overcome but a factor to be weaponized. The Wild's cavalry ride bears and other animals because the covenant with the living world includes the living animals that other faiths treat as resources or as symbols and the Wild treats as partners.

What the Wild offers that no other covenant can provide: the world itself as an ally. A dynasty aligned with the Wild at full development has access to forms of strategic advantage that emerge from the land rather than from industry. The terrain fights for them. The animals fight with them. The sacred ground of the First Grove amplifies their faith not as a building amplifies a resource but as a living place amplifies what it has always been. In certain terrains and at certain levels of faith development, a Wild dynasty's relationship with its environment is an asymmetric advantage that no manufactured military force can fully neutralize.

The internal tension: the Wild's theology does not privilege human life over the logic of the living world. The living world does not run on mercy. It runs on predation, on the thinning of the weak, on the seasonal logic that determines who survives a long winter and who does not. A dynasty that follows the Wild deeply enough will eventually be asked by its own theology to accept this logic fully — to stop applying to the world the human preference for mercy that the living world does not honor. This is the fault line inside the covenant. The Wild at its light extreme maintains communion with nature while preserving certain human commitments. The Wild at its dark extreme accepts the living world's logic as its own, without the human exceptions, and acts accordingly.

**Light Doctrine — Harmony and the Hunt:** The sacred is in balance. The dynasty that follows light doctrine within the Wild maintains its relationship with the living world through hunting traditions that acknowledge the kill's cost, through animal kinship that treats the animals who fight alongside them as partners rather than weapons, through cultivation practices that cooperate with rather than impose on the land. The Thornrider at Level 3 is a cavalry unit with a relationship to its mount that is qualitatively different from the relationship a horse cavalry unit has with its horse. The covenant is visible in how they move together. The Forest Sovereign at Level 4 is the full expression of what that relationship produces across a lifetime of deepening commitment to the wild's terms.

**Dark Doctrine — The Living World's Law:** The world does not honor mercy. The strong consume the weak because that is the mechanism that produces the strong. A dynasty following dark doctrine within the Wild has accepted this without exception. They do not apply human sentiment to the logic of the living world. The Bear Rider at Level 3 is the dark doctrine's military expression — a rider with a bear mount whose covenant is not the light doctrine's partnership but the dark doctrine's alignment with the bear's own nature, amplified and directed. The Bonebreaker at Level 4 is the endpoint of that path: a unit that has internalized the living world's predatory logic so completely that it fights the way the wild hunts — without hesitation, without mercy, without the strategic restraint that other military traditions use as a courtesy that the living world does not extend. Civilizations following dark doctrine within the Wild do not wage war by the conventions that civilized military traditions use. They use terrain, animal allies, and the world's own ruthlessness as their primary weapons.

## Section 2: Faith Intensity System — Full Specification

### The Five Tiers

**Latent (0-20%)**

Latent faith is not the absence of faith. It is the presence of faith without active investment. A dynasty at Latent has aligned with a covenant — the selection has been made, the worldview adopted — but the material expression of that alignment is minimal. There are no dedicated faith buildings. There are no religious specialists among the bloodline. The population may carry the covenant's symbols in small private ways, and the dynasty's governance may reflect the covenant's values in its informal character, but the faith is not yet a strategic resource.

What is available at Latent: the covenant is chosen and cannot be changed without consequences. The dynasty's worldview alignment is established and begins to affect how foreign parties perceive it. No faith-specific units, rituals, or buildings are accessible. No faith intensity effects are applied to territory or population. The faith exists as identity without yet existing as capability.

What is not available: any faith unit, any faith building, any faith ritual, any faith-based diplomatic effect, any faith-based military bonus. The covenant has been selected but nothing has been built on that selection.

**Active (20-40%)**

At Active faith, the dynasty has begun investing. The first faith building has been constructed — for the Old Light, the Wayshrine; for Blood Dominion, the Blood-Altar; for the Order, the Lawpost; for the Wild, the Grove Marker. These structures represent the dynasty's first material commitment to the covenant and begin producing faith intensity through their presence in the territory.

What changes at Active: the population begins to show faith alignment in observable ways. Markets and gathering spaces in the territory show the covenant's symbols. The dynasty's governance takes on a mild religious character that affects population loyalty in small but real ways. Bloodline members may begin expressing faith alignment in their visual identity. Basic religious observances are possible — not the major rituals unlocked at higher tiers, but the daily and seasonal practices that form the foundation of the covenant's relationship with the dynasty's population.

What unlocks: faith building construction becomes available (first tier only). Basic faith observances. The Bloodline Member faith role — Faith Leader or Covenant Officiant — can be assigned, but their early development is slow and their capacity is limited while intensity remains below Devout. Some diplomatic effects begin to apply: dynasties following the same covenant with Active or higher faith are more inclined toward positive diplomatic relations. Dynasties following opposing covenants at Active or higher begin expressing mild wariness.

**Devout (40-60%)**

Devout is the threshold where faith becomes a genuine military and strategic resource. At Devout, Level 3 faith units become available — the first doctrine-specific soldiers the faith produces. For the Old Light light doctrine: the Flame Warden. For dark doctrine: the Inquisitor. For Blood Dominion light doctrine: the Covenant Priest. For dark doctrine: the Blood Rider. These units represent the first moment where faith is visibly expressed through the dynasty's military identity rather than only through its buildings and governance.

What changes at Devout: the dynasty's faith identity becomes visible to the world in a way that carries strategic weight. Other dynasties begin treating the faith alignment as a meaningful characteristic rather than an aesthetic choice. The covenant's theological stance begins to have observable effects on how the population behaves — in Old Light dynasties, there is a quality of ordered civic engagement; in Blood Dominion dynasties, a more ritualized relationship with power; in the Order, a noticeably structured administrative character; in the Wild, a quality of territorial awareness and natural alignment that manifests in how populations relate to their land.

What unlocks: Level 3 faith units (both doctrine paths). Second-tier faith buildings can now be constructed — the Hall of Remembrance, the Covenant Hall, the Hall of Mandate, the Spirit Lodge. Bloodline Member faith roles become significantly more capable — the Faith Leader can perform proper Grand Rites with real effects; the Covenant Officiant can perform significant Bindings. The covenant's specific ritual catalog begins to open, with ceremonies that produce effects unavailable through any other system. A dynasty at Devout has committed enough to the faith that walking it back is no longer costless.

**Fervent (60-80%)**

Fervent faith is the point of no return in character if not in mechanics. A dynasty at Fervent has built its civilization around the covenant in ways that cannot be quickly dismantled without losing much of what has been built. The faith is structural at this point — it is in the laws, in the military organization, in the nature of the bloodline members who have committed to faith roles, in the relationship between the dynasty and its population.

What changes at Fervent: the dynasty becomes eligible for spiritual manifestations — rare, powerful events that cannot be reliably produced but can occur when the conditions align. Fallen bloodline generals may return during war as something other than memory. The covenant's presence in the dynasty's territory is thick enough that it affects how enemies experience entering that territory — there are properties of Fervent faith territories that work against those who do not share the covenant. Diplomatic effects intensify: dynasties following the same covenant at Fervent or above have a strong natural pull toward alliance; dynasties following opposing covenants at Fervent recognize the intensity and respond accordingly, with either wariness or targeted opposition.

What unlocks: eligibility for spiritual manifestations (not guaranteed, but possible under the right conditions). The capability to pursue Sacred Ground aggressively — a dynasty at Fervent faith that places a shrine on Sacred Ground receives dramatically amplified intensity. Level 4 faith units become attainable for dynasties approaching this tier, accessible at the transition to Apex. The Covenant Test becomes theoretically preparable — a dynasty approaching Fervent should already be thinking about what the Covenant Test will demand, because at Apex it fires.

**Apex (80-100%)**

Apex faith is the endpoint of the intensity arc and the beginning of what the covenant was always capable of producing in a dynasty that paid the full price of commitment. At Apex, the faith has become the dynasty's defining characteristic to the outside world — it is not merely a strategic choice but an identity that precedes reputation, precedes military power, precedes diplomatic posture. Other dynasties encounter an Apex faith dynasty as they encounter a force of nature: with full awareness that what they are dealing with has crossed into territory that cannot be managed by ordinary means.

What unlocks: Level 5 apex units — The Unbroken (Old Light), The Sacrificed (Blood Dominion), The Mandate (the Order), The First Wild (the Wild). One or two of these may exist per match per faith. Their creation requires the full architecture of the faith at Apex intensity. They are not units in the conventional sense. They are the covenant made physical and armed. Their loss is significant in ways that go beyond the tactical — an apex unit lost in battle is a covenant signal, an announcement to the world about the dynasty's condition.

The Covenant Test fires at Apex. The test demands a specific act — grand construction, sacrifice, legal subjugation, destruction of civilization — and the dynasty must complete it or face intensity loss that drops them back below Apex. Success unlocks what the covenant's highest tier can produce in terms of political, military, and spiritual capability. Failure is not catastrophic, but it is costly, and it tells the world that the dynasty flinched at the moment the covenant demanded its full price.

### How Faith Intensity Is Generated

Faith intensity is not produced automatically through covenant alignment. It is produced through active investment and maintained through ongoing attention.

**Buildings** are the primary generation source. Each faith building tier produces intensity within a radius of effect around the structure, with the radius scaling to the building tier. A Wayshrine produces modest intensity across a small area. The Eternal Cathedral produces significant intensity across a large area and reinforces any lesser buildings within its range. Multiple buildings in proximity do not stack multiplicatively — they have diminishing returns at high building density — but sparse coverage across a large territory is the primary enemy of consistent intensity maintenance.

**Active rituals** produce intensity spikes that supplement passive building generation. A Grand Rite performed by a Faith Leader, or a Covenant Binding by a Covenant Officiant, raises intensity across the dynasty's territory for a defined period. These spikes can push intensity past thresholds that passive generation cannot quite reach, enabling access to mechanics that would otherwise remain locked. The cost of rituals prevents them from being the primary generation mechanism — they are a complement to building investment, not a substitute for it.

**Territory coverage** determines how much of the dynasty's claimed land is actually within faith intensity range. A dynasty that has built faith infrastructure in its core territory but holds outlying territories without any buildings is generating intensity for a fraction of its domain. The unmaintained territories gradually drift toward Latent intensity, which creates a practical consideration: a dynasty that expands rapidly without expanding its faith infrastructure will find its average intensity declining even if its core remains Fervent or Apex.

**Bloodline Member devotion** provides a continuous personal generation bonus proportional to the member's own faith development. A Faith Leader at Apex faith generates more intensity than one at Active. A Covenant Officiant who has completed multiple Bindings generates more than one who has just begun. The death of a highly developed faith-role member produces a generation loss that can be felt across multiple tiers.

**Population faith composition** — the proportion of the dynasty's population that actively practices the covenant — acts as a multiplier on all other generation sources. A population that is 80% active practitioners produces more intensity from the same buildings than a 30% practitioner population. This creates a reinforcing loop: high faith intensity creates conditions that deepen population practice, which raises intensity further. It also creates a vulnerability: anything that disrupts population faith — a competing faith's presence, military defeat, diplomatic isolation, population loss — affects intensity through this multiplier.

---

### How Faith Intensity Decays

Faith intensity requires continuous investment to maintain. The decay systems are not arbitrary attrition — they represent the real conditions under which a faith loses its grip on a people.

**Time without maintenance** is the foundation of decay. A dynasty that stops building, stops performing rituals, and stops attending to its faith infrastructure will see intensity erode over time. The pace of decay is slow at higher tiers — Apex intensity does not collapse overnight — but it is real and cumulative. A dynasty that abandons faith investment to focus entirely on military buildup for an extended period will emerge from that period at a lower tier than it entered.

**Enemy structures** present in the dynasty's territory produce active intensity suppression. A rival dynasty that builds its own faith buildings in contested or newly captured territory does not merely build its own faith — it also pushes against the existing faith's presence. The more dominant the rival faith's building density in a given area, the more forcefully it suppresses the existing covenant's intensity in that area.

**Military defeat in home territory** produces an intensity shock that reflects what military defeat does to a population's faith in the covenant that is supposed to protect them. A dynasty that loses battles on its own soil does not merely lose population and territory — it loses the argument that the covenant's power is real. Intensity drops following significant home-territory defeats, with the magnitude of the drop scaling to the severity of the defeat.

**Bloodline Member death or apostasy** produces intensity loss proportional to the member's development within the faith role. A Faith Leader who dies at Apex faith leaves a gap that is felt immediately across the dynasty's intensity levels. A Bloodline Member who formally converts to a rival covenant — apostasy — produces a crisis event that combines intensity loss with diplomatic and political consequences. Apostasy is rare but its effects are severe because of what it signals to the world about the dynasty's internal cohesion.

## Section 3: Doctrine Path Selection and Consequences

### When Selection Happens

Faith selection — choosing which of the four covenants to align with — occurs at the end of Stage 1, Level 1. This is after the dynasty has encountered the wider world: the ruins of what existed before The Great Frost, the tribes that survived it in different ways, the sacred sites that still carry the echo of the covenants that existed before the collapse. The dynasty has seen enough to make a considered choice. It has not yet committed so deeply to any path that the selection is predetermined.

Doctrine path selection — choosing light or dark within the covenant — does not happen at the same moment. It happens through investment. The player who begins building Inquisitor infrastructure, who commits Bloodline Members to the enforcement roles of the dark doctrine, who invests in the covenant halls and binding ceremonies of the dark path — that player is selecting dark doctrine through action rather than through a single choice interface. The doctrine emerges from what the dynasty does rather than from a declaration.

This matters mechanically: early in the covenant relationship, a dynasty exists in a zone of doctrinal ambiguity. Their buildings might lean light; their bloodline members might lean dark. The divergence closes as investment concentrates in one direction or the other, and by Level 3 — when the doctrine-specific units appear — the doctrine path is effectively established. Changing it after that point requires abandoning built infrastructure and Bloodline Member development trajectories, which is costly enough that most dynasties continue on the path they are on rather than reverse course.

### What Doctrine Path Selection Means

The doctrine path determines which faith units the dynasty can recruit, which ritual capabilities are available, which Bloodline Member roles are accessible, and which set of political and diplomatic effects the faith generates in the world.

It does not determine the dynasty's morality. This is the design principle that overrides everything else in the faith system: Light Doctrine Old Light and Dark Doctrine Old Light are both Old Light. The covenant is the same. The worldview is the same. What differs is the means. And the means are separate from Conviction — which is the system that actually tracks the dynasty's moral character. A dynasty can follow dark doctrine in good faith. A dynasty can follow light doctrine with a Cruel conviction axis. The two systems interact but do not determine each other.

### The Doctrine Investment Trajectory

A dynasty that chooses light doctrine within the Old Light at Level 1 is building a particular kind of civilization. Its first faith building, the Wayshrine, is a place of remembrance and community observance. Its Faith Leader role produces a Bloodline Member who performs Grand Rites of consecration and blessing. Its Level 3 military unit, the Flame Warden, is a defender whose combat identity is shaped by what is being protected rather than what is being punished. By Level 4, the Sunblade Champion is the culmination of what a dynasty built around light doctrine produces in military terms: a fighter whose power comes from the full expression of what the covenant has built.

A dynasty that chooses dark doctrine within the Old Light at Level 1 is building a different civilization with the same founding theology. Its Wayshrine becomes a center of doctrinal enforcement rather than community observance. The Bloodline Member who carries the faith forward is a different kind of figure than the Faith Leader — more enforcement-oriented, more concerned with what cannot be brought inside the law than what is already inside it. Its Level 3 military unit, the Inquisitor, is not a defender. It is an investigator and enforcer. By Level 4, the Purge Templar is the culmination of a doctrine path that has consistently asked the same question about the world and answered it the same way: it must comply or it must be removed.

These two dynasties, meeting on the battlefield or across a diplomatic table, are following the same covenant but look and fight and govern in ways that are visibly different. This visible divergence is what the doctrine path system produces, and it is irreversible in the sense that matters: the institutional and military infrastructure built along one path cannot simply be converted to the other. The buildings are not neutral. The Bloodline Members who have spent years developing in one direction do not simply redirect. The Level 4 unit that has been produced by one path cannot be retrained as the other path's Level 4 unit.

### Why Reversal Is Costly

The design principle here is the same Irreversible Divergence principle that applies to Level 4 broadly. A dynasty that has reached Level 3 or Level 4 faith units has built the infrastructure, developed the Bloodline Members, recruited the population into the specific practices, and organized its governance around the doctrine's requirements. All of that is real investment that carries real weight. Pivoting mid-development is possible only by accepting that most of what has been built is now producing diminishing returns.

The player can change doctrine direction. They cannot make the change cheap.

## Section 4: Faith Unit Roster — Full Design

### The Old Light

**Flame Warden (L3 — Light Doctrine)**

The Flame Warden is not a crusader. Crusaders travel to other people's lands. The Flame Warden protects. They are produced by dynasties that have built their covenant relationship around the idea that what has been rebuilt is sacred and what threatens it is the enemy, and they fight accordingly — most effectively when defending structures and territories that carry faith significance, less effectively when deployed in offensive campaigns that take them away from what they were trained to protect. They carry the Old Light's symbols visibly, not as decoration but as covenant marker — they are fighting in the covenant's name and the covenant's name is on them. Against an Inquisitor from the same faith on the dark doctrine path, the Flame Warden fights as a demonstration that the light path holds. The confrontation is theological as well as military.

**Inquisitor (L3 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Inquisitor is a product of the question: what do you do with what cannot be brought inside the covenant's law? The Inquisitor is the dynasty's answer. They operate across the dynasty's territory and beyond it, tracking doctrinal deviation, moving against those the dynasty has identified as threats to the covenant's integrity. They are not shock troops. Their military effectiveness comes from targeted action — they are more capable against specific high-value targets than against mass formations. An Inquisitor hunting a rival dynasty's Faith Leader or faith-role Bloodline Member is in its element. An Inquisitor deployed against an open field battle is outside it. Their production requires that the dynasty has committed to dark doctrine infrastructure. They are not available to a dynasty following light doctrine. The covenant is the same; the path is not.

**Sunblade Champion (L4 — Light Doctrine)**

The Sunblade Champion represents what a dynasty fully committed to light doctrine within the Old Light produces at the apex of its conventional military capability. They are heavily invested in consecration — the process through which the dynasty's rites, its Faith Leader's Grand Rites, and its accumulated institutional integrity translate into individual combat capability. A Sunblade Champion with a dynasty in full Devout or Fervent faith behind them is a more powerful unit than the same Champion fighting for a dynasty whose faith intensity has eroded. The covenant's backing is real. This unit is where it shows most clearly in military terms.

**Purge Templar (L4 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Purge Templar is what the Inquisitor tradition produces when it reaches Level 4 commitment. Where the Inquisitor investigates and targets, the Purge Templar executes at scale. They are equipped and organized specifically for the suppression and elimination of what the covenant identifies as unacceptable — entire territories brought into doctrinal compliance by force, rival faith structures systematically dismantled, populations with high concentrations of opposing covenant followers subjected to the full weight of what the dark path can produce. A dynasty that has built Purge Templar capacity has built an institutional force capable of faith-based ethnic and religious cleansing at the strategic level. This is what the dark doctrine produces when it has had time and resources to fully develop. It is not evil within the covenant's framework. It is the covenant's protection of itself taken to its logical end.

**The Unbroken (L5 — Apex, Old Light)**

The Unbroken is the only unit of its kind in the match. One can exist per match per faith, and its existence is announced when it is created — the world knows. The Unbroken has gone through the full development of the Flame Warden and Sunblade Champion trajectory and has been further consecrated through the Covenant Test's demands, emerging as something that is no longer simply a very powerful soldier. The Unbroken has become an argument. The argument is that the covenant is real, its backing is real, and the dynasty that produced this individual has done what the covenant demanded. The Unbroken on a battlefield changes the nature of what is happening — not just tactically but narratively. Their death is not merely a unit loss. It is the covenant's argument losing its most visible proof. A dynasty that loses The Unbroken in combat faces a faith intensity crisis that reflects what that loss means to the population and to the covenant's relationship with the dynasty.

### Blood Dominion

**Covenant Priest (L3 — Light Doctrine)**

The Covenant Priest is not a support unit in the conventional sense. They manage the relationship between the dynasty's military operations and the covenant's demands — they are present when significant acts are performed because the covenant requires witnessing, they conduct the rites that consecrate a campaign before it begins, and they manage the blood sacrifices that the light doctrine requires to maintain the covenant's satisfaction. Their combat capability is secondary to their ritual function, but they are not helpless. A Covenant Priest who has conducted the pre-campaign rite on an army provides that army with a sustained faith bonus that conventional military units do not have access to. The covenant is paying out what it was paid. The Priest is how the payment is transferred.

**Blood Rider (L3 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Blood Rider is mounted cavalry consecrated through the dark doctrine's rites in ways that the light doctrine's Covenant Priest cannot perform. The rites required to produce a Blood Rider involve acts that the light doctrine does not permit, which means that a dynasty following light doctrine cannot recruit this unit regardless of its military resources. The Blood Rider fights with the additional weight of what was paid to create them — not as a mystical bonus in a vague sense, but as a tangible effect that represents the covenant's acknowledgment that the price was paid. They are more dangerous than standard cavalry by a margin that reflects the cost of their production. That cost is not abstract.

**Covenant Scion (L4 — Light Doctrine)**

The Covenant Scion is what the light doctrine path produces at Level 4: a Bloodline-adjacent figure who has grown up inside the covenant's accounting, understands the terms of the relationship between the dynasty and what it has bargained with, and can represent the dynasty in those terms at the highest level of intensity the light doctrine permits. The Covenant Scion is present at the dynasty's major rituals, reinforces the Faith Leader's capacity, and in military contexts operates as a faith-anchored commander whose presence raises the morale and combat capability of units around them through the covenant's own backing rather than through conventional leadership authority.

**Bloodbound (L4 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Bloodbound is the endpoint of what the dark doctrine's military path produces in Blood Dominion: a fighter who has entered into a personal covenant with the faith's demands at a level that goes beyond unit membership. The Bloodbound has paid a personal price — the nature of that price is not available for public knowledge, but the effects of having paid it are visible. They fight differently from any other Level 4 unit in the game. Where other units grow more effective with numbers and coordination, the Bloodbound's effectiveness has a personal quality — they are dangerous in ways that do not scale cleanly with formation, that operate on targets in ways that reflect what the covenant extracts and what it produces in exchange. They are the dark doctrine's military proof.

**The Sacrificed (L5 — Apex, Blood Dominion)**

The Sacrificed is misnamed only from outside the covenant's perspective. From within it, the name is accurate: this individual has been through a sacrifice of something that could not survive it and has survived anyway. Not metaphorically. The Covenant Test at Apex Blood Dominion demanded a price — the nature of that price determined whether the Sacrificed exists at all — and what emerged from the payment is not what went in. The Sacrificed is the covenant's most extreme proof: a demonstration that the accounting is real, that what is paid produces what the covenant promised, and that the dynasty has gone further into the terms of the relationship than any dynasty that would not make this sacrifice. Their presence on the field is a statement. The statement is that the dynasty has no remaining hesitations about the terms of what it follows.

### The Order

**Iron Legionary (L3 — Light Doctrine)**

The Iron Legionary is the military expression of what the Order produces when it has invested in its governing principle as a genuine faith rather than as institutional efficiency. The Legionary fights within a structure that they understand as sacred, not just as tactically sound. Formation discipline, chain of command, the hierarchy of authority — these are not merely military conventions for the Legionary. They are the covenant's content made physical. This produces a specific kind of military unit: one that is exceptionally effective in sustained, organized operations and that performs particularly well when operating as part of a larger coordinated force, because coordination is what they exist to embody.

**Warden-Enforcer (L3 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Warden-Enforcer is the Order's enforcement instrument. Where the Iron Legionary defends the covenant's structure, the Warden-Enforcer extends it by force. Their role is to impose the covenant's hierarchy on territory and populations that have not yet been ordered — or that have rejected ordering and must be brought into compliance by means other than persuasion. The Warden-Enforcer operates in pairs and small units rather than mass formations, because their work is targeted: specific individuals who represent resistance to ordering, specific communities that require the demonstration of the covenant's reach, specific territories where the presence of the enforcement apparatus changes the political calculation for everyone watching.

**Iron Sentinel (L4 — Light Doctrine)**

The Iron Sentinel is the culmination of what the Order's light doctrine military path produces: a fighting force that operates with the precision and coordination of a system rather than the initiative of individuals. Where individual units operate at the quality of their individual members, Iron Sentinels operate at the quality of the system that produced and coordinates them. Against mass charges, against cavalry, against the chaotic engagements that favor agile and individually powerful units, the Iron Sentinel formation is the answer — it does not break, because breaking is not what systems do. They adapt. They hold. They execute the doctrine's instruction until the instruction is complete.

**Dominion Arbiter (L4 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Dominion Arbiter carries both enforcement authority and the full apparatus of the dark doctrine's administrative-military capabilities. They are the Order's argument made armed and given independent judgment within the covenant's framework. A Dominion Arbiter can be deployed into a newly captured territory and rapidly establish the administrative and enforcement architecture that brings the territory into compliance — not through governance in the light doctrine sense, but through the installation of a fear-based structure that does not require the population's agreement, only their behavioral conformity. They are, in the Order's vocabulary, efficient. In any other vocabulary, they are something else, but the Order's vocabulary is the one that applies here.

**The Mandate (L5 — Apex, The Order)**

The Mandate is the Order's apex unit and its most formal statement. The Mandate does not exist as an individual in the way that other units do. It exists as the incarnation of the covenant's organizing principle in a single person who has been through the full development of what the Order's faith path can produce. When The Mandate appears on the battlefield, it is not the dynasty demonstrating military power. It is the dynasty demonstrating that its governing principle has produced something the world has not seen since before The Great Frost — a fully realized expression of what organized authority becomes when it takes the covenant completely seriously. Other armies looking at The Mandate are looking at an argument that their own organizational principles cannot match. The argument is correct. It is also deeply, specifically what the Order has been building toward.

### The Wild

**Thornrider (L3 — Light Doctrine)**

The Thornrider is cavalry whose relationship with its mount reflects the light doctrine's vision of harmony between the human and the living world. They are fast, effective in terrain that other cavalry finds difficult, and capable of operations that horse cavalry cannot perform in dense forest, broken highland, or other environments where the Wild's territorial knowledge provides asymmetric advantage. The relationship between the Thornrider and their mount is a practiced one — not the command-and-execute relationship of conventional cavalry, but the coordinated movement of two beings who have learned each other's capabilities across extended time. This relationship is visible in how they move and fight.

**Bear Rider (L3 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Bear Rider exists because the dark doctrine's interpretation of what it means to have a covenant with the living world includes the living world's most dangerous expressions. The bear is not trained in the conventional sense. The covenant between the rider and the bear is specific to the dark doctrine's framework — it is an alignment with the bear's own nature rather than an imposition of human purposes on an animal. The Bear Rider fights in ways that reflect this: the bear's combat behavior is not entirely controllable, because that is not the covenant's promise. The covenant's promise is that the bear fights alongside the rider, not that it follows tactical instructions. Against infantry in open terrain, a Bear Rider is one of the most dangerous combat assets in the game. Against organized defensive formations with spear walls and disciplined hold posture, it is more complicated, because the bear does not adjust tactics. The bear attacks.

**Forest Sovereign (L4 — Light Doctrine)**

The Forest Sovereign is the culmination of what years of deep investment in the Wild's light doctrine military path produces: a commander who operates with the living world as a genuine ally rather than as terrain to navigate. The Forest Sovereign does not merely benefit from favorable terrain. They change what the terrain does. Their presence in a forest, highland, or river valley environment produces effects that stem from the covenant's depth — the territory itself responds to the dynasty's presence at this level of faith. Enemy forces entering territory where a Forest Sovereign is committed find the land working against them in ways that are not supernatural in appearance but are systematically hostile in effect. The paths close. The animals are not neutral. The weather favors the Wild.

**Bonebreaker (L4 — Dark Doctrine)**

The Bonebreaker is named by those who encountered it, not by those who produce it. The Wild's dark doctrine does not have a name for what this unit is other than the covenant's full expression — the living world's predatory logic taken to its Level 4 military conclusion. The Bonebreaker fights the way the wild hunts when it has no reason to hold back: with total commitment, without the tactical hesitation that accompanies the calculation of cost, without mercy in the precise sense that the wild world does not extend mercy to prey. It is devastating against units that rely on morale — the presence of a Bonebreaker unit in a battle has a fear effect on enemy formations that builds as the engagement continues. Against an organized, disciplined force that holds formation under fear pressure, the Bonebreaker is still dangerous but must work harder. Against an army that breaks, it is a catastrophe for what breaks.

**The First Wild (L5 — Apex, The Wild)**

The First Wild has not existed in the world since before The Great Frost. The name is not metaphor — within the Wild's theology, The First Wild is what walked the world before civilization arrived and wounded it. What the match produces when a dynasty reaches Apex Wild intensity and passes the Covenant Test is not quite that — it is a living person who has been through the covenant's full demand and emerged having become something the world does not currently have a category for. The First Wild on the map is an event, not a unit placement. Other dynasties notice. They react. The political and military weight of the Wild covenant at this level is not merely the capability of the unit — it is the statement the unit's existence makes about what the dynasty has aligned with and how completely it has aligned with it. The First Wild changes the perception of every other party in the match. It announces that the wild has produced something capable of walking with it at full depth. This has consequences.

## Section 5: Faith Buildings — Architecture and Significance

### The Old Light

**Wayshrine**

The Wayshrine is the Old Light's first permanent faith structure, and it is intentionally humble relative to what it eventually becomes. The theology here is specific: civilization was built step by step, and the covenant honors that process by making its first building a marker rather than a monument. The Wayshrine is placed at the edge of a claimed territory, at a crossroads, at the entrance to a community, or at a site where something significant happened — a battle, a birth, a death, a moment the community wants to remember. It is a physical act of memory.

Architecturally: stone, always — wood is too temporary for what the Old Light commits to permanence. Square footprint, modest height, a carved inscription in the dynasty's form of the covenant's language, and a flame that the community is responsible for maintaining. The flame is not decorative. If the community lets the flame go out, the Wayshrine's intensity generation drops until it is relit. This is intentional — the building produces what the community puts into it.

Mechanically: modest faith intensity generation within a small radius. No ritual capability beyond basic observances. Unlocks the ability to assign a Bloodline Member to the faith Leader role. Costs: stone primarily, modest gold for inscription and materials. Construction time: short — this is the foundation, not the cathedral.

**Hall of Remembrance**

The Hall of Remembrance is where the Old Light's theology of memory becomes architectural. This is a serious structure — not grand in the way the Eternal Cathedral is grand, but serious. The names of the dead are recorded here. The decisions made by past heads of bloodline are preserved here. The legal judgments, the treaties, the significant events of the dynasty's governance — all maintained in physical form, available to be consulted, referenced, and returned to. The Old Light believes that civilization that cannot access its own past is civilization that will repeat its collapse.

Architecturally: the Hall is built to endure. Its walls are thick. Its interior is organized around accessibility of record — long shelves, illuminated when matches allow, with the dynasty's history in physical form on them. The exterior carries the dynasty's symbols in the Old Light's interpretation — not as ornamentation but as identification, the structure announcing its purpose. Light enters through high windows, falling on the records below. The building is not warm. It is serious. It honors seriousness.

Mechanically: significant faith intensity generation across a larger radius than the Wayshrine. Enables major Old Light rituals, including the Grand Rite of consecration for new territory. Bloodline Member Faith Leader development accelerates. Unlocks a specific political effect: territories adjacent to a Hall of Remembrance gain a permanent loyalty bonus as the population recognizes the dynasty's investment in institutional memory. Costs: stone heavily, gold moderately, some wood for interior fittings. Construction time: significant — this building takes time and that is by design.

**The Eternal Cathedral**

The Eternal Cathedral is the Old Light's proof. It is what a dynasty that has fully committed to the covenant eventually builds when it has reached the combination of resources, faith intensity, and political stability required to construct something at this scale. The Covenant Test at Apex Old Light will demand this construction — the covenant literally requires that the dynasty demonstrate its commitment by building it. The Cathedral is not a building for the dynasty alone. It is a building for the civilization the dynasty is reconstructing, and it is built at a scale appropriate to that ambition.

Architecturally: massive. Stone construction of the highest quality the dynasty can access, which at this development stage means extensive iron reinforcement, carved facades, spires that are visible from significant distance across the territory, interior space that is explicitly intended to be overwhelming in its scale — the covenant wants the person standing inside it to feel the weight of what has been built and what it represents. The Eternal Cathedral contains all of the Hall of Remembrance's record-keeping functions, expanded. It contains ceremonial space for the dynasty's major faith events. It contains a dedicated space for the Faith Leader at whatever intensity they have developed to. The flame in the Cathedral is permanent — not a community obligation but a structural feature that cannot go out.

Mechanically: the highest faith intensity generation in the game for the Old Light. Its construction at Apex intensity unlocks apex-tier faith capabilities including The Unbroken's production eligibility. The Cathedral's presence in a territory produces faith intensity across a radius large enough to cover a significant portion of the dynasty's domain. Its construction satisfies the Covenant Test's requirement for Apex Old Light dynasties. Costs: stone massively, iron significantly, gold significantly. Construction time: the longest of any faith building — this is a match-defining investment.

---

### Blood Dominion

**Blood-Altar**

The Blood-Altar is not a subtle structure. It does not pretend to be something other than what it is, which is exactly the covenant's theological point — other faiths perform their transactions in language that obscures what is being exchanged. The Blood-Altar announces the exchange plainly. It is an altar, built from stone and marked with the covenant's symbols in ways that require no interpretation, and it is a place where blood is given and something is received. This is the start.

Architecturally: lower than the Old Light's structures, broader, with a flat top surface that serves the altar's purpose without ambiguity. The stone is not pale — it is dark, darkened further by what has been performed on it over time. The Blood-Altar grows visually with the dynasty's faith development: the weathering, the staining, the additional carved channels that carry blood away from the altar surface toward the ground, all accumulate as the dynasty deepens its commitment. A Blood-Altar in use looks different from one that is new. The difference is visible.

Mechanically: modest faith intensity generation, primarily within the immediate community. Enables basic Blood Dominion ceremonies — animal sacrifice rites, the seasonal blood observances that maintain the covenant's basic satisfaction. Unlocks the Covenant Officiant bloodline member role. Costs: stone primarily, with specific materials that the dynasty must have available or trade for. Construction time: short.

**Covenant Hall**

The Covenant Hall is where the blood covenant becomes institutional. This is the building that houses the dynasty's formal relationship with what it has bargained with — not just the altar where offerings are made, but the administrative apparatus of the covenant, the records of what has been given and what has been received, the management of the faith's ongoing demands at a scale beyond what a single altar can process.

Architecturally: enclosed, deliberately interior-facing. The Covenant Hall is not a building meant to be admired from outside. It is a building meant to function correctly on the inside. Its exterior is significant — the dynasty's symbols in Blood Dominion's visual language, which carries the covenant's mark in ways that are immediately legible to anyone who knows what to look for — but the interior is where the design investment is. Chambers for ceremony. Storage for the materials the covenant requires. A permanent space for the Covenant Officiant's records of transactions. The smell of the interior is specific and not entirely pleasant to those outside the covenant.

Mechanically: significant faith intensity generation. Enables major Blood Dominion rituals, including the more demanding ceremonies that require both the building's infrastructure and the Covenant Officiant's full development. Unlocks dark doctrine capabilities at higher investment levels. Provides access to a specific diplomatic effect: dynasties that know the Covenant Hall exists and understand what it means treat the dynasty differently — some with wariness, some with the specific respect that attaches to a party that will pay prices others will not. Costs: stone, specific covenant materials, gold. Construction time: moderate.

**The Wound**

The Wound is what the covenant eventually demands be built when the dynasty reaches the apex of what Blood Dominion can produce. Its name is the covenant's own — not a human description of what it looks like, but the covenant's designation for what this structure is in the relationship between the dynasty and what it follows. The Great Frost was a wound in the world. The Blood Dominion's theology holds that the Wound, properly opened and maintained, is a source of power that nothing else in the world can match — because it goes directly to the cost that the covenant has always charged for the power it provides.

Architecturally: unlike any other building in the game's visual language. The Wound does not look like a building in the conventional sense. It looks like something that has been done to the landscape — as though the ground itself has been opened and the structure has emerged from what was opened. The materials are stone, but the stone carries a quality that reflects what has been performed here over the course of the dynasty's faith development. The interior is not accessible in any conventional sense — it is the operative space of the covenant's highest demands, and what happens inside it is the Covenant Officiant's domain, not the player's direct management.

Mechanically: the highest faith intensity generation available to Blood Dominion, activating across a radius that reflects the covenant's full reach. Its construction satisfies the Covenant Test's requirement for Apex Blood Dominion dynasties. Enables the production of The Sacrificed — the apex unit. Enables the full range of dark doctrine capabilities including the sacrifice mechanics that the covenant unlocks at this tier. Costs: stone, iron, specific covenant materials, and a sacrifice that the game treats as a construction cost rather than a gameplay action — the building requires something that the dynasty must provide at the moment of construction, and that cost is real.

### The Order

**Lawpost**

The Lawpost is the Order's most direct and honest building. It is a post. It carries the law. The law is the covenant's content — not scripture, not theology, not prayer instructions, but the governing code that the Order treats as the expression of divine will. The Lawpost is planted in a community and the community's relationship to the Order begins at the moment of its installation. Not because the installation is pleasant. Because the law is now physically present and the consequences of the law's presence are visible to everyone who can read a posted notice.

Architecturally: functional above all. Stone base, iron fixtures — this is not decorative. The Lawpost is built to endure and to be unambiguous about what it is. The dynasty's symbols are on it, the Order's covenant marks are on it, and the law as the dynasty has codified it is on it. The post is positioned in the highest-traffic location in the community. Its position is strategic: maximum visibility, maximum reminder of what governs here.

Mechanically: modest faith intensity generation within the community radius. Immediately establishes the Order's legal presence in the territory — which has specific effects on population loyalty (mixed: populations with Order alignment respond positively, others less so) and on the territory's administrative efficiency. Unlocks the Order's basic ritual ceremonies, which are administrative rather than ceremonial in character — formal readings of law, public adjudications, civic observances. Costs: stone, iron, minimal gold. Construction time: short.

**Hall of Mandate**

The Hall of Mandate is where the Order's governing principle becomes institutional architecture. This is a serious administrative and faith building simultaneously — the Order does not separate these categories, which is the point. The Hall is where the dynasty's legal proceedings happen, where the Order's ceremonial functions are performed, where the dynasty's administrative officials receive their authority from the covenant's mandate. The Hall is both court and cathedral, because the Order's theology holds that these are not different things.

Architecturally: austere but significant. The Order's architectural language does not use ornamentation for decoration — every element of the building's exterior communicates the dynasty's standing, the covenant's authority, and the specific hierarchy that governs within. The interior is organized around the adjudication chamber at its center — a formal space where the dynasty's legal authority is exercised and where the covenant's mandate is explicitly invoked as the source of that authority. The walls carry the covenant's symbols in the Order's interpretation — not warm, not welcoming, but unambiguous.

Mechanically: significant faith intensity generation. Enables major Order rituals, including the formal ceremonies of territory acquisition that bring new lands under the covenant's mandate — a specific ceremony distinct from simple military conquest. Accelerates the development of the dynasty's administrative capabilities. Provides a specific bonus to conviction point generation through moral-axis actions within the territory's governance — the covenant's structure makes certain kinds of governing actions produce clearer conviction effects. Costs: stone and iron primarily, gold for the administrative apparatus. Construction time: moderate.

**The Iron Spire**

The Iron Spire is the Order's most visible structure and its most direct statement about what the covenant believes. It is tall. It is visible from far away. It is built from iron and stone in a combination that no other faith building uses, because the Order's covenant holds that iron is civilization's material — worked by human hands from what the world provides, transformed by human industry into something the world did not produce. The Spire is that transformation made architectural: the world's raw material ordered by human will into an expression of the mandate that governs correctly.

Architecturally: vertical. The Iron Spire reaches higher than any other faith building in the game's visual landscape. Its profile from a distance is immediately identifiable — the player, looking at the map, can see where the Order is present. The interior of the Spire carries the covenant's full administrative and ceremonial apparatus — everything the Hall of Mandate provided at larger scale, with the addition of the Spire's unique function as a sight line and information tower. The Order believes that governance requires information, and the Iron Spire is where that information is processed.

Mechanically: the highest faith intensity generation available to the Order, extending across a radius that reflects the Spire's physical dominance of the landscape. Its construction satisfies the Covenant Test's requirement for Apex Order dynasties — the covenant demands that the dynasty demonstrate its commitment to ordered civilization through the most demanding construction the covenant knows. Enables production of The Mandate. Provides a specific intelligence bonus: the dynasty's information visibility of adjacent territories increases, reflecting the Spire's function as a sight line. Costs: stone and iron heavily, gold significantly. Construction time: the longest available, and the Order considers this appropriate.

### The Wild

**Grove Marker**

The Grove Marker is not a building in the conventional sense. It is a designation. It is the Wild covenant's formal identification of a place as belonging to the living world's protection — not the dynasty's territory in the administrative sense, but the living world's acknowledged domain. The Marker itself is minimal: stone, carved with the Wild's covenant symbols, placed in a location that has meaning within the covenant's framework — a grove, a highland, a river's edge, a specific tree formation that the dynasty has recognized as significant. The Marker says: this is here, and the covenant knows it is here.

Architecturally: the Grove Marker is deliberately incompatible with the architectural language of the other three faiths. It is not built — it is placed. The stone is not cut into a building's shape. It stands as it was found, marked with symbols that are cut rather than constructed, in a location that the land chose rather than the dynasty chose. As the dynasty's faith intensity grows, the Grove Marker's surroundings change — the vegetation around it grows differently, the local animal populations become more present, the quality of the site begins to reflect the covenant's acknowledgment of it.

Mechanically: modest faith intensity generation within the area surrounding the Marker. Unlocks basic Wild covenant ceremonies — hunting rites, seasonal observances, the first animal kinship practices that form the basis of the Bear Rider and Thornrider roles. Establishes the Wild's territorial claim in the covenant's terms, which has specific interactions with Sacred Ground terrain. Costs: stone (for the marker itself), minimal gold. Construction time: very short — this is a placement, not a construction.

**Spirit Lodge**

The Spirit Lodge is where the Wild covenant's relationship with the living world becomes communal rather than individual. This is the building where the dynasty's community gathers in the covenant's framework — not for administrative or legal purposes, but for the actual practice of the Wild's relationship with what it follows. The Lodge is where the Hunt is organized and ritualized. Where the seasonal transitions are observed. Where the community's relationship with the animals who live in the territory is managed, developed, and deepened into the kind of partnership the light doctrine cultivates or the kind of alignment the dark doctrine pursues.

Architecturally: built from natural materials in a specific way — the Wild covenant holds that what is built from the living world should look like it emerged from the living world rather than being imposed on it. Wood and stone, not cut into sharp edges, arranged to work with rather than against the terrain. The Spirit Lodge sits low. It is wide rather than tall. Its interior is organized around a central fire and around the wall space where the covenant's symbols are expressed not in carved stone but in materials gathered from the territory itself — antlers, bones, bark, specific plants that the Wild associates with the covenant's content. The smell of the Spirit Lodge is the smell of the territory it sits in.

Mechanically: significant faith intensity generation across a larger area than the Grove Marker. Enables major Wild covenant ceremonies, including the animal kinship rites that unlock Bear Rider production on the dark doctrine path and the deeper Thornrider partnerships on the light path. Provides specific terrain bonuses to the dynasty's military operations within the Spirit Lodge's radius — the living world's awareness of the covenant's presence produces effects that benefit armies aligned with the Wild. Costs: wood primarily, stone, specific materials gathered from the territory. Construction time: moderate.

**The First Grove**

The First Grove is the Wild covenant's apex structure, and it is the most unusual of the four faiths' grand buildings in that it is not built at all in the conventional sense. The First Grove is grown. It is the cultivation of a site that has been marked since the Grove Marker was placed — the dynasty's long-term investment in the living world's relationship with the land it claims — into something that has become, over the course of the match, a space where the covenant's full depth is expressed in the living earth itself. A dynasty that reaches The First Grove has done something that the other three faiths cannot do: it has created something that was not created. It emerged.

Architecturally: The First Grove is a large area of living forest within the dynasty's territory that has been shaped by the covenant's presence into something distinct from the surrounding landscape. The trees are older than they should be — not supernaturally old, but aged beyond what the match timeline would explain. The fauna present within the Grove is specific and dense — the animals of the Wild's covenant are here in numbers not found elsewhere. The light within the Grove falls differently. The Marker that began the process is somewhere at the center, now barely visible under what has grown around it. There is no sharp boundary between the Grove and the surrounding land. The Grove simply becomes more itself as you move deeper into it.

Mechanically: the highest faith intensity generation available to the Wild, operating on a different logic than the other faiths' grand buildings — it radiates intensity proportional to the Grove's living health, which can be disrupted by enemy forces entering the area and improved by the dynasty's ongoing covenant investment. Its existence satisfies the Covenant Test's requirement for Apex Wild dynasties. Enables production of The First Wild. The First Grove territory is extremely difficult for non-Wild-aligned forces to operate within — the terrain properties, the animal populations, and the covenant's investment in the site make it the most hostile environment in the game for armies that do not carry the Wild's covenant. Costs: no gold or iron — the First Grove's cost is measured in time and in the continuing covenant investment across the match. The dynasty must have maintained faith intensity, performed the required ceremonies, and completed the Covenant Test.

## Section 6: The Covenant Test

### What It Is and When It Fires

The Covenant Test is a faith event that fires when any dynasty reaches Level 4 progression with an active Apex faith intensity. It is not optional. It cannot be deferred. When the conditions are met — Apex faith intensity reached, Level 4 progression active — the Covenant Test fires and the dynasty must respond.

The design principle behind the test: Apex faith intensity must represent something more than resource investment and building construction. A dynasty that has maintained 80-100% faith intensity has paid the material price of commitment, but the covenant demands more than material investment at its highest tier. The Covenant Test is the moment where the covenant asks the dynasty to demonstrate that its commitment is not merely systematic but genuine — that when the covenant demands a grand act, the dynasty does it.

The test is visible to all other dynasties from the moment it fires. The announcement that a dynasty is undergoing the Covenant Test is a world event — everyone knows, everyone watches, everyone adjusts their calculations based on whether the covenant is satisfied.

### What Each Faith Demands

**Old Light:**

The Old Light's Covenant Test demands construction. Specifically: the construction of the Eternal Cathedral. The covenant does not accept partial completion — the Cathedral must be complete and consecrated. The resources required are massive, the construction time is significant, and the test fires at a moment when the dynasty is typically managing multiple strategic pressures simultaneously. Building the Cathedral is a statement about priorities. It tells the world that this dynasty, at this moment, is putting the covenant's demand above military expansion, economic optimization, and political maneuvering.

The demand has a secondary layer: the Cathedral must be built in the dynasty's primary territory, not in a distant outpost. The covenant wants the grand expression of faith where the dynasty's heart is — where the head of bloodline lives, where the population the dynasty is responsible for can see it. This is the Old Light's theology in its most demanding form. The civilization is the evidence of the divine. Build the evidence where it matters most.

**Blood Dominion:**

The Blood Dominion's Covenant Test demands sacrifice. The specific nature of the sacrifice is determined by the doctrine path the dynasty has followed:

Light Doctrine test: a sacrifice of specific cost that the covenant designates at the moment the test fires. This may be a significant portion of the dynasty's current animal resources, a specific type of sacrifice appropriate to the covenant's demands, or a ceremony that requires the full participation of the dynasty's faith infrastructure. The light doctrine test is costly but does not require what the dark doctrine demands.

Dark Doctrine test: a sacrifice of blood at a scale that goes beyond what the light doctrine's ceremonies involve. The covenant may demand the sacrifice of a named bloodline member — a dormant or peripheral member, presented by the game as an eligible list from which the player chooses — or a sacrifice of unbeliever population at a scale that triggers world-level consequences. The dark doctrine Covenant Test is the moment where the dynasty that has followed the dark path must prove it will pay the dark path's full price. Failure at this point — refusing the sacrifice — costs significant faith intensity and produces a covenant breach event that has diplomatic and political consequences. Success is the covenant's most direct proof: the dynasty paid what was asked.

**The Order:**

The Order's Covenant Test demands subjugation. The covenant designates a specific territory — adjacent to the dynasty's current domain or within a defined proximity — and demands that it be brought under the Order's legal mandate. Not merely conquered. Subjected: a Hall of Mandate must be constructed within the territory within the test's timeframe, the territory's previous governance structure must be formally dissolved through a specific Order ceremony, and the territory's population must be processed through the Order's legal apparatus to establish compliance.

This is more than a military challenge — it requires the complete administrative investment in a new territory under time pressure. Dynasties pursuing the Order's Covenant Test are doing something military, administrative, and ceremonial simultaneously. Failure produces faith intensity loss and a specific diplomatic event: the Order's covenant marks the dynasty as having failed its mandate, which other Order-aligned dynasties notice.

**The Wild:**

The Wild's Covenant Test demands destruction. The covenant designates a specific high-value structure belonging to a rival dynasty — most commonly a grand faith structure, a major economic building, or a fortification of specific symbolic importance — and demands its destruction within the test's timeframe. This must be not merely damaged but demolished: reduced to rubble in a way that the living world can reclaim.

The Wild's theology is clear about why: civilization is a wound, and the Wild's commitment to its covenant is demonstrated by showing that the wound can be closed. The destruction of the designated structure is the covenant's demand that the dynasty reduce the world's wound rather than expand it. The dynasty must do this actively — it cannot be done through proxy, and it cannot be done through the slow economic pressure of siege. The structure must come down, and the Wild's dynasty must be the one that brings it down.

---

### Failure and Success

**Failure:** If the dynasty cannot complete the Covenant Test's demand within the designated window, the covenant registers the failure. Faith intensity drops — the magnitude of the drop scales to how far below the test's requirements the dynasty fell. A dynasty that made a serious attempt but fell short loses less than a dynasty that failed to attempt the test at all. The world event of covenant failure is announced — all dynasties know that this dynasty flinched at the moment the covenant demanded its full price. Diplomatic and political consequences follow. The dynasty can rebuild toward Apex intensity and attempt the test again, but the world's record of the failure persists.

**Success:** Completing the Covenant Test's demand produces the covenant's acknowledgment in full. Faith intensity is confirmed at Apex and stabilized — the dynasty does not immediately face intensity decay at the moment of success. The apex unit becomes producible. The grand faith building's construction is enabled if it was not the condition of the test itself. A world event announces the dynasty's success, which has diplomatic consequences in the opposite direction: dynasties aligned with the same covenant recognize what was accomplished; dynasties opposed to the covenant understand that they are now dealing with something that has completed its covenant in full.

## Section 7: Faith Interactions with Other Systems

### Faith and Conviction — The Independence Principle

Faith and Conviction are independent systems that interact but do not determine each other. This is the most important structural rule in the faith system.

A dynasty that follows the Old Light with Cruel conviction is not a contradiction. It is a dynasty that believes in the sacred duty of rebuilding civilization — genuinely believes it, builds the buildings, performs the rites, commits Bloodline Members to the faith roles — while governing through fear, taking prisoners without mercy, and accumulating conviction points in the negative direction. The Old Light's covenant does not require that the dynasty be kind. It requires that the dynasty commit to the covenant's institutional obligations. The Inquisitor tradition of the dark doctrine is explicitly designed for dynasties that can hold those two things simultaneously without resolving the tension between them.

A dynasty that follows the Wild with Moral conviction is following a covenant that, at its dark doctrine extreme, holds that mercy is a civilization's invention that the living world does not honor — and doing so with a leadership that consistently makes the morally positive choice in its governance. The Wild does not require Cruel conviction. The Wild requires commitment to the covenant's content, which in the light doctrine is compatible with Moral governance. The dark doctrine has a tension with Moral conviction that is part of the design — following the dark path with Moral conviction limits what the covenant can demand and what the doctrine can produce, because some dark doctrine acts require conviction thresholds the Moral player will not reach.

This interaction — faith doctrine limiting what conviction allows, conviction limiting what faith doctrine can demand — is a system relationship, not a locked gate. The systems interact through the Conviction threshold mechanics: dark doctrine acts that require extreme Cruel conviction are unavailable to Moral dynasties not because the game prevents them, but because the Moral conviction score is never in the range that unlocks them.

### Faith Spread Mechanics

Faith spreads through the world by the following mechanisms, all of which operate simultaneously:

**Cultural influence** operates through trade and border contact. Populations that share trade routes with a faith-practicing dynasty gradually develop exposure to that faith through the contact that trade produces. The rate of spread through cultural influence is slow and requires sustained contact — a dynasty that trades with the world for twenty match years produces measurable faith spread along its trade routes, but not a rapid conversion.

**Conquest** produces immediate and intense faith pressure. A dynasty that conquers territory populated by followers of a rival covenant faces a choice: suppress the existing faith through enforcement, install its own faith infrastructure and allow natural conversion to proceed, or govern the mixed population and manage the tensions that multiple covenants produce in close proximity. Active suppression through force is a dark doctrine tool — it produces faster conversion at the cost of population loyalty damage and conviction point costs. Passive installation of faith buildings and allowing cultural influence to operate is slower but produces genuine conversion rather than compliance.

**Missionary activity** is a specific faith investment that speeds the spread process beyond passive cultural influence. Dynasties at Devout or higher intensity can deploy Bloodline Members in a missionary role that actively works to spread the covenant in adjacent territories. This is most effective in territories without a strong competing faith presence. Against territories with Devout or higher rival faith intensity, missionary activity is resisted and may produce a Faith Conflict event between the two dynasties.

**Shared borders** produce gradual passive spread in both directions. Two dynasties with different faiths sharing a long border will see their respective faith populations in the border regions slowly shift toward mixed practice over extended match time. This cannot be entirely prevented without active enforcement — the populations in contact simply develop exposure to each other's covenants. The rate is slow enough that it is not a major factor in short engagements, but in a ten-hour match between neighbors with different covenants, the border regions will look different from the interior in terms of faith composition.

### Global Faith Tracking

The game tracks the global distribution of faith across all population segments continuously. This information is available to the player through the Trueborn City's information network — a dynasty with active trade relations with the city receives accurate, timely data on global faith distribution. A dynasty cut off from the city's trade network receives degraded or delayed information.

What global faith tracking produces mechanically:

**Holy war triggers:** When a covenant reaches a defined threshold of global population share — and a rival covenant has been identified as responsible for active suppression of the dominant faith — the dominant faith's aligned dynasties receive a casus belli bonus for war against the suppressor. Holy wars declared through this mechanism carry different diplomatic costs than conventional wars.

**Coalition formation:** The global faith map is one of the inputs that drives coalition formation. Dynasties that share a covenant at Devout or higher have a natural diplomatic alignment that the game represents through reduced coalition formation costs and enhanced alliance proposal quality between them. This creates a faith-based political layer that operates alongside the military and economic alliance systems.

**Civilizational division:** When the global faith map shows two or more faiths in rough equilibrium — no single covenant dominant — the world is in a state of civilizational division that produces specific political event triggers. These events tend toward conflict at the fault lines where different faith populations are in contact. They also produce opportunities: a dynasty skilled at navigating faith differences, or one that has deliberately cultivated relationships across covenant lines, can exploit the division rather than being consumed by it.

**Divine Right prerequisites:** The faith victory path — Divine Right declaration — requires that the declaring dynasty's faith be established as the globally dominant covenant before the declaration can be made. The global faith tracking is what determines whether this prerequisite has been met, and it is public information — every dynasty knows how close any covenant is to the dominance threshold.

### The Trueborn City as Faith-Neutral Ground

The Trueborn City does not favor any covenant. This is not neutrality born of indifference — it is neutrality born of the city's foundational understanding that it survived The Great Frost precisely because it maintained a space that all factions could use without compromising their own commitments. The city's neutrality is its value proposition, and the city's leadership — the Trueborn — understands this completely.

In practical terms: faith buildings from all four covenants can exist within and around the Trueborn City's zone. No covenant is suppressed. No covenant is privileged. The diplomatic processes that happen in the city — negotiations, marriages, coalition formations, treaty ratifications — are not conducted under any covenant's auspices. All four covenants can negotiate through the city on equal terms.

This makes the city uniquely valuable as a diplomatic venue for dynasties whose faith alignment would otherwise make cross-covenant negotiation difficult. A Blood Dominion dynasty in hostile relations with an Old Light dynasty can negotiate in the city without the faith dimension overriding the diplomatic content. The city provides a context where the covenant does not govern the interaction. This is rare enough in the game world that it is genuinely significant.

The Trueborn's own position on faith is complex: the Trueborn have a historical relationship with all four covenants that predates The Great Frost, and their neutrality is informed by that history. They do not practice any covenant publicly. They maintain what functions as a fifth position — the position of a people who remember what the world was before the covenants divided it.

### Bloodline Members in Faith Roles

Bloodline Members who commit to faith roles are the dynasty's primary interface with the covenant's demands and capabilities. Their development is not separate from the faith intensity system — it is tied to it.

The Faith Leader and Covenant Officiant are the two named faith roles (CB003, established), with the Faith Leader serving the light doctrine path and the Covenant Officiant serving the dark doctrine path. Beyond these two primary roles, Bloodline Members can express faith alignment through the visual identity system — their appearance at Devout, Fervent, and Apex intensity reflects the covenant's hold on them — and through conviction interactions that create tension between a member's personal conviction posture and the doctrine path the dynasty is following.

A Bloodline Member assigned to a faith role who later experiences a conviction event that pushes them away from the doctrine's requirements creates a Faith Instability event specific to that member. A Moral conviction member assigned as a Covenant Officiant who is asked to perform a dark doctrine Binding they cannot execute without violating their conviction — this is a real game state, and it has consequences for both the faith intensity and the member's development trajectory.

The death of a faith-role Bloodline Member produces, in addition to the intensity loss documented in the intensity section, a specific grief event among the population segments that were most closely associated with that member's practice. An Old Light Faith Leader at Apex who dies in battle leaves a religious community that will experience a period of genuine uncertainty — not mechanically expressed as population revolt necessarily, but as the faith intensity's passive decay rate temporarily increasing until a replacement is identified and developed.

Priests and priestesses — Bloodline Members on the light doctrine path of any covenant — generate faith intensity through their presence and their performance of the covenant's ongoing ceremonial requirements. They are not just flavor. They are infrastructure. A dynasty with two developed Bloodline Members in faith roles is building its faith intensity through a different mechanism than one that relies entirely on buildings, and the resilience of that generation against the building-targeting strategies that rival dynasties might pursue is higher.

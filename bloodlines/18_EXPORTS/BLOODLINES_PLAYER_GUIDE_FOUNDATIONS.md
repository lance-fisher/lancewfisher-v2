# Bloodlines
## Foundational Player Manual
### Volume I: A New Ruler's Guide to Dynasty, War, Faith, and Rule

> **Edition note:** This opening volume is written as the foundational official player guide for Bloodlines. It teaches the strategic identity of the game, the role of each Dynasty, and the major systems that define play. Later volumes can deepen exact roster sheets, matchup doctrine, and advanced system interactions, but the guidance in this document reflects the current player-facing truth of Bloodlines: this is a game about ruling a living Dynasty, not merely directing an army.

---

## Welcome to Bloodlines

Bloodlines is a grand medieval real-time strategy game built around dynastic rule, territorial command, population pressure, faith, conviction, and long-form strategic consequence. It carries the battlefield readability and faction clarity of a classic Command and Conquer-style RTS, but it extends far beyond a simple contest of build speed and unit count. In Bloodlines, the realm itself is part of the battlefield.

You do not command a disposable war machine. You govern a Dynasty. Your armies come from your people. Your economy rests on food, water, labor, land, and infrastructure. Your rulers, heirs, governors, commanders, envoys, priests, merchants, and spymasters are living bloodline members whose survival and placement matter. Your faith shapes your covenantal identity. Your conviction records the kind of rule you actually practice. Your victories create political and moral consequences that continue after the battle ends.

This means Bloodlines rewards coherence more than spectacle. A realm with disciplined growth, protected population, reliable water, secure food, stable loyalty, well-used bloodline members, and a clear strategic identity can defeat a louder realm that expanded too fast, fought too recklessly, or ruled too carelessly. Bloodlines is not a game that flatters impatience. It tests whether your Dynasty deserves to endure.

## What Bloodlines Is

At its core, Bloodlines is a large-scale dynasty-driven RTS with five major layers operating at once:

1. **Realm building:** housing, labor, food, water, materials, and civic structure.
2. **Territorial control:** marches, settlements, loyalty, stabilization, governance, and frontier risk.
3. **Military command:** clear battlefield roles, sieges, raids, sustainment, fortifications, and decisive engagements.
4. **Dynastic rule:** bloodline members, heirs, governors, commanders, marriages, capture, succession, and legitimacy.
5. **Moral and spiritual identity:** covenantal faith, faith intensity, doctrine path, conviction, and remembered conduct.

Bloodlines begins as a severe realm-founding RTS and grows into a broader dynastic struggle. A match moves through five stages:

| Stage | Name | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| I | Founding | Secure people, food, water, housing, local defense, and the first shape of your realm. |
| II | Expansion and Identity | Expand beyond the first perimeter, choose a covenant, establish your governing posture, and make your Dynasty recognizable. |
| III | Encounter and Establishment | Rival Dynasties matter directly, trade and diplomacy harden, the neutral city becomes strategic, and territorial politics deepen. |
| IV | War and the Turning of Tides | Sustained campaigns, sieges, faith pressure, conviction strain, and bloodline risk define the match. |
| V | Final Convergence | Sovereignty, Divine Right, coalition response, apex systems, and the final shape of the world order are decided. |

The game is designed so that strategy does not collapse into one question. Building the biggest army helps, but it is never enough by itself. A starving army breaks. A thirsty army weakens. A hated governor loses the territory he just took. A dead heir can wound a realm more deeply than a lost outpost. A victorious dynasty that rules through terror may find itself feared, isolated, and resisted by the wider world. A pious realm with no military seriousness may discover that good intentions do not hold walls.

### Bloodlines Versus A Simpler RTS

| In a simpler RTS | In Bloodlines |
|---|---|
| Territory touched is territory owned. | Territory must be held, stabilized, and accepted. |
| Economy is mostly money and production. | Economy is people, food, water, housing, labor, trade, materials, and sustainment. |
| Leaders are optional flavor. | Bloodline members are strategic assets with real political consequence. |
| Morality is cosmetic or absent. | Conviction is tracked through action and changes what kind of realm you become. |
| Religion is a small bonus tree. | Faith is a covenantal system with intensity, doctrine, rites, structures, pressure, and strategic cost. |
| Winning a battle is the whole story. | Post-battle rulings, prisoner handling, bloodline capture, civilian treatment, and regional stability can matter more than the clash itself. |

## Why These Systems Matter

New players often understand Bloodlines more quickly when they stop asking only, "What should I build?" and start asking, "What kind of realm am I creating?"

| Question | Why It Matters In Bloodlines |
|---|---|
| **Why does choosing a Dynasty matter?** | Because each Dynasty carries a different opening rhythm, pressure profile, civic tendency, and long-term way of ruling. |
| **Why does protecting people matter?** | Because your population is your labor, army pool, tax base, recovery base, faith participation, and future. Losing people is structural damage. |
| **Why is economy more than gold?** | Because prosperity in Bloodlines is built from food, water, housing, labor, materials, and political stability, not from treasury alone. |
| **Why do food and water matter so much?** | Because they govern growth, morale, sustainment, and the realm's ability to survive war without rotting from within. |
| **Why do bloodline members matter?** | Because commanders, governors, heirs, envoys, and spiritual leaders change what your Dynasty can do and what it risks losing. |
| **Why does faith matter?** | Because faith is a covenantal commitment with structures, rites, doctrine, and obligations that shape the civilization over time. |
| **Why does Conviction matter?** | Because it determines what your Dynasty has actually become through action, and the world responds to that truth. |
| **Why does tactical conduct matter?** | Because how you fight influences morale, survival, legitimacy, and the memory of your rule. |
| **Why do post-battle decisions matter?** | Because prisoners, captives, civilians, and conquered settlements become the next political and moral chapter of the match. |
| **Why is expansion alone not enough?** | Because land that cannot be fed, watered, housed, defended, and governed becomes a burden before it becomes power. |

Bloodlines is built to make these questions inseparable. A Dynasty does not win only by acquiring more. It wins by making what it acquires livable, governable, and worth preserving.

## Core Principles of Play

### 1. You Rule a Dynasty, Not a Base

Your command hall, keeps, armies, and workers matter, but they are expressions of a ruling bloodline rather than the whole game. The condition of your Dynasty shapes how well those tools perform. A battle led by a strong commander is not the same battle led by no one. A frontier with a rooted governor is not the same frontier held only by force. A succession-secure realm is not the same realm as one whose heir is dead or captive.

### 2. Population Is the First Military Resource

Population is not background flavor. People are your workforce, your army pool, your faith participants, your tax base, your recovery capacity, and the living body of the realm. Every soldier recruited came from the population. Every soldier lost is a real cost. Every famine, massacre, harsh levy, plague, or attritional war marks the Dynasty long after the immediate fight is over.

Protecting population is therefore not sentimental play. It is sound strategy.

### 3. Food and Water Are Strategic, Not Decorative

Food feeds the people and sustains campaigns. Water supports growth, settlement health, and armies in the field. A realm that neglects either can look strong for a short time and then break under strain. Bloodlines treats water and food as attackable, defendable systems. Raiding farms, severing wells, cutting convoy links, or isolating a force from its water anchors can decide wars without a single glorious field battle.

### 4. Housing Is the Skeleton of Growth

A Dynasty that wants more people must build the capacity to keep them. Housing is not a minor support structure. It is the foundation that makes population growth real. If you recruit and expand without building housing and civic support, the realm begins to hollow out behind the front.

### 5. Loyalty Turns Occupation Into Rule

A conquered province is not truly yours until the people, the local structures, and your governing apparatus accept the fact of your rule. Low loyalty reduces revenue, weakens recruitment, increases sabotage risk, and turns every failure into a possible revolt. Bloodlines does not ask only whether you took land. It asks whether you can keep it.

### 6. Bloodline Members Matter Because Politics Matters

Heads of bloodline, heirs, commanders, governors, envoys, ideological leaders, merchants, and spymasters are not passive portraits. They change what your realm can do. They are also vulnerable. A bloodline member killed in battle, captured during a siege, or married into a rival house can reshape the political meaning of the entire match.

### 7. Faith Gives the Realm Sacred Shape

Faith is a covenantal commitment. It changes how your Dynasty understands order, sacrifice, law, nature, legitimacy, and the sacred obligations of rule. It is maintained through structures, participation, rites, and intensity. A Dynasty that claims a covenant but never truly supports it will feel spiritually thin and strategically inconsistent.

### 8. Conviction Gives the Realm Moral Shape

Conviction records how your Dynasty behaves in victory, defeat, punishment, mercy, coercion, and rule. It is not a cosmetic roleplaying label. It is one of the clearest ways Bloodlines decides what kind of power you have built.

### 9. War Continues After the Battle

The clash itself matters, but the aftermath matters too. Captives, wounded leaders, destroyed farms, shattered loyalty, executed prisoners, pardoned rebels, ransomed heirs, displaced populations, and the memory of how a Dynasty fought all become part of the long game.

### 10. Expansion Must Be Sustained To Matter

Bloodlines rewards growth, but only when growth makes the realm stronger rather than thinner. The correct question is never, "Can I take one more territory?" It is, "Can I feed it, water it, staff it, hold it, and keep it loyal while the rest of my realm remains functional?"

## The Living Realm: Population, Rule, and Territory

Bloodlines is easiest to understand when you stop thinking in separate boxes and start seeing one living cycle:

people work the land, the land feeds the people, the people sustain the army, the army takes territory, the territory must be governed, the governors and commanders must survive, the faith must remain believable, and the Dynasty's conduct determines whether the whole structure becomes stronger or more fragile.

### Economy Is More Than Gold

Gold matters, but it is not the whole economy and it is rarely the first thing that breaks a Dynasty.

What actually keeps a realm alive is the combined condition of:

- population
- food
- water
- housing
- labor allocation
- materials and infrastructure
- loyalty and tax health
- bloodline continuity
- military sustainment

Money can accelerate many things. It cannot by itself make an undersupplied realm healthy, a thirsty army effective, or a hated province loyal.

### Population, Food, Water, and Housing

These four belong together.

| System | What it does | What happens if you neglect it |
|---|---|---|
| **Population** | Supplies labor, recruitment, faith participation, tax base, recovery, and long-term strength | The Dynasty hollows out and loses both present force and future growth |
| **Food** | Sustains people and armies, underwrites growth, and supports recovery after war | Famine, lowered morale, loyalty loss, weaker campaigns |
| **Water** | Supports settlement health, population stability, irrigation, and campaigning armies | Growth stalls, armies weaken in the field, crisis pressure spreads |
| **Housing** | Determines how much growth the Dynasty can actually hold and organize | Crowding, stalled population development, weakened productivity, future recruitment strain |

The lesson for a new player is simple: do not build a war machine that your people cannot survive.

### Loyalty And Governance

Military force can seize land. Governance is what turns that seizure into rule.

Loyalty is shaped by:

- whether the population is fed and watered
- whether the local territory is protected
- whether governors, bloodline authority, and civic support are present
- whether the Dynasty rules with legitimacy, fairness, or naked coercion
- whether the people experience the realm as order or burden

A province with low loyalty is not just "less efficient." It becomes hesitant, resentful, and dangerous. Taxes weaken. Recruitment slows. Sabotage becomes easier. Rebellion moves from possibility to expectation.

This is why overexpansion is so dangerous in Bloodlines. Each new holding increases not only opportunity, but governing responsibility.

### Bloodline Rule

A Bloodlines realm is not governed by faceless modifiers. Bloodline members stand inside the structure of rule.

The player is not only deciding what to build, but who will carry the Dynasty's authority into the world:

- a **commander** gives armies morale, cohesion, and political meaning
- a **governor** roots rule into territory and suppresses instability
- an **heir** secures succession and reduces the terror of dynastic transition
- an **envoy** turns diplomacy, marriage, and release negotiations into real strategic tools
- an **ideological leader** or covenant authority turns faith into living civic force
- a **merchant** or trade anchor converts routes into durable leverage
- a **spymaster** changes how much of the world can remain hidden from you

When one of these figures dies, is captured, or is misplaced, the realm does not simply lose a bonus. It loses a pillar.

### Territorial Command

Territory in Bloodlines is more than a colored shape on the map. It is a governed responsibility.

A good ruler asks of every march, settlement, and frontier:

- Can it feed itself or be fed?
- Can it access water securely?
- Can it be reinforced?
- Does it have enough civic support and bloodline attention?
- Is it stable enough to contribute, or only quiet enough to endure for now?

Territorial command rewards depth, not touch. The Dynasty that captures land cleanly, stabilizes it, seats authority into it, and protects its lines becomes harder to dislodge than the Dynasty that merely spread wider.

### Warfare Is An Extension Of The Realm

War in Bloodlines is never fully separate from civic life.

Armies carry the condition of the Dynasty with them:

- well-governed realms campaign longer
- well-watered armies endure longer
- well-fed populations replace losses more safely
- loyal provinces support the front more reliably
- strong bloodline leadership keeps morale from collapsing
- disciplined rule makes conquest more sustainable

This is why Bloodlines punishes the player who chases military excitement before survival. The battlefield is not a break from the realm. It is the realm under strain.

### The Stable-Realm Test

If you are unsure whether your Dynasty is healthy, ask:

- Is my population growing or merely being consumed?
- Are food and water secure enough for both peace and war?
- Is housing ahead of need or trailing behind crisis?
- Are governors and bloodline roles placed where the realm actually needs them?
- Are my newest territories productive, loyal, and governable?
- Is my army supported by the realm behind it?

If too many of those answers are weak, the realm is not strong yet, no matter how large the army looks.

## Bloodline Members: The Pillars of Rule

Bloodlines does not treat rulers, heirs, commanders, governors, envoys, and other bloodline figures as decorative heroes. They are the living structure through which the Dynasty acts.

That is why bloodline management feels different here than in a simpler RTS. You are not attaching a generic buff unit to an army and forgetting about it. You are deciding who carries command, who anchors succession, who keeps a difficult province loyal, who speaks for the house abroad, and who can still hold the realm together if war goes badly.

### What Bloodline Members Actually Do

| Bloodline role | Why it matters to the player | What losing that member can mean |
|---|---|---|
| **Head of Bloodline** | Embodies legitimacy, dynastic continuity, and the public authority of the realm | Succession shock, legitimacy loss, political instability, opportunistic rivals |
| **Heir Designate** | Secures the next transfer of power and reassures the Dynasty that rule will continue cleanly | Fearful succession, weakened confidence, more dangerous transition after death or capture |
| **Commander** | Gives armies morale, cohesion, and political meaning in the field | Weaker campaigning, broken battlefield confidence, losses that spread beyond one army |
| **Governor** | Stabilizes territories, roots authority locally, and helps conquered land become governable | Loyalty decay, frontier fragility, higher risk of unrest or revolt |
| **Diplomat or Envoy** | Turns negotiation, marriages, ransoms, and alliance work into real leverage | Harder treaty play, weaker release options, reduced political flexibility |
| **Ideological Leader** | Gives faith visible authority and helps covenant life become public reality | Faith weakness, reduced spiritual coherence, weaker sacred legitimacy |
| **Merchant** | Anchors trade, treasury leverage, and the conversion of prosperity into influence | Commercial fragility, worse route value, weaker economic diplomacy |
| **Spymaster** | Expands intelligence, counters covert pressure, and makes hidden danger more legible | Blinder rule, weaker counterplay, increased vulnerability to sabotage and targeted disruption |

Some realms may also cultivate darker or more specialized bloodline paths, including arcane or sacrificial authorities, but the lesson for a new player is unchanged: bloodline members are not side pieces. They are pillars.

### Why This Changes How You Play

Once you understand bloodline members correctly, several Bloodlines habits make more sense:

- You stop thinking of every war as a clean arithmetic problem, because important people can be captured or killed.
- You stop treating succession as a late-game abstraction, because even an early heir loss can darken the whole match.
- You stop seeing governors as optional polish, because territory without authority is often only temporarily yours.
- You stop treating diplomacy as menu text, because the right envoy can turn marriage, ransom, and release into strategic weapons.

Bloodline members make the Dynasty feel personal, but they also make it more demanding. A reckless realm may still win battles. It may not survive the deaths those victories cost.

### How To Use Bloodline Members Well

The best new-player approach is disciplined, not timid.

- **Protect succession early.** Make sure the house can survive a sudden death or capture without falling into dynastic panic.
- **Put commanders where the match will actually turn.** Do not waste bloodline risk on theaters that do not matter.
- **Seat governors where loyalty is a real question.** A stable heartland can wait; a fragile frontier often cannot.
- **Do not expose every important member at once.** A Dynasty that stacks all its meaning in one risky campaign invites catastrophe.
- **Match the role to the realm's problem.** If the realm is starving, a perfect war commander will not solve the true crisis. If the frontier is collapsing, another merchant may not be the next answer.
- **Think politically after every capture or death.** The loss of a member changes confidence, leverage, and the story the world tells about your Dynasty.

### Bloodline Risk Is Part Of Realm Identity

Bloodlines asks not only whether your bloodline members lived, but what their use says about the house.

A careful ruler may keep the line secure, assign authority deliberately, and build a Dynasty that feels ordered, durable, and difficult to destabilize.

A reckless ruler may gamble heirs in symbolic campaigns, leave new territory unguided, or ignore succession until a battlefield death tears the center out of the realm.

Neither path is neutral. Both create a recognizable Dynasty.

### First-Match Bloodline Priorities

In your first matches, do not try to play bloodline members theatrically. Play them correctly.

1. **Secure the line of rule.**
2. **Assign the first high-value role to the pressure your realm is actually facing.**
3. **Keep a capable member close enough to steady the heart of the Dynasty.**
4. **Avoid preventable bloodline losses during low-value early risks.**
5. **Treat capture, ransom, release, and succession as strategic events, not side stories.**

If you learn this early, Bloodlines becomes much easier to read. You stop seeing portraits. You start seeing the machinery of rule.

## Faith and Conviction: The Soul of a Dynasty

No new player truly understands Bloodlines until they understand this distinction:

- **Faith is what covenant your Dynasty follows, how intensely it follows it, and how that covenant is practiced.**
- **Conviction is how your Dynasty actually behaves in rule, warfare, justice, punishment, and mercy.**

They are related. They are not the same.

### What Faith Is

Faith is your Dynasty's covenantal and spiritual alignment. Bloodlines contains four ancient covenants:

| Covenant | Core Promise | What It Tends To Reward |
|---|---|---|
| **The Old Light** | Civilization, protection, sacred law, stewardship, defense of the innocent | Legitimate order, defensive seriousness, civic reconstruction, visible duty |
| **The Blood Dominion** | Power with acknowledged cost, sacrifice, conquest, blood-price, grim honesty | Military intensity, sacrificial power, hard authority, severe spiritual transaction |
| **The Order** | Mandate, structure, institution, doctrine, hierarchy, law as sacred form | Administrative control, obedience, formal governance, disciplined state power |
| **The Wild** | Land, ancient force, balance, root, beast, terrain, primal survival | Environmental asymmetry, adaptive warfare, living territory, nature-bound pressure |

Faith in Bloodlines has its own internal layers:

- **Covenant:** which of the four ancient faiths you follow.
- **Faith intensity:** how deeply the population participates and how strongly the covenant grips the realm.
- **Doctrine path:** the way that covenant is expressed, including lighter and darker traditions within the same faith.
- **Sacred structures and rites:** the buildings, obligations, ceremonies, and late-stage powers through which the covenant becomes real.

Faith answers questions such as:

- What sacred order does this Dynasty belong to?
- How public, demanding, and deep is that commitment?
- What rites, structures, leaders, and obligations sustain the covenant?
- What benefits or pressures come from remaining faithful to it?
- How much of the population actually lives inside that covenant?

Faith is chosen and maintained. It is a commitment.

### What Conviction Is

Conviction is the lived moral and governing posture of the Dynasty. It is shaped by repeated action over time. Bloodlines watches how you rule, not just what you claim to believe.

Conviction answers questions such as:

- How do you wage war?
- How do you treat the defeated?
- What do you do with prisoners?
- How do you govern the weak, the conquered, and the disloyal?
- Do you rule through justice, order, duty, mercy, fear, cruelty, sacrifice, or cold opportunism?
- What kind of memory does your Dynasty leave behind?

Conviction is shaped by decisions including:

- battlefield conduct
- treatment of prisoners and captives
- treatment of civilians and local populations
- pardons, reprisals, and punishments
- forced labor, ransom, execution, assimilation, or resettlement
- handling of rebellion and dissent
- conquest policy in newly taken settlements
- sacrifice versus preservation
- opportunistic betrayal versus honorable restraint
- bloodline decisions that reveal what matters most to the Dynasty

Conviction is not chosen in a menu. It is lived into existence.

### Faith and Conviction Are Separate Systems

| Question | Faith Answers It | Conviction Answers It |
|---|---|---|
| What higher order does the Dynasty follow? | Yes | No |
| How devout is the realm? | Yes | No |
| Which sacred buildings, rites, and doctrine shape play? | Yes | No |
| How does the Dynasty actually behave in war and rule? | No | Yes |
| Is this Dynasty merciful, just, severe, ruthless, or predatory? | No | Yes |
| Does the population feel inspired, fearful, burdened, or betrayed by rule? | Partly | Strongly |
| Can the realm be spiritually committed but morally compromised? | Yes | Yes |

The important lesson is this: a Dynasty may be faithful and still be cruel. It may be spiritually severe and still be just. It may speak the language of mercy while ruling through fear. Bloodlines is built to preserve that tension.

### How They Interact

Faith and Conviction can reinforce each other, or they can sit in active tension.

Examples:

- **A pious but ruthless Dynasty:** intense covenant commitment, powerful rites, strong religious identity, but a cruel pattern of rule that frightens subjects and darkens the realm's reputation.
- **A disciplined but less zealous Dynasty:** lower faith intensity, fewer overt sacred escalations, but orderly and stable governance with respectable conviction.
- **A merciful realm with strong faith:** a Dynasty whose covenant and conduct both support protection, sacrifice-with-purpose, and legitimacy.
- **A stable but severe Dynasty:** not wantonly cruel, but hard, punitive, and feared enough to hold dangerous frontiers.
- **A morally fractured Dynasty:** one whose faith claims one thing while its daily conduct teaches the population another.

When faith and conviction align, the realm often feels coherent. When they diverge, the realm becomes strategically interesting and politically volatile.

### Why Conviction Matters Mechanically

From the player's perspective, Conviction helps shape:

- morale identity
- internal loyalty and willingness to contribute
- how conquered populations respond to your rule
- how other Dynasties remember or fear you
- what kinds of strategic tools feel natural, credible, or unstable for your realm
- how bloodline members are perceived
- how harshly the wider world reacts to your extremes

A morally grounded realm may gain trust, resilience, and voluntary contribution. A cruel realm may gain fear-compliance, harder intimidation, and darker escalations, but at the cost of true loyalty, long-term growth, and broader acceptance.

### Why This Matters From The First Match

New players often assume Conviction begins in the late game. It does not.

It begins the moment you:

- decide whether a quick victory is worth collateral damage
- starve an enemy settlement rather than negotiating
- execute or ransom captives
- let a frontier suffer because it is inconvenient to protect
- punish a revolt in a way that restores order or deepens hatred
- use faith as a genuine structure of rule or as a cover for coercion

Bloodlines is not only about which Dynasty you choose.

It is also about what kind of Dynasty you become.

## War, Siege, and Tactical Consequence

Bloodlines is a war game, but it is not a game where war floats free of everything else.

Your armies are drawn from population, sustained by food and water, stabilized by governance, sharpened by doctrine, strengthened or strained by faith, and morally defined by Conviction. A campaign that looks impressive on the field may still be a strategic disaster if it empties the population pool, exhausts supply, kills irreplaceable bloodline members, or leaves newly taken territory ungovernable.

### How War Changes Across A Match

| Match period | What warfare usually looks like | What the player should learn |
|---|---|---|
| **Founding** | Local defense, tribal pressure, small raids, survival skirmishes | Protect the realm first. Early all-in house warfare is usually bad strategy. |
| **Consolidation** | Border clashes, node contests, first serious field armies, pressure on weak frontiers | Learn how economy, loyalty, and military tempo interact before pushing too far. |
| **Expansion and ideological struggle** | Larger campaigns, faith-backed armies, formal territorial wars, command risk | Learn that battle quality and command structure matter more than raw mass. |
| **Late war and convergence** | Serious sieges, coalition responses, prestige wars, sovereignty contests, bloodline-risk escalation | Learn that every offensive must answer to sustainment, legitimacy, and consequence. |

Bloodlines does not reward the player who tries to force the late game into the opening. In the early phases, nearby tribes, exposed routes, and local territorial struggles are usually the real military problem. Premature house-on-house obsession often creates a realm that looks brave and dies poor.

### What Actually Wins Battles

In a simpler RTS, the answer is often speed, numbers, and clean production. In Bloodlines, field success is determined by a harder combination:

| Battlefield factor | Why it matters | Common beginner error |
|---|---|---|
| **Command presence** | Armies with real commanders fight with better morale, direction, and political weight | Throwing troops into battle as if leadership is optional |
| **Formation and unit role** | Pike, cavalry, ranged pressure, shielded lines, and support pieces each matter differently by terrain and threat | Building one favorite unit type and expecting it to solve every battle |
| **Terrain** | Forest, open ground, walls, chokepoints, and approach lines change what is actually possible | Fighting on bad ground because the army "looks stronger" on paper |
| **Supply and sustainment** | Hungry, thirsty, or overextended armies lose effectiveness before they lose all their bodies | Treating campaigning range as infinite |
| **Morale and cohesion** | Bloodlines armies break as moral systems, not just as hitpoint bars | Assuming a battle is safe as long as raw damage is high |
| **Realm condition behind the army** | Stable populations and loyal provinces replace losses and support long wars | Winning one battle while hollowing out the Dynasty behind it |

The most important lesson is that battles in Bloodlines are not isolated transactions. Their outcome depends on the state of the realm that sent the army and the doctrine of the Dynasty that led it there.

### Sieges Must Be Earned

A serious fortress is not supposed to fall because infantry reached it first.

Bloodlines treats siege as a campaign commitment that must be prepared with:

- siege engines
- engineer specialists
- supply camps and line security
- scouting and intelligence
- breach planning
- elite assault troops
- faith-backed or covert enablers when available

If those layers are absent, the attacker is not conducting a proper siege. The attacker is wasting people.

This is one of Bloodlines' clearest strategic doctrines: wave-spam against developed fortifications is a losing branch. A strong keep is meant to inflict attritional cost, slow your tempo, create relief windows for the defender, and punish impatience.

### Fortification Is A Real Strategic Path

Defensive play in Bloodlines is not passive delay. It is a form of power.

Border settlements, forts, trade towns, strongholds, and primary keeps do not all defend the same way. The deeper a Dynasty invests in layered walls, towers, gates, garrisons, engineers, reserves, and supply, the more an attacker is forced to answer with actual campaign preparation.

Fortification also interacts directly with bloodline rule:

- a commander in the keep stiffens the defense
- a governor makes supply and order last longer
- an heir's presence raises the symbolic stakes
- a faith authority can turn sacred infrastructure into defensive leverage
- a merchant or spymaster can change how long the fortress resists or how much the attacker really knows

This is why Stonehelm, Whitehall, Oldcrest, and some Trueborn builds can become so difficult to uproot. They do not simply own territory. They organize it to survive attack.

### Tactical Conduct Still Shapes Conviction

The battlefield is one of the places where Conviction becomes visible fastest.

Your Dynasty's conduct in war is not limited to post-battle rulings. It is also shaped by what you normalize during the fighting itself:

- reckless assault versus disciplined timing
- preservation of troops versus expendable slaughter
- honorable siege versus treacherous sabotage
- protection of local infrastructure versus scorched opportunism
- measured punishment versus theatrical cruelty

A Dynasty that wins by wasting its own people, burning land it intends to rule, and escalating severity at every opportunity may gain short-term terror and breakthrough, but it is also teaching the world what kind of power it represents.

### First-Match Warfare Advice

For a new player, warfare should be approached in this order:

1. **Deter threats before chasing glory.**
2. **Use early armies to secure survival, resources, and nearby pressure points.**
3. **Fight tribal and frontier conflicts cleanly before forcing a house war you cannot sustain.**
4. **Bring mixed forces and leadership instead of one-type excitement.**
5. **Do not begin a serious siege without engines, supply, and a plan for the breach.**
6. **Stop after a win long enough to ask what the victory cost the realm.**

The player who learns this rhythm will usually beat the player who only knows how to march faster.

## Governance, Loyalty, and Holding What You Take

Bloodlines does not consider a territory truly yours because your banner touched it first.

A province must be controlled militarily, stabilized civically, and accepted by enough of its population to function as part of the realm. Until then, it is not secure power. It is responsibility under strain.

### Territory Is Held In Two Ways

Every meaningful territory in Bloodlines has two conditions beneath its surface:

1. **Military control**
2. **Population loyalty**

If either one is weak, the territory is unstable.

A province held only by force may still give you map presence, but it is more likely to provide:

- weaker taxes
- thinner recruitment
- sabotage risk
- resistance to faith policy
- revolt pressure after setbacks

This is why Bloodlines feels different from a simpler conquest game. Expansion is not validated by contact. It is validated by durable rule.

### What Builds Loyalty

Loyalty does not rise from one thing. It is the cumulative judgment a population makes about whether your rule is survivable, believable, and worth enduring.

| Source of loyalty | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Food and water security** | Hungry or thirsty populations do not become trustworthy subjects for long |
| **Housing and civic order** | A population that can live, work, and recover under your rule is easier to integrate |
| **Visible protection** | People support Dynasties that can actually defend roads, fields, and settlements |
| **Governance structures** | Settlement halls, administrators, and local civic support turn presence into functioning rule |
| **Governor presence** | A rooted governor gives the province a human face of authority rather than distant occupation |
| **Faith handling** | Tolerance, alignment, or pressure all shape whether the local population sees your covenant as order or threat |
| **Conviction and conduct** | Justice, restraint, cruelty, or fear all leave political memory in the province |
| **Economic usefulness** | Prosperity makes rule easier to accept; extraction without return makes it fragile |

Loyalty collapse often arrives quietly before it arrives dramatically. Families contribute less willingly. Infrastructure repairs slow. Local resistance becomes more confident. Then one bad campaign, one captured heir, one food shock, or one brutal punishment turns strain into fracture.

### Occupation Is Not Integration

New players often make the same mistake after a successful campaign: they assume the battle solved the province.

In Bloodlines, conquest usually creates a new list of problems:

- the local population may hate you
- the food line may be damaged
- the faith balance may now be unstable
- a governor may be needed immediately
- the province may produce only partial value until control deepens
- nearby rivals may see the new holding as easy to destabilize

Taking land starts the governance test. It does not end it.

### Why Overexpansion Breaks Realms

Every new territory increases:

- supply burden
- administrative burden
- loyalty burden
- bloodline attention burden
- military exposure

This is why the strongest Bloodlines players do not ask only, "Can I take the next province?"

They ask:

- Can I feed it?
- Can I water it?
- Can I defend the roads to it?
- Can I spare a governor or civic structure for it?
- Can I keep its population from becoming my next internal crisis?

If the answer is no, expansion may still succeed tactically and fail strategically.

### Governance Can Become Its Own Power

Bloodlines supports real governance strength, not just military strength.

A well-ruled Dynasty can become dangerous in ways that do not look theatrical:

- newly taken provinces stabilize faster
- existing territories stay loyal under pressure
- volunteer contribution rises
- trade routes remain safer
- revolt windows shorten
- smaller territories may eventually seek integration rather than resist it

This is one reason Whitehall, Stonehelm, Hartvale, and Oldcrest feel so different from purely tempo-driven houses. They can convert order into strategic depth that aggressive players often underestimate.

### First-Match Governance Priorities

In your first matches, treat governance as an active discipline from the opening, not as cleanup after conquest.

1. **Make the starting territory stable before chasing distant glory.**
2. **Use food, water, housing, and early civic buildout to create a realm people can live inside.**
3. **Do not add new territory faster than loyalty can settle.**
4. **Assign governors and bloodline attention where instability is actually forming.**
5. **After every victory, ask whether the new holding is becoming productive or merely quiet.**

If you learn to read loyalty early, Bloodlines becomes easier everywhere else. You stop confusing silence with consent, and you stop mistaking width for power.

## Understanding Dynasties

Dynasties in Bloodlines are not small statistical variants layered over one generic RTS shell. They are different civilizational identities with different default strengths, blind spots, pressures, and natural routes through a match.

When you choose a Dynasty, you are choosing:

- what kind of opening feels natural
- which resources and civic systems are easiest to stabilize
- what sort of military tempo your realm prefers
- how easily your territories hold together
- what kinds of faith relationships feel intuitive
- what kind of conviction drift your decision-making will naturally invite
- how your Dynasty wants to win in the long run

### Dynasty At A Glance

## Choosing Your First Dynasty

Your first Dynasty should teach you how Bloodlines works, not simply give you the sharpest toy.

The best first choices are usually these:

- **Stonehelm** if you want to learn stable openings, fortification timing, territory holding, and why restraint matters.
- **Whitehall** if you want to learn governance, loyalty, diplomacy, and the value of information.
- **Highborne** if you want a strong balanced introduction built around bloodline quality, prestige, and measured expansion.
- **Hartvale** if you want to learn the population, food, recovery, and realm-health side of Bloodlines.

Dynasties that teach aggressively but punish mistakes faster:

- **Ironmark** rewards directness, early war, and decisive military planning, but it will expose bad population and sustainment habits.
- **Westland** rewards tempo, expansion, and mobility, but it punishes overextension more sharply than almost any other Dynasty.

Dynasties for players who think in higher-order leverage:

- **Goldgrave** is ideal if you enjoy prosperity, timing, pressure through markets, and wars won before the armies fully clash.
- **Oldcrest** suits players who like long memory, deliberate faith play, and strategic patience.

Dynasty with the highest symbolic weight:

- **Trueborn** is powerful, prestigious, and deeply rewarding, but it asks the player to understand legitimacy, reputation, coalition response, and moral scrutiny. It is often better appreciated once you already understand the deeper systems.

### Do Not Choose A Dynasty By One Unit

A unique unit matters, but Bloodlines is not won by the best icon on a roster sheet. A Dynasty must be judged by:

- how it grows
- how it handles strain
- how it governs expansion
- how it supports population
- how it treats loyalty
- how it carries faith and conviction into the mid and late game
- how it survives when the match stops being easy

The right first Dynasty is the one whose full rhythm you can actually carry from opening to late pressure.

## Dynasty-By-Dynasty Guide

The entries below present each Dynasty as a complete player-facing strategic identity. The signature military and civic units are included to help new players immediately understand what each house is trying to do on the field and in the realm.

## Trueborn

Trueborn is the burden of legitimacy made playable. It does not begin with the raw military pressure of Ironmark or the economic ruthlessness of Goldgrave. Instead, it begins with something rarer: the weight of a name that the world already remembers. Trueborn is a Dynasty for players who want every decision to matter politically, spiritually, and symbolically.

- **Core identity and theme:** Ancient legitimacy, remembered lineage, neutral-city gravity, the burden of living up to a sacred name.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Measured expansion, careful diplomacy, high-value bloodline use, stable governance, and disciplined responses to provocation.
- **Early-game strengths:** Broad trust, easier access to information and cooperation, more stable opening loyalty, and less initial friction with faith choice.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Strong coalition leverage, better diplomatic standing, and a realm that can integrate territory cleanly when it rules credibly.
- **Late-game strengths:** Exceptional prestige, stronger world-order influence, and powerful leverage with the Trueborn City and anti-hegemon coalitions.
- **Economic tendencies:** Stable rather than explosive. Trueborn thrives on orderly development and trusted exchange, not reckless extraction.
- **Military tendencies:** No automatic battlefield crutch. Trueborn armies win through leadership, preparation, and legitimacy-backed cohesion.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Strong. Trueborn is naturally good at making rule feel lawful, accepted, and historically grounded.
- **Faith tendencies:** More open than most Dynasties to all four covenants. Old Light resonance is strong, but Trueborn is not spiritually locked.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Highly visible. Merciful or principled rule gains unusual force here; cruelty carries disproportionate reputational cost.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** High expectations, no free military spike, and greater punishment when the Dynasty acts beneath its symbolic station.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Low to medium. Powerful, but most rewarding after you already understand Bloodlines' political depth.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who want prestige, diplomacy, legitimacy, and the feeling of ruling under history's gaze.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Higher trust with the Trueborn City, stronger baseline population stability, broader covenant openness, and easier coalition participation.
- **Notable system implications:** Trueborn cruelty is remembered harder; Trueborn justice is believed more deeply. Bloodline losses and faith hypocrisy are especially costly.
- **Signature military units:** **Oathguard**, an elite protective guard defined by loyalty and body-defense; **Coalition Marshal**, a late-stage command-centered field force built for allied war leadership.
- **Signature civic units:** **Compact Envoy**, a diplomatic specialist tied to city politics and coalition management; **City Arbiter**, a legitimacy-focused magistrate who stabilizes rule and public order.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Trueborn if you want the highest symbolic stakes in the game and a realm whose reputation can shape the whole match.

## Highborne

Highborne is the Dynasty of refined blood, old wealth, and elevated governance. It believes rule should belong to the capable and the cultivated. That gives Highborne polished leadership, better internal quality, and strong prestige tools. It also gives it an enduring problem: the people do not automatically love a realm that openly believes in hierarchy.

- **Core identity and theme:** Aristocratic excellence, selective bloodline cultivation, prestige, education, and refined authority.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Controlled expansion with strong bloodline planning, elite leadership, and a medium-sized but well-run realm.
- **Early-game strengths:** Stronger starting wealth, better trade posture, and higher-value early bloodline development.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Excellent governors, strong envoys, skilled heirs, and a polished realm that feels ahead in capability even when not ahead in size.
- **Late-game strengths:** Elite dynastic leadership, high administrative quality, and strong prestige-driven control if popular resentment has been managed.
- **Economic tendencies:** Trade- and treasury-friendly. Highborne converts prestige into smoother commerce and cleaner high-value growth.
- **Military tendencies:** Quality over volume. Highborne prefers capable officers, disciplined household troops, and armies led well rather than raised cheaply.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Strong. Highborne governs elegantly, but never for free; public support must be cultivated.
- **Faith tendencies:** Often comfortable with The Order or The Old Light, where hierarchy, law, and cultivated duty reinforce the house identity.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Disciplined, paternal, or severe. Highborne can become noble stewards or aloof rulers who mistake superiority for legitimacy.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Popular resentment, expensive mass recruitment, and internal strain when marriages or diplomacy challenge bloodline purity.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Medium to high. A solid first Dynasty for players who want balance with a real political edge.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who like elite leadership, clean infrastructure, and winning through quality rather than raw aggression.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Favorable opening trade, stronger bloodline development, better academic and tutor effects, and higher prestige leverage.
- **Notable system implications:** Highborne is strongest when it converts elite leadership into visible public order. If it looks proud but brittle, loyalty becomes a constant tax.
- **Signature military units:** **Gilded Lancer**, a noble shock cavalry built around quality and status; **High Court Guard**, a disciplined retainer infantry that protects bloodline authority.
- **Signature civic units:** **Court Chamberlain**, a ceremonial-administrative officer who sharpens governance efficiency; **Lineage Tutor**, a bloodline educator who accelerates leader development.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Highborne if you want a polished, prestige-driven realm that wins through quality, leadership, and controlled ambition.

## Ironmark

Ironmark is the war Dynasty. It knows iron, barracks, morale, and the cost of power paid in blood. It is the house that can take command of a battlefield quickly, but it is also the house most likely to discover that winning wars is easier than building a healthy kingdom after them.

- **Core identity and theme:** Forged militarism, early iron, hard campaigning, institutional war memory, blood-cost escalation.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Seize iron early, field decisive infantry, pressure rivals before they stabilize, then convert victory into sustainable control before the population bill comes due.
- **Early-game strengths:** Early military infrastructure, strong morale, immediate martial credibility, and the live Axeman power spike.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Veteran armies, strong siege follow-through, battlefield confidence, and a realm that knows how to keep fighting.
- **Late-game strengths:** Fearsome professional war capacity and strong transition into severe faith or hard state power if the realm remains intact.
- **Economic tendencies:** Mining, iron, and military infrastructure first. Agriculture and civic softness tend to lag if not managed deliberately.
- **Military tendencies:** Heavy infantry, hard morale, reliable offensive tempo, and real willingness to trade blood for battlefield advantage.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Serviceable but under pressure. Ironmark can hold territory, but it prefers to solve problems through force before administration.
- **Faith tendencies:** Blood Dominion and The Order are natural fits, though duty-bound Old Light Ironmark can be formidable.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Duty, severity, and battlefield honor sit close to outright ruthlessness. Ironmark players define the difference by conduct.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Low agricultural grace, population strain, war fatigue, and a dangerous tendency to keep escalating after the realm needed consolidation.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Medium. Excellent for learning war, unforgiving if you ignore cost.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who want pressure, directness, and battlefield consequence to define the match.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Early iron access, higher troop morale, stronger military infrastructure, and the Axeman's heavier warfighting potential.
- **Notable system implications:** Ironmark's unique escalation has a living price. Training Axemen increases blood-production burden and makes population management more urgent, not less.
- **Signature military units:** **Axeman**, the first live Ironmark unique unit with heavier blood levy; **Iron Banner Veteran**, a hardened command infantry formed by repeated campaigning.
- **Signature civic units:** **Camp Reeve**, a frontier organizer who keeps war logistics from collapsing the realm; **Foundry Quartermaster**, a military-industrial steward who keeps iron flowing.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Ironmark if you want a Dynasty that can seize the initiative early and teach you that every war-winning habit must eventually answer to population, supply, and rule.

## Goldgrave

Goldgrave understands that wealth is power long before a sword is drawn. It is the Dynasty of markets, leverage, negotiation, ransom, debt, and the dangerous truth that economic dependence can conquer before armies arrive. Goldgrave rewards players who think in systems of pressure rather than simple territorial appetite.

- **Core identity and theme:** Prosperity, transaction, fiscal influence, leverage politics, and the long hand of wealth.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Secure trade routes, build stable markets, turn prosperity into influence, force rivals into bad choices, and fight only the wars that serve your balance sheet.
- **Early-game strengths:** Stronger gold posture, faster commercial lift, favorable negotiation terms, and smoother economic openings.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Trade-network power, leverage over weaker Dynasties, better ransom and diplomatic exchange, and flexible resource positioning.
- **Late-game strengths:** Currency dominance, world-economic pressure, and the ability to turn prosperity into geopolitical command.
- **Economic tendencies:** Best in the game for market-centered play, route value, and indirect pressure through wealth concentration.
- **Military tendencies:** Competent but expensive. Goldgrave prefers professional forces, paid solutions, and decisive spending over endless attrition.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Strong in revenue, moderate in loyalty unless prosperity is visible and shared.
- **Faith tendencies:** Often comfortable with The Order's institutional discipline, but can also pursue Blood Dominion or Old Light in transactionally coherent ways.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Naturally pragmatic. Goldgrave often sits near neutral conviction unless the player deliberately drives it toward generosity or predation.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Suspicion from rivals, expensive military upkeep, and danger when money outruns loyalty or military credibility.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Medium. Excellent for thoughtful players, weaker for those who want every answer to be a frontal assault.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who like leverage, bargaining, strategic timing, and winning through structure instead of theatrical aggression.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Stronger market income, better ransom terms, higher economic leverage, and stronger routes toward currency-based victory.
- **Notable system implications:** Goldgrave can look safe while being strategically exposed. If trade breaks or military respect vanishes, the whole posture changes quickly.
- **Signature military units:** **Coinguard**, a disciplined professional force loyal to pay and prestige; **Contract Lancer**, a fast striking formation fielded through wealth-backed war planning.
- **Signature civic units:** **Mintmaster**, a fiscal officer who strengthens currency reach and treasury control; **Caravan Factor**, a trade-network specialist who improves commercial corridors and negotiation.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Goldgrave if you want to rule through prosperity, leverage, timing, and the slow conversion of wealth into power.

## Stonehelm

Stonehelm is the Dynasty of fortresses, endurance, and refusal. It does not advance lightly, but once it commits to holding land it becomes one of the hardest realms in Bloodlines to break. Stonehelm teaches one of the game's deepest truths: a realm that cannot be casually breached shapes the choices of everyone around it.

- **Core identity and theme:** Fortification, endurance, disciplined territorial command, defensive seriousness, and sustainable rule.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Build stable openings, invest in walls and infrastructure, secure food and water, raise enough force to deter predation, and expand only when the next holding can truly be governed.
- **Early-game strengths:** Safe openings, cheap fortification buildout, stronger defensive tempo, and clear strategic focus.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Strong keeps, high territorial stability, disciplined governor value, and punishing responses to enemy overextension.
- **Late-game strengths:** Excellent sovereignty play, powerful fortress networks, high siege resilience, and some of the best held-territory endgames in Bloodlines.
- **Economic tendencies:** Strong in stone, storage, infrastructural seriousness, and the conversion of defensive investment into long-term stability.
- **Military tendencies:** Garrison quality, engineering, methodical field armies, and reliable defensive attrition. Mobility is secondary.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Excellent. Stonehelm naturally supports rooted governors, strong frontier seats, and loyalty that survives pressure.
- **Faith tendencies:** The Old Light and The Order are natural pairings, though Stonehelm can also express a stern Wild defensiveness in the right terrain.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Duty-bound, ordered, and severe without necessarily being cruel. Stonehelm often wins through firmness rather than spectacle.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Slow offensive tempo, weak cavalry bias, risk of letting the map drift away while building a perfect fortress that matters nowhere.
- **Beginner friendliness:** High. One of the best first Dynasties in the game.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who like stability, discipline, defensive power, and the ability to make enemies pay for impatience.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Canonical 20% fortification cost discount, faster fortification build speed, stronger territorial hold, and high defensive value from patient play.
- **Notable system implications:** Stonehelm gets paid for coherent expansion. It gets punished for pretending to be Westland. Build what you can hold.
- **Signature military units:** **Bastion Guard**, an elite defensive infantry for holds and relief defense; **Gatehouse Warder**, a structure-linked defender specialized in fortified approaches.
- **Signature civic units:** **Mason Reeve**, a fortification superintendent who accelerates durable buildout; **Holdfast Surveyor**, a territorial planner who prepares frontier seats for long rule.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Stonehelm if you want to learn Bloodlines properly: stable openings, meaningful defense, disciplined expansion, and the value of holding ground honestly.

## Westland

Westland lives at the edge. It thrives on motion, pressure, first claims, and the willingness to strike before safer Dynasties think the war has truly begun. It can feel brilliant in the hands of a player who reads terrain, tempo, and risk well. It can also tear itself open if the frontier appetite outruns the state's ability to govern.

- **Core identity and theme:** Frontier aggression, speed, mobility, advance pressure, and dangerous initiative.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Scout aggressively, claim fast, raid early support lines, fight in enemy space, and convert movement into advantage before defensive Dynasties settle.
- **Early-game strengths:** Strong scouting, fast infantry, faster first claims, and immediate initiative on open maps.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Powerful raiding, morale in hostile territory, mobile harassment, and the ability to define the pace of the front.
- **Late-game strengths:** Can remain dangerous through map pressure and attritional maneuver, but only if the realm does not collapse under its own breadth.
- **Economic tendencies:** Frontier extraction, quick setups, and opportunistic growth. Administrative polish is weaker than momentum.
- **Military tendencies:** Fast infantry, light cavalry, raiders, escorts, and offensive campaigning where terrain and movement matter more than static power.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Limited. Westland produces pioneers and fighters more easily than patient administrators.
- **Faith tendencies:** The Wild and Blood Dominion are natural partners for Westland's appetite for exposed ground, though severe Old Light crusading is also possible.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Bold, opportunistic, and easily pushed toward extremes. Westland players define whether frontier aggression becomes honor or predation.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Overextension, weak administrative recovery, frontier loyalty problems, and poor late-game position if expansion was never consolidated.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Low to medium. Exciting, but punishes mistakes fast.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who love tempo, scouting, aggression, and forcing the enemy to react first.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Faster claims, stronger hostile-territory morale, better movement culture, and high value from early initiative.
- **Notable system implications:** Westland teaches that territory breadth without governance is not strength. Its biggest wins often create its biggest future vulnerabilities.
- **Signature military units:** **Red March Rider**, a fast raiding cavalry built for deep pressure; **Frontier Skirmisher**, a mobile light infantry specialized for exposed fights and harassment.
- **Signature civic units:** **Trail Reeve**, a frontier organizer who keeps new holdings from immediately slipping away; **Pioneer Surveyor**, a fast territorial-development specialist for early expansion.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Westland if you want to play on the map's edge, define tempo, and learn exactly how costly overreach can become.

## Hartvale

Hartvale is the Dynasty of cultivation, provision, and recovery. It remembers hunger more sharply than glory, and that memory shapes everything. Hartvale is not weak. It is a realm whose strength begins in food, population, and visible care for the land and people. The player who understands how to convert that civil strength into timely security can become one of the hardest rulers in Bloodlines to starve, exhaust, or erase.

- **Core identity and theme:** Harvest, population, restoration, agrarian durability, humane stewardship, and local loyalty.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Build a strong agrarian core, secure food and water early, keep housing ahead of demand, protect growth with disciplined defense, then turn surplus into decisive mid-game strength.
- **Early-game strengths:** Food security, population growth, stable openings, and some of the best recovery foundations in the game.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Strong civilian depth, better rebound after losses, and powerful defensive support from settlement-centered play.
- **Late-game strengths:** Large sustainable realm, strong recovery from defeats, resilient long wars, and excellent governance-through-provision.
- **Economic tendencies:** Best sustenance profile in Bloodlines. Farms, ranches, and growth support are central to the Hartvale economy.
- **Military tendencies:** Defensive and support-oriented early, increasingly strong once population surplus is converted into disciplined field power.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Strong in visible care, recovery, and settlement health. Hartvale wins trust through practical stewardship.
- **Faith tendencies:** The Old Light and The Wild fit Hartvale naturally, especially when the Dynasty plays as a guardian of people and land.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Moral stewardship is the natural path, but famine, invasion, or sacrifice can push Hartvale into painful severity that feels especially consequential.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Slow coercive ramp, softer early war posture, and danger if a richer or harder foe strikes before Hartvale's realm depth becomes military depth.
- **Beginner friendliness:** High. A strong learning Dynasty for players who want to understand population and realm health.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who want to build a living realm, protect people, and still have the means to endure hard wars.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Strongest food economy, high population growth and recovery, strong agricultural loyalty, and the Verdant Warden's settlement support.
- **Notable system implications:** Hartvale is one of the clearest reminders that population is power. Lose the people and you lose the house.
- **Signature military units:** **Verdant Warden**, the locked Hartvale guardian unit that strengthens settlement defense and local loyalty; **Hearth Guard**, a disciplined local-defense force that turns prosperity into protection.
- **Signature civic units:** **Harvest Steward**, a sustenance specialist who improves food depth and realm recovery; **Hearth Physician**, a civic healer who supports resilience after siege, famine, or battle.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Hartvale if you want to learn how strong a realm becomes when food, people, and recovery are treated as the center of strategy rather than the background.

## Whitehall

Whitehall is the Dynasty of administration, diplomacy, and useful knowledge. It survives by being believed, by being present, and by understanding that control often begins before the order is given. Whitehall rarely looks like the loudest realm on the map, but loudness is not the same thing as command.

- **Core identity and theme:** Institutional authority, diplomacy, intelligence, administrative competence, and political presence.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Build stable infrastructure, integrate territory carefully, read rivals early, use alliances and marriages intelligently, and fight from an informed position.
- **Early-game strengths:** Immediate diplomatic reach, strong information posture, easier contact, and a clean introduction to governance-centered play.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Better alliance maintenance, stronger negotiation, cleaner territory integration, and a more coherent political map around the Dynasty.
- **Late-game strengths:** Excellent state stability, good coalition management, strong intelligence networks, and high value from long-term relationships.
- **Economic tendencies:** Reliable, balanced, and governance-friendly rather than explosive. Whitehall makes ordinary systems work better.
- **Military tendencies:** Serviceable and disciplined, but not terrifying. Whitehall relies on timing, posture, and support more than brute force.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Excellent. Few Dynasties understand rule, paperwork, diplomacy, and internal order better.
- **Faith tendencies:** The Order is the most obvious fit, but Whitehall also pairs well with The Old Light when legitimacy and orderly duty matter most.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Naturally resistant to extremes. Whitehall can become just, restrained, manipulative, or quietly severe, but it does not drift into fanaticism casually.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Limited military peaks, danger when diplomacy substitutes for hard power too long, and loss of leverage if key bloodline members are killed.
- **Beginner friendliness:** High. One of the best teaching Dynasties in Bloodlines.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who like information, control, diplomacy, and realms that stay functional under pressure.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Universal diplomatic access tendency, strong alliance maintenance, better espionage maintenance, easier marriage politics, and fast administrative integration.
- **Notable system implications:** Whitehall wins by making rule easier and rivals more readable. If forced into a pure strength contest with no preparation, it loses its greatest advantage.
- **Signature military units:** **Chamber Guard**, a disciplined internal-security infantry for bloodline and state defense; **Ledger Pike**, a precise line unit built for controlled battlefield order rather than reckless assault.
- **Signature civic units:** **Chancellor-Envoy**, a high-value diplomatic officer who turns treaties into power; **Quiet Registrar**, an administrative intelligence specialist who binds information to governance.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Whitehall if you want to learn Bloodlines through diplomacy, loyalty, information, and the power of a realm that remains coherent.

## Oldcrest

Oldcrest is the Dynasty of memory. It carries records, traditions, and ancient continuity that other houses only imitate. Oldcrest often begins slower than more aggressive rivals, but it knows how civilizations fail, and that knowledge is its own weapon. It is a house for players who want to win by seeing further ahead than the people around them.

- **Core identity and theme:** Ancient record, long memory, tradition, faith depth, measured certainty, and durable integration.
- **Recommended playstyle:** Expand patiently, plan faith commitments deliberately, prepare for later turns of the match, and rely on foresight and stability rather than speed.
- **Early-game strengths:** Broad covenant readiness, seasoned leaders, calmer openings, and better strategic reading of future pressure.
- **Mid-game strengths:** Strong faith development, durable integrated provinces, and better ability to predict or absorb mid-game instability.
- **Late-game strengths:** Excellent long-game cohesion, durable loyalty, broad doctrine competence, and strategic depth that rewards patience.
- **Economic tendencies:** Conservative and stable. Oldcrest prefers reliable buildout to spikes and gambles.
- **Military tendencies:** Disciplined, traditional, and dependable, but slower to innovate or pivot than more volatile Dynasties.
- **Civic and governance tendencies:** Strong once established. Oldcrest builds loyalty that lasts and remembers how to maintain it.
- **Faith tendencies:** Highly flexible. Oldcrest's preserved record gives it a natural head start toward any covenant, especially for players who want deep faith play.
- **Conviction tendencies:** Slow-moving. Oldcrest does not whip between mercy and cruelty easily; once it settles into a posture, it feels historically grounded.
- **Risk profile and weaknesses:** Slow growth, aging bloodline pressure, innovation lag, and vulnerability to faster Dynasties if Oldcrest mistakes patience for immunity.
- **Beginner friendliness:** Medium. Rewarding for careful players, less natural for those who want immediate action.
- **What kind of player will enjoy it:** Players who love foresight, historical weight, layered faith play, and long strategic arcs.
- **Distinctive bonuses:** Easier faith build-up, stronger inherited experience, slower loyalty decay once provinces are integrated, and a unique memory-driven intelligence posture.
- **Notable system implications:** Oldcrest often becomes stronger with time, but only if it survives the part of the match when quicker Dynasties try to define the map first.
- **Signature military units:** **Ancestral Spear**, a traditional elite line unit built for disciplined durability; **Memory Guard**, a veteran protective force trained from old doctrine rather than fashionable innovation.
- **Signature civic units:** **Archive Warden**, a keeper of state memory who improves continuity and strategic foresight; **Chronicle Judge**, a legal-historical officer who strengthens settled loyalty and orderly rule.
- **Why choose this Dynasty?** Choose Oldcrest if you want to outlast, outread, and outremember rivals rather than outrush them.

## Comparing Dynasty Playstyles

The easiest way to compare Dynasties is not by asking which one is strongest. The better question is what kind of strength they produce and what kind of weakness they naturally create.

### Broad Comparison Matrix

| Dynasty | Aggression | Defense | Economy | Governance | Conviction Pressure | Beginner Ease |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Trueborn | Medium | Medium | Medium | High | High because the world judges it sharply | Low-Medium |
| Highborne | Medium | Medium | Medium-High | High | Medium; social resentment punishes arrogance | Medium-High |
| Ironmark | High | Medium | Medium | Medium-Low | High; war conduct defines the realm fast | Medium |

### Which Dynasties Favor Which Styles?

- **Most aggressive:** Ironmark and Westland.
- **Most defensive:** Stonehelm, Hartvale, and Oldcrest.
- **Most economically leverage-driven:** Goldgrave, with Highborne as a more prestige-driven second.
- **Most politically stable:** Whitehall and Stonehelm, with Trueborn becoming exceptionally stable when ruled well.
- **Most conviction-sensitive in feel:** Trueborn, Ironmark, Westland, and Hartvale.
- **Best for disciplined territorial control:** Stonehelm, Whitehall, and Oldcrest.
- **Best for military tempo:** Ironmark and Westland.
- **Best for flexibility:** Highborne, Whitehall, and Oldcrest.
- **Best for wealth and leverage:** Goldgrave.
- **Best for careful long-term planning:** Oldcrest, Whitehall, and Trueborn.
- **Best for mobility and frontier maneuver:** Westland first, with Ironmark as the more direct marching war power.
- **Best for order and political control:** Whitehall, Stonehelm, and Highborne.
- **Most naturally severe in expression:** Ironmark and Westland, with Stonehelm as the firmer but more stable version of severity.

### Simple New-Player Summary

- Choose **Stonehelm** if you want order and stability.
- Choose **Whitehall** if you want governance and information.
- Choose **Highborne** if you want a balanced prestige realm.
- Choose **Hartvale** if you want strong sustenance and resilient growth.
- Choose **Ironmark** if you want to learn aggression through real consequences.
- Choose **Westland** if you want high-risk frontier tempo.
- Choose **Goldgrave** if you want to think in leverage, prosperity, and pressure.
- Choose **Oldcrest** if you want long planning and faith depth.
- Choose **Trueborn** if you want prestige, symbolism, and the weightiest form of rule.

## First Match Guidance for New Players

The most important thing a new player can do in Bloodlines is build a stable opening.

Your first priority is not domination.

Your first priority is coherence.

### The Correct Opening Order

1. **Establish the foundation of population.**
2. **Secure food and water.**
3. **Build housing and early civic support.**
4. **Ensure labor is being used productively.**
5. **Scout the local environment and immediate risk.**
6. **Build enough military presence to avoid becoming an easy target.**
7. **Expand only when the expansion can actually be supported.**

### What That Means In Practice

#### Establish population first

People are your long-term power. Do not treat early workers and early recruitment as separate concerns. Your first workers, your first housing, and your early realm support are the beginning of everything else you will do later.

#### Secure food and water before ambition outruns survival

A realm that grows hungry or dry becomes weaker in ways that may not look dramatic immediately, but those weaknesses compound. Bloodlines is designed so that food and water problems become military and political problems if left unattended.

#### Build housing before the realm hits a wall

Do not wait until you feel capped. Build for growth before the pressure arrives. Housing is what makes your population base sustainable. Dynasties that delay it often end up with armies larger than their future.

#### Use labor deliberately

An impressive building lineup means nothing if the realm does not have the people to work it. Make sure your food line, water line, material line, and construction line are all being meaningfully supported by your population.

#### Scout early, but do not confuse scouting with premature war

You need to know where your nearby resources, sacred sites, risks, chokepoints, and rivals are. But your first scouts are for knowledge, not for proving courage.

#### Field enough military to deter punishment

Bloodlines does not reward naked greed. Build enough force that raiders, tribes, or opportunistic rivals do not read you as easy prey. This is not the same as trying to end the match in the first rush.

#### Expand only when the next holding strengthens the Dynasty

New land brings people, expectations, loyalty burden, supply strain, and political surface area. Take it when you can actually govern it, feed it, protect it, and keep it loyal.

### Faith Timing In The First Match

Do not rush faith commitment in the opening minutes simply because the system is important. Faith matters enormously, but Bloodlines expects faith to emerge from a realm that has actually encountered the wider world and can sustain the commitment.

Choose a covenant when:

- you understand your local geography and risks
- you know what kind of Dynasty you are beginning to become
- you can support the structures and obligations that follow

Faith chosen too quickly can create mismatch. A covenant is not a skin. It is a demand.

### Conviction Begins Earlier Than You Think

Conviction does not wait for a formal late-game crisis. It begins in your first serious acts of rule.

If you:

- punish too quickly
- exploit prisoners casually
- let civilians suffer when you could have stabilized them
- rule a frontier through fear because it is convenient
- fight wars that your population cannot carry

then your Dynasty is already becoming something.

### Post-Battle Decisions Can Matter More Than The Battle

A victory can create:

- a captured heir
- a hostile population
- a devastated food line
- a loyalty shock in newly taken land
- a conviction shift that changes how the realm is perceived
- a chain of bloodline or world-pressure consequences

Do not think only in terms of winning engagements. Think in terms of what kind of realm that victory leaves behind.

## After Battle: The Realm Remembers

One of the deepest habits Bloodlines tries to teach is that battles do not end when the fighting stops. The field clears. The rule begins.

After a serious victory, the player is often not only deciding how to exploit an opening, but how the Dynasty will be judged by its own people, by the conquered, and by the wider world. This is where Faith and Conviction often stop being abstract ideas and become lived political reality.

### The Questions A Ruler Must Ask After Victory

- What will be done with prisoners?
- What happens to captured bloodline members?
- Will the local population be protected, punished, displaced, or made an example?
- Is the settlement to be integrated, stripped, terrorized, or rebuilt?
- Will rebels be pardoned, resettled, ransomed, executed, or pressed into labor?
- Does this decision make the realm more stable, more feared, more hated, or more hollow?

### Common Post-Battle Paths

| Post-battle posture | Short-term gain | Long-term risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Ransom and negotiated release** | Gold, leverage, reduced immediate hatred | A spared rival may recover and return stronger |
| **Execution** | Finality, terror, removal of future threat | Conviction darkens, enemies harden, populations remember |
| **Forced labor or enslavement** | Immediate economic extraction and fear-based compliance | Loyalty rot, moral decay, resistance, wider backlash |
| **Assimilation and resettlement** | Potential long-term stabilization and productive integration | Slow, expensive, and vulnerable if not protected |
| **Mercy and restoration** | Legitimacy, loyalty recovery, stronger moral standing | Can look weak if not backed by force and order |
| **Punitive severity** | Rapid fear, sharp deterrence, fast submission | Brittle obedience, sabotage, rebellion when power slips |

### Conviction Is Often Written Here

Battlefield heroics matter, but many Dynasties reveal their true character after they have already won. A ruler who is restrained only while danger is present is not restrained. A ruler who can afford mercy but chooses spectacle is telling the realm what kind of future it is entering.

This is why Bloodlines places such weight on:

- prisoner handling
- civilian protection
- punishments and pardons
- rebellion policy
- frontier settlement rulings
- the treatment of captured bloodline figures

The game is interested in what kind of Dynasty emerges from repeated rule, not from one isolated gesture.

### Faith And Post-Battle Rule

Faith can intensify these moments rather than replace them. A covenant may justify mercy, order, sacrifice, purification, restoration, hierarchy, or severity, but Faith does not erase the decision. It frames it. The actual act still shapes Conviction.

A Dynasty that follows The Old Light but rules conquered land through cruelty will be read differently from one that actually reconstructs and protects. A Blood Dominion realm that practices hard sacrifice but governs justly will feel different from one that falls into indiscriminate predation. The Order can become stable administration or cold submission. The Wild can become rooted guardianship or frightening culling.

The player should therefore think of every post-battle ruling as both a strategic choice and a declaration of identity.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- **Building army size before building a realm.** A large army without housing, food, water, and labor depth is borrowed strength.
- **Choosing a Dynasty by one flashy unit.** Dynasties are rhythms of growth, strain, faith, and governance, not isolated roster tricks.
- **Ignoring water because food looks healthy.** Bloodlines treats water as a separate strategic pillar; one does not replace the other.
- **Expanding faster than loyalty can settle.** Occupation is not control. Fragile frontier land becomes revolt, sabotage, or easy recapture.
- **Treating faith as a passive buff.** A covenant requires intensity, structures, participation, and serious commitment.
- **Treating Conviction as roleplay flavor.** Your conduct changes morale identity, loyalty behavior, reputation, and the long-term character of the Dynasty.
- **Using bloodline members carelessly.** A dead commander, captive heir, or displaced governor can do more damage than the loss of ordinary troops.
- **Winning battles destructively.** Burning farmland, exhausting armies, mistreating captives, or terrorizing new subjects can turn a tactical win into strategic decay.
- **Confusing fear with stability.** Fear can suppress dissent for a time; it does not create deep loyalty.
- **Refusing to recover after a setback.** Bloodlines supports comeback play, but only if you re-secure food, water, morale, loyalty, and bloodline order instead of panic-escalating.

## Final Strategic Advice For New Players

The strongest new player habit in Bloodlines is learning to ask one question again and again:

**Is my Dynasty becoming stronger, or merely louder?**

A loud Dynasty may:

- win the first fights
- expand quickly
- frighten weak neighbors
- look dominant on the map

But a strong Dynasty:

- has people worth defending
- has food and water depth
- has housing that supports growth
- has bloodline continuity
- has loyalty that survives strain
- has a faith it can honestly sustain
- has a conviction that matches how it means to rule
- has a territory network it can actually hold

This is why Bloodlines feels different from a simpler RTS. The game remembers how you became what you are. It remembers whether your Dynasty held its covenant seriously, whether it ruled through justice or fear, whether it protected its people or consumed them, whether it conquered responsibly or collapsed under the size of its own victories.

If you are new, do not try to master every advanced layer at once. Learn the shape of a stable opening. Learn how population, food, water, housing, and loyalty fit together. Learn what your chosen Dynasty naturally wants to do. Learn how faith commitment changes the realm. Learn how Conviction is formed in action. Learn why protecting bloodline members matters. Learn why some victories cost too much.

Then keep playing until you can feel, before the collapse arrives, when a realm has gone wrong.

That is where Bloodlines truly begins.

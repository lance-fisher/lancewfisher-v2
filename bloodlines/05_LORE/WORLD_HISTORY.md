# Bloodlines — World History

The narrative history of the Bloodlines world, from its mythic origins to the present era of warring dynasties.

---

### World History — Full Canonical Narrative (Added 2026-03-18, Fifth Session)

---

## THE AGE BEFORE — The World That Was Lost

No one alive remembers the Age Before. What survives of it comes in three forms: ruins half-buried in frost-scarred earth, fragments of doctrine preserved by the ancient faiths who existed through it, and the bloodlines themselves — genetic memory encoded in silver hair and dark gold and vivid red, carried forward by families who remember, even if they no longer remember why.

The Age Before was not a golden era. It was a world of competing city-states and early kingdoms — iron-age civilizations built along rivers and coasts and protected mountain valleys. There were wars. There were famines. Empires rose and ground themselves into smaller pieces over generations. But the world was connected. Trade roads crossed the known world. Ships moved between coasts. Ideas traveled.

Iron was the age's defining material. Foundries produced tools, weapons, and structural components that the Age of Reclamation has still not fully matched. The great smelting complexes of the pre-Frost era — some of which become Iron Ridge territories in the present era — were industrial in scale, powered by forests of wood and generations of expertise. When the Frost came and those foundries fell silent, the knowledge that kept them running was among the first things lost.

**The Four Ancient Faiths** predate every dynasty, every house, every kingdom of the Age Before. They did not emerge from political need. They emerged from something older — from the first human encounters with forces they could not explain, forces that answered when addressed in the right way, under the right conditions. The four covenants disagreed about almost everything, but they agreed on one thing: the world is not merely physical. Something listens. The question is what, and what it asks in return.

**The Trueborn Bloodline** was the most prominent lineage of the Age Before — not the most powerful militarily, and not the wealthiest, but the most respected. They were old in a way that even the Age Before recognized as old. Their silver hair appeared in records going back to the earliest documented dynasties. They occupied the city that would later become the neutral Trueborn City — a natural trade hub at the geographic center of the known world, positioned where three river systems converged and where travelers from every direction had reason to stop.

The nine bloodlines that would eventually become the founding houses of the Age of Reclamation were not dominant powers in the Age Before. They were secondary houses — significant, established, durable — but not hegemons. The Age Before's most powerful civilizations are gone. Their names are not known. Their ruins are what the nine founding houses will now fight over.

## THE GREAT FROST — The Collapse

It did not begin with cold.

It began with the sky.

In the third year of the last king of the age, the sky turned wrong. Not at night — in daylight. Scholars later described it as a dimming, a thinning of the light, as if something vast had moved between the world and the sun. The crops that year grew, but not vigorously. Animals born that spring were smaller than expected.

The second year brought the first real cold: winters that arrived three weeks early and stayed two weeks past their end. Stores ran short. Livestock died. In the mountain kingdoms, the passes closed earlier than any living person had seen. No one panicked yet. A cold year was not unknown. People had weathered cold years before.

By the fifth year, it was clear this was not a cold year.

The consensus of historians in the Age of Reclamation — working from the faith documents that survived, from physical evidence in the terrain, from oral traditions preserved by the Wild covenant in particular — is that the Great Frost descended over approximately twenty years, reached its most extreme severity around year thirty, and maintained that severity for roughly a century and a half before slowly, unevenly, releasing.

The mechanisms of the Frost are unknown. All four faiths have explanations. None agree. The Old Light holds that the world's accumulated moral failures finally broke the compact between creation and its guardians. The Blood Dominion holds that the world demanded sacrifice and received insufficient offerings. The Order holds that the failure of legitimate governance across the known world created a structural void that the natural world filled with cold. The Wild holds that no explanation is needed — the world breathes in and out over long cycles, and the Frost was an exhale.

**What the Frost Did:**

In year thirty, during the deepest period, rivers froze solid enough to walk across. Coastal regions that had never seen ice saw ice. The growing season in temperate regions contracted to six weeks in good years, zero in bad years. Food production collapsed globally. The human population of the known world, which no one counted precisely but which the Order's fragmentary records suggest was in the tens of millions, fell catastrophically. Estimates from the Reclamation scholars suggest a loss of more than half the pre-Frost population, concentrated in the first fifty years.

Cities were abandoned. Not stormed, not burned — simply left. When a city cannot feed its population, the population disperses. The roads that connected the trade networks were buried under ice and snow, then eroded when the thaw came, then grown over. The great foundries went cold. The ships rotted in ports or were burned for heat. The institutional structures of the Age Before — governments, courts, trade guilds, military organizations — dissolved within a single generation because institutions require surplus, and the Frost eliminated surplus.

The ruins that modern houses fight over are the bones of that abandonment.

**The Faiths During the Frost:**

The four ancient faiths are the reason the world has any continuity at all. They survived when institutions did not because they offered something institutions could not: a reason.

The Old Light organized communal survival. Temples became refuges. The covenant's theology of protection and stewardship was not abstract during the Frost — it was operational. Old Light communities built networks of stored grain, shared wells, and rotating labor. Their records from the Frost period are the most complete because preservation of knowledge was itself a moral act within their doctrine. The Old Light entered the Frost as one of four covenants. It emerged as the one most people had encountered at their worst moment.

The Blood Dominion fractured during the Frost, then unified. In the first decade of crisis, Blood Dominion communities splintered — some turned inward and sacrificial to the point of self-destruction, some turned outward and predatory. By the fiftieth year, a darker, harder expression of the covenant had survived: one that understood survival as proof of divine approval, and that saw the other groups' suffering as confirmation. Blood Dominion communities of the deep Frost era were among the most militarily capable surviving groups — and among the most feared.

The Order attempted to preserve governance. In some regions they partially succeeded — the Frost-era Order communities maintained something resembling administrative structure, kept records, enforced internal laws. But institutional theology is very good at managing a stable world and has difficulty when the world becomes genuinely unstable. The Order's records from the mid-Frost are fragmentary and often internally contradictory, showing a theology in crisis trying to justify what was happening within its framework of divine mandate and structured governance.

The Wild was the covenant that adapted most naturally. The Wild had never depended on cities or trade routes or institutional structures. Its communities were already forest-based, already practiced in reading the land for food and water, already in relationship with animals as partners rather than resources. The Wild did not thrive during the Frost — no one thrived — but it survived with its practices most intact. The knowledge the Wild preserved is practical: which roots survive under frost, how to read ice for water access, which animals can be partnered with and how. This practical knowledge made Wild communities valuable to their neighbors even when their theology was deeply foreign.

**The Trueborn During the Frost:**

The historical record on the Trueborn is the most fragmented. What is preserved comes primarily from other covenants' documents, which mention them obliquely and with a quality that could be mourning. The dominant narrative — and the narrative that has become culturally significant in the Age of Reclamation — is that the Trueborn used their position at the great crossroads city to organize the final major resettlement effort before the deep Frost hit, moving populations from the most exposed regions to better-sheltered locations. And then they stayed. And then the city was sealed by ice.

Whether any Trueborn survived is the most politically loaded question in the world of Bloodlines. The answer changes everything.

## THE AGE OF SURVIVAL — One Hundred and Fifty Years in the Dark

History does not work during the Age of Survival. There is no history in the sense of events recorded by institutions and preserved by states. What exists is fragments: individual records, oral traditions, faith documents from communities that maintained enough stability to keep records, and the material evidence — settlement patterns, burial sites, tool types — that the Reclamation-era scholars interpret.

What can be said:

**Tribal structures replaced dynastic ones.** Without the surplus to support specialized governance, the complex political structures of the Age Before dissolved into smaller, more mobile units organized around kinship, skill, and necessity. The nine bloodline families did not disappear — they fragmented. Lines of descent became uncertain. The distinctions between a "true" Ironmark and a family that had intermarried with Ironmark for three generations became philosophically interesting but practically irrelevant.

**The four faiths became the primary organizing structure of human society.** Not because anyone planned it, but because the faiths had two things that tribal structures alone did not: a connection to something beyond immediate survival, and a network. The Old Light's network of refuge communities; the Blood Dominion's warrior bands; the Order's preserved institutional knowledge; the Wild's practical land-knowledge — each of these provided something that drew people to the covenant not from spiritual conviction but from existential need. Many people came to the faiths during the Frost as recipients of aid. Some of them stayed. This is why, in the Age of Reclamation, the four faiths have a membership and a reach that far exceeds what they had in the Age Before.

**Iron was rediscovered from the ruins.** The great foundries were gone, but iron itself was not. The pre-Frost civilization had embedded iron in its structures, its tools, its roads. As Frost-era communities sheltered in abandoned cities and buildings, they stripped iron from what they found. Eventually, communities near the Iron Ridge territories began extracting raw ore again — crudely at first, then with increasing skill as the knowledge was reconstructed from fragments. Iron became a marker of survival capability. A community with iron tools could do things a community with only bone and wood tools could not. Iron began accumulating power as a resource centuries before the Age of Reclamation made it strategically central.

**Animal partnership deepened.** With the reduction of crop agriculture, animal husbandry became more important than it had been in the Age Before. The Wild covenant's practices spread into communities that would never have called themselves Wild followers. Working relationships with horses, cattle, and — in the most Wild-adjacent communities — more exotic animals became embedded in survival culture. The cavalry culture of the Age of Reclamation has roots here: in the Frost-era communities where a horse meant the difference between reaching a food source and not reaching it.

**The bloodlines reasserted themselves.** In the later Survival era — approximately a century in — something interesting happened. As conditions slowly improved (the world was not yet Reclaiming itself, but the worst was passing), communities began asserting identity claims. Families with strong memory of their pre-Frost lineage began using those names again. A man who knew his grandfather had been Ironmark began calling himself Ironmark. A woman who could trace her line to the old Goldgrave merchants began calling herself Goldgrave. The names carried meaning even when their original institutional context was gone. By the last generation of the Survival era, the nine founding bloodlines existed again — not as kingdoms or dynasties, but as living identity claims held by families with varying degrees of legitimate connection to the original bloodlines.

## THE AGE OF RECLAMATION — The World the Game Takes Place In

The Reclamation began not with a declaration but with a thaw.

The thaw came gradually, unevenly, frustratingly slowly. It did not arrive as a gift — it arrived as a problem, because melting ice floods rivers, collapses shelters built for frozen ground, and creates the conditions for a second wave of crisis before the first is fully over. The communities that navigated the thaw successfully were not necessarily the ones that had survived the Frost most comfortably. They were the ones that could adapt quickly to changing conditions.

The defining event of the early Reclamation — approximately eighty years before the present moment of the game — is the establishment of the nine founding dynasties as formal political entities. This was not simultaneous. It was a rolling process that happened over about thirty years, as bloodline families with significant community support began formalizing their authority, establishing territorial claims, and making the transition from "community leaders" to "rulers." The distinction matters: rulers make laws, hold territory, command armies, and negotiate with each other as peers. That transition is what the Age of Reclamation created.

**The Trueborn City's Founding:**

The most symbolically significant event of the early Reclamation is the establishment of the Trueborn City. Not by the Trueborn themselves — who are still believed by most of the world to be extinct — but by the other founding dynasties, collectively, in what the historical record calls the Compact of the Crossroads.

The great crossroads city of the Age Before — the one the Trueborn had held, the one that was sealed by ice — was found mostly intact when the thaw reached it. The ice had preserved rather than destroyed. And it was found without inhabitants. No Trueborn emerged from it. No evidence of survival was present. What was found was the physical structure: the buildings, the roads, the trade infrastructure of the pre-Frost world, mostly intact, at the geographic center of the known world.

The founding dynasties made a collective decision, remarkable given how contentious everything else about their relationships was, to hold the city in trust rather than contest it. The compact established the city as neutral ground — sovereign in itself, governed by a council of representatives from each dynasty, no single house dominant. The reason was partly philosophical (the Trueborn name, and the things people associated with it, created a kind of moral gravity that made claiming it feel wrong) and partly practical (every dynasty that wanted to claim it would have to fight every other dynasty that also wanted to claim it, and none of them could afford that war this early).

The Trueborn City has functioned as the neutral hub of the known world for eighty years. It is the most prosperous settlement in the Age of Reclamation, because every dynasty trades through it and every faith maintains a presence within it. It is also the world's most complicated political entity, because everyone has interests there and no one controls it.

**The Current Political Moment — Where the Match Begins:**

The game begins eighty years into the Age of Reclamation. The world is settled enough to have real kingdoms, real armies, real political stakes. It is not settled enough to have stability.

The nine founding dynasties have spent eighty years establishing territories, fighting border wars, making and breaking alliances, and navigating the four ancient faiths' competing claims over their populations. Some dynasties have consolidated power. Some are still fighting for their basic territorial integrity. Lesser houses have emerged, been granted land, fractured, merged, gone to war with their parent dynasties, and made peace again.

The four faiths are in genuine competition. In the first generation of the Reclamation, faith affiliation was fluid — people followed whatever covenant had helped them survive. Now, with three generations of settled life, faiths have become political identities. A dynasty's faith choice affects its diplomacy, its population dynamics, its military capability, and its relationship with every other dynasty.

And the question of the Trueborn has not been answered.

Every generation since the Compact, someone has made the claim. A wandering family with silver hair. A community in a northern valley that says they never stopped using the name. A Bloodline Member from another dynasty who claims a Trueborn ancestor through a maternal line. The Trueborn City's council investigates each claim, usually quietly, and most claims dissolve under scrutiny. But the question never closes. And the political implications of a legitimate Trueborn heir — what happens to the Compact, to the City, to the entire neutral political structure of the known world — mean that every dynasty has a stake in whether the answer is yes or no.

The match begins inside this world. The player is a ruling house. The Age of Reclamation's eighty-year peace — such as it was, punctuated by war — is over. Something has shifted. The balance of power has become unstable enough that every player on the map is moving at once.

What comes next is what the player decides.

### A Note on Chronology

The following is the working chronological framework for the Bloodlines world:

| Era | Duration | Approximate Years Ago |
|-----|----------|-----------------------|
| The Age Before | Unknown antiquity | 400+ years ago |
| The Great Frost (descent) | ~20 years | ~370 years ago |
| The Great Frost (peak severity) | ~150 years | ~350 to 200 years ago |
| The Great Frost (thaw) | ~30 years | ~200 to 170 years ago |
| Age of Survival | ~120 years total | ~370 to 110 years ago |
| Age of Reclamation begins | — | ~80 years ago |
| Current game moment | Year 80 of Reclamation | Present |

The Great Frost is not fully over. In the northern territories and high mountain passes, conditions from the Frost period persist. Tundra terrain is the modern manifestation of what the entire world once was. The Frost Ruin territories are places the thaw has reached but not fully healed. The world is recovering — not recovered.

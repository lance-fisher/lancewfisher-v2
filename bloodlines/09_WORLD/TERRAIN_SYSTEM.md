# Bloodlines — Terrain and Geography System

The world of Bloodlines is geographically shaped by the Great Frost. Every terrain type bears the mark of what the world went through and what it is still becoming. Terrain is not decorative — it has mechanical weight that interacts with every major game system: resources, military, faith, population, and conviction.

### Added 2026-03-18, Fifth Session

---

## Design Philosophy

Terrain serves three functions simultaneously:

**Strategic resource function:** Different terrain produces different resources, creating natural economic differentiation between starting positions and contested territories.

**Military function:** Terrain shapes how armies move, where cavalry is effective, where formations are viable, and which unit types have home terrain advantage.

**Faith function:** Different terrain types have natural affinity with different covenants. The Wild is strongest in forests. The Order is strongest in administrative centers. This is not a hard restriction — any faith can operate in any terrain — but the terrain creates advantage and disadvantage gradients that skilled players exploit.

## The Ten Terrain Types

---

### 1. Reclaimed Plains

The most common terrain in the central and temperate regions of the map. These are lands that were farmland before the Frost, were buried under ice and snow during it, and have been slowly returning to agricultural productivity during the Age of Reclamation.

**Appearance:** Open, gently rolling land. Remnants of old field boundaries. Occasional ruins of farm structures. Recovering soil with patches of older fertility zones still clearly visible.

**Resource Production:**
- Food: High (the best farming terrain in the game)
- Water: Moderate (rivers and streams recover, but irrigation investment is needed for maximum yield)
- Gold: Moderate (settled populations generate tax revenue)
- Wood: Low (few trees remain — cleared before the Frost, slow to regrow)
- Stone: Low
- Iron: None without finding a deposit

**Military Properties:**
- Open terrain — cavalry operates at full effectiveness
- No forest cover or concealment
- Siege weapons deploy without terrain penalty
- No natural defensive features — fortifications must be built

**Faith Affinity:**
- Old Light: High affinity — agricultural communities, settled life, community welfare
- The Order: Moderate affinity — administrative centers, tax structures, formal governance
- Blood Dominion: Low affinity — no natural sacrifice focal points
- The Wild: Very low — cleared land actively counters Wild mechanics

**Population Capacity:** High — the most livable terrain, easily expanded with housing

**Strategic Notes:** The starting territory for most dynasties. Economically productive and militarily exposed. The player who secures multiple Reclaimed Plains territories has a powerful economic foundation but must invest heavily in fortification and cavalry to defend them.

---

### 2. Ancient Forest

The great forests of the world that existed before the Frost and survived it. In some cases, these forests are genuinely ancient — centuries or millennia old, with root systems that persisted through the ice. The Wild covenant considers these forests sacred in the most literal mechanical sense.

**Appearance:** Dense canopy. Minimal light penetration at ground level. Old-growth trees measuring several body-widths in diameter. Hidden clearings that appear only to those who know where to look. The forest floor is layered, thick, navigable only to those who know it.

**Resource Production:**
- Wood: Very high — the primary wood source of the world
- Food: Moderate (hunting, foraging, berry cultivation, mushroom cultivation — not farming)
- Water: Moderate (forest springs and streams, reliable but not large)
- Stone: Low
- Iron: Low (occasionally, deposits form in proximity to certain ancient geological features)
- Gold: Low (difficult to generate tax revenue from dispersed forest communities)

**Military Properties:**
- Cavalry is severely impaired — horses cannot charge in dense forest, and turning radius is dramatically reduced
- Infantry movement is slower and more fatiguing
- Forest cover provides significant stealth and concealment — units stationary in forest are not visible to enemy units beyond short range
- Ambush premium: units attacking from forest concealment gain a significant first-strike bonus
- Siege weapons cannot be deployed in dense forest
- Wild faith units and certain specialized forces (Thornriders, Forest Sovereign) operate at peak effectiveness

**Faith Affinity:**
- The Wild: Very high — this is home territory; all Wild mechanics are amplified
- Old Light: Moderate — the forest as a place of quiet faith resonance
- Blood Dominion: Moderate — old blood rites have forest components
- The Order: Low — the forest's disorder resists institutional structure

**Population Capacity:** Moderate — dispersed settlement patterns; not impossible to populate but not as dense as plains

**Strategic Notes:** The Wild faction's core territory. An enemy invading a Wild dynasty's forest territory is at maximum disadvantage. But forests also represent the highest wood production in the game, making them economically contested by every dynasty regardless of faith.

---

### 3. Stone Highlands

Elevated, rocky terrain. Mountain foothills and plateaus composed primarily of exposed stone. The natural home of defensive play. Stonehelm's ancestral territory type.

**Appearance:** Exposed rock faces. Narrow passages through ridgelines. Natural chokepoints that one hundred soldiers can hold against thousands. Sparse vegetation. Hard winters that are harder than the surrounding lowlands.

**Resource Production:**
- Stone: Very high — the primary source of large-scale construction stone
- Iron: Moderate (stone highlands often overlie iron-bearing geological strata)
- Water: Moderate (snowmelt from higher elevations, spring-fed)
- Food: Low (thin soil, cold climate, limited agricultural land)
- Wood: Low (sparse trees at elevation)
- Gold: Low (isolated from trade routes)

**Military Properties:**
- Cavalry is severely limited — the terrain prevents charges and impairs movement
- Infantry in high-ground position gains significant defense bonus
- Natural chokepoints force attackers into narrow approaches that defenders can hold with much smaller forces
- Siege weapons require flat positioning — difficult but not impossible to deploy
- Movement speed reduced for all non-specialized units

**Faith Affinity:**
- The Order: High — institutional authority, law carved in stone
- Blood Dominion: Moderate — elevated sacrificial sites, traditional blood altar territory
- Old Light: Moderate — high places as sacred sites
- The Wild: Moderate — wilderness character, though not the forest affinity

**Population Capacity:** Low — harsh conditions limit population density. Stone Highlands support smaller populations with greater defensive capability.

**Strategic Notes:** The home of Stonehelm advantage. Defensible beyond all other terrain types. Stone production makes these territories strategically essential for late-game fortification. The economic cost of holding them is low population and limited food — a dynasty that holds Stone Highlands must be fed from elsewhere.

---

### 4. Iron Ridges

A specific subtype of rocky terrain characterized by iron-bearing geological deposits near the surface, often in bands that run along ridgelines and through valleys. The presence of iron transforms this terrain from strategic to essential in the mid-game.

**Appearance:** Rusty ochre soil exposures. Stained rock faces from mineral seepage. Old Frost-era mining infrastructure — collapsed, but the tunnels and cut-paths that Reclamation-era miners follow. Iron Ridge territories always show evidence of prior extraction, because the Age Before also knew what this terrain held.

**Resource Production:**
- Iron: Very high — the primary iron source in the game
- Stone: Moderate
- Wood: Low (cleared for mining access)
- Food: Very low
- Water: Low (Iron Ridge streams are often mineral-contaminated — not potable without a Well)
- Gold: Low inherently, but the value of iron produced here generates significant secondary wealth

**Military Properties:**
- Narrow access routes create chokepoints similar to Stone Highlands but less pronounced
- Moderate cavalry limitation (terrain is rough but not as extreme as highlands)
- Iron-adjacent terrain generates a slight morale bonus to armies that control it — the soldiers know what they are protecting

**Faith Affinity:**
- The Order: High — industrial faith, the divine mandate of structure and extraction
- Blood Dominion: Moderate — iron as the material of war and sacrifice
- Old Light: Low — the environmental cost of intensive mining sits uneasily with Old Light doctrine
- The Wild: Very low — mining is the opposite of what The Wild holds sacred

**Population Capacity:** Low — mining communities are dense but small; not farmland

**Strategic Notes:** Iron Ridges become the most contested terrain type from mid-game onward. Every dynasty needs iron for swordsmen, cavalry, and fortifications. A dynasty that controls multiple Iron Ridges gains a compounding military advantage that accelerates through the match. Denying an opponent their Iron Ridge territory is as effective as defeating an army.

---

### 5. River Valleys

The most stable starting territory. River Valleys are sheltered, water-secure, and agriculturally productive. The great civilizations of the Age Before were built in River Valleys. The great cities that became Frost Ruins were built in River Valleys. The Trueborn City is built in the most important River Valley in the world — where three river systems converge.

**Appearance:** Flat, fertile land running between gentle slopes. Visible river systems. Established settlement patterns from centuries of human habitation. Rich dark soil. The most "civilized" looking terrain in the game.

**Resource Production:**
- Water: Very high — most secure water access in the game
- Food: High (rich soil, natural irrigation, reliable growing season)
- Gold: High (trade route adjacency, established market activity)
- Wood: Moderate (riverside forest strips)
- Stone: Low
- Iron: Rare

**Military Properties:**
- Open terrain with cavalry effectiveness
- River crossings become strategic chokepoints — controlling a ford or bridge means controlling movement
- Flat terrain means no defensive elevation advantage
- River itself can be used tactically: armies that control the far bank can impede crossing forces

**Faith Affinity:**
- Old Light: Very high — rivers as sources of life, community gathered around water
- Hartvale dynasty bonus (not a faith, but a house affinity)
- The Wild: High — rivers are sacred natural systems
- The Order: High — rivers as natural administrative boundaries, established settlements
- Blood Dominion: Moderate

**Population Capacity:** Very high — the most population-friendly terrain type

**Strategic Notes:** The preferred starting territory. Economically stable, population-supportive, trade-network-friendly. The vulnerability is military exposure — no natural defensive features. River Valleys are the prize, and they are always vulnerable without investment in fortification or military presence.

---

### 6. Coastal Zones

Where the land meets the sea. In the Age Before, coastal zones were the centers of the trade world — ships moved goods faster than roads, and coastal cities accumulated wealth faster than inland ones. In the Age of Reclamation, the coasts are coming back online. Naval capability is re-emerging. The dynasties that understand coastal territory understand what is coming.

**Appearance:** Variable — rocky coastline in some regions, broad beaches in others, estuarine marsh at river mouths. The physical remnants of the Age Before's coastal cities are more visible here than anywhere else — piers, docks, harbor structures partially intact and partially collapsed.

**Resource Production:**
- Gold: Very high (trade network adjacency; coastal trade route access)
- Food: High (fishing supplementing agriculture)
- Water: Moderate (coastal freshwater access requires Well infrastructure; saltwater is plentiful)
- Wood: Moderate (coastal forests)
- Stone: Moderate (coastal rock)
- Iron: Low

**Military Properties:**
- Naval access is the defining military property of this terrain type — only Coastal Zone territory can support naval construction and operations
- Open terrain on land side — cavalry effective
- Harbor positions can be fortified into significant defensive structures
- Naval blockade of a dynasty's Coastal Zone territory is a major economic weapon

**Faith Affinity:**
- The Wild: High — the sea as a primal force, the Wild extending beyond the forest
- Old Light: High — communities gathered around natural abundance
- Blood Dominion: Moderate — the sea as a place of power and danger
- The Order: Moderate — ports as administrative centers

**Population Capacity:** High — coastal communities are historically dense

**Strategic Notes:** The gateway to naval warfare and trade network dominance. A dynasty pursuing economic victory almost certainly needs Coastal Zone territory. A dynasty pursuing military victory needs to decide how much to invest in naval capability versus land forces. A dynasty that ignores the coast can survive — but it will always be aware of what it cannot do.

---

### 7. Frost Ruins

The bones of the Age Before. These territories have been reached by the thaw but not healed by it. The structures of the old civilization stand — partially, unevenly, sometimes dramatically — in the recovering landscape. Frost Ruins are the most historically significant territory type in the world, and the most mechanically complex.

**Appearance:** Old structures in various states of preservation. Streets that are still mostly intact under decades of windblown soil. Warehouses and administrative buildings whose roofs have collapsed but whose walls stand. In some areas, the ruins are large enough to constitute a ruined city — a place that could be settled again if enough population moved in. In others, they are a single ruined complex surrounded by recovering land.

**Resource Production:**
- Special: Lore Cache (unique to this terrain — specific discoveries that provide knowledge bonuses, faith intensity bonuses, or historical intelligence about other dynasties)
- Variable: Depending on the specific ruin, may contain stored iron, preserved wood, or architectural stone that can be salvaged with Siege Engineer units
- Gold: Moderate (salvage trade value)
- All other resources: Low to None

**Military Properties:**
- Ruins provide significant cover for infantry units
- Cavalry is impaired (rubble, narrow streets, unstable ground)
- Ruins can be fortified using existing structures as a base — faster and cheaper than building fortifications from scratch
- Ruins cannot be fully cleared (the design treats the ruins as a permanent landscape feature — they can be built within, but not removed)

**Faith Affinity:**
- Oldcrest: Unique bonus — Oldcrest's Memory mechanic generates additional intelligence from Frost Ruins (old records, preserved documents, architectural evidence)
- The Wild: High — the ruins as reclaimed territory, nature returning to human spaces
- Old Light: High — ruins as sacred space, the memory of what was lost
- Blood Dominion: Moderate
- The Order: High — ruins as evidence of prior legitimate governance to be reclaimed

**Population Capacity:** Variable — a large ruin complex can support significant population with investment; a small ruin has low capacity

**Special mechanic — The Frost Echo:** In certain conditions (late-game, northern campaign), a Frost Echo event can trigger in Frost Ruins territory, temporarily restoring Frost conditions and producing unique resource caches accessible only during the event.

**Strategic Notes:** The most asymmetric territory type. A dynasty that knows how to exploit Frost Ruins can find economic and intelligence advantages that terrain-ignorant dynasties miss entirely. But ruined territory is also contested precisely because it is valuable — and holding it without fortification is difficult.

---

### 8. Badlands

Nobody wants Badlands. This is not quite true — but it is close. The Badlands are the territories that the Frost hit hardest and the thaw has reached least. Inhospitable, low-resource, difficult to develop, slow to populate. The strategic value of Badlands lies entirely in their position, not their resources.

**Appearance:** Cracked earth. Dry streambeds. Harsh scrub vegetation. Minimal tree cover. The landscape of the thaw's aftermath — ground that froze, expanded, fractured, and has been slowly settling since. Not covered in ice, but scarred by it.

**Resource Production:**
- All resources: Very low
- Stone: Occasional small deposits
- Iron: Occasional trace deposits not worth mining except in desperation

**Military Properties:**
- Extremely harsh terrain for all unit types
- Movement is slow and exhausting — armies in Badlands consume more food and morale per movement than in any other terrain
- The terrain provides natural chokepoints at the edges but no defensive value within
- No cavalry advantage
- No forest concealment
- Watchtowers placed at Badlands edges provide excellent visibility (open terrain, long sight lines)

**Faith Affinity:**
- None inherent — Badlands are theologically neutral

**Population Capacity:** Very low — populations placed here suffer happiness and loyalty penalties over time

**Special mechanic — The Strategic Buffer:** Badlands serve as natural strategic buffer zones between kingdoms. A dynasty that holds Badlands between itself and an enemy is harder to raid economically — the enemy cannot sustain a march through Badlands without supply line support. This makes Badlands worth controlling as a denial mechanic even when they produce nothing.

**Strategic Notes:** No one builds an economy in Badlands. But armies march through them, and controlling a Badlands territory that sits between two more valuable territories can be worth more in strategic terms than the territory produces in resources.

---

### 9. Sacred Ground

Sacred Ground exists wherever the pre-Frost faiths made enduring contact with what they believed in. These are not simply religious sites in a cultural sense — they are places where the mechanical systems of faith are amplified in ways that cannot be replicated through construction alone. The four ancient covenants disagree about almost everything, but they agree that Sacred Ground is real.

**Appearance:** Visually distinct from surrounding terrain. Old Light Sacred Ground: clearings filled with extraordinary natural light. Blood Dominion Sacred Ground: ancient blood-stained stone formations, the visual signature of pre-Frost ritual activity. The Order Sacred Ground: the ruins of the most ancient formal temples, more organized and deliberate than Frost Ruins. The Wild Sacred Ground: ancient groves where the trees are so old that their root systems connect visibly above ground, and where animals gather without fear.

**Resource Production:**
- Faith Intensity: Dramatic — a Shrine built on Sacred Ground generates faith intensity equivalent to a Temple elsewhere. A Temple on Sacred Ground generates intensity equivalent to a Grand Sanctuary.
- All material resources: Very low to None — Sacred Ground resists exploitation

**Military Properties:**
- By Trueborn Compact: Sacred Ground is considered protected status — it cannot be fortified under the Compact's terms. This is one of the few absolute rules the Compact established.
- Armies can move through Sacred Ground but not garrison it for military purposes
- Faith-based units may gain a minor bonus on Sacred Ground belonging to their covenant

**Faith Affinity:**
- Each Sacred Ground is affiliated with one of the four covenants — the affiliation is fixed and permanent. A Sacred Ground affiliated with The Wild does not generate Old Light intensity.

**Population Capacity:** Low — Sacred Ground resists dense human settlement in the same way it resists fortification

**Special mechanic — The Contest:** All four covenant faiths understand the value of Sacred Ground. When a dynasty controls Sacred Ground affiliated with a different covenant, they produce faith intensity for the site's covenant rather than their own — essentially subsidizing an enemy's faith intensity. This creates a design tension: holding enemy Sacred Ground is strategically important (denial) but actively counterproductive if the site generates for the wrong faith. The solution is to build enough of your own faith presence to suppress the site — but the Compact prohibits fortification, so military control without settlement means constant vulnerability.

**Strategic Notes:** Sacred Ground is among the most contested terrain types in the game because the faith amplification it provides is irreplaceable. A dynasty that secures Sacred Ground affiliated with its covenant has a faith intensity advantage that cannot be matched through construction alone.

---

### 10. Tundra

The north. The last holdout of the Great Frost. Tundra is what the entire world was during the peak of the Frost — and it is what the northern regions still are. The thaw has not reached here. The growing season is nonexistent. The winters are absolute.

**Appearance:** Permafrost visible at the surface. No agricultural vegetation. Hardy scrub and the occasional frost-resistant tree species. Ice at the surface in winter, frozen mud in summer. The most hostile living environment in the game.

**Resource Production:**
- Iron: Occasional surface-exposed deposits (the freeze-thaw cycle surfaces mineral deposits over centuries)
- Stone: Moderate (frost-heaved stone is abundant at the surface)
- All other resources: Minimal to None

**Military Properties:**
- All units take attrition damage over time in Tundra (cold damage, supply line stress)
- Food consumption of armies is dramatically increased
- Cavalry is impaired on frozen/muddy ground
- The cold reduces morale over time for armies not adapted to it
- Wild faith units have reduced attrition in Tundra — their covenant includes cold-weather survival tradition

**Faith Affinity:**
- The Wild: Moderate — the Wild in its harsh expression recognizes the tundra as powerful and worth understanding
- All others: Low to Very Low — the tundra resists civilization in every form these faiths represent

**Population Capacity:** Extremely low — only the most committed settlement with significant infrastructure investment can support even a small population

**Special mechanic — The Frost Echo:** Late-game climate events can extend Tundra conditions south into adjacent territories temporarily. During a Frost Echo, Frost Ruins territories take on Tundra conditions and generate unique resource caches. The Frost Echo is a world-state event visible to all players.

**Strategic Notes:** Tundra is not a place to build a kingdom. It is a place to control late, defensively, to deny the enemy access to surface iron deposits and to prevent a northern flank attack. The dynasty that ventures deep into Tundra voluntarily is almost certainly pursuing something specific — a resource, a sacred site, or a denial objective.

## World Generation and Map Design Principles

The world is not randomly assembled from terrain tiles. It is generated with geographic logic that mirrors real-world relationships between terrain types:

**Geographic logic:**
- River Valleys run from highlands to coasts, carrying water from mountains to sea
- Stone Highlands and Iron Ridges are adjacent — the same geological conditions that create elevation create iron deposits
- Ancient Forests exist at the edges of Reclaimed Plains, filling the spaces where agriculture has not reclaimed
- Frost Ruins concentrate in former River Valley cities — the places where population was highest before the Frost
- Badlands occur between mountain systems and in the wake of major old battle sites (the Frost-era violence left landscape scars)
- Sacred Ground appears throughout all terrain types — it is not concentrated but is not uniformly distributed
- Tundra dominates the northern map edge and thins into Badlands and then Stone Highlands moving south
- Coastal Zones border any edge where the land meets a body of water

**Starting position principles:**
- Every starting position is guaranteed at minimum one River Valley or Reclaimed Plains territory within immediate reach
- Every starting position is within range of either a forest or a stone source within two territory moves
- Iron Ridges are not starting position resources — they are mid-game targets placed between dynasties or requiring expansion to reach
- Sacred Ground appears in the mid-map more than at edges — it is contested by design

**Map scale:**
Approximately ten times the scale of typical RTS maps at minimum. Large maps will have multiple river systems, multiple ranges of Stone Highlands, and enough interior territory that players in the first several hours of the match may not have found all of it.

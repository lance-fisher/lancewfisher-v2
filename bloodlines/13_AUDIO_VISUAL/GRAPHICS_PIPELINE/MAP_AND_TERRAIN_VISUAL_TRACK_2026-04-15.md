# Map And Terrain Visual Track

This document governs Bloodlines battlefield terrain, biome readability, and environmental composition.

## 1. Core Goal

The map must remain readable at active RTS height while still feeling like a real, inhabited, fought-over world.

## 2. Biome Standards

Bloodlines biome work should anchor to the canonical terrain system and map them into production families:

- reclaimed plains
- ancient forest
- stone highlands
- iron ridges
- river valleys
- coastal zones
- frost ruins
- badlands
- sacred ground
- tundra

Secondary production families may extend these into practical art bundles such as:

- grassland
- mud and churned battlefield ground
- dry earth and exposed clay
- rocky surfaces
- marsh and wetland edges
- snow and frost overlays
- shoreline transitions

## 3. Elevation Readability Rules

- Elevation change must be visible through contour, shadow, cliff profile, and texture shift.
- Walkable slopes must read differently from impassable cliff faces.
- High-ground edges must be readable without camera rotation tricks.
- Defensive high ground should visually advertise its tactical value.

## 4. Road And Path Language

- Primary roads connect keeps, towns, markets, bridges, and major production nodes.
- Secondary roads connect farms, wells, quarries, camps, and watch points.
- Worn paths mark worker and levy movement, not state infrastructure.
- Road width must scale with strategic importance.

## 5. River Readability

- Main channels need clear banks and crossing logic.
- Shallow edge zones must be visually distinct from non-traversable water.
- Ford locations must read before the player commits movement.
- Bridge approaches must align with route logic and settlement logic.

## 6. Bridge Framework

Bridge families:

- timber frontier bridge
- stone civic bridge
- fortified gate bridge
- ruin-span salvage bridge

Each bridge must support:

- ownership marking
- approach clarity
- choke visibility
- destruction or damage read where relevant

## 7. Defensive Terrain Readability

The player must be able to identify:

- natural chokepoints
- high-ground artillery or tower positions
- forest ambush zones
- exposed plains
- siege-friendly approach lanes
- water-secure defensive lines

## 8. Settlement Placement Logic

Settlements should emerge from terrain logic, not arbitrary spacing.

- Hartland towns favor water, fields, and route junctions.
- Fortified seats favor ridges, chokepoints, and layered approach visibility.
- Neutral trade hubs favor crossing and market geometry.
- Ruin settlements grow around salvageable walls and pre-existing street logic.

## 9. Resource Placement Logic

- Farms and agrarian surfaces belong in visible productive belts.
- Wells sit where settlement and field support make sense.
- Quarries and iron nodes should produce local ground treatment and route wear.
- Lumber territories must show cut-path logic and storage traces.

## 10. Battlefield Clutter Control

- Tree lines define lanes, they do not consume them.
- Fences, debris, and props can mark edges but must not hide selection or pathing.
- Ruin rubble should show danger and history while preserving unit visibility.
- Corpses and battle remnants must remain readable as aftermath, not visual fog.

## 11. Edge Blending

Transitions between terrain families must be purposeful:

- farm to path via wheel wear
- path to mud via traffic and water
- grass to stone via exposed slope
- shoreline to dry land via reeds, wet edge, and sediment
- sacred ground to surrounding terrain via distinct but controlled boundary treatment

## 12. Fog-Of-War Compatibility

Terrain must still read under reduced visibility.

- strong silhouettes over micro detail
- roads and rivers visible through reduced lighting
- settlement crowns readable in silhouette
- watchtowers, gates, and bridges still identifiable at partial reveal

## 13. Large-Theatre Support

Theatre-scale view must preserve:

- continent and coastal logic
- route networks
- chokepoint ownership
- water barriers
- regional biome identity

Do not rely on tiny prop work to carry theatre-level information.

## 14. Zoom Rules

When zoomed out:

- major paths, rivers, settlement crowns, walls, and forests carry orientation

When zoomed in:

- materials, wear, production traces, and environmental storytelling become legible

The same terrain asset family must support both without contradiction.

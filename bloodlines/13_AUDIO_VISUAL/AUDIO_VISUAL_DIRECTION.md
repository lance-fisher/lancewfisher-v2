### Visual Identity and Character Style Direction (Added 2026-03-18)

## Visual Identity System (Foundational Framework)

Each Bloodline is defined by a unified identity system consisting of:

* Hair color (primary identifier)
* Color palette
* Symbol family (not a single symbol)
* Architectural design language
* Material tone
* Conviction-based visual modifiers
* Faith-based visual modifiers

## Hair Color System (LOCKED)

Each founding bloodline has a fixed hair color identity:

* Trueborn: Silver
* Highborne: Dark Gold (with highlights)
* Ironmark: Black
* Goldgrave: Bright Gold
* Stonehelm: Brown
* Westland: Vivid Red
* Hartvale: Orange
* Whitehall: Neutral Brown
* Oldcrest: Black / Gray / Pepper

Hair color serves as the fastest visual identifier of bloodline.

## Color Palette System

Each bloodline includes a primary color, secondary color, and accent color. These are applied across UI highlights, unit accents, banners, selection indicators, and ability visuals (subtle use).

## Symbol Family System (LOCKED)

Each bloodline must have a core defining symbol and a family of symbol variations. Symbols must NOT be singular or static. They must support variation across UI, units, buildings, banners, and rank indicators. Unused symbol variants may be assigned to lesser houses.

## Dynamic Visual Evolution

All visual representation is influenced by three layers simultaneously:

1. Bloodline (base identity)
2. Faith (overlay identity)
3. Conviction (tone modifier)

**Conviction Visual Modification:**

Dark Expression: sharper architecture, dim lighting, fire elements, aggressive visual tone.
Light Expression: open structures, natural lighting, clean geometry, balanced visual tone.

**Faith Visual Modification:**

Faith introduces unique buildings, ritual structures, environmental props, and ceremony-specific layouts.

Example — Blood Dominion:
Dark path: blood altars, ritual execution spaces.
Light path: ceremonial structures, organized worship areas.

## Character Visual Style (Realism Direction)

Characters must adhere to realistic proportions, natural anatomy, and non-cartoon presentation.

Explicitly rejected: oversized limbs, stylized exaggeration, cartoon-like proportions.

Accepted: grounded realism, historically inspired clothing, material-based design (leather, steel, cloth), subtle facial variation, visible age progression.

**Bloodline Member Readability**

Despite realism, Bloodline Members must remain identifiable via hair color (primary identifier), garment design (role + bloodline palette), symbol accents, and subtle silhouette differentiation. They must be instantly recognizable, visually distinct, and non-cartoonish.

## Gameplay and Visual Integration

All systems must reinforce one another. Naming reinforces lineage recognition. Hair color reinforces bloodline identity. Symbols reinforce faction recognition. Architecture reflects belief systems. UI reflects dynasty and empire state. No system exists in isolation.

### Full Audio/Visual Direction — Sessions 1-9 (Added 2026-03-19)

## Section 1: Overall Aesthetic Reference Points

This game looks like the world behind the story — the part that isn't shown in a single frame but accumulates across hours of experience. The visual register is large-scale medieval realism: the weight of stone against grey sky, the specific visual quality of iron that has been used, armies composed of people who are cold and tired and following orders. The scale is massive — a world that was once larger than the match can contain, and the match takes place in the living fraction of that world, with the rest implied by what the ruins suggest.

The architectural weight comes from medieval Europe at its most material — not the cleaned-up version presented in tourism, but the version where stone buildings were built by people who thought carefully about load-bearing, where a great hall smells like fire and old wood, where a market town's prosperity is visible in the variation of building heights and the presence of a second story. The textile richness of the historical medieval period is present: garments differentiated by material weight and weave, color that required real cost to produce, the visual difference between a wealthy household's livery and a conscript's rough-dyed equipment. Hollywood medieval uniformly gives everyone bright colors and clean clothes. This world does not.

The color language of the world is calibrated to history and condition. The Frost ended two centuries ago, but its memory is geological — the world has recovered, but not uniformly. A prosperous valley that has been under stable governance for three generations looks warm: stone is maintained, fields are enclosed, the settlement has color in its banners and its gardens. A frost ruin in a territory still contested looks cold: the colors have bled out, the stone is dark with moisture, whatever warmth once lived in the place has been replaced by the specific grey of abandonment. A dynasty in political crisis registers as grey even in summer — not because the world has changed, but because the management that keeps warmth present has fractured, and the underlying grey of a harsh world shows through the gaps.

The adult tone of the game is carried in the visual specificity of its hard things. War looks like war: not glorious formation charges on bright fields, but the collapse of formation under pressure, the rearward flight of a unit that has decided surviving is more important than holding, the aftermath visible in territory that has been fought over. Poverty in an undeveloped settlement looks like poverty: sparse structures, visible resource limitation in the materials used, the absence of things that prosperity produces. Faith structures look like places where people take things seriously — not decorative set dressing placed to indicate "this faction believes something" but buildings designed by people who thought hard about what the ritual required and built the space to serve it. Old Light structures orient deliberately. Blood Dominion spaces enclose deliberately. The design intention is legible in the building because it was legible to the people who built it.

The world has history in its surfaces. The Frost happened. The Reclamation happened. Faith conflicts happened. A structure built before the Frost looks different from a structure built during it — materials changed, design priorities changed, what people were building for changed. A building that has been repaired rather than replaced shows the repair: different stone, a patched roof, a newer door in an older frame. The world is not uniformly aged — it is specifically aged by the specific events that have aged it, and the visual direction must reflect that specificity at every scale, from a settlement's skyline to the surface of a veteran Bloodline Member's shield.

## Section 2: Architecture by House and Faith

**Trueborn:** The Trueborn build for legitimacy and permanence. Their architectural language is old-world institutional — clean dressed stone, covered walkways connecting structures so that movement through the settlement is dignified rather than exposed, archive structures whose proportions signal that what is kept inside matters. The overall effect is of a government that has been governing for a long time and expects to continue. Their Keep reads as a seat of administration rather than a fortress: it is large, formal, and organized around the business of governance — chambers for record-keeping, spaces for counsel, a physical environment that supports the accumulation and preservation of authority. The visual language is competent and deliberate, not ornate. They do not decorate for decoration's sake. What decoration exists communicates function: the symbol of the house on every significant threshold, the careful placement of light in formal chambers, the visible distinction between public and private spaces in a building that handles both.

**Highborne:** The Highborne build to express the assumption that beauty is their right. The architectural language is aristocratic refinement — tall windows that let in controlled light, carved stonework at every transition point, proportions that communicate effort and resource without announcing it loudly. Their buildings look like they were designed by people who have always had the means to do things properly and for whom doing things properly is simply the standard. The distinction between Highborne architecture and wealthy architecture from another house is that Highborne architecture is old-money: it does not try to impress, it assumes. The domestic spaces are visually luxurious in the specific way of a great house that has furnished and refurnished over generations — things that were once fashionable are still present because they were made well enough to last, mixed with more recent additions that reflect current taste. The Keep has the proportions of a palace that began as a fortified residence and was extended deliberately, each addition increasing the visual statement of the family that commissioned it.

**Ironmark:** The Ironmark build for use. Their architectural language is industrial function — buildings designed by people who thought about what the building was for and built it to do that, hard. Forge complexes dominate the settlement's visual profile: multiple chimneys, material handling infrastructure, the visible organization of a production operation. Barracks are sized for capacity. Armories are built to secure their contents. The Ironmark Keep is a working place — the planning chambers, the logistics spaces, the physical organization of a house that fights for a living and needs its home to support that. The forge smoke is always present in the visual environment: not as dramatic effect but as the ambient condition of a settlement where metalwork is continuously underway. The materials of Ironmark construction are what they have — stone where stone is available, heavy timber where it is not, iron fittings and hardware throughout because they produce iron and it goes into everything. Nothing is ornamental. If something looks like a design choice rather than a functional one, it is a mistake in the design direction.

**Goldgrave:** The Goldgrave build for visibility and confidence. Their architectural language is commercial — clean lines, visible signage in the form of banners and symbols placed at distances where they can be read, buildings designed to be recognized and found rather than discovered. The settlement layout reflects a house that thinks about the movement of goods and people: wide approaches, market infrastructure, storage capacity visible as a feature rather than a necessity. Their Keep has the character of a headquarters: efficient, well-maintained, projecting prosperity without excess because excess signals instability and Goldgrave knows it. The material quality is high but the ornamentation is controlled — what is present serves the brand of the house rather than the vanity of its occupants. Goldgrave architecture communicates: we are good at this, we have been good at this for a long time, and we intend to remain good at this. The visual confidence is earned rather than projected.

**Stonehelm:** The Stonehelm build for the scenario where everything goes wrong. Their architectural language is fortress design applied without compromise — walls that are thicker than structural necessity requires because the extra thickness is the point, gates designed with explicit consideration of what happens when someone tries to breach them, towers at intervals calculated for mutual fire support. Everything is permanent. Everything is built to absorb punishment. The visual language is not grim — Stonehelm settlements have the particular warmth of people who feel genuinely secure — but it is unambiguous: this place was designed by people who took the possibility of attack seriously and built accordingly. The Keep is the innermost layer of a defense-in-depth, not a statement of power. It is a place where the family survives if everything outside it has already been lost.

**Westland:** The Westland build pragmatically and then extend as resources allow. Their architectural language is frontier construction that has grown beyond its origins — a settlement that began as something modest and was added to repeatedly as the house stabilized its position. The Keep reflects this history: the original structure is visible in the core, older stone, smaller proportions, practical rather than deliberate. The extensions are visible as extensions — built to a similar standard but in a later style, with different materials where the originals were no longer available in the same form. The visual identity is adapted from the frontier: practical design, hard-used materials, the specific aesthetic of a place that has been weathered rather than curated. Westland construction is not shabby — it is the result of real competence under real constraints, and competence under constraint has its own visual language that reads differently from poverty.

**Hartvale:** The Hartvale build in relationship to the land they manage. Their architectural language is agricultural — the settlement layout organized around food production rather than defense or commerce, with terraced fields visible from the Keep, irrigation systems whose channels appear in the landscape plan, granaries positioned and proportioned to communicate their importance to the people who built them. The materials are warm: warm stone where the quarries provide it, clay roofing tiles in the harvest gold of their palette, structural timber in their construction because their territory produces wood. The Hartvale Keep is embedded in the settlement rather than elevated above it — these are people whose power comes from what they grow and how they manage it, and the architecture reflects the relationship between governance and land rather than governance and force. The palette of the house appears in the built environment: gold in the stonework, deep red in the roofing and the banners, the visual warmth of a house that has built a relationship with a productive territory over generations.

**Whitehall:** The Whitehall build for administrative function. Their architectural language is organized competence — ledger rooms with proper light, administrative halls organized for the business of managing a complex territory, courier infrastructure that reflects the volume of correspondence a house in their position handles. The distinction from Trueborn architecture is in the orientation: Trueborn builds for the accumulation and display of institutional authority, Whitehall builds to make governance work efficiently. The visual language is organized rather than imposing: everything is where it should be, everything serves a purpose, there are not decorative spaces because decorative spaces are not what the house does. The Keep conveys that the people who occupy it know where everything is and how everything connects, which is a form of power that does not require visible display.

**Oldcrest:** The Oldcrest build on top of what was already there. Their architectural language is accumulated time — not any single style but the visible layering of eras of construction, each responding to the conditions of its period and the priorities of the generation that built it. The oldest foundations are visible in the lower courses of the most significant buildings: pre-Frost construction, built by people with different resources and different expectations. The Frost-era repairs are visible as repairs: hasty where the situation demanded haste, durable where the resources were found. The Reclamation-era extensions read as the work of people recovering confidence — larger rooms, more deliberate proportions, the beginning of restoration rather than just maintenance. The contemporary construction reflects a house that has been here for a very long time and builds as a house that expects to continue being here. The visual language of Oldcrest is depth: you are looking at a settlement that has existed across multiple historical periods, and the historical periods are visible in its surfaces.

**Faith architectural overlays:** Each faith modifies the architecture of any house that follows it, overlaying the house's base language with the faith's design priorities. Old Light architecture is characterized by light-direction as a structural principle — windows positioned to admit specific light at specific times, interior spaces organized around the path light travels through the building, minimal interior decoration because the faith's aesthetic is one of clarity rather than richness. The buildings are not bare; they are intentional. Blood Dominion architecture is characterized by ceremonial space with specific dimensional and material requirements — the altar dimensions, the stone or metal specification for ritual surfaces, the enclosed character of spaces where the covenant is practiced. Dominion spaces have a weighted, resonant quality: heavy materials, controlled light, rooms that feel consequential. The Order's faith overlay introduces formal hierarchy into the architecture of whatever house follows it — explicit symbol placement at every threshold, proportions that communicate rank and sequence, a visual language of structured authority that sits on top of the house's base language as an organizing layer. The Wild's overlay is the most visually distinctive: natural materials used structurally rather than decoratively, living elements present in the design rather than as incidental growth, building forms that accommodate the existing environment rather than displacing it. A Hartvale settlement following The Wild looks different from a Westland settlement following The Wild, but both carry the faith's material vocabulary legibly.

## Section 3: Unit Visual Design Direction

The foundational principle of unit visual design is that each unit is a person. When five people constitute a squad, the player sees five people — with mass, with equipment, with the specific physical character of the unit type. This is not an abstraction of mass combat; it is the specific visual reality of the scale at which this game operates, where a squad of five heavy infantry is a meaningful military element and looks like one. The visual degradation of a squad under pressure must be literal: squad members fall, the formation contracts around the survivors, and the final member of a reduced squad fights alone in a way that reads visually and emotionally as a person fighting alone rather than a sprite at low health. The loss of squad members is a visual event, not just a numerical one.

The material language of units tracks progression and status in a way that is legible without explanation. Early-game units wear leather and rough cloth — not because leather is inaccurate but because leather reflects what a dynasty in its founding stage can supply and maintain. Mid-game iron equipment is visible as iron: heavier, more reflective, carrying the specific visual character of metal that has been worked and fitted. The distinction between house-colored equipment and anonymous conscript gear communicates something real about the unit's relationship to the dynasty that fields it: house troops wear the colors and carry the symbols, conscripts wear what they were given. Bloodline Members occupy the highest tier of the material language — their equipment is the finest the dynasty can produce, fitted to their house colors, marked with their house symbols, and tailored to their specific body rather than issued from a common store. A Bloodline Member does not glow. They do not carry oversized weapons. The distinction is in the quality and coherence of what they carry, which is a real distinction that human beings register immediately in a visual field.

Faith visual integration on units develops as faith investment deepens. A unit that has not been touched by a faith covenant looks like a house unit in standard equipment. A Level 3 or higher faith unit carries visible faith signals that are specific to the covenant — not decorative additions to make the unit look different, but the material expressions of actual commitment. Old Light units at depth carry devotional objects that are part of their equipment, visible holster points on the body where the covenant's physical practices are expressed, and a clean precision in their gear that reflects the doctrine's aesthetic. Blood Dominion units at depth carry the covenant's material markers: specific colors that exceed house palette, specific symbols that indicate the nature of the commitment they have made, something in their physical presence that communicates the trade they have accepted. Order units carry mandate symbols in formal placement, their gear organized hierarchically in a way that mirrors the covenant's structure. Wild units carry natural materials in structural roles — not attached for appearance but actually integrated, animal-derived components in their armor and equipment, a visual vocabulary that says the covenant has changed how they relate to their own material existence.

Combat visual direction is governed by the principle that the battlefield should look like a medieval battlefield — not as spectacle, but as operational reality. Formation combat is the primary visual mode: squads holding or advancing in formation, the specific visual shape of a shield wall or a cavalry wedge or an archer line. The visual chaos of melee is present but not celebrated — it is the condition that occurs when formation breaks, and it looks messier and more dangerous than formation combat because it is. The Ironmark Ferocity Under Loss rule must be carried visually: as an Ironmark squad loses members, the survivors' behavior becomes more intense in a legible way — tighter footwork, less overhead in their combat movement, an economy of motion that comes from people who have decided they are going to finish this fight regardless of its cost. The effect is not dramatic for spectacle purposes. It is intense because these specific people have made a specific decision, and their bodies are showing it.

## Section 4: Sound Design Direction

The soundscape philosophy is mass and materiality. Everything in this game sounds like it has weight. The clash of iron sounds like the specific acoustic event of iron on iron — not a cleaned-up, processed game sound effect but the sound of actual iron meeting iron at force, with all the roughness and ring that event produces. The collapse of a building sounds like stone and timber under structural failure — each material contributing its own acoustic character to the event. The ambient audio of a prosperous settlement sounds like people living in it: the particular mix of voices, tools, animals, and movement that constitutes a working community, not a looped ambient track placed to indicate "this place is inhabited."

Combat audio is designed from the squad outward. Squad-level combat should have the specific sonic texture of five people fighting — the individual breathing of people in exertion, the impact sounds of weapons and shields as discrete events rather than mass battle noise, the particular sound signature of different weapon types against different defensive materials. Cavalry has a distinct approach audio that is not just hoofbeats — it is the specific combination of hoofbeat patterns, equipment noise carried in motion (mail rattling, sword against scabbard, the creak of leather under rider weight), and the breathing of horses moving at speed. This combination of sounds should be recognizable and carry weight before the cavalry element is visible, because cavalry historically announced itself through sound, and the player should feel that same pre-contact pressure. Siege weapons have a three-part audio event: the mechanism under tension, the release, and the impact — each with the specific acoustic character of the physical process involved. The Axeman unit's audio signature reinforces its offensive specialization through a specific swing weight and impact sound that is heavier than what a lighter weapon produces — not exaggerated, but accurate to the physics of a heavy axe in motion and contact.

Building audio is organized around the principle that active buildings sound active. Construction sounds match materials: quarrying and cutting stone sounds different from splitting and shaping timber, both sound different from the metalwork of an Ironmark forge. Active buildings produce the ambient sounds of their function in continuous operation — the market has voices, transaction sounds, the movement of goods; the forge has fire, bellows, metal on metal at the specific rhythm of working iron; the training ground has impact and instruction and the particular audio character of people learning to fight rather than fighting. Faith structures are the most distinct category. Old Light structures produce the measured, structured sound of a faith whose practice is organized around deliberate action — the sound of bells at specific intervals, the particular acoustic character of a tall stone space with minimal soft furnishing, the measured cadence of spoken practice. Blood Dominion spaces produce something more resonant and enclosed — the acoustic effect of a space built to concentrate rather than release sound, the specific vocal quality of a covenant practice that is not addressed outward. The Order uses formal, rhythmic sound that reflects its hierarchical structure. The Wild uses the sound of the natural environment, amplified and organized: a Wild faith structure sounds like the outdoor world has been invited inside and given architectural form.

Bloodline Member audio is individuated in a way that generic unit audio is not. Voice lines for commands must communicate that these are specific people the player has been watching develop across the match — not generic "unit acknowledged" prompts but vocal character that reflects the member's traits and their arc in the session. A Bloodline Member carrying the Ferocity trait sounds different from one carrying the Calculated trait in the specific way those traits would manifest in a person's vocal output under pressure. Death audio for a Bloodline Member must be distinguishable from squad member death not by being dramatically louder or more emotionally scored but by carrying a different weight — a sound that the player's ear will register as significant in the same way that any individually known person's departure is registered differently from the departure of people who were present but not known.

Ambient world audio is calibrated to terrain and condition. Ancient Forest terrain sounds like forest that has been old for a long time — the specific acoustic character of a mature canopy, the deep ambient noise floor of a space with significant biomass, the particular quality of sound moving through dense vertical growth. Iron Ridges sound like wind across stone at altitude — the tonal variation of wind through geological features, the absence of soft surfaces that would absorb and diffuse sound. Coastal terrain has the specific layered audio of shore environments: wave patterns at the frequency of the actual tidal regime, the calls of coastal species, the way sound travels differently over water than over land. Frost Ruins produce the particular acoustic quality of abandonment — the absence of sound sources that should be present, the way a space that was designed for human occupation and is now empty reads acoustically as emptiness, the occasional structural sound of buildings in slow decline. Day and night transitions are present in the audio as the actual acoustic transitions of a natural environment: birds change, insects change, the ambient noise floor of human activity drops and rises. These are not theatrical. They are the real transitions of the natural world, handled with enough accuracy that a player who does not consciously notice them still feels the difference.

## Section 5: Music Direction

The score is a compositional presence, not atmospheric texture. It tracks what the match is doing — not by underlining events like a film score underlining its picture, but by responding to the state of the dynasty in the way that a dynasty's emotional register would respond: building as things build, contracting as things are lost, carrying the weight of decisions that cannot be undone. The music is orchestral in its primary instrumentation because orchestral music carries the range of dynamics, intimacy, and weight that this material requires. It does not sound like a generic medieval game score because it is not organized around setting a medieval tone — it is organized around tracking the specific experience of a specific dynasty in a specific match.

Each founding house has a recognizable musical motif — not a full theme scored for full orchestra, but a signature constructed from a small number of recognizable elements that can be developed, varied, harmonized, and transformed across the match's duration. Ironmark's motif is built on rhythmic percussion and low brass: something that feels forged, that has the weight of metal work in its construction, that can be heard accelerating when Ironmark forces are engaged and stripped down when Ironmark has taken significant losses. Highborne's motif is strings-dominant, elevated in register, carrying the particular sound of something that was composed carefully and expects to be listened to carefully — it can be aged, it can become faded and distant when the dynasty is in decline. Goldgrave's motif has the confident forward motion of commercial activity — something that moves, that implies transaction and arrival, that can be elaborated when the house is prosperous and simplified when it is not. Stonehelm's motif communicates immovability: the specific compositional quality of something that does not resolve when it might, that sits on its note and waits. Westland's motif carries open country in its instrumentation and its interval structure — something that implies space and the expectation of motion. Oldcrest's motif has the quality of accumulated time: a phrase that sounds like it has been played before, like it is being played in recognition of something rather than in announcement of something new.

The four faiths have distinct musical voices that modify the house's base musical identity when the house commits to a covenant. Old Light uses choir and brass in a specific structural relationship: the choir providing the harmonic content, the brass marking the structural points, the overall composition ascending in register as the faith deepens. Blood Dominion uses low strings in sustained playing and a specific percussive element that is not rhythmic in the way battle percussion is rhythmic but ceremonial — measured, weighted, the sound of a covenant being observed. The Order uses rhythmic brass and disciplined string structure: music that is formally organized, that has explicit hierarchy in its orchestration, that sounds like it was composed with authority rather than expressed with feeling. The Wild uses natural-world instrumentation in a compositional framework that prioritizes organic texture over formal structure — drums that are struck in patterns that feel discovered rather than composed, woodwind elements that suggest the sound of wind through actual wood, a score that feels like it grew in the specific territory where the match is taking place.

The conviction arc of a dynasty is carried musically in the development of the house's base motif. A dynasty moving toward Moral conviction has a motif that gains warmth as it develops — harmonic resolutions arrive where before they were withheld, the orchestration becomes more complete, something that was open and uncertain acquires groundedness. A dynasty moving toward Cruel conviction has a motif that gains edge: dissonance that was not there in the early game enters the harmonic language of the theme, the orchestration becomes more angular, something that was whole acquires a sharpness that the player will hear as the expression of the choices that produced it.

Match-phase music tracks the structural arc of the game. Stage 1 (Founding) is sparse and open: relatively quiet orchestration, themes in their earliest and simplest form, the sound of possibility without certainty. The world is open and the score reflects that openness without romanticizing it. Stage 2 (Consolidation) develops the orchestration as the dynasty establishes its position — themes begin to interact, the score becomes fuller and more complex in response to the world becoming more populated and more complicated. Stage 3 (Ideological Expansion) introduces faith themes prominently for the first time: the covenant music enters the dynasty's base motif, and the resulting combination is distinct from either element alone. Political tension tracks are present during Stage 3 because the world has become a place where multiple fully-developed dynasties are in contact and their musical identities are in proximity to each other. Stage 4 (Irreversible Divergence) carries the full weight of committed positions: the faith themes are no longer modifiers, they are structural to the dynasty's musical identity. The military themes are heavier, resolved in the direction of their house's specific character. The political themes are unresolved because the political situation is unresolved. The late-game escalation events — the Great Reckoning, the Trueborn Rise, the Divine Right declaration — each carry a specific musical moment, not a stinger placed for drama but a shift in the score's character that reflects the shift in the match's reality. These shifts are weighted rather than theatrical. The player who notices them consciously may identify them as significant musical moments. The player who does not notice them consciously will still feel the change.

Death and loss are handled with restraint that gives the events their actual weight. Squad member deaths produce no specific musical response because they are casualties — part of the arithmetic of the match, not moments the score needs to underline. A Bloodline Member's death produces a specific musical response: not a dramatic sting, not a moment of silence, but a change in the score's texture in which something that was present is no longer present. An element of the dynasty's motif quiets or drops from the orchestration, creating an absence that the player will feel before they identify it, and which will persist for some duration as the score processes the loss before reconstituting. Head of Bloodline death and succession produces a transitional musical moment — the score holds something that sounds like the space between one thing and another, not triumphant for the successor and not elegiac for the departed, but loaded with the weight of what succession is in a dynasty: everything passing from one person to another, with all the uncertainty that accompanies any transfer of power.

## Section 6: UI Audio

The principle governing interface audio is that sound must earn its presence in the player's attention. A player making routine decisions — moving units, checking resource levels, opening an administrative panel — should experience interface audio as clean and nearly invisible: present enough to confirm the action, absent enough to not compete with the world audio that is providing the match's ambient context. A player making a meaningful decision, or being informed of a meaningful event, should experience interface audio that is present and weighted in proportion to the decision or event's actual significance. The interface audio is a priority system, not a texture system — everything cannot be loud and everything cannot be quiet.

Resource threshold warnings are individuated by resource type. Each resource in the game has a distinct audio signal for its depletion warning, chosen to evoke the resource's character rather than to be generically alarming. These signals are recognizable enough that a player can identify which resource is warning without reading the alert text, because the audio identity of the warning is tied to the visual and functional identity of the resource it represents.

Political event notifications are graduated by importance across multiple levels. Minor events (a diplomatic inquiry, a trade disruption, a minor political development in a neighboring territory) have minimal audio signal — enough to flag that something happened, not enough to demand attention. Moderate events have proportionally more audio presence. Major events — a Covenant Test, the Great Reckoning trigger, a Bloodline Member death, a succession event — carry a distinct escalating signal that is designed to ensure the player notices regardless of what they are doing in the interface at that moment. The audio of a major event notification is not the same as the audio of any other notification at higher volume. It is a different kind of signal that the player's ear will learn to distinguish as qualitatively different from the routine audio of the interface.

The Trueborn Summons is the most serious diplomatic event in the game and its audio notification reflects that status. The signal is heavier and more formal than any other interface notification — it carries the specific acoustic character of something official and consequential, something that has been designed to communicate weight rather than merely alerting attention. A player who hears the Trueborn Summons signal for the first time, before they understand what it signifies, should feel that something important has happened before they read the notification text.

The diplomacy interface uses audio to distinguish the weight of agreements. A Military Alliance being finalized carries audio that reflects the weight of a formal commitment between houses — something that sounds like a conclusion rather than a transaction. A Trade Agreement sounds like what it is: a commercial arrangement, professional and final, but without the weight of a military or political commitment. These distinctions reinforce the design intent of the diplomacy system, where different types of agreements carry different levels of commitment and consequence.

The succession interface occupies its own audio register. When the succession panel opens because a Head of Bloodline has died, the ambient audio environment of the game shifts — not dramatically, not silently, but perceptibly. The normal ambient texture of the match quiets, not to silence but to a reduced level that creates the acoustic space for the succession interface's own audio register to occupy without competition. The succession interface audio is quiet, weighted, and specific: it sounds like a formal process being conducted under significant circumstances, with the seriousness of an event that will determine who the dynasty is going forward. A player completing succession should feel, through the audio alone, that they have just done something that matters.

**Status:** PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON
**Date:** Design Content — 2026-03-19, Creative Branch 003

**Scope:** Living family structure, functional commitment roles, succession system, generational scale, Keep visualization

---

## OVERVIEW

The bloodline is not an abstract roster of units. It is a family. A real family, with a patriarch or matriarch at its center, a spouse, children who grow up during the match, political marriages arranged for strategic gain, grandchildren born to members who were themselves children when the match began. Over a 2-10 hour session, the player watches a dynasty live across multiple generations — watching the grandfather's legacy shape the choices available to his granddaughter.

This is the emotional core of Bloodlines. Not the armies, not the territory, not the faith mechanics — those are the frame. The family is the picture. Every system in the game ultimately registers its consequences through the family: a famine culls your children, a bad succession triggers a revolt, a political marriage opens a border, a member lost in battle leaves a functional hole you feel for the rest of the match.

Managing the family is deep, consequential, and never overwhelming by design. The 20-active-member cap is the discipline that forces real choices: you cannot keep everyone useful. You must make decisions about who rises, who waits in dormancy, who marries into another bloodline, who you are willing to sacrifice.

## SECTION A: THE LIVING BLOODLINE — FAMILY STRUCTURE

PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON

### How a Dynasty Begins

At match start, the player controls a bloodline that already exists — it did not spring from nothing. The head of bloodline is established: a man or woman in their late thirties to mid-forties, past their youth, not yet in decline. Their best years as a direct combatant or administrator may be behind them. Their best years as a strategist, political operator, and parent are now.

The player has three meaningful starting configurations to choose from during match setup:

**Configuration 1 — The Established Dynasty**
The head has a spouse. Two or three children exist, ranging from young adolescent to early adult. One child may already be committed to a role (the eldest son who has been training for war, the eldest daughter already being positioned for marriage). The family is established but not fully formed. This is the most common starting point.

**Configuration 2 — The New Union**
The head is recently married. No children yet, or one infant. The dynasty is fresh — the family will grow entirely during the match. This configuration rewards long-game thinking and patient building. Early matches will feel thin. Late matches will feel like the dynasty has become something the player built entirely.

**Configuration 3 — The Widowed Patriarch / Matriarch**
The head lost their spouse. Children exist from that marriage. The head may remarry during the match, producing a second generation with a different bloodline inheritance. This creates natural half-sibling political dynamics within the family and is the most complex starting configuration.

The player names the head of bloodline. Family names are fixed by house (the Drevhar name is always Drevhar; the Cassiel name is always Cassiel) but given names are player-customizable. Children inherit the family name. Children born of political marriages to other bloodlines carry hyphenated or contested identity — see the marriage system below.

### Marriage and Political Alliances

Marriage in Bloodlines is a strategic transaction with personal consequences. It is never purely one or the other.

**Who can marry whom:**
Any bloodline member of appropriate age (late adolescence onward by match timeline standards) can be offered in marriage. The other party may be:
- A member of another player's bloodline (direct diplomatic negotiation)
- A member of a lesser house or vassal family (internal alliance-building)
- A member of an AI kingdom's ruling family (foreign diplomatic play)
- A commoner of exceptional attribute (rare, lower political value, possible stigma from higher houses)

**Arranging a marriage:**
The player initiates marriage offers through the diplomacy interface or through a Diplomatic Envoy committed member. Marriage offers include proposed terms — what the offering family provides (dowry, territory access, trade route, military non-aggression) and what they expect in return. The other party negotiates. A marriage is a contract. Breaking it has consequences.

**Cross-bloodline marriages and children:**
A child born of two different bloodlines is something new. Their attribute inheritance draws from both parents. Their faith inheritance can pull toward either parent's faith alignment — this is a source of family tension and a meaningful design space. Their political identity is contested: the bloodline that holds custody of the child considers them theirs; the bloodline they married into may dispute that claim.

**New dynasty formation:**
Children of cross-dynasty marriages who survive to adulthood and accumulate sufficient political standing (territory held, armies led, faith role performed) can found their own dynasty as a separate player faction — if the parent player chooses to split the bloodline, or if an event forces the issue. This is rare, consequential, and represents a late-game branching outcome rather than a routine occurrence.

**Political marriages within lesser houses:**
A bloodline member married into a lesser house elevates that house's loyalty and binds it more tightly to the bloodline. The married member is essentially placed in a governor role within that house, blending the Defense commitment role with a familial anchor. If that member dies or is recalled, the lesser house's loyalty may fracture.

### How Children Are Born and Develop

Children are not born as game units. They are born as dependents and progress through developmental stages before becoming active members.

**Birth:**
A child is born based on match time elapsed, faith bonuses to fertility, and dynasty health conditions. A match year is not a real-time year — the match clock compresses historical time. In game terms, a marriage produces children over the course of several match years if conditions are favorable. Harsh winters, famine, illness, and the mother's health (which can be affected by stress, conviction, and faith) all modulate fertility.

**Childhood stage (not yet active):**
A child born in match Year 1 is not available as an active member until Year 4 or Year 5 by match clock standards. During childhood, the player assigns the child's birth path — which direction they will grow. This is a declaration of intent, not a guarantee. The child trains along that path during the childhood stage. The player can see the child in the Keep, growing visibly over time.

**Adolescent stage (limited availability):**
By mid-childhood a member transitions to adolescent — present in the Keep as a recognizable person, beginning to be visible as a developing member. They can be placed in limited supporting roles (accompanying a War General on campaign to gain experience, traveling with a Trade Anchor to learn the routes). They cannot yet lead independently.

**Adult transition:**
When a member reaches adulthood in match terms, they become eligible for full active commitment. If a slot exists among the 20 active members, they can be committed immediately. If the roster is full, they enter dormancy — present, tracked, aging, but not deployed.

### Children, Caps, and Dormancy

The 20-active-member cap interacts with a large family in a specific and intentional way.

There is no cap on total family members. A bloodline can have 50 living members across three generations if conditions favor it. The cap governs who is deployed and actively contributing. The rest exist in dormancy — they are alive, they are aging, they produce children, they can be affected by events, and they can be elevated to active status if a slot opens.

When a new member is born, they are not immediately consuming an active slot. They grow through childhood and adolescence without counting against the cap. When they reach adulthood, the player must decide whether to activate them (if a slot is available) or let them remain dormant.

This creates a perpetual management tension: every death opens a slot. Every death also means a person is gone. Opening a slot by losing an active member is never a clean gain.

### Generational Overlap

A long match produces generational overlap. The founding head of bloodline may still be alive — aged, reduced in some capacities, elevated in others — when his grandchildren are born and begin to develop. This is not a problem to be managed. It is the point.

An aged head of bloodline carries the accumulated weight of decades of match history. They have conviction shaped by every choice made under their leadership. Their faith intensity may have deepened into Fervent or Apex territory. Their presence in the Keep radiates a kind of gravity that younger members do not yet have. But they are also slowing. Their capacity for direct action is diminishing. The dynasty will, at some point, have to transition to a new generation — and the player has been watching that next generation grow.

The grandfather who watches his grandson take his first command role is a moment the game is designed to produce. It should feel like something.

## SECTION B: FUNCTIONAL COMMITMENT ROLES — FULL DESIGN

PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON

Commitment roles are distinct from birth paths. A member's birth path shapes what they are capable of and what they are inclined toward. Their commitment role is what they are actually doing in the match — how they are deployed, what they contribute, what is lost if they die.

Birth path determines the ceiling. Commitment role determines the current function.

A member born on the Warlord path committed to the War General role is the optimal configuration. A member born on the Scholar path committed to the War General role can do it — but they will never be as capable, and the mismatch shows.

---

### ROLE 1: WAR GENERAL

**What the member does:**
The War General commands armies in the field. Their presence in a battle formation provides a command anchor — every unit under their direct command benefits from morale bonuses, coordination bonuses, and reduced rout risk. A large army without a War General fights as a collection of units. A large army with a strong War General fights as a single instrument. The General's personal attributes (combat skill, conviction, faith intensity) modulate which specific bonuses apply and how strong they are. A Fervent War General fighting a defensive war under Moral conviction generates different effects than a Neutral conviction General fighting an aggressive campaign.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Vanguard Surge — the War General personally leads a concentrated push against a specific enemy formation. This is a high-risk direct action. If successful, it breaks the targeted formation and generates a temporary army-wide morale surge. If the General is wounded or killed during the Surge, the army suffers an immediate morale collapse. The Vanguard Surge cooldown is several match minutes and should not be used carelessly.

**Passive presence effect:**
While committed and present in a campaign, the War General provides a persistent command radius — all units within it receive the baseline command bonuses without any active trigger. The strength of the passive radius scales with the General's experience and conviction alignment.

**Absence consequence:**
When a War General dies or is captured, every army they were commanding loses its command anchor immediately. Units suffer morale penalties. Rout thresholds drop. If the dead General was also the head of bloodline, succession triggers simultaneously — producing a period of maximum vulnerability where the dynasty has no military leader and no political certainty at the same moment. Enemies who track this will attack.

**Development:**
War Generals accumulate Campaign Experience through battles won, battles survived, and specific feat events (first major siege, first victory against a superior force, etc.). Each Campaign Experience milestone improves the command radius, the effectiveness of the Vanguard Surge, or unlocks a specific combat doctrine (aggressive, defensive, siege-oriented). Faith intensity deepens naturally through the witness of death and the weight of command — a War General who has survived ten years of campaign may find themselves drifting toward Fervent faith regardless of the player's intent. Conviction is shaped by how the player conducts wars — whether prisoners are taken or executed, whether civilians are protected or burned, whether allied lesser houses are honored or exploited.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The War General is visually marked by campaign wear — the signs of years in the field. Scarring, weathered skin, the kind of posture that comes from carrying armor for decades. Their faith alignment shows in their personal iconography: an Old Light General may wear a small sigil at the throat; a Blood Dominion General carries something darker. Conviction shows in the set of the jaw, the condition of the weapons on the wall behind them, the way they hold still versus the way they move. A Moral conviction General looks like a man who has seen much and kept his humanity. A Cruel conviction General looks like a man who has discarded parts of himself to become more effective.

### ROLE 2: TRADE ANCHOR

**What the member does:**
The Trade Anchor commits to a trade route or caravan operation, traveling with or remaining stationed along an economic corridor. Their presence transforms a basic commercial route into a high-value exchange — merchant partners along the route increase their willingness to engage, diplomatic credibility opens higher-tier goods and services, and the Anchor's personal reputation functions as a guarantee of the dynasty's reliability. A dynasty without a Trade Anchor can still conduct commerce, but access to luxury goods, high-tier resources, and cross-bloodline economic diplomacy requires one.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Strategic Negotiation — the Anchor brokers a specific deal that could not be executed through standard trade mechanics. This may open a new resource connection, extract a favorable treaty term disguised as a commercial arrangement, or secure a short-term economic advantage during a rival's crisis. Strategic Negotiations require time, carry a failure risk (the Anchor's skill and conviction both matter), and on failure may sour the relationship with the negotiating party.

**Passive presence effect:**
All trade routes with the Anchor committed provide a passive income multiplier and a loyalty bonus to lesser houses along the route who benefit from the trade. The Anchor's presence signals investment — the dynasty is committed to this corridor, not just extracting from it.

**Absence consequence:**
When a Trade Anchor dies or is captured while on a route, all active trade in that corridor pauses or degrades. Merchant partners lose confidence. Lesser houses along the route see a loyalty dip. High-tier diplomatic trade requires re-establishment from the beginning. If the Anchor was also serving as a political liaison with a foreign bloodline (as they often do), that relationship enters an uncertain state that the player must address.

**Development:**
Trade Anchors develop through successful negotiations, route expansions, and economic crisis management events. As they develop, their network of contacts grows — represented as a Merchant Network attribute that quietly expands the number of routes and partners available to the dynasty. Faith and conviction shape how they conduct negotiations: a Moral conviction Anchor is trusted broadly and may access parties that a Cruel Anchor cannot reach, but a Cruel Anchor may extract more favorable terms through intimidation and leverage.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Trade Anchor is the best-dressed member of the family. They carry the prosperity of the dynasty's commercial relationships in how they look — fine materials, understated wealth, the kind of appearance that projects reliability without ostentation. A deeply faith-committed Trade Anchor may carry devotional objects or dress in colors associated with their faith. Their quarters in the Keep reflect their contacts — maps, correspondence, objects from distant territories. When the Anchor is lost, those details go quiet.

### ROLE 3: TERRITORY GOVERNOR

**What the member does:**
The Territory Governor commits to a specific claimed region and serves as the dynasty's permanent presence there. They do not roam — they anchor. Their commitment suppresses local revolt risk, generates a loyalty bonus to the population of the territory, and ensures that the player's claim to that land has a human face attached to it. A territory governed by a bloodline member is qualitatively more stable than one governed only by appointed administrators. Lesser houses within the territory respond to the Governor's conviction and faith alignment — a Governor whose personal character aligns with the local population's values generates significantly stronger stability effects.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Governor's Proclamation — a formal act by the Governor directed at the territory's population or lesser houses. A Proclamation can suppress an emerging revolt before it fires, offer a one-time loyalty surge to all lesser houses in the region, or formally recognize a lesser house's service (binding that house more tightly to the bloodline). Proclamations have a long cooldown and their effectiveness scales with the Governor's established reputation in the region — a new Governor's Proclamation carries less weight than one issued by a Governor who has held the territory for many match years.

**Passive presence effect:**
The Governor provides a persistent stability field across the territory. Revolt thresholds are raised (it takes more accumulated grievance to trigger). Taxation efficiency improves. If the dynasty holds multiple territories, only territories with committed Governors receive these effects. Ungoverned territories are held by force and administrative appointment alone — functional but unstable.

**Absence consequence:**
When a Territory Governor dies or is captured, their territory enters an instability window immediately. Revolt risk rises sharply. Lesser houses in the region begin to recalculate their loyalty. If the Governor was the local population's connection to the bloodline, their absence is felt as abandonment. The longer the territory goes ungoverned, the higher the probability that a revolt fires or that a rival bloodline's diplomatic overtures gain traction.

**Development:**
Governors develop through years of service in the territory — not through dramatic events but through accumulated presence. A Governor who has held a territory for ten match years has roots there. Local families know them. The territory's population has a personal relationship with the dynasty through them. This accumulated presence is represented as a Roots attribute that grows slowly and cannot be transferred — if the Governor dies, the Roots go with them. A replacement Governor starts from scratch. Conviction shapes how the population develops under them: Moral governance creates resilient populations with genuine loyalty; Cruel governance creates obedient populations with concealed resentment that fires dangerously under stress.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Territory Governor appears in the Keep in the colors and textures of the territory they govern. Their appearance has taken on something of the land — the people they live among have shaped them. A Governor ruling a coastal trading territory looks different from one governing a highland fortress region. Their faith alignment is visible in small devotional details. If the territory is prosperous, they carry that prosperity. If the territory is suffering, it shows on them too.

### ROLE 4: FAITH LEADER (OLD LIGHT PATH)

**What the member does:**
The Faith Leader is the dynasty's spiritual representative within an Old Light faith tradition. They perform rites, lead observances, bless armies before departure, consecrate claimed lands, and serve as the human bridge between the dynasty's political ambitions and the sacred order they claim to serve. Their presence communicates to the population that the dynasty is not merely powerful but righteous — that its victories are sanctioned and its governance has moral weight. The Faith Leader does not just manage a spiritual resource. They are a living argument that the dynasty deserves what it has.

The rites they perform cost something real. Consecrating a territory before a campaign requires a formal sacrifice of resources and time — animals, grain, objects of value, and the Faith Leader's personal endurance. The consecration is not symbolic. It depletes what it touches in exchange for what it provides. A Faith Leader who performs too many rites too quickly degrades. They age faster. Their faith intensity may push too hard into Fervent or Apex territory, where the spiritual weight they carry becomes physiologically visible.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Grand Rite — a significant formal ceremony that produces a major faith-based effect for the dynasty. The Grand Rite can consecrate a newly claimed territory (raising its permanent loyalty ceiling), bless an army before a decisive campaign (providing a sustained morale and endurance bonus for the duration of a specific conflict), or perform a rite of passage for a bloodline heir (accelerating their development along their birth path). Grand Rites require preparation time, consume significant resources, and age the Faith Leader measurably. They cannot be performed back-to-back.

**Passive presence effect:**
The Faith Leader's committed presence raises the baseline faith intensity of the dynasty's population over time. Communities near the Faith Leader's center of activity trend toward Active and Devout faith engagement. This passive effect produces long-term loyalty stability and access to faith-based building upgrades that would otherwise not be available.

**Absence consequence:**
When a Faith Leader dies, the dynasty loses its formal spiritual representative. Faith intensity across the population does not drop immediately — but without renewal, it will slowly erode. Consecrations already performed remain in effect but decay over time without maintenance. Communities that had strong Old Light engagement may begin to express doubt or drift toward rival faith traditions. If the dead Faith Leader was a beloved figure in the community, their death may trigger a grief event that temporarily raises faith intensity before it begins the long decline.

**Development:**
Faith Leaders deepen through the performance of rites over time. Each Grand Rite pushes their faith intensity one step higher — and each step higher makes subsequent rites more powerful and more costly. A Faith Leader at Fervent faith has become something the population looks at differently. A Faith Leader at Apex faith has become the most spiritually potent individual in the dynasty, capable of effects no other member can produce — but they are also burning. Their body shows it. Their years are shortened. The player must decide how hard to push them.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Faith Leader is marked by the accumulated weight of their devotion. Early in their commitment, they look like a priest or scholar — composed, set apart, carrying the visible symbols of their faith tradition. As they deepen toward Fervent and Apex, the physical markers intensify. At Fervent: their bearing has changed, their eyes carry something present and distant simultaneously, their skin is beginning to show the cost of what they carry. At Apex: they look like a person who has been partly consumed by something larger than themselves. They are still a person. But the faith is visible in their bones. They are always present in the Keep in a dedicated space — an altar, a devotional corner, a library of sacred texts — and that space grows more elaborate as their intensity deepens.

### ROLE 5: COVENANT OFFICIANT (BLOOD DOMINION / DARK DOCTRINE PATH)

**What the member does:**
The Covenant Officiant is the dynasty's instrument within the darker doctrinal paths — Blood Dominion or equivalent faith systems that extract power through deliberate cost. They do not perform blessings. They perform bindings. Covenants with forces that demand payment. The Officiant is the person who knows the terms, who carries the negotiation, and who executes the act. This is not metaphor. The Covenant Officiant participates in acts of genuine consequence: ritual sacrifice, blood oaths that bind lesser houses through fear and obligation rather than loyalty, and acts that transgress the moral conventions of the broader world in exchange for power that conventional faith cannot access.

The population knows what the Officiant does. Or they suspect. The dynasty that employs a Covenant Officiant is broadcasting something about itself — that it will pay prices others will not. This generates fear. Fear has strategic value. It also generates enemies who believe they have a moral obligation to stop you.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Covenant Binding — a formal act of dark doctrine that extracts a significant benefit in exchange for a real, irreversible cost. The nature of the binding depends on the doctrine in play. Under Blood Dominion: a Binding may sacrifice a lesser member of the bloodline (the player chooses the sacrifice from a list of eligible dormant or peripheral members) to produce a permanent, dynasty-wide power bonus. The sacrifice is permanent. The member is gone. The power is real. Alternative Bindings may sacrifice territory fertility, a lesser house's autonomy, or a significant quantity of population in exchange for military or economic advantages that cannot be obtained any other way.

**Passive presence effect:**
The Covenant Officiant's presence generates a Fear Aura that covers the dynasty's political footprint. Lesser houses in the dynasty's territory are less likely to revolt — not because they are loyal, but because they are afraid. Enemy bloodlines that know the Officiant is active factor that into their diplomatic calculations. Some potential allies will refuse contact with a dynasty known to practice dark doctrine. Others will seek it out.

**Absence consequence:**
When a Covenant Officiant dies, any active Covenants they were maintaining enter an unstable state. Bindings that required ongoing maintenance begin to unravel. The power they provided starts to drain. Some Covenants, if their Officiant dies mid-process, produce consequences — the entity or force that was being bound does not simply go away. The dynasty may face a period of active backlash from the doctrine's demands. The Faith Instability event that fires when an Officiant dies is darker and more dangerous than any other role-death event in the game.

**Development:**
Covenant Officiants develop by completing Bindings and surviving their consequences. Each successful Binding deepens their understanding of the doctrine's terms and expands what they can offer and extract in subsequent Bindings. Their conviction axis is typically Cruel — not because all Cruel members become Officiants, but because the Officiant role requires the capacity to perform acts that a Moral conviction member cannot execute. A Moral conviction member assigned to this role will refuse certain Bindings, limiting what the doctrine can produce. The Officiant's appearance changes with each major Binding — they carry the marks of the transactions. Not theatrical darkness. Real weight. They look like someone who has traded significant parts of themselves for power and has not fully come to terms with the accounting.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Covenant Officiant occupies a distinct space in the Keep that the other members give a wider berth than is visible or acknowledged. Their space is not decorated. It is functional. The items present are the tools of the doctrine — objects the player will not always be able to identify but will recognize as purposeful. The Officiant themselves are calm. Extraordinarily calm. They have moved past the kind of agitation that disturbs surface behavior. What they carry is below the surface, and it shows in a specific flatness — a quality of stillness in the eyes that is not peace. Other family members in the Keep visual do not look at the Officiant directly. Their children do not play near the Officiant's space. The Keep knows what the Officiant does. The player can feel this if the visualization is executed correctly.

### ROLE 6: SPYMASTER

**What the member does:**
The Spymaster builds and operates the dynasty's covert intelligence network. They do not appear on battlefields. They do not govern territory publicly. They are the person who knows things — about rival bloodlines, about the loyalty of lesser houses, about the personal vulnerabilities of enemy leaders. A dynasty with a committed Spymaster operates with significantly more information than one without. They can detect revolts before they fire, identify traitors within lesser houses, and track the movements of rival bloodline members.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger an Intelligence Operation — a specific covert action executed through the Spymaster's network. Operations include: targeted assassination of a rival bloodline member (high-risk, catastrophic if traced back to the dynasty), loyalty subversion of a lesser house within a rival's territory (slow, deniable, powerful), intelligence extraction from a captured or accessible source, and counter-intelligence operations that purge compromised members from the dynasty's own administrative structure. Each operation consumes network resources and carries exposure risk.

**Passive presence effect:**
The Spymaster provides passive intelligence coverage across a radius scaled to their network's development. Within coverage, the player sees hidden loyalty values of lesser houses, receives early warning of rival diplomatic negotiations that might affect the dynasty, and gets notification when a rival bloodline member moves into a position of unusual significance (military buildup, religious appointment, etc.).

**Absence consequence:**
When a Spymaster dies, the intelligence network goes dark. Not immediately — the agents and contacts exist independently — but without a central coordinator, the network fragments over time. Operations that were in progress are abandoned or misfire. Intelligence coverage becomes patchy. The dynasty goes partially blind, and in the period before a replacement Spymaster can rebuild coverage, rivals who are aware of the vacancy may move aggressively.

**Development:**
Spymasters develop by running successful operations and surviving exposure events. Their network grows through accumulated contacts — represented as an Agent Web attribute that maps their coverage area and operational capacity. A senior Spymaster with a decade of match time has agents embedded in rival courts and lesser houses across the map. They are nearly impossible to fully neutralize without killing them directly. Conviction shapes their methods: a Moral Spymaster prefers intelligence gathering and blackmail over assassination, while a Cruel Spymaster is willing to execute informants who become liabilities and run dirtier operations.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Spymaster is the least visually distinctive member of the family by design. Their appearance is controlled — not austere, just carefully managed. Nothing about them announces what they do. In the Keep visualization, they appear as a calm, composed figure who is always slightly peripheral — present in every gathering but never at the center. Their space, if the player looks closely, contains correspondence. Many letters, carefully organized, with nothing identifying left visible. Their faith alignment is the most private aspect of their appearance — they may carry small devotional objects, but they carry them hidden.

### ROLE 7: DIPLOMATIC ENVOY

**What the member does:**
The Diplomatic Envoy is the dynasty's formal representative in relations with other bloodlines, foreign kingdoms, and the political institutions of the broader world. They travel. They negotiate. They carry the dynasty's reputation in their person — when a Diplomatic Envoy arrives at a foreign court, they are the bloodline made visible. Their personal attributes (charisma, faith alignment, conviction) determine how they are received and what they can accomplish. A dynasty without a committed Diplomatic Envoy can still conduct diplomacy, but critical negotiations, alliance formations, and marriage arrangements between bloodlines all require an Envoy's direct participation to achieve optimal outcomes.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Grand Embassy — a formal diplomatic initiative where the Envoy leads a significant delegation to a foreign party. A Grand Embassy can ratify a major alliance, dissolve a long-standing conflict through formal negotiation rather than continued war, open a new trade corridor at the diplomatic level (which the Trade Anchor then operates), or negotiate the release of a captured bloodline member. Grand Embassies take extended match time and tie up the Envoy for the duration. If the Envoy is killed or captured during an Embassy, the initiative collapses and diplomatic relations with the target party deteriorate severely.

**Passive presence effect:**
The committed Envoy generates a Diplomatic Presence bonus that slowly improves the dynasty's standing with all foreign parties — not dramatically, but consistently. Foreign bloodlines and AI kingdoms send better-quality proposals. Minor diplomatic incidents are absorbed more gracefully. The dynasty is perceived as a party that invests in its relationships, which opens options that purely military-dominant dynasties do not have.

**Absence consequence:**
When a Diplomatic Envoy dies or is captured, the dynasty's diplomatic relationships enter a review period. Alliances do not immediately break, but their terms become renegotiable. Foreign parties who were close to agreement on proposals pause. The dynasty must appoint a new Envoy and invest time in reestablishing its diplomatic presence before major initiatives can resume.

**Development:**
Diplomatic Envoys develop through successful negotiations, crisis mediations, and the accumulated reputation of their portfolio. Each significant successful initiative adds to a Reputation attribute that compounds — a senior Envoy is welcomed differently than a new one. They develop personal relationships with specific foreign leaders that the player can leverage. Faith alignment and conviction shape their diplomatic style: an Old Light Envoy from a Devout dynasty is trusted as a principled actor; a Cruel conviction Envoy is respected as a hardnosed negotiator who honors agreements precisely.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Diplomatic Envoy is the most cosmopolitan-looking member of the family. They have traveled, and it shows — not in wear and weathering (that is the War General's mark) but in exposure. Objects in their space come from places the dynasty has relationships with. Their dress reflects awareness of the wider world while maintaining the dynasty's colors and symbols. They carry gifts given to them by foreign parties — evidence of their network of relationships made material.

### ROLE 8: HEIR DESIGNATE

**What the member does:**
The Heir Designate is the formally named successor to the head of bloodline. They are not yet in power. They may be in a secondary commitment role simultaneously (an Heir Designate who is also a functioning War General is common). But their formal designation changes how the world treats them — foreign parties negotiate with them as future heads of bloodline, lesser houses calibrate their loyalty to include the Heir's future leadership, and the broader political world begins the long process of acclimating to the coming transition.

The Heir Designate's formal status does something important mechanically: it smooths the succession event. When the head of bloodline dies and a formal Heir is already designated, the succession interface resolves faster, the revolt risk from the transition is lower, and the Heir accesses the head's full attribute bonuses more quickly. The cost of designating an Heir is that the dynasty is telegraphing its succession plan — enemies may specifically target the Heir to force the player into a more complicated succession.

**Active ability:**
The player can trigger a Shadow Governance period — a formal process where the Heir begins assuming certain administrative functions of the head of bloodline while the current head is still alive. This accelerates the Heir's development and creates a visible continuity signal to the broader world. Shadow Governance can be used during a period when the head is elderly or in poor health (signaling stability and managed transition) or as a strategic move to establish the Heir's legitimacy before a dangerous campaign.

**Passive presence effect:**
The Heir Designate generates a Succession Stability bonus that reduces the dynasty's overall revolt risk during any succession event. The longer they have been formally designated, the more established their position, and the stronger this bonus becomes.

**Absence consequence:**
When a designated Heir dies, the dynasty must re-designate — and the gap in formal succession planning fires an immediate Political Instability event. Lesser houses that had already aligned their loyalty to the dead Heir recalibrate. Foreign parties renegotiate their diplomatic assumptions. The dynasty looks vulnerable until a new Heir is named and the designation process begins again.

**Development:**
The Heir Designate develops through the Shadow Governance mechanic and through any secondary role they hold. They develop a distinct political identity — their own conviction posture, their own faith alignment, their own relationships with lesser houses. A dynasty where the Heir has been functioning in Shadow Governance for years has effectively pre-installed their next leader. The transition, when it comes, is less a crisis and more a formal handover.

**Visual identity in the Keep:**
The Heir Designate occupies a specific position in the family visualization — adjacent to the head of bloodline, visibly positioned as the next-in-line. Their appearance echoes the current head in certain respects (the dynasty's visual identity is maintained) while showing the generation gap. If the Heir is young, they look younger. If they have been in the role for many years and the head has aged significantly, the contrast between them tells a story. When Shadow Governance is active, the Heir's visual presence in the Keep increases — more central, more prominent in the hierarchy display.

### ADDITIONAL PROPOSED ROLES (DESIGN GAPS ADDRESSED)

**Scholar / Chronicler**
A member committed to the intellectual and historical preservation of the dynasty. They do not fight, govern, or trade. They record, analyze, and advise. Their commitment provides research bonuses (access to advanced building upgrades, doctrine refinements, and unit improvements that require scholarly development), advisory bonuses to the head of bloodline's decision events, and a generational legacy effect — when the current head dies and a new one takes over, the Scholar's chronicles provide the new head with a compressed but real knowledge transfer, reducing the period of post-succession inefficiency.

**Court Physician / Healers**
A member committed to the health and recovery of the bloodline. Their presence reduces the mortality risk of ill or wounded members, extends the active lifespan of aging members, and provides event options during disease crises that a dynasty without a Court Physician cannot access. Their presence in the Keep is felt most acutely when someone is sick.

**Bloodline Enforcer**
A member committed to internal discipline — the person who maintains the dynasty's internal order, investigates potential traitors, manages the dynasty's prison and detention functions, and executes punishments. They overlap somewhat with the Spymaster's functions but operate internally rather than externally. Their presence suppresses internal betrayal events. Their conviction alignment matters significantly: a Moral Enforcer is thorough but constrained; a Cruel Enforcer is comprehensive and leaves marks on the dynasty's population loyalty.

## SECTION C: SUCCESSION SYSTEM — FULL DESIGN

PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON

### The Death of the Head of Bloodline

When the head of bloodline dies, the game does not pause. The world continues. Armies keep fighting. Trade routes keep moving. Lesser houses keep calculating their loyalty. The succession event fires into a live match — and that is intentional. The dynasty's vulnerability at the moment of transition is not a mechanical abstraction. It is a real window that enemies can and will exploit.

The player receives a notification: the head of bloodline is dead. The nature of that notification depends on the circumstances. A death in battle is immediate — the player was likely present for it. A death from illness is preceded by warning events over several match days during which the player could begin positioning. A sudden death from assassination fires with minimal warning.

The succession interface opens. The world does not stop.

### The Succession Interface

The succession interface is the most important individual UI moment in the game. It arrives at a moment of elevated stress — someone is dead, enemies may be moving — and requires the player to make a decision that will define the next phase of play. It must give the player everything they need to make that decision while not drowning them in information.

**The interface layout:**

The background is the Keep. Not a menu screen — the Keep itself, visible and present. The family is there, in the aftermath of the head's death. The body may not yet be removed. The atmosphere is not cinematic drama for its own sake — it is the functional reality of a family in crisis.

The left panel shows the current state of the dynasty: active territories, active alliances, active military commitments, current resource levels, faith state across the population. This is the inheritance — the player is about to hand all of this to someone new.

The center panel shows the eligible successors. Each candidate occupies a slot with their portrait, their name, their current commitment role, their age, and four key attribute readings:

1. **Political Legitimacy** — how well-established is their claim? A formally designated Heir scores highest. A nephew from a cadet branch scores lowest. The score reflects how much of the political world already accepts this candidate as the next head.

2. **Capability Profile** — a condensed summary of their core attributes across the main functional areas (military, diplomatic, economic, spiritual). Not a list of every stat — a readable assessment of where they are strong and where they are weak. Visual bar graphs, not numbers.

3. **Bloodline Impact Projection** — a forward-looking calculation showing what the dynasty's key metrics are expected to look like under this candidate's leadership over the next 15-20 match minutes. Projected loyalty among lesser houses, projected population sentiment, projected military capacity. This is presented as projected ranges, not certainties. The interface is honest that it is projecting.

4. **Revolt Risk Indicator** — which specific territories and lesser houses are at elevated revolt risk if this candidate is chosen. Not a generic risk score — specific named entities. The player can mouse over each to see why. "The House of Arren was loyal to the previous head personally — they do not yet know this candidate." "This territory was governed by the previous head's brother, who is a rival candidate."

The right panel shows the world outside the Keep. It is live — units moving, territories pulsing with their current stability levels, rivals' positions visible on a compressed map. This is the clock. The world is not waiting.

**The decision timer:**

There is no hard timer. The player can take as long as they need. But the world does not pause, and the succession interface shows the live map. If an enemy army is marching toward an ungoverned territory while the player deliberates, the player sees it. The pressure is real and visible, not artificial. There is no countdown forcing a bad decision — but there is the live consequence of delay.

**External pressure:**

An ally with a strong preference for a specific candidate may send an urgent diplomatic message during the succession window. The message appears in the interface as a priority notification: "House Vael requests the succession of [candidate]. They consider this a matter of alliance continuity." This is not a command. It is information. The player can honor it, ignore it, or use it as leverage later ("I chose the candidate you preferred — I expect something in return.").

A threatening enemy may also communicate during the window — not to influence the choice, but to announce that they are watching and that the transition period is not going to be peaceful. This is not a mechanic. It is atmosphere. It is real.

### Succession Eligibility

**Primary eligibility tier:**
Direct children of the deceased head. Children from all marriages are included. The eldest child has traditional legitimacy advantage but no mechanical requirement — the player chooses freely. A formally designated Heir has the highest legitimacy score but is not forced on the player.

**Secondary eligibility tier:**
Siblings of the deceased head, if still living. This tier is only eligible if the head died without living adult children. A dynasty where the head died young with children too young to rule represents a specific succession problem — see the Regency scenario below.

**Extended eligibility tier:**
Nephews, nieces, cousins — accessible if both primary and secondary tiers are unavailable or if the player explicitly designates one as successor through the Shadow Governance mechanic before the death event.

**Non-family succession:**
A legendary War General who is not bloodline can be proposed as successor in extremis — typically only accessible if the family has been devastated by war, plague, or sacrifice and no eligible blood candidates remain. Non-family succession triggers a severe revolt event from any remaining family members who feel their claim was bypassed, and the new head cannot access certain bloodline-specific abilities. This is an emergency option, not a preferred path.

**Regency scenario:**
If the head dies while children exist but the eldest viable successor is still in adolescence (not yet adult in match terms), a Regency fires. A regent — chosen from the secondary or extended eligibility tier, or from a committed senior member — governs until the designated heir reaches adulthood. The Regency period has its own political dynamics: the regent may or may not be loyal to the young heir's future, and the player must manage that relationship carefully.

**Multiple marriages:**
A head with children from multiple marriages creates contested succession automatically. Children from the first marriage have traditional primacy. Children from the most recent marriage may have stronger political backing from the living spouse's network. The succession interface shows these competing legitimacy sources as a visible political map — which lesser houses back which candidates based on their relationships.

### Contested Succession — Revolt Mechanics

**Which successions trigger revolts:**

Any succession where the chosen candidate has a Political Legitimacy score below a certain threshold triggers a Contested Succession event. Low legitimacy scores result from: choosing a non-designated heir when a formal Heir Designate exists, choosing a candidate with personal characteristics that important lesser houses actively oppose (a Cruel conviction candidate in a territory that was governed under Moral convention, for example), choosing a candidate whose faith alignment conflicts sharply with the dominant faith of key territories, or bypassing an older sibling for a younger one without established justification.

A succession that triggers no revolt is a success. It means the player has managed the family, the political relationships, and the designation process well enough that the transition is accepted. This should feel earned when it happens.

**Severity of a revolt:**

Revolt severity is determined by:

1. How many lesser houses were primarily loyal to the deceased head personally rather than to the bloodline institutionally — personal loyalty does not automatically transfer.
2. The legitimacy gap between the chosen successor and the most legitimate alternative (the candidate who would have had the highest Legitimacy score but was not chosen).
3. The personal conviction alignment of the successor versus the political character of the revolting entities.
4. Whether any rival bloodline is actively stoking the revolt — offering the revolting houses support, shelter, or alliance in exchange for breaking from the dynasty.

A minor revolt fires one or two lesser houses in a single territory. They declare independence — their territory goes from dynasty-held to contested. The player must reconquer or negotiate.

A major revolt fires multiple lesser houses across several territories simultaneously, potentially cutting the dynasty's effective territorial control by a third. This is a crisis. It coincides with the post-succession period when the new head is still establishing their command. A rival who correctly times an attack into a major succession revolt can break a dynasty that was previously dominant.

**Preventing revolts:**

The most reliable prevention is the Heir Designate system. A formally named heir with a long designation period and high legitimacy inherits smoothly. Beyond that: political marriages that bind lesser houses to the bloodline (a house whose daughter married the chosen successor has personal investment in his success), Governors who have built strong Roots in their territories, and a Diplomatic Envoy who has been working relationships with key lesser houses in advance of the succession event.

A player who has thought about succession — who has managed the family's political relationships with the next transition in mind — will navigate the event with damage contained. A player who has not thought about it will face a crisis.

**Negotiating down without reconquest:**

Not all revolts require war to resolve. Options:

- **Tribute offer:** The new head formally acknowledges the revolting house's grievance and offers a material compensation. This works best when the grievance is about the process of succession rather than deep opposition to the chosen head personally. Cost: resources. Gain: house returns without war, but with a memory of having successfully leveraged the dynasty.

- **Marriage offer:** The new head offers or accepts a marriage proposal with the revolting house's family. This is the most durable resolution — it converts the revolt into a political alliance. Cost: a bloodline member's commitment. Gain: lasting stability. Risk: it takes time, and the house must be willing to negotiate.

- **Faith mediation:** A committed Faith Leader or Covenant Officiant can perform a rite or ritual that formally acknowledges the transition under sacred terms. For houses whose primary loyalty was faith-based rather than personal, this can be decisive.

- **Show of force:** The new head assembles an overwhelming military presence at the border of the revolting territory and makes clear that reconquest is possible but not preferred. This can cause a revolt to collapse without actual combat if the revolting house has insufficient military capacity to resist. It requires available military force during a period when the dynasty may be stretched thin.

### The Transition Period

Immediately after a succession choice is confirmed, the dynasty enters a brief Transition Window — typically 5-10 match minutes — during which:

- All bonus effects attributed to the previous head lapse. The new head's bonuses activate at a reduced level initially and scale up as the Transition Window closes.
- All diplomatic relationships enter a status of "acknowledged but pending" — foreign parties are not hostile, but they are waiting to see how the new head performs. Alliances remain technically in force but are less reliable until reconfirmed.
- The new head's committed role activates fully (if they were the Heir Designate in Shadow Governance, this transition is seamless; if they were a War General who is now suddenly the head of bloodline, there is a brief period of reassignment as they vacate the War General role).
- Any active Revolt events fire during the Transition Window. This is by design. The vulnerability is real.

**How other bloodlines respond:**

AI bloodlines that are tracking the dynasty's state (which senior Spymasters in AI kingdoms do) will identify the Transition Window as a strategic opportunity. An AI bloodline that has been waiting for the right moment to move on a contested territory or press a dormant dispute will assess whether the transition creates that moment. The player should expect that strong rivals will test them during succession. This is not unfair. It is the system working as intended.

## SECTION D: GENERATIONAL SCALE — THE OPEN PROBLEM AND PROPOSED SOLUTIONS

PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON

### The Problem Stated Clearly

A 10-hour match with a living family system, realistic fertility, and natural aging can produce a large number of living family members. If a founding couple has four children, and each of those children eventually has three children, the dynasty reaches twelve grandchildren in addition to the four founding children — sixteen family members in two generations. Add marriages that produce their own offspring, add the potential for some founding members to still be alive, and a long match can plausibly track 40-60 living family members.

This is not a failure of the system. It is the system working. The question is how to make it manageable for the player without eliminating the depth that makes it meaningful.

### Option 1: Hard Tier System — Direct Management Only for Active Roster

Only the 20 active members receive direct player attention. All other family members exist in a tracked but automated state — they are alive, they age, they produce children (at a background rate), and they die of natural causes, illness, accident, and old age without requiring player decisions.

Exceptional dormant members surface automatically: when a dormant member crosses a threshold of development (their birth path produces something remarkable, an event occurs that brings them to prominence, they reach an age where they are clearly more capable than some active members), the player receives a notification. The player then decides whether to promote them to active status by displacing someone from the active roster or accepting that they remain in dormancy.

**Tradeoffs:** Clean management experience. The player is never overwhelmed. But dormant members feel like a tracking system rather than people. The family's depth becomes a background hum rather than a foreground presence. The option works mechanically but may underdeliver emotionally.

### Option 2: Event-Driven Culling — Natural Population Pressure

The match generates periodic large-scale events that naturally reduce the living family population without direct player agency. Long winters kill the youngest and oldest members first. Plague events move through the population non-discriminatorily. Civil wars and territorial conflicts kill members caught in contested areas. A dynasty that over-invests in family growth without attending to the health and security of the population will face natural culling events that trim the family back.

This does not cap family size artificially. It makes the size of the family a reflection of conditions the player has managed — or failed to manage. A dynasty that keeps its territory stable, its people fed, and its borders secure can sustain a larger family. A dynasty in perpetual crisis will lose family members at a higher rate.

**Tradeoffs:** Feels organic and consequential. Every death in a culling event matters because it was a preventable death — the player had agency over the conditions that caused it. But it creates emotional unpredictability: a beloved dormant member who the player was developing for future activation can die in a famine event they had no direct control over. This may feel punishing rather than dramatic if the culling is not telegraphed well.

### Option 3: Nested Family Units — Branch Management

The bloodline is organized into branches. The inner circle (head of bloodline, spouse, direct children, designated heir and their immediate family) is managed individually. Extended family is organized into branches — the branch of the head's brother, the branch of the head's eldest married daughter, etc. — and managed as collective units.

Each branch has a Branch Head (usually the eldest or most capable member of that sub-family) who the player can interact with directly. The rest of the branch exists as a block — they contribute resources, provide minor bonuses, and produce offspring in aggregate. When a branch member distinguishes themselves (through a birth path event, a military feat, an unexpected development), they surface for individual attention.

The player can elevate a branch member to the inner circle if a slot opens. They can send a branch into a specific territory or role context as a unit. They can politically manage the relationship between branches (keeping cadet branches loyal versus managing their ambitions).

**Tradeoffs:** Creates a believable sense of a large, living family without requiring the player to track every individual. But introduces a new management layer (branch politics) that is its own complexity. The emotional connection to individual members may be diluted by the abstraction.

### Option 4: Role-Based Visibility — Only the Committed Are Directly Managed

Only family members in active commitment roles receive direct player attention. All others exist as a reserve — visible in a roster view, trackable by name and basic attribute, but not actively demanding decisions. When a committed member dies or is captured, the succession interface for that role presents the available reserves as candidates. The player chooses a replacement from the reserve. That replacement becomes directly managed for the duration of their commitment.

This means the player's attention is always focused on the 8-12 committed members at any given time, not the full active roster of 20. It reduces the effective management surface to a workable set while preserving the reserve as a meaningful resource pool.

**Tradeoffs:** Cleanest management surface. The player knows what they're managing at all times. But the reserve members may feel purely instrumental — they exist to fill holes, not as people. The depth of the family system is most visible in the committed members. The reserve is texture, not substance.

### Recommended Combined Approach

The four options are compatible with each other at different scales. The recommended combination:

**Tier 1 — Committed members** (8-12 active role-bearers): Fully managed individually. These are the people the player knows, develops, and mourns. Role-Based Visibility governs this tier.

**Tier 2 — Active roster, uncommitted** (up to 20, minus those committed): Tracked individually with key attributes visible. Event-Driven Culling applies more heavily here — these are the members most exposed to match conditions. When a committed member dies, the player selects a replacement from this tier.

**Tier 3 — Dormant family** (beyond the 20-active cap): Managed through the Nested Branch system. The player sees them as branches, not individuals, unless a specific member surfaces through an exceptional development event. Event-Driven Culling is the primary limiter on this tier's growth.

The result: the player directly manages a focused group of 8-12 committed people. They interact with a broader roster of up to 20 active individuals at a higher level. They are aware of a larger family beyond the cap as a living, aging, growing, dying entity managed at the branch level. The family is always present and always large, but the management surface scales with the player's attention span.

**Design flag:** The transition from Tier 2 (individual tracking) to Tier 1 (committed management) is a moment that should feel significant. When a dormant reserve member is elevated to a committed role, the player should feel like they have brought someone into the center of the story. The game should mark this.

## SECTION E: KEEP VISUAL REPRESENTATION

PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH 003 — NOT YET CANON

### The Keep as a Living Space

The Keep is not a menu. It is a room — or a series of rooms — that the player observes, and the family lives in it. The family's presence in the Keep is always visible but not always demanding attention. The player can zoom into the Keep view for detail or let it sit in the background as ambient information.

The Keep's visual state is the most information-dense surface in the game. Everything the player needs to read the dynasty's current condition — health, faith, conviction, relationships, prosperity, crisis — can be read from the Keep without opening a statistics panel.

### The Immediate Family Hierarchy

**The head of bloodline** occupies the center of the Keep's primary gathering space. Their position is literally central — other family members are arranged around them, visually weighted toward their presence. The head's current condition radiates outward: their posture, their bearing, the quality of the garments they wear, the expression that their face settles into at rest. A healthy head in a prosperous dynasty in the middle of their prime looks visibly different from an aging head whose territory is under siege.

**The spouse** is positioned adjacent to the head, slightly offset. The nature of their relationship with the head shows in their spatial distance and orientation — a marriage of deep personal bond looks different from a politically arranged one. The spouse's faith alignment may differ from the head's, and both are visible simultaneously, creating a small visual conversation about the dynasty's internal tensions or harmonies.

**Children** are arrayed by age from eldest to youngest. Eldest children who have been committed to roles carry the visual markers of those roles. A committed War General son stands differently from an uncommitted younger son still in adolescence. The younger children are present as children — not miniature adults, not placeholder tokens, but the visual age that matches their developmental stage. An infant is an infant. A twelve-year-old looks twelve. The player watches them age.

**Dormant members** are present in the background — visible in peripheral spaces of the Keep, near the edges of the visual field, doing background activities that signal their existence and general state without demanding attention. They are there if the player looks. They are not pressing forward.

### Faith Alignment — Visual Signals

Faith alignment shows not in UI indicators but in how characters look and what surrounds them.

**Old Light faith (Latent to Active):** Small personal devotional objects carried or worn. Clean, somewhat spare personal aesthetics. A quality of composure in the face that suggests interior order.

**Old Light faith (Devout to Fervent):** The devotional objects become more prominent and more integrated into daily dress. The face carries a quality of active engagement — something present and alert behind the eyes. The character's space in the Keep has grown more explicitly devotional — a dedicated altar area, candles, specific arrangements that make clear this person's relationship to the sacred is functional and daily.

**Old Light faith (Apex):** The person is visibly carrying something that has weight. Not dramatic physical transformation — not visible fire or divine light. The weight. The posture under load. The eyes that have seen things that most people have not and will not. An Apex faith member in the Keep has a gravity that visually pulls the scene toward them whether the player intends to look or not.

**Blood Dominion (Latent to Active):** Similar to early Old Light — small objects, subtle signals, nothing pronounced. The objects are different. They carry different connotations. A viewer who knows what to look for will recognize them.

**Blood Dominion (Devout to Fervent):** The faith markers have become harder to ignore. Not theatrical darkness. Specific materials. Specific colors. The character carries their doctrine in ways that are visible to other family members in the Keep, and the other members respond to this — subtle behavioral distance, spatial awareness of where this person is in the room.

**Blood Dominion (Apex):** The character occupies the Keep at a remove from everyone else. Not because they have been excluded — because the remove is what they require now. Their appearance has taken on something formal and final, as though they have arrived at a conclusion about what they are. Other family members do not avoid them exactly. But they treat them differently. The player can see this in the visual behavior of the Keep — how the crowd distributes around the Apex Dominion figure.

**Mixed faith family:** When the head and spouse carry different faith alignments, their respective halves of the Keep gradually take on different visual textures. The head's side may be Old Light-influenced; the spouse's side may carry Blood Dominion markers. The children are caught between. Their personal faith alignments have not yet settled, and this shows in their visual presentation as an ambiguity — they do not carry their parents' signals cleanly. This visual ambiguity is one of the most interesting information states the Keep can produce.

### Conviction — Visual Signals

**Moral conviction:** The space is organized. Not pristine — a dynasty at war is never pristine — but organized in the sense that things are where they belong and people are treated as though they matter. The head of bloodline makes eye contact with the people around them in the Keep visualization. Servants and lesser staff have some presence. The dynasty's symbols are displayed with care rather than domination.

**Neutral conviction:** The space is functional. The visual language is clean without warmth or coldness. Things are arranged for utility rather than for meaning. The dynasty's symbols are present because they are required, not because they are believed in. The people in the Keep are there to do what they do. This is not cruel — it is indifferent. The indifference shows.

**Cruel conviction:** The space is austere in a specific way — not bare, but stripped of softness. The dynasty's symbols are everywhere, positioned to remind rather than to honor. The head of bloodline's posture when at rest is the posture of a person who does not extend trust naturally. Other people in the Keep give them more space than is socially normal. Lesser staff is less visible — they have learned not to be noticed. The Keep is not a home. It is a command post with beds.

### Health and Illness

**Healthy members:** Full visual presence, engaged posture, appropriate age-matched appearance.

**Ill members:** The illness shows before the player is notified by an event. The member begins to appear slightly diminished — less present, posture shifted, face carrying something that is not quite right. By the time an illness event fires, the player who has been watching the Keep will already have noticed something was wrong. This is intentional — the Keep as an early warning system.

**Aging members:** The aging process in the Keep is gradual and visible. A member who was in their prime at match start is visibly older by hour five. Their hair has changed. Their posture has shifted. Their movement has slowed slightly. The dignity of their age is present — they look like someone who has lived, not like a deteriorated version of their earlier self. An aged head of bloodline commands respect in the Keep visualization. The room organizes differently around a patriarch at seventy than it does around one at forty-five.

### Death and Absence

**When a member dies:**

The visual change in the Keep is immediate and specific. The space they occupied is not erased — it is emptied. An empty chair at the dining arrangement that was their place. Their devotional objects still present but unattended. Their personal effects not yet removed. The other family members have redistributed visually, but the gap is visible as a gap rather than closed over. The family is not yet finished grieving.

Over time (measured in match minutes), the Keep adjusts. The empty space slowly integrates. Other members begin to occupy the gap in small ways. The dead member's personal effects may be relocated or formally preserved depending on the dynasty's conviction posture: a Moral conviction dynasty preserves them as memorial; a Cruel conviction dynasty clears them quickly as a signal that sentiment is not governance.

A portrait of the dead member appears at some point after their death in the Keep's longer walls — the dynasty's gallery of its own dead. This gallery grows over the course of a long match. By hour eight, the player who has been watching it grow has a visual record of everyone they have lost.

**When a member is captured:**

The Keep does not treat capture as death. The member's space is maintained. Their effects remain. The other family members display a visual state of suspended attention in relation to that space — not grief, not acceptance, something unresolved. The household is waiting. This visual state persists until either the member is ransomed back (they return to their space, visibly marked by the experience of captivity) or they die in captivity (the space then transitions to the death visualization).

### A Dynasty in Full Strength

A dynasty at peak strength in the Keep looks like this: the head of bloodline is in their prime or approaching it, bearing the marks of experience without the marks of decline. Their faith intensity is at Devout or Fervent, and the visible markers of that depth are integrated into their appearance rather than displayed. Their conviction is Moral, and the Keep reflects this — the space is organized with genuine care, the family is present and engaged, the lesser staff have faces. The children are growing — some already committed and visibly marked by their roles, others still in development. The spouse is present and their relationship with the head is visible as warmth rather than political arrangement. The gallery of dead is not yet long.

The feeling of a dynasty in full strength should be the feeling of watching a family that is doing exactly what a family at the height of its power looks like. Capable. Watchful. Not entirely at ease — a dynasty at this scale is never entirely at ease — but confident in its capacity to meet what comes. The threat is always present. The strength is real. Both visible at once.

### A Dynasty in Crisis

A dynasty in crisis looks like this: the head of bloodline is either newly succeeded — young, not yet fully established, their posture carrying the uncertainty of someone who has not yet proven they deserve the room they are standing in — or old and failing, with the transition visibly overdue. Multiple member spaces in the Keep carry the emptiness of recent death or the unresolved tension of capture. The gallery is long. The children who were growing are now committed and stressed, the marks of their roles deepened past comfort into something that looks like strain.

The faith state of the dynasty is divided — a recent political marriage or a succession that brought in a head with different faith alignment has created visible tension in the Keep's religious landscape. The conviction posture may have shifted from what it was under the previous head — the new head's Cruel conviction has already begun changing the ambient temperature of the space. The lesser staff are less visible. The family is more isolated within itself.

The feeling of a dynasty in crisis should be the feeling of watching a family under load that may be reaching the limit of what it can carry. Nothing has broken yet. Everything might. The Keep is still a dynasty's home. It is a dynasty's home at three in the morning during the worst year of its existence. The player should feel the weight of it when they look.

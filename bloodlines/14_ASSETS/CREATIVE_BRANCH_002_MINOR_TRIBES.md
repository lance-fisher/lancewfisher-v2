# SECTION A: WHAT MINOR TRIBES ARE

## Historical Identity

Minor tribes are the forgotten survivors. When the Great Frost came and the world broke, not every community had the organization, the resources, the military strength, or the territorial position to anchor a founding house. The nine founding houses represent the survivors who built lasting political structures — walled settlements, inherited governance, resource extraction networks, and enough consolidated military power to claim and hold territory into the Age of Reclamation.

Minor tribes are everyone else who survived.

Some tribes descend from pre-Frost civilizations whose political structures did not survive the collapse but whose people did. These are the oldest tribes — remnant cultures carrying knowledge, ritual, and memory of a world before the nine houses existed. They do not think of themselves as failures of the Frost. They think of themselves as the original inheritors of the land, watching upstart houses build walls on soil their ancestors walked for generations.

Some tribes coalesced during the Great Frost itself, formed not from prior civilization but from desperate necessity — bands of survivors who found each other in the ice and built loyalty through shared suffering rather than inherited bloodline. These tribes have no political memory before the Frost. The Frost is their founding myth. Their conviction structures, faith alignments, and military identities were all forged in deprivation.

Some tribes are the remnant populations of what could have been a tenth house but was not. A dynasty that nearly made it — strong enough to survive, not strong enough to anchor a political structure that held. These near-house tribes often carry resentment toward the nine houses, a sense of stolen destiny. They have names, histories, and in some cases written records of the leadership lines that died in the Frost. They know who they almost were.

A small number of tribes are nomadic by choice, not by failure. They survived the Frost by moving rather than by fortifying. They developed military cultures optimized for movement, terrain reading, and rapid response. They have no permanent settlements to defend and no interest in building them. These tribes interact with founding houses the most unpredictably — they cannot be threatened with siege, they cannot be economically pressured through territory control, and they move the moment a relationship becomes unfavorable to them.

The founding houses largely do not think about these distinctions. To a house with walls, armies, and diplomatic apparatus, all minor tribes are "the outside" — potential resources, potential threats, potential nuisances, but not equals. This condescension is historically and politically significant. Tribes remember how houses treat them.

## Count and Distribution

A typical match contains **8 to 14 minor tribes**. The minimum is 6 (on smaller maps or maps with concentrated terrain). The maximum is 18 (on large maps with high terrain diversity). The standard configuration for a full 10-player match on a standard-sized map is 10 to 12 tribes.

Tribes are distributed across the map at world generation time, placed in terrain zones that match their archetype. No two tribes of the same archetype occupy the same match — each tribe archetype appears once per match, preserving distinct identity. The world generation system selects from the pool of available archetypes based on which terrain types were generated and places appropriate tribes into those zones.

Tribes are not placed in the core starting territories assigned to founding houses. They occupy:
- The buffer zones between founding house territories
- The deep wilderness sections of the map where no house has yet expanded
- Strategic resource nodes that are too far from any house's starting position to be immediately claimed
- Terrain zones with high defense value (highlands, ancient forests, river valley crossings) where a small population can hold a strong position

The practical effect is that every founding house encounters minor tribes when they begin expanding. The first third of a match is largely about which houses are building relationships with neighboring tribes and which houses are simply trying to push through them.

## Growth and Development

Minor tribes are not static. They develop on a slower timeline than founding houses but they do develop.

A tribe that is left alone and not pressured will grow modestly across the match. Population increases, military strength increases slightly, and if the tribe's terrain provides sufficient resources, they may expand to an adjacent territory. This expansion is limited — tribes do not have the political and economic machinery to expand aggressively — but a tribe ignored for long enough may control two or three territories by the mid-game.

A tribe that is actively engaged by a dynasty — through trade agreements, tribute arrangements, or faith alignment activity — gains development faster. A dynasty that invests in a tribe is effectively building it up as an asset. The tribe becomes more militarily useful, and the dynasty gains diplomatic and strategic returns on that investment.

A tribe that is repeatedly raided, has agreements broken, or is subjected to faith persecution does not simply weaken and die. It can radicalize. A tribe pushed into hostility may contact neighboring houses with offers of military cooperation against a common enemy. A tribe with faith alignment to a covenant may receive support from houses of the same faith. A tribe that was nearly exterminated may reappear in the late game as part of a coalition force under a different house's command.

## Tribal Independence and Interaction

Minor tribes do interact with each other independently of player actions, though these interactions are limited in scope. Tribes sharing a terrain zone or sharing a faith alignment have a base relationship. Tribes of opposing faith alignments have background tension. These relationships are visible to players who invest in intelligence — a dynasty with a high-level Trueborn City connection or strong diplomatic infrastructure can observe tribal inter-relations.

Specific tribal interactions that happen independently:

- Two tribes sharing the same terrain zone and the same faith alignment may form a loose confederation, sharing military response when either is attacked.
- A tribe with a hostile relationship to a dynasty may send emissaries to neighboring tribes, effectively warning them. This raises the diplomatic baseline difficulty for the dynasty with the offended tribe's neighbors.
- A tribe that falls below a survival threshold — through disease, raiding, or starvation during the Long Winter event — may voluntarily seek absorption by an adjacent tribe rather than by a house, combining their remaining forces and territory.

These are slow background processes. They are not dramatic events that reshape the map. They create a persistent sense that tribes exist in the world as real entities with their own relationships rather than as map objects waiting for player interaction.

## Default Relationship to Founding Houses

The default diplomatic baseline between any founding house and any minor tribe is Neutral, but the historical texture underneath that neutrality varies significantly.

Some tribes have a generationally positive memory of a specific founding house — ancestors helped during the Frost, a marriage alliance three generations back, a shared faith that predates the current match. These tribes start with a hidden positive modifier that makes moving from Neutral to Cordial easier than normal.

Some tribes have a generationally negative memory of a specific founding house — ancestors displaced by house expansion, a broken agreement in a prior generation, a faith persecution by a house of opposing covenant. These tribes start with a hidden negative modifier that makes moving from Neutral to Hostile faster than normal and makes achieving Cordial status costly.

Tribes descended from near-house populations carry relationship complexity based on which house most directly displaced or competed with their ancestor lineage. A tribe descended from a near-house population that lost territory to Stonehelm might have deeply negative baseline modifiers toward Stonehelm specifically, while remaining genuinely neutral toward Hartvale.

The founding house that begins closest to any given tribe's territory has a natural first-contact advantage. Proximity creates opportunity, but it also creates the most friction if expansion goes badly.

# SECTION B: MINOR TRIBE ATTRIBUTES

## Attribute Framework

Every minor tribe in a match is defined by the following attributes:

**Size** — Ranges from Small (1-2 territory zones, limited military) to Medium (2-3 territory zones, capable military) to Large (3-4 territory zones, serious military presence). Size is set at world generation and changes during the match based on player interaction and the Long Winter event. Large tribes at match start represent survivor populations that had geography or resources on their side. Small tribes represent remnant populations that barely made it through the Frost.

**Faith Alignment** — Which of the four covenants the tribe follows, and at what intensity tier. Not all tribes have strong faith alignment — some are Latent or Neutral, carrying cultural memory of a faith without active practice. Faith intensity affects how much a tribe responds to faith-based diplomacy and what faith-related mechanics trigger around them.

**Conviction Posture** — Where the tribe's governance falls on the Moral/Neutral/Cruel axis. This affects how they interact with dynasties of different conviction postures, what kinds of agreements they are willing to enter, and what political events they participate in.

**Terrain Home** — The primary terrain type this tribe inhabits and defends. Their military units are optimized for this terrain. Attacking them in their home terrain is always a disadvantageous fight. Their resource production is shaped by this terrain.

**Resource Specialty** — The primary resource this tribe controls or produces in excess. This is the primary economic reason a dynasty wants a relationship with them.

**Diplomatic Baseline** — Their default posture toward founding houses generally: Friendly (will enter agreements readily, require little convincing), Neutral (standard interaction required), Hostile (require significant investment or specific preconditions to engage diplomatically), or Wary (will engage but only after specific trust-building actions — often tribes with historical grievances against houses generally).

**Military Character** — What unit types they field and their general combat style. Tribes do not have organized house armies. They fight as their terrain and culture dictate.

**Cultural Identity** — The one defining characteristic that makes this tribe distinct and memorable in play.

## Named Minor Tribe Archetypes

### 1. THE ASHWALKERS

**Terrain Home:** Badlands — the burned, ashen wastelands that were scorched during the Great Frost's collapse of civilization.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Blood Dominion, typically Active to Devout intensity. The Ashwalkers believe the Frost was a purification event and that sacrifice is the correct response to hardship. They practice ritual scarring and blood offering. Their faith is not gentle.

**Military Specialty:** Skirmish raiders. Fast, light infantry who fight in broken terrain with hit-and-fade tactics. They use fire and terrain denial as weapons — burning chokepoints, poisoning water sources, making the approach to their territory psychologically costly before the first sword is drawn. Their units deal higher damage in Badlands terrain and suffer penalties fighting in open plains.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Iron — the Badlands contain surface-exposed iron deposits that the Frost pushed up from below. The Ashwalkers harvest this iron through methods that would be environmentally catastrophic for a founding house to attempt but are sustainable for a small population.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Hostile. The Ashwalkers have had too many encounters with expanding houses that saw their iron and sent armies. They are not interested in negotiation by default. They will engage diplomatically only after a dynasty has refrained from attacking them for a sustained period (game time — roughly 15-20 minutes on standard speed) or has offered iron tribute in advance of requesting an audience.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** A dynasty with an Ashwalker treaty gains iron trade income, access to their raider units for hire, and a buffer zone of hostile territory around the Badlands that acts as a natural deterrent to house expansion in that direction. The raider units, when available as hired forces, are among the most effective harassment tools in the early-to-mid game.

**Cultural Identity:** They do not fear death. In combat, Ashwalker units fight to a lower morale threshold than any founding house unit — they route less easily and in some cases fight harder when their numbers are reduced, which is terrifying to face in ambush situations.

### 2. THE HOLLOWBORN

**Terrain Home:** Frost Ruins — the collapsed, ice-locked remnants of pre-Frost civilizations.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Old Light, Devout to Fervent intensity. The Hollowborn believe the ruins they live in are the sacred remains of the civilization Old Light was supposed to protect. They are custodians of a faith that promises restoration. Their religious practice involves preserving and documenting pre-Frost artifacts and structures.

**Military Specialty:** Defensive fortification. The Hollowborn have spent generations learning the structural geometry of ruined buildings and they fight behind improvised fortifications with exceptional skill. Their units have elevated defense ratings when stationed inside any ruin or fortified position. They are weak in open-field combat but nearly impossible to dislodge from held positions.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Ancient knowledge — not a resource in the traditional sense, but the Hollowborn have accumulated pre-Frost documents, architectural plans, and cultural records. A dynasty that enters a Faith Alliance with the Hollowborn gains research bonuses — reductions in the cost or time required to develop certain technologies or buildings, because the Hollowborn's archive contains prior solutions.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Wary. They have seen too many houses try to loot the ruins rather than respect them. They will engage diplomatically with any house that approaches with a Moral conviction posture, but they will immediately turn hostile toward any Cruel-conviction house that contacts them, sensing exploitation.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The research bonuses from a Hollowborn Faith Alliance are some of the most efficient development advantages available in the game. A house aligned with Old Light that also has a Hollowborn Faith Alliance gains compounding bonuses. Additionally, the Hollowborn's knowledge of Frost Ruins terrain makes them an invaluable intelligence partner — they can identify hidden passage routes through ruin zones that other dynasties cannot access.

**Cultural Identity:** They write everything down. A dynasty integrated with the Hollowborn gains a persistent historical record mechanic — events in their territory are logged and can be reviewed, providing intelligence advantages that purely reactive dynasties lack.

### 3. THE CINDERMARKED

**Terrain Home:** Sacred Ground — terrain designated by the world generation system as spiritually significant, often centered on unusual geological features or sites where Old Light or Blood Dominion rituals were performed en masse before the Frost.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Multi-covenant complexity — the Cindermarked live on Sacred Ground and have developed a syncretic faith that incorporates elements of multiple covenants without fully committing to any. They are spiritually influential but doctrinally unpredictable. They are Neutral on faith alignment, which means they respond to faith diplomacy from any covenant, but the response is muted. They are most strongly connected to The Wild, whose view of sacred natural forces most closely matches their own theology.

**Military Specialty:** Ritual combat specialists. Cindermarked warriors invoke pre-battle ceremonies that provide temporary combat bonuses to their own units and, uniquely, to allied house units fighting alongside them in the same engagement. They are not individually the strongest fighters, but a battle that includes Cindermarked participants has higher outcome variance — the ritual bonuses are powerful but unpredictable in their magnitude.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Conviction regeneration — any dynasty with an active agreement with the Cindermarked gains a slow, passive regeneration of conviction toward their baseline position. For Cruel-drifting dynasties, this is a stabilizing force. For Moral-conviction dynasties, it is a reinforcing force. This is an unusual resource — it is social and spiritual rather than material.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Neutral with a Wary secondary modifier. The Cindermarked have been approached by too many dynasties seeking to exploit Sacred Ground for resource extraction. They read intent well. A dynasty that approaches with obvious resource exploitation in mind will encounter the Wary modifier. A dynasty that approaches with demonstrated faith engagement — high faith intensity, a recent Born of Sacrifice event, or an active covenant structure — will encounter standard Neutral engagement.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The conviction stabilization bonus is uniquely valuable for houses playing aggressive or high-sacrifice strategies that tend to drift toward Cruel conviction. The ritual combat bonus for allied units can swing a contested battle. The Sacred Ground territory itself is a powerful defensive location if integrated into house territory.

**Cultural Identity:** The Cindermarked do not have a single leader. Their governance is a council of seven elders, each representing a different interpretation of their syncretic faith. Diplomatic outcomes with the Cindermarked require satisfying a threshold of the council, not a single relationship. This makes their diplomacy slower and more complex than any other tribe but also more stable once achieved — a council-approved agreement is harder to break than a personal dynasty relationship.

### 4. THE STONEGUARD

**Terrain Home:** Stone Highlands — the mountainous, high-altitude terrain that was among the most defensible during the Frost.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** The Order, Active to Devout intensity. The Stoneguard are disciplined, hierarchical, and rule-bound. The Order's emphasis on covenant structure and lawful practice appeals to a people who survived the Frost through rigid communal discipline. They enforce internal laws with documented process, have written records of agreements made and broken across generations, and hold other parties to the precise terms of agreements — no more, no less.

**Military Specialty:** Heavy defensive infantry. Stoneguard units are among the most durable tribal units in the game. They field heavy stone-and-iron equipment maintained through generations of careful craft. They fight best in highland terrain and hold chokepoints against far larger forces. They are slow and have poor offensive power in open terrain — they are defenders to their core.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Stone — a primary resource — in substantial quantities. Stone Highlands territories controlled by Stoneguard typically contain the highest-yield stone deposits on the map. A dynasty controlling or allied with the Stoneguard can field fortifications faster and at lower cost than any competitor.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Neutral, with a strong rule-bound component. They will not initiate diplomatic contact but they respond promptly and honestly to any dynasty that approaches through proper channels. "Proper channels" means a formal embassy mission rather than a military incursion followed by negotiation. A dynasty that attacks first and negotiates later will find the Stoneguard write that history down permanently — the relationship modifier from a first-strike offense against the Stoneguard does not decay and cannot be overcome except through sustained Material tribute over many game years.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The stone production benefit is the most straightforward material advantage any tribe offers. The Stoneguard's heavy infantry, available through Military Alliance, are the most effective tribal units for defensive assignments. A dynasty with Stoneguard allies defending a mountain pass can hold a strategic position that would otherwise require constant active military attention.

**Cultural Identity:** The Stoneguard keep a Book of Debts — a record of every agreement made, broken, honored, and violated across all their diplomatic relationships. When the player views their relationship with the Stoneguard, they can see the full history. Other tribes track relationship through a single modifier number. The Stoneguard show you the receipts.

### 5. THE DRIFTSONG

**Terrain Home:** River Valleys — the fertile, water-rich corridors that became arterial trade routes during the Age of Reclamation.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** The Wild, Active to Devout intensity. The Driftsong experience The Wild as the force of moving water — constant, patient, indifferent to individual ambition, powerful in aggregate. Their spiritual practice involves river rituals, seasonal offerings, and the belief that hoarding resources against a river's flow invites catastrophe. They are generous with what flows through their territory and expect generosity in return.

**Military Specialty:** River skirmishers and amphibious units. The Driftsong field light river fighters who move through River Valley terrain with significant speed bonuses and can cross river obstacles without the movement penalties that founding house armies incur. They are not powerful in open battle but they are nearly impossible to catch when defending their home river zones.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Water and food in combination — River Valley control provides both. A dynasty with a Driftsong trade relationship gains consistent food and water income that insulates them against the Long Winter event more effectively than most resource strategies.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Friendly. Of all the tribe archetypes, the Driftsong are the most naturally inclined toward diplomatic engagement. They understand trade, they benefit from it, and they have enough resource abundance in their territory that they do not feel the zero-sum resource pressure that makes other tribes defensive. However, their friendliness has limits — they respond to Cruel-conviction dynasties with wariness, and a dynasty that poisons or dams their river territory triggers an immediate and severe hostility response that overrides their Friendly baseline.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The food and water buffer against Long Winter is among the most strategically important benefits any tribe offers. Their river skirmisher units make exceptional scouts and can pursue retreating enemy forces through river terrain that other units cannot navigate effectively. A Driftsong trade agreement early in the match is often the difference between a dynasty that weathers the Long Winter comfortably and one that faces starvation.

**Cultural Identity:** The Driftsong do not build cities. They build boats. Their settlements are semi-mobile — river-edge camps that can pack up and move upstream or downstream within days. This means that sieging Driftsong territory is difficult in a conventional sense: their population moves before the siege closes. A dynasty that attempts a military takeover of Driftsong territory may capture the land and find no one there.

### 6. THE PALE REMNANT

**Terrain Home:** Tundra — the cold, featureless northern or southern extremes of the map, among the last territory any founding house would choose to expand into.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Blood Dominion, Fervent to Apex intensity. The Pale Remnant practice the most extreme form of Blood Dominion faith of any tribe or house on the map. They survived the Great Frost through sacrifice — literal sacrifice, systematic, methodical, over generations. Their faith intensity is not adopted belief. It is survivor identity. They are Fervent or Apex faith almost universally, and the Born of Sacrifice mechanic interacts with them in ways it interacts with no other faction.

**Military Specialty:** Endurance warriors. Tundra survival selects for physical and psychological hardness that no founding house fully replicates. Pale Remnant units have highest cold-terrain combat effectiveness of any unit type, do not suffer morale penalties from long campaigns (they are accustomed to suffering), and can sustain sieges and counterattacks at depleted unit strength that would break any founding house formation.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Rare animal resources — furs, hunting yields, and specific biological materials used in certain advanced Born of Sacrifice rituals. A dynasty aligned with Blood Dominion that trades with the Pale Remnant gains access to sacrifice components unavailable elsewhere, enabling higher-tier Born of Sacrifice events.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Hostile by default, but specifically responsive to Blood Dominion faith alignment. A dynasty of any other covenant will find the Pale Remnant nearly impenetrable diplomatically. A dynasty with active Blood Dominion faith and high faith intensity (Devout or above) can reach the Pale Remnant through faith channels that bypass normal diplomatic prerequisites. They are not hostile to Blood Dominion — they are simply uninterested in any other approach.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** Access to advanced Born of Sacrifice components is the primary draw. The Pale Remnant also provide intelligence about Tundra terrain that no other source can offer — their knowledge of Tundra movement, survival, and position makes them uniquely valuable for any dynasty planning operations in the far reaches of the map.

**Cultural Identity:** They practice a form of governance through sacrifice that is genuinely alarming to outside observers. Their leadership rotates not through heredity or election but through ritual sacrifice of the prior leader when the tribe decides a transition is necessary. This is not violence — it is, within their covenant structure, the highest honor. Dynasties encountering the Pale Remnant for the first time must navigate their cultural framework carefully to avoid triggering offense through misunderstanding.

### 7. THE VERDANT

**Terrain Home:** Ancient Forest — the deep, old-growth woodland territories where the canopy is so dense that the Frost had reduced impact, preserving more life through the cold than any other terrain type.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** The Wild, Devout to Fervent intensity. The Verdant are the most fully realized practitioners of The Wild faith on the map. For them, The Wild is not a theology about nature's indifference — it is a direct and personal relationship with the forest as a living entity. They speak to trees in a way that is not metaphorical within their cultural framework. The forest responds, in their understanding. They have been here longer than the houses.

**Military Specialty:** Forest ambush and trapping. Verdant units are devastating in Ancient Forest terrain and are functionally invisible to conventional scouting while inside forest zones. They use the environment as a weapon — tree falls, pit traps, animal disruption. An army moving through Verdant territory that has not secured safe passage through diplomacy or military clearance will take attrition damage before it ever engages in direct combat.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Wood in exceptional quantity, plus certain unique herbal and medicinal resources that enable healing and population health bonuses unavailable from other sources.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Neutral, but environmentally conditional. Any dynasty that begins clear-cutting Ancient Forest for wood supply triggers immediate hostility from the Verdant regardless of prior diplomatic relationship. The Verdant's relationship with a dynasty is partly measured by what that dynasty has done to forest territory — not just Verdant territory, but any forest. A dynasty with a long record of resource extraction from Ancient Forest zones will find the Verdant already hostile on first contact without any direct offense.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The wood supply is substantial and sustainable through a trade agreement, compared to the finite supply available through direct extraction. The medicinal resources provide population health bonuses that reduce plague and disease event severity. The forest ambush units, available through Military Alliance, are the single best counter-scouting and counter-expansion tool in the game — they can disrupt a dynasty's early expansion across woodland terrain entirely.

**Cultural Identity:** The Verdant do not consider themselves to own the forest. This has diplomatic implications — they will not sign a territorial transfer agreement covering Ancient Forest because in their view the territory is not theirs to give. Integration of Verdant-controlled territory requires a different approach than standard territorial diplomacy, one that treats the forest itself as a party to the arrangement.

### 8. THE DEEPKEEP

**Terrain Home:** Iron Ridges — the high, mineral-rich mountain terrain where iron deposits are most concentrated.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** The Order, Active to Devout intensity. The Deepkeep are craftspeople whose entire civilization is organized around metal production. The Order's emphasis on structure, hierarchy, and lawful covenant resonates with a culture that has maintained craft guilds and quality standards across generations of survival. They are methodical, suspicious of passion, and deeply skeptical of any faith that prioritizes sacrifice or wild abandon over deliberate practice.

**Military Specialty:** Armored infantry and siege specialists. The Deepkeep field the most heavily armored tribal units in the game — not as well-organized as a founding house's iron infantry, but individually more durable than any other tribal unit type. They also have siege knowledge that makes them effective at both attacking and defending fortified positions.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Iron in abundance — Iron Ridge territories are the richest iron zones on the map. A dynasty in trade relationship with the Deepkeep gains iron income that dramatically accelerates military development timelines.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Neutral with a craft-respect component. The Deepkeep respond positively to dynasties that demonstrate material sophistication — houses that have built advanced production structures signal to the Deepkeep that they understand the value of craft. A dynasty at early development state that attempts to trade with the Deepkeep before demonstrating production capacity will find them uninterested, not because they are hostile but because they do not yet see a peer.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The iron production benefit is the most direct military accelerant available from any tribe. A dynasty that locks in a Deepkeep trade agreement in the early game can field iron-equipped armies sooner than any competitor who lacks this relationship. The Deepkeep's armored infantry, available through Military Alliance, provide a defensive capability that is expensive but durable.

**Cultural Identity:** The Deepkeep do not accept payment in gold. Their trade relationships are material-for-material — wood for iron, stone for iron, food for iron. This creates a barter constraint that affects how a dynasty prepares to negotiate with them. A dynasty that has invested exclusively in gold-generating economic structures will find it difficult to offer the Deepkeep what they want.

### 9. THE ASHVEIL

**Terrain Home:** Coastal Zones — the littoral territories along the map's edge, where sea access creates trade and raiding opportunities unavailable to inland populations.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Old Light, Latent to Active intensity. The Ashveil carry Old Light faith in a weakened form — it sustained them through coastal survival, gave them navigation rituals and storm-prayer traditions, but they are not doctrinally rigorous. Their faith is personal and practical rather than institutional. They are not hostile to other covenants, and they have absorbed elements of The Wild (for weather and sea) without formal acknowledgment.

**Military Specialty:** Coastal raiders and marine units. The Ashveil field units optimized for coastal assault and amphibious operations. They can move military forces along coastal terrain without the road network limitations that constrain founding house armies. They are not powerful in inland combat — remove them from coastal terrain and they fight at significant disadvantage.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Trade route access — Coastal Zone control provides unique trade route options that connect to distant territories faster than overland routes. A dynasty with Ashveil cooperation gains expanded trade route options and reduced trade route establishment costs.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Friendly, with a mercenary undertone. The Ashveil are pragmatic survivors whose cultural model is exchange. They are willing to engage with nearly any dynasty but they always have a price, and they will negotiate with competing dynasties simultaneously unless formally bound by agreement. They are not treacherous — they will honor agreements — but before the agreement is signed, they are available to anyone who can pay.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The trade route advantages are compounding over a long match. A dynasty that establishes Ashveil cooperation early can build a trade network that out-produces any land-based trade strategy. Their coastal raider units are the best tool available for naval harassment and coastal landing operations.

**Cultural Identity:** The Ashveil remember every dynasty that has raided their coast. They have generational records of coastal incursions, and these records exist not for vengeance but for commerce — they know what a dynasty's aggression is worth in tribute to stop. They are the only tribe that will approach a dynasty that has attacked them with a formal offer of payment to cease, treating violence as a negotiating opening rather than an offense.

### 10. THE GREYWOOD

**Terrain Home:** Reclaimed Plains — the wide, flat agricultural territories that were the most devastated by the Frost and are now slowly being restored.

**Faith Alignment Tendency:** Old Light, Active to Devout intensity. The Greywood believe the Reclaimed Plains represent Old Light's promise of restoration — that the land will return, that what was destroyed can be rebuilt, that persistence across the Frost was not in vain. Their faith is agricultural and seasonal, tied to planting cycles, harvest festivals, and the specific rituals Old Light prescribes for land healing.

**Military Specialty:** Massed light infantry. The Greywood field more individual units than any other tribe — their plains environment supports population density that highland and forest tribes cannot match. Their units are individually weak but they come in numbers. A Greywood military engagement involves numerical pressure and attrition rather than individual unit quality.

**Resource They Protect or Control:** Food in large quantities. Reclaimed Plains have the highest food production potential on the map. A Greywood trade agreement provides food income that supports rapid population growth in a dynasty's own territories.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Friendly. The Greywood have survived through cooperation and they understand its value. They are the most straightforward diplomatic partner of any tribe — direct engagement with reasonable offers produces reliable results. They are not pushovers (they have numbers and they will defend their territory), but they are genuinely interested in stable relationships.

**What Makes Them Worth Engaging:** The food production benefit is the most directly population-accelerating benefit any tribe offers. A dynasty with Greywood food trade can sustain population growth through events that would stall other dynasties. Their massed infantry, while weak individually, can absorb punishment and create space in combined-arms situations.

**Cultural Identity:** The Greywood practice collective leadership — no single chief, decisions made by community assembly. This makes them slow to change political direction (an advantage for a dynasty that has invested in the relationship — the relationship does not flip easily) but also slow to make concessions (a disadvantage when trying to negotiate quickly in a crisis).

# SECTION C: DIPLOMACY WITH MINOR TRIBES

## Which Agreement Types Are Available

**Trade Agreement:** Yes, fully available. The most common and most accessible diplomatic tool for engaging with minor tribes. Trade Agreements with tribes provide resource income in exchange for either resource payment or gold. Every tribe archetype has a resource specialty that makes Trade Agreements with them uniquely valuable.

**Non-Aggression Pact:** Yes, available. A Non-Aggression Pact with a tribe creates a formal ceasefire that prevents both parties from initiating attacks. For a dynasty, this secures a flank without requiring the full investment of a trade relationship. For a tribe, it provides a guaranteed period of non-attack during which they can develop. Non-Aggression Pacts with tribes are shorter-duration by default than those with founding houses — tribes do not have the institutional stability to maintain long-term non-aggression without periodic renewal.

**Military Alliance:** Yes, available, but conditionally. A Military Alliance with a tribe provides the dynasty with access to tribal military units for specific engagements. Tribes do not send their forces on open-ended campaigns — they commit units to defined defensive or offensive objectives. A Military Alliance with a tribe means the tribe will send units when the dynasty calls for help in a specific conflict, not that the tribe's army becomes an auxiliary of the house. The distinction matters: tribal military contribution is event-based, not standing.

**Faith Alliance:** Yes, available, and this is one of the most powerful agreement types when faith alignment matches. A Faith Alliance with a tribe requires that the dynasty's active covenant matches the tribe's faith alignment at a minimum Active intensity on both sides. When established, a Faith Alliance provides accelerated faith intensity gains for both parties, conviction alignment benefits, and in some cases access to unique rituals or ceremonies only available to tribes of that specific faith expression.

**Marriage Agreement:** Yes, available, with unusual mechanics. A Marriage Agreement with a tribe does not follow the same dynastic bloodline structure as a Marriage Agreement between founding houses. Tribes do not have noble bloodlines in the same hierarchical sense. A tribal marriage agreement represents the joining of a dynasty's minor family member to a tribal chief's household — a genuine cultural exchange that is understood as a deep commitment. The benefit is a significant and permanent positive modifier to the tribal relationship that does not decay. The cost is that the family member integrated into the tribe is effectively gone from the dynasty's political structure, cannot be recalled, and over time adopts tribal identity. Mechanically: strongest available relationship stabilizer, but it costs a dynasty a bloodline member permanently. Some players will consider this unacceptable. Some will consider it the most elegant possible opening investment.

**Tributary Arrangement:** Yes, available, and here the direction is specific to tribal relationships. A Tributary Arrangement with a tribe typically means the tribe pays the dynasty — the dynasty extracts a portion of the tribe's resource production in exchange for formal protection. However, this is not the only direction. A dynasty that is militarily weaker than a large, hostile tribe may enter a tributary arrangement as the paying party — offering tribute to the tribe in exchange for non-aggression in a context where they cannot enforce a better outcome. The paying-dynasty arrangement is diplomatically damaging (it signals weakness to other houses) but practically useful in early-game situations where a tribe controls a strategic zone the dynasty cannot yet contest.

**Trade Route Establishment:** Yes, available, with terrain-specific benefits. A Trade Route Establishment agreement with a tribe creates a permanent trade corridor through their territory. The diplomatic value is that the dynasty gains access to territory and resources on the far side of the tribe's zone without having to conquer that territory. This is the most common use case for tribes as transit facilitation. A dynasty that needs to move trade goods or military forces through tribal territory without triggering hostility uses Trade Route Establishment as the formal agreement that grants that transit right.

## Relationship Building from Scratch

First contact with a neutral tribe is initiated by sending an envoy mission — a specific diplomatic action that costs a small gold investment and takes a defined number of game turns to resolve. The envoy reaches the tribe and initiates a first contact event. This event produces a relationship modifier based on the tribe's archetype, the dynasty's conviction posture, the dynasty's faith alignment relative to the tribe's, and any historical modifiers in effect.

Following first contact, the relationship sits at whatever modifier resulted from that event. Moving from Neutral to Cordial requires:

- A period of non-aggression (the dynasty has not attacked or raided in the tribe's vicinity)
- At least one of: a gift mission, a trade offer, or a faith engagement action if faith alignments match

Gift missions represent direct material transfer — the dynasty sends resources to the tribe with no immediate expectation of return. This is the fastest relationship builder but has a diminishing returns curve. A dynasty cannot spam gift missions to achieve Cordial status overnight. The tribe needs time to process the gesture. Gift missions have a cooldown between meaningful applications.

Trade offers represent a proposed economic relationship rather than a gift. The tribe evaluates the offer based on what is being proposed. A trade offer that does not respect the tribe's resource specialty (offering them what they have plenty of rather than what they lack) will be declined. A trade offer that genuinely meets a tribal need can move the relationship from Neutral to Cordial in a single successful negotiation.

Faith engagement actions are available when faith alignments match. These include sending a faith emissary (a clergy figure from the dynasty's covenant structure who engages with tribal religious practice directly), participating in a shared covenant ritual, or demonstrating faith intensity increase in the dynasty's own territories. Faith engagement is the fastest path to Cordial status with a tribe whose faith alignment matches.

## The Tribute System

When a founding house and a tribe enter a Tributary Arrangement with the tribe as the paying party, the mechanics work as follows:

The tribe pays a percentage of their resource specialty production each game period (measured in years within match time). The percentage is negotiated at the time of the agreement and can range from 10% (token, maintains relationship without strain) to 40% (maximum extraction, strains tribal development over time). Higher tribute rates produce more immediate resource income but accelerate tribal population decline and, past a threshold, trigger a review event where the tribe attempts to renegotiate or, if refused, transitions to Hostile.

In exchange, the dynasty provides formal protection — they commit to defending the tribe against third-party attacks. This is not symbolic. If another founding house or rival tribe attacks the tributary tribe, the dynasty is obligated to respond militarily or the Tributary Arrangement is void and the relationship drops sharply. The tributary protection obligation is a genuine military commitment.

When the dynasty is the paying party:

The dynasty pays gold or resources to the tribe each game period. The tribe commits to non-aggression and, in some Tributary Arrangements, to providing early warning of hostile forces moving through their territory. The dynasty pays the tribute at the established rate or the agreement lapses. This arrangement is primarily useful as a temporary measure during early expansion when a dynasty's military is not yet strong enough to contest a hostile tribe's territory directly.

## Tribal Integration

Full integration of a minor tribe into a dynasty is possible but requires specific conditions and a sustained investment period.

Prerequisites for integration:
- The tribe must be at Cordial or Allied diplomatic status
- The dynasty must have maintained a Trade Agreement or Military Alliance with the tribe for a minimum match-time period (approximately 20-25 match years at standard speed)
- The tribe's conviction posture must not be directly opposed to the dynasty's (a Moral-conviction dynasty cannot integrate a Cruel-conviction tribe without the tribe undergoing a conviction shift first)
- The dynasty must have territory adjacent to the tribe's home territory

Integration is initiated through a formal diplomatic action that takes significant game time to resolve. During this period, the dynasty invests resources into the tribe's settlement — upgrading their structures, integrating their economic production into the dynasty's resource network, and establishing the dynasty's governance authority over the tribe's territory.

Upon completion, a successfully integrated tribe becomes:

- Their population becomes dynasty population, contributing to the dynasty's population-based resource production and military capacity
- Their territory becomes dynasty territory, subject to the dynasty's governance and providing the relevant terrain bonuses
- Their military units are retired (they do not simply convert to dynasty units) but a portion of their warriors are absorbed into the dynasty's military structure as specialized units, maintaining some of their terrain expertise
- Their cultural identity produces a lasting modifier on the dynasty — the dynasty gains a trait associated with the tribe's cultural identity that persists for the remainder of the match. The Stoneguard bring their record-keeping. The Verdant bring forest affinity. The Driftsong bring river navigation knowledge.

A failed integration attempt — where the dynasty initiates but does not meet the conditions, or where a competing dynasty disrupts the process — produces a major hostility response from the tribe and cannot be reinitiated for a sustained period.

## Tribal Military Contribution

Tribes will contribute military forces to a dynasty in three specific contexts:

**Allied Tribe Contribution:** A tribe at Allied diplomatic status (the highest relationship tier) will respond to a direct military call for aid when the dynasty is under attack. They send a force proportional to their size to assist in the defense of dynasty territory adjacent to their own. This response is not unlimited — they will not cross the map to defend distant dynasty territory, and they will not respond to the same call more than once per conflict cycle without renewed diplomatic investment.

**Hired Defense:** Any tribe at Cordial or above diplomatic status can be hired to provide defensive support for a specific territory or objective. This requires a gold payment that scales with the tribe's size and the duration of the requested commitment. Hired defense is temporary and ends when the agreed period expires. Unlike Allied Contribution, the tribe does not need to be under attack — they provide hired defense as a pure service.

**Faith Alliance Military Support:** A tribe in a Faith Alliance with the dynasty will contribute military forces to actions that directly defend or advance the shared covenant's interests. If the dynasty is engaged in a conflict that has been designated as a faith conflict (defending covenant territory, responding to faith persecution, participating in The Great Reckoning against a hegemon of opposing faith), the tribe will contribute forces without requiring the standard gold payment. This is the only context where tribal military forces are fully free to the dynasty.

## What Causes Tribal Hostility

A tribe that has a positive diplomatic relationship with a dynasty will move toward hostility under the following conditions:

**Direct Military Attack:** The dynasty attacks the tribe's territory, units, or population. Immediate transition to Hostile regardless of prior relationship level.

**Raiding Behavior:** The dynasty's military units operate in the tribe's territory in a raiding posture (destroying resources, taking population) without formal declaration. If not addressed with a diplomatic apology mission and reparations within a defined period, this transitions to Hostile.

**Agreement Breaking:** The dynasty breaks a formal agreement — fails to pay tribute, fails to honor a Military Alliance obligation, abandons a Trade Agreement without proper diplomatic termination. The severity of the hostility response scales with which agreement was broken (breaking a Military Alliance obligation while the tribe is under attack is the worst possible offense).

**Faith Persecution:** The dynasty persecutes practitioners of the tribe's covenant. Tribes whose faith alignment is Active or stronger will immediately respond to faith persecution of their covenant by transitioning toward Hostile, even if they have no other grievance. This is not negotiable — a dynasty cannot compensate a tribe for persecuting their faith through other means.

**Conviction Crossing:** A dynasty whose conviction posture shifts into Cruel territory will experience a relationship degradation modifier with all tribes that have a Moral conviction posture. This degradation is passive and ongoing — as long as the dynasty remains in Cruel conviction, the relationship with Moral tribes slowly erodes. It will not immediately trigger Hostile but will eventually.

**Environmental Destruction:** Specific tribes (the Verdant, the Driftsong) are triggered by environmental actions that other tribes would not notice. Clear-cutting Ancient Forest or damming/polluting river zones will trigger hostility from these tribes regardless of prior diplomatic investment.

**Long Winter Desperation:** During the Long Winter event, tribes have a passive hostility tendency that overrides their normal diplomatic posture if they fall below a survival threshold. A tribe that is starving during the Long Winter may raid dynasty territory even if they are at Cordial relationship status. This is not a diplomatic offense they initiate — it is a survival response. A dynasty that has ensured the tribe has food supply through their Trade Agreement (food or water resource specialty) will not trigger this response.

## The Faith Alliance with Tribes — Specific Dynamics

When a dynasty's active covenant matches a tribe's faith alignment at a minimum of Active intensity on both sides:

The Faith Alliance option appears in the diplomatic menu and requires a formal establishment action. Once established:

- Both parties gain faith intensity regeneration — a slow movement toward the next tier of intensity, accelerated beyond what normal covenant practice provides alone.
- The dynasty gains access to the tribe's faith-specific ritual or ceremony. Each tribe archetype has a unique ceremony available only through their Faith Alliance. These ceremonies have match-meaningful effects: the Hollowborn ceremony provides a research breakthrough on a specific technology, the Pale Remnant ceremony enables a higher-tier Born of Sacrifice event, the Stoneguard ceremony provides a permanent population loyalty bonus in a specific territory.
- The tribe will not engage with any diplomatic contact from a dynasty of an opposing covenant while the Faith Alliance is active. This provides a diplomatic exclusivity benefit — a competing house that shares the opposing covenant cannot build a relationship with this tribe while the Faith Alliance holds.
- Conviction alignment between the dynasty and the tribe trends toward convergence. If the dynasty is Neutral conviction and the tribe is Moral, the Faith Alliance creates a passive Moral-direction pressure on the dynasty's conviction posture. This is subtle but real over a long match.

# SECTION D: MINOR TRIBES IN THE POLITICAL EVENTS SYSTEM

## The Great Reckoning

The Great Reckoning is triggered when a dynasty achieves hegemony threshold — territorial and military dominance that threatens all other houses simultaneously. The event calls for a coalition response.

Minor tribes participate in The Great Reckoning under specific conditions:

A tribe will join the anti-hegemon coalition if:
- The hegemon has previously broken an agreement with that tribe
- The hegemon has committed faith persecution against the tribe's covenant
- The tribe is at Allied status with any dynasty that joins the coalition
- The hegemon's expansion has consumed or threatens the tribe's home territory
- The tribe's conviction posture is Moral and the hegemon is operating at Cruel conviction

A tribe will not join the coalition regardless of other factors if:
- The tribe is in a current, active Tributary Arrangement as the paying party to the hegemon (they are effectively bound by that arrangement until it lapses)
- The hegemon has an active Faith Alliance with the tribe
- The tribe's size is Small and the hegemon is large enough that the tribe's survival calculus pushes toward appeasement

Tribes that join the coalition contribute their military forces in the same manner as allied military contribution — they send a force proportional to their size that operates independently but directionally aligned with the coalition. They do not come under any coalition member's direct command. They are fighting for their own survival and interests, which happen to align with the coalition's goal.

A dynasty that has cultivated good relationships with multiple tribes before The Great Reckoning triggers will find those tribes appearing in the coalition as a force multiplier. This is one of the strongest arguments for tribal investment across the early and mid game.

## The Trueborn Summons

The Trueborn Summons is the Trueborn City's activation of its world-knowledge and diplomatic role — a formal convening of all factions for a world-level resolution event.

Minor tribes respond to the Summons based on:
- Their relationship with the Trueborn City (all tribes have an independent relationship with the Trueborn — see Section F)
- Their current diplomatic postures with founding houses
- Their faith alignment relative to the Summons' stated purpose

Tribes with a positive Trueborn relationship and a Cordial or above status with at least one founding house will send a representative to the Summons. They do not send military forces — they send a cultural ambassador who participates in the Summons' resolution mechanic. Tribal participation in the Summons contributes to the outcome's legitimacy. A Summons that has tribal participation produces more durable resolution outcomes than one that does not. Houses that have invested in tribal diplomacy effectively have additional voting weight in the Summons outcome.

Tribes with a hostile relationship to the Summons' calling dynasty, or with no Trueborn relationship, may actively disrupt the Summons by refusing to send representatives and, in some cases, by conducting raids during the Summons period when other factions' military attention is directed elsewhere.

## Faith Persecution Events

When a founding house initiates a formal Faith Persecution event — suppressing active practitioners of a specific covenant in their territory — the effects propagate outward beyond the dynasty's borders.

Tribes whose faith alignment matches the persecuted covenant respond in graduated stages based on intensity:

- Tribes at Latent intensity: passive relationship degradation, no active response.
- Tribes at Active intensity: the tribe's diplomatic posture toward the persecuting dynasty immediately shifts one tier toward Hostile. If already Hostile, they begin providing intelligence and passive military support to any dynasty opposing the persecutor.
- Tribes at Devout intensity: the tribe issues a formal condemnation, which is visible to all players and creates a reputational modifier for the persecuting dynasty. The tribe enters a non-aggression suspension — they will not honor any existing agreements with the persecuting dynasty.
- Tribes at Fervent or Apex intensity: the tribe transitions to active hostility and may begin raiding the persecuting dynasty's territory. If the persecuting dynasty has a strong Trueborn connection, the Trueborn City may intervene diplomatically to contain the escalation. If not, the tribal raid response can become a sustained insurgency in the persecuting dynasty's territory.

Tribes of the covenant being strengthened by the persecution (the dynasty is persecuting Old Light while practicing Blood Dominion) gain a faith intensity increase from the event. Persecution of opposition covenants strengthens the persecutor's faith ecosystem. This is the mechanic that makes faith persecution strategically attractive for faith-committed dynasties despite its significant diplomatic costs.

## The Long Winter

The Long Winter is a map-wide famine event that depletes food and water resources across all territory types. All factions are affected but the severity varies by terrain.

Minor tribal behavior during the Long Winter:

Tribes whose resource specialty is food or water (the Driftsong, the Greywood) are least affected. They may actually gain diplomatic leverage during the Long Winter as their resource production makes them uniquely valuable.

Tribes whose resource specialty is non-food (the Stoneguard, the Deepkeep, the Ashwalkers) enter a survival stress state. Their population declines if the Long Winter is sustained. Their diplomatic behavior becomes less reliable — they may renegotiate existing agreements downward (asking for less tribute-out or more support-in) or, if pushed past survival threshold, initiate raiding.

Tribal raiding during the Long Winter is not the same as hostile military action. It is desperation behavior. A dynasty that has provided food support to a tribe before the Long Winter hits (through an active Trade Agreement involving food resources) will not be raided by that tribe. A dynasty that has ignored tribal food needs and then finds tribal raiders hitting their supply lines during the Long Winter has experienced a preventable consequence.

Tribes that cannot find food support and reach a collapse threshold during the Long Winter may combine with another tribe as a survival mechanism, creating an unusually large merged entity. This merged tribe represents a significant threat after the Long Winter ends — a large, recently starved, survival-hardened tribal force that may push into founding house territory as the Long Winter's agricultural damage makes even developed territories temporarily vulnerable.

## Refugee Crisis

When a dynasty experiences severe internal disruption — a crushing military defeat, a plague event, a forced population displacement through occupation — refugees flee the affected territory.

Refugees from a dynasty's territory may seek shelter with a nearby tribe if:
- The tribe is geographically adjacent to the affected territory
- The tribe is not actively hostile to the dynasty (hostile tribes will not shelter the dynasty's population)
- The tribe has population capacity (a Small tribe may not be able to absorb significant refugee influx)

Tribes that shelter refugees gain a population increase — a meaningful benefit for tribes whose size category might otherwise limit their development. The sheltered refugees carry the dynasty's faith alignment and conviction posture, which creates internal cultural complexity in the receiving tribe. Over match time, a tribe that has absorbed significant refugees from a specific dynasty will shift slightly toward that dynasty's faith and conviction attributes.

For the dynasty: the refugees are lost to immediate production and military capacity but they are not dead. A dynasty that later reconciles with a tribe housing its refugees may negotiate their partial or full return as part of a diplomatic resolution, recovering some of what was lost. This creates a genuine strategic reason to maintain relationships with tribes near territory that might become contested.

# SECTION E: TRIBAL MILITARY UNITS

## Design Philosophy

Tribal military units should feel irregular, terrain-adapted, and dangerous in the right context. They are not weaker versions of founding house units — they are differently capable. A founding house army is organized, equipped, and sustained by a political and economic infrastructure that has been building for eighty years. A tribal force is improvised, resilient, terrain-read, and motivated by survival rather than strategy. The unit design should make this tangible.

General parameters: Tribal units have lower armor ratings than iron-equipped founding house units but higher terrain-specific combat multipliers. They rout less easily in their home terrain but more easily in unfamiliar terrain. They cost no production resources to recruit (they are fielded by the tribe, not trained by the dynasty) but they are not unlimited — the tribe has a finite military capacity.

---

### UNIT: RIDGE STALKER

**Terrain Affinity:** Stone Highlands, Iron Ridges

**Combat Profile:** Light offensive unit with high movement in highland terrain. Attacks from elevation deal bonus damage. Attacks from lowland against highland position deal penalty damage. Defense is moderate — the Ridge Stalker fights by choosing engagement conditions, not by absorbing hits.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** A dynasty that positions this unit on elevated terrain before an engagement begins has effectively established a damage-multiplied firing line. Against founding house infantry advancing uphill, Ridge Stalkers are disproportionately effective. They counter the tendency of founding house players to advance their main force without considering elevation. Recruiting them for a mountain pass defense is one of the most cost-efficient defensive investments available.

---

### UNIT: BOG CREEPER

**Terrain Affinity:** River Valleys, Badlands (wetland sections)

**Combat Profile:** Ambush specialist. Normal combat strength is low. When attacking from a concealed position (riverbank cover, marshy terrain) against an unsuspecting enemy, the Bog Creeper's first-strike damage is significant. After the initial strike, they are outmatched in sustained combat and designed to disengage. Their movement through water terrain incurs no penalty.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** Bog Creepers are the best early-warning disruption unit available to any dynasty with River Valley or wetland expansion ambitions. Deployed ahead of a main army's approach, they identify enemy positions and pick off scouts. As a recruited unit in a dynasty's light force composition, they serve as the advance element that prevents the opposing army from knowing where the main force is until engagement.

---

### UNIT: ASHBORNE RAIDER

**Terrain Affinity:** Badlands, Frost Ruins

**Combat Profile:** Medium offensive unit built for extended independent operation. Higher than average hit points relative to other tribal units. Does not require supply line proximity as long as they are operating in Badlands or Frost Ruins terrain (they are self-sufficient in their home environment). Attack strength is above average, defense is below average — they engage and disengage rather than holding lines.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** The supply independence in their home terrain is the distinctive mechanic. A founding house army chasing an Ashborne Raider force into Badlands encounters a unit that can sustain an indefinite harassment campaign without the logistics vulnerability that founding house units carry. Recruiting them gives a dynasty a strike force that can operate far from supply infrastructure, which is uniquely useful in late-game extended campaigns.

---

### UNIT: THORNWALL DEFENDER

**Terrain Affinity:** Ancient Forest, Reclaimed Plains (tree-cover sections)

**Combat Profile:** Defensive unit with exceptional fortification skill. When stationed behind a prepared position — a natural tree line, an improvised barricade, a ruin wall — the Thornwall Defender's defense rating is among the highest of any non-house unit. Outside prepared positions, they are mediocre. They move slowly and do not pursue.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** The prepared position defense is the counter to mass unit assault that founding houses tend to rely on. A dynasty that deploys Thornwall Defenders in chokepoints forces any attacker to either find an alternate route or pay the cost of a frontal assault against a defense rating that exceeds what the attacker's units expect. Recruiting them is primarily useful for a defensive dynasty that intends to hold territory rather than project force.

---

### UNIT: TUNDRA ENDURER

**Terrain Affinity:** Tundra, Frost Ruins

**Combat Profile:** Heavy infantry with cold-terrain bonuses. In Tundra terrain, damage output and defense are both elevated significantly. In any other terrain, combat effectiveness drops to below founding house standard. Morale is essentially non-existent as a limiting factor — Tundra Endurers do not rout except at near-zero unit strength. They are the last unit type to break.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** The no-rout mechanic is uniquely dangerous in the late game when founding house armies rely on routing mechanisms to convert numerical advantage into practical advantage. A force containing Tundra Endurers forces the attacker to inflict casualty-based attrition rather than morale-based rout, which takes longer, costs more, and denies the attacker their fastest-win scenario. Recruiting them for Tundra or adjacent operations is the clearest force multiplication available for cold-terrain campaigning.

---

### UNIT: RIVER LANCER

**Terrain Affinity:** River Valleys, Coastal Zones

**Combat Profile:** Mounted light cavalry variant — not standard horse cavalry but mounted on river-adapted animals with enhanced amphibious movement. Fast, capable of crossing rivers without penalty, able to raid supply lines and flanking positions with minimal engagement time. Combat strength in direct confrontation is low — they are raiding units, not fighting units.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** The river crossing without penalty is the decisive characteristic. Founding house cavalry cannot cross rivers without bridge infrastructure or significant movement cost. River Lancers make river crossings tactical opportunities rather than logistical obstacles. A dynasty with River Lancers in their composition can threaten flanking approaches that the opposing army has assessed as protected by water terrain.

---

### UNIT: CANOPY HUNTER

**Terrain Affinity:** Ancient Forest exclusively

**Combat Profile:** Ranged specialist optimized for forest combat. In Ancient Forest terrain, the Canopy Hunter is effectively invisible to standard scouting and deals full damage from concealment. Against scouted or detected enemies in forest terrain, their damage drops significantly — they rely entirely on not being seen. Outside forest terrain, they are nearly useless.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** In the hands of a dynasty defending Ancient Forest territory, Canopy Hunters create a zone where any advancing army takes damage from sources it cannot locate. The combat is not about winning engagements — it is about making the advance so costly in attrition that the attacker recalculates whether the territory is worth taking. As a recruited unit, they are highly situational but nearly unbeatable in their specific context.

---

### UNIT: PLAINS RUNNER

**Terrain Affinity:** Reclaimed Plains, River Valleys

**Combat Profile:** Light fast infantry. Highest movement speed of any tribal unit. Combat strength is the lowest of all tribal unit types — they are not fighters. They are messengers, scouts, and skirmishers who apply harassment damage and retreat before engagement becomes sustained. Mass deployment creates numerical pressure through sheer unit count.

**What Makes Them Worth Facing or Recruiting:** Plains Runners are the cheapest and most numerous tribal unit available. Recruited through a Greywood Military Alliance, a dynasty gains access to a disposable forward force that extends their scouting radius significantly and forces the enemy to respond to multiple simultaneous light contact points rather than a single advance. They are not meant to win individual engagements — they are meant to occupy enemy attention while the dynasty's main force moves.

# SECTION F: THE TRUEBORN CITY — SPECIAL TRIBE STATUS

## Framework Application

The Trueborn City occupies the structural position of a major minor tribe — it is a non-player entity with territory, population, military capacity, diplomatic relationships, and faith neutrality. Applying the minor tribe attribute framework to the Trueborn City with elevated parameters explains its unique world-stabilizing role in mechanical terms rather than simply treating it as a special exception.

## Trueborn City Attributes

**Size:** Exceptional. The Trueborn City is the largest non-player entity on the map by population count, significantly exceeding any minor tribe. Its territory is the central neutral zone, comprising a single high-value city territory with exceptional resource density. It cannot expand territorially — this is a hard constraint, not a circumstantial limitation. The Trueborn City's territorial boundary is fixed, which is part of why founding houses allow it to persist.

**Faith Alignment:** Formally Neutral across all four covenants. The Trueborn City maintains active diplomatic and scholarly relationships with Old Light, Blood Dominion, The Order, and The Wild simultaneously. This is not doctrinal ambiguity — it is deliberate institutional policy. The Trueborn City's role as a world knowledge conduit requires that it remain accessible to all covenant practitioners. Their internal religious practice is a private matter kept entirely out of external relations. No dynasty can form a Faith Alliance with the Trueborn City. They respond positively to all covenants without exclusive alignment.

**Conviction Posture:** Institutionally Neutral with a Moral-direction tilt that they do not formally acknowledge. The Trueborn City's governance produces outcomes consistent with Moral conviction (they protect refugees, maintain diplomatic neutrality even at cost, refuse to participate in faith persecution regardless of which covenant is targeted) but they do not describe their governance as Moral. They describe it as neutral process. In practice, a dynasty with Cruel conviction will find the Trueborn City's diplomatic responses cooler and more formal than a Moral-conviction dynasty would.

**Terrain Home:** The Trueborn City occupies a unique terrain designation — Trueborn Central — that is functionally Sacred Ground with elevated attributes. Its geographical position at the map's center is not accidental. World generation places the Trueborn City at the geographic intersection of the most terrain types, giving it naturally defensible geography while making it equally accessible from all directions.

**Resource Specialty:** World knowledge — the Trueborn City's resource is information, diplomatic access, and institutional memory. Every founding house that maintains a relationship with the Trueborn City gains a passive intelligence benefit: improved visibility on events across the map, reduced fog of war in specific circumstances, and advance notice of certain political events. This is the resource that makes the Trueborn City worth engaging with beyond mere neutrality. It is also the resource that makes it dangerous — the Trueborn City knows things, and what it knows it can choose to share or withhold.

**Diplomatic Baseline:** Universally Cordial as a starting position. Every founding house begins the match at Cordial status with the Trueborn City. This is not earned — it is institutional. The Trueborn City has extended diplomatic recognition to all nine founding houses as a condition of the current political order. From Cordial, a house can move to Allied through sustained diplomatic investment and contribution. A house can also move toward Hostile through specific offenses — most notably, attacking the Trueborn City's territory, breaking agreements made under Trueborn City auspices, or committing atrocities that the Trueborn City's institutional memory formally records and circulates.

**Military Character:** The Trueborn City fields no military force of its own in the conventional sense. It has Wardens — a defensive force of exceptional individual quality that protects its territory. The Wardens do not project force beyond Trueborn City territory. They do not participate in founding house conflicts. They defend the central zone against any aggressor with equal response regardless of which house is attacking. The Wardens are not tribal military units — they are institutional guardians. Any founding house that attacks the Trueborn City's territory will face Wardens who fight with above-standard capability and trigger a global diplomatic response in which all other founding houses receive a notification of the attack and an implicit opportunity to respond.

**Cultural Identity:** The Trueborn City remembers everything. Its institutional memory is the defining characteristic. Every diplomatic agreement ever made, every atrocity ever committed, every political event ever resolved — the Trueborn City has records. These records are not shared automatically. They are available to dynasties that maintain strong Trueborn relationships, and they can be selectively released as diplomatic tools. The Trueborn City does not leverage this memory aggressively — it does not blackmail or threaten. But a dynasty that knows the Trueborn City has the evidence of their ancestors' agreement-breaking, and that the City is in relationship with their current rival, will factor that into their decision-making.

## The Trueborn City's World-Stabilizing Role — Mechanical Expression

The Trueborn City's neutrality is not passive. It actively works to prevent any single dynasty from achieving hegemony before the political process of The Trueborn Summons can address it. Its specific world-stabilizing mechanisms:

**The Summons Trigger:** When a dynasty approaches hegemony threshold, the Trueborn City can initiate the Trueborn Summons event. This is not an automatic trigger — it requires that the Trueborn City maintain Allied status with at least two other founding houses. A hegemon that has isolated the Trueborn City diplomatically before reaching the hegemony threshold has disabled this mechanism.

**The Neutral Corridor:** The Trueborn City's central territory creates a physical separation between opposing houses' expanding territories. A house on one side of the map cannot move military forces to attack a house on the opposite side without either negotiating Trueborn passage or going around — adding significant movement time to any cross-map campaign.

**The Intelligence Symmetry Doctrine:** The Trueborn City shares intelligence with all houses at the same tier of relationship. It does not provide exclusive intelligence to favorites. A house that achieves Allied status with the Trueborn City gets the full intelligence benefit, but so does any other house that achieves Allied status. This creates a structural incentive for all houses to invest in Trueborn relations, rather than allowing one house to monopolize the information advantage.

**The Trueborn Veto:** For agreements made under Trueborn City auspices — a specific diplomatic service the City offers where it witnesses and formally recognizes a bilateral agreement between two founding houses — breaking that agreement produces a more severe Trueborn relationship penalty than breaking any other agreement. Dynasties that want stable long-term agreements formalize them through the Trueborn. Dynasties that want to preserve breakout flexibility avoid Trueborn formalization. This creates a meaningful choice in agreement-making with strategic implications.

## Minor Tribe Relationships with the Trueborn City

Every minor tribe has an independent relationship with the Trueborn City that operates parallel to their founding house relationships.

The Trueborn City's universal Cordial baseline extends to tribes as well. Every tribe begins the match at Neutral (not Cordial — the Trueborn does not extend automatic diplomatic recognition to tribes, only to founding houses). But the path from Neutral to Cordial for a tribe is shorter than the path from Neutral to Hostile.

Tribes with high faith intensity in any covenant receive passive positive Trueborn relationship modifiers — the Trueborn City values covenant-committed actors, regardless of which covenant, because covenant commitment produces predictable behavior.

Tribes with institutional memory of a specific founding house's grievance can register that grievance with the Trueborn City through a diplomatic action. This does not produce immediate consequences but creates an official record that other founding houses can access through their Trueborn intelligence benefit. It is how a tribe makes their grievance known to the entire political order without having the military capacity to enforce redress directly.

A tribe at Allied status with the Trueborn City gains one unique benefit available to no founding house: protection from tribal absorption. A founding house cannot integrate a Trueborn-allied tribe by force. Forced integration of a Trueborn-allied tribe triggers the same global diplomatic response as attacking the Trueborn City's territory directly. This makes the Trueborn City relationship uniquely valuable to tribes as a sovereignty protection mechanism — and creates a dynamic where smaller, weaker tribes have a strong incentive to invest in Trueborn relations as their primary protection against house expansion.

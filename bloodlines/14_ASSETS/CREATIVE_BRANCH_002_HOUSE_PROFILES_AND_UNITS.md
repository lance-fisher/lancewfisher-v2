## House Trueborn

**Hair color:** Silver

**Strategic archetype:** Diplomatic broker — the house that wins without being the most powerful, by being the most necessary

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5 (neutral — Trueborn militias are no better or worse than any other, which is philosophically intentional)
- Swordsmen: 5/5
- Spearmen: 5/5
- Hunters: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Trueborn scouts range farther, gather more intelligence, and are trained to act on it)
- Bowmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Trueborn ranged units are trained to hold ground and protect assets, not to push lines)

**Unique unit: The Mediator**
Category: Support. A mounted civilian-soldier hybrid — not a fighter in the traditional sense, but a figure of institutional authority whose presence on or near the battlefield has measurable effects on morale and negotiation. The Mediator can enter diplomatic contact with neutral settlements to convert them to Trueborn alignment without combat, and in the presence of two clashing armies can briefly suppress aggression in a small radius (reducing incoming attack speed for nearby enemy units). They are extremely fragile and cannot survive extended combat — their value is entirely situational and positional.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Trueborn players prioritize expansion breadth over depth. They establish contact with neutral settlements early, securing resource access points rather than building military infrastructure. Their opening investment is heavily weighted toward the Hunters line — rapid map knowledge is their early advantage. They rarely commit to aggressive first strikes. They prefer to let other houses bloody each other while consolidating soft control over unclaimed territory.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Diplomatic broker — Territorial Governance (broad soft control, not hard conquest)
- Dynastic Prestige (Trueborn's lineage is culturally treated as the reference point for legitimacy; they accumulate prestige more cheaply through neutral conversions and political marriages)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Neutral. Trueborn's cultural identity is rooted in arbitration and balance. Their institutional memory tells them that cruelty destabilizes and virtue is expensive — the pragmatic middle is where power is sustainable. Players who push toward either extreme are diverging from the house's natural grain, which is both possible and interesting.

**Flavor text:** Trueborn is the house everyone tolerates and nobody fully trusts. Their silver-haired emissaries appear at the edges of conflicts they didn't start, offering terms that favor everyone slightly less than they'd hoped for but considerably more than continued fighting would yield. Other houses call them brokers to their faces and schemers in private, which is accurate in both cases. Playing Trueborn feels nothing like playing any other house — you are not building the biggest army or the most profitable economy. You are building a web. Your Hunters know the map better than anyone else by the time the first real battles happen, and that knowledge is currency. You convert villages with words, not swords. You absorb other houses' conflict as opportunity. When Trueborn wins, it wins late, quietly, and with minimal casualties, which other houses find deeply offensive. The house that was never the most dangerous somehow ended up owning the most territory. The silver hair on a Trueborn lord signals to every table: someone here is doing the math you aren't.

## House Highborne

**Hair color:** Darker gold with highlights

**Strategic archetype:** Faith anchor — the house most deeply invested in ideological infrastructure, and the one that gains the most from an early faith commitment

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5
- Swordsmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Highborne swords are household guards as much as soldiers, trained to protect, not press)
- Spearmen: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Highborne spear formations are ceremonially aggressive, trained to advance on sacred ground)
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 5/5

**Unique unit: The Cantor**
Category: Support. A faith practitioner embedded in the military structure, not a combat unit but a force multiplier. The Cantor moves with armies and periodically performs rites that restore a small amount of morale to nearby units and temporarily reduce the Conviction cost of aggressive actions (the house can fight more fiercely without paying the full Conviction toll it would otherwise incur). The Cantor is killed easily but is difficult to reach inside a formation. Their value compounds over long engagements.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Highborne players build faith infrastructure faster than anyone else. Their second or third construction priority is typically a shrine or faith gathering site rather than additional military production. They are not passive — their ceremonially trained Spearmen are slightly better in the early skirmish — but their core intention is to arrive at the faith divergence point (Level 3 unlock) ahead of the field. A Highborne player who reaches faith-specific units first has a significant window of advantage.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Faith Divine Right (the most natural and reinforced path — Highborne gets more from faith infrastructure than any other house)
- Dynastic Prestige (their bloodline is perceived as ritually significant, which translates to prestige accumulation through faith acts)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Moral. Highborne culture reads virtue as a strategic investment — the faith systems reward it, and Highborne's institutional identity is built around the idea that legitimate power requires moral justification. Players who go Cruel with Highborne are fighting their own house's grain at every step.

**Flavor text:** Highborne believes it was chosen before the war began. Their darker gold hair carries the weight of divine appointment in their own cultural mythology — they are the original custodians of the faith traditions, the house whose ancestors first interpreted the signs, first built the shrines, first named the gods. Whether any of this is true is irrelevant to how they operate. Highborne players feel a constant pull toward ritual investment, and when that investment pays off — when the faith units unlock and the Cantors are singing in the middle of a desperate battle — there is nothing in the game quite like it. Other houses fight better with more soldiers. Highborne fights better with more belief. The gold-haired lords who appear at diplomatic summits arrive with ceremonial gravitas that makes even experienced opponents feel like they're being assessed, not negotiated with. This is entirely intentional.

## House Ironmark

**Hair color:** Black

**Strategic archetype:** Industrial military — the forge-house, the house that converts raw material into military supremacy through production efficiency and attrition pressure

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Ironmark militias come from the forge culture, where physical labor and fighting are not far apart)
- Swordsmen: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Ironmark steel is better even at Level 1, before iron infrastructure is fully built)
- Spearmen: 5/5
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 5/5

**Unique unit: The Axeman**
Category: Infantry. A heavy two-handed axe fighter drawn from Ironmark's forge labor class — the men and women who swing heavy tools all day adapted that strength into a weapon. The Axeman is slower than Swordsmen but hits harder on each strike, excels at breaking armored formations and destroying structures, and is almost impossible to route quickly because of their physical conditioning and cultural pride in holding ground. They are not versatile — they are bad against fast cavalry and poor in open-field pursuit — but inside chokepoints, against walls, and against heavy infantry, they are exceptional. The Axeman is the reason Ironmark siege timelines are shorter than any other house.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Ironmark players build production infrastructure before military numbers. They invest in iron access early — even before iron is technically critical at Level 1, having the infrastructure ready when Level 2 unlocks is worth the opportunity cost. Their opening army is usually smaller than their opponents but hits harder. Ironmark players tolerate early pressure because they know the midgame belongs to them if they survive it.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Military Conquest (the most direct and reinforced path — Ironmark's production advantage becomes overwhelming if the match extends into the iron era)
- Economic Dominance (Ironmark's forge infrastructure, if not devoted entirely to military production, generates trade goods that translate to gold accumulation)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Neutral leaning Cruel. The forge culture does not romanticize war, but it does not flinch from it either. Ironmark players who optimize for military output find that the most efficient path involves choices the game codes as Cruel — burning, forcing, overwhelming. The house's institutional memory is not morally squeamish. Players who run a virtuous Ironmark are playing against the current, which is possible but requires conscious redirection.

**Flavor text:** Ironmark smells like charcoal and heated metal. Their black-haired lords wear their forge-dust as a mark of status rather than hiding it — the house that makes weapons does not pretend it doesn't. In the field, an Ironmark army looks slightly smaller than it should for the resources spent on it, and then it hits an enemy formation and the numbers stop mattering. Their Axemen are terrifying not because they are fast or elegant but because they do not stop. Other houses produce armies. Ironmark produces tools that happen to fight. The efficiency of their production chain means that a late-game Ironmark player who has survived the early skirmish period is operating with a kind of compound interest no other house can match. You fight Ironmark knowing that every engagement you don't win quickly is an engagement you are slowly losing.

## House Goldgrave

**Hair color:** Bright gold

**Strategic archetype:** Economic powerhouse — the house that turns resource accumulation into military and political leverage, and that fights best when it has spent generously on everything else first

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5
- Swordsmen: 5/5
- Spearmen: 5/5
- Hunters: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Goldgrave hunters are traders and surveyors as much as scouts, trained to avoid conflict and return with information)
- Bowmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Goldgrave ranged units are hired professionals, which means they are reliable under pressure but not willing to be martyrs)

**Unique unit: The Coin Sergeant**
Category: Support / Infantry hybrid. A mercenary coordinator who enables Goldgrave to temporarily hire neutral or rogue units from the map pool — independent skirmishers, sell-swords, and displaced fighting men who will work for gold but not for loyalty. The Coin Sergeant does not fight directly but increases the gold cost efficiency of unit production and can field a small number of hired auxiliaries in a given match that bypass standard tech tree progression. These hired units are weaker than equivalent native units, more expensive per kill, and cannot be used to garrison structures — but they can absorb pressure and screen Goldgrave's core economy during vulnerable transitions.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Goldgrave players expand resource nodes aggressively and convert gold surplus into infrastructure early. They do not open with a military rush. They open with a trade. Every neutral settlement converted in the first fifteen minutes is a tax base, not just a population source. Goldgrave's food and water infrastructure is usually ahead of the field because they understand that economic throughput depends on population, and population depends on sustenance. They will have more gold than anyone by the midgame — what they do with it determines whether they win.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Economic Dominance (the most direct and reinforced path — Goldgrave's entire architecture points here)
- Territorial Governance (wide resource control is the foundation of Goldgrave governance, and they accumulate territory efficiently through economic leverage rather than military conquest)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Neutral. Goldgrave has no ideological center of gravity — the house's culture is transactional. They are not cruel because cruelty is bad business, and they are not virtuous because virtue is expensive. They sit in the practical middle and optimize for throughput. Players who push toward either conviction extreme are making a cultural statement that the house's institutional identity doesn't naturally support, though the game does not prevent it.

**Flavor text:** Bright gold hair on a Goldgrave lord is not coincidence — the house has cultivated its appearance as a brand signal for so long that the symbolism has become biological fact, or close enough that no one remembers a distinction. Goldgrave plays differently from every other house because the question the player is always answering is not "how do I win this fight" but "how do I make this fight unnecessary." Their armies are expensive and professionally assembled, not numerous and conscripted. When Goldgrave does commit military force, it is because the economic calculation has tipped to the point where investment in violence is the highest-return option available. This makes them feel dangerous in a way that purely military houses don't — when they come for you, it means someone has done the math and your continued existence was the losing position. Other houses fight out of ambition, necessity, or faith. Goldgrave fights because the contract finally ran out.

## House Stonehelm

**Hair color:** Brown

**Strategic archetype:** Defensive fortress — the house that builds terrain advantages into an economy of attrition, and that wins by outlasting rather than overwhelming

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Stonehelm militia are wall-garrison trained before they're field-trained)
- Swordsmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Stonehelm swords are trained in the context of defending positions, not pressing them)
- Spearmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — see unique unit below for why Stonehelm's Spearmen are distinctive)
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Stonehelm bowmen are wall archers before they are field archers)

**Unique unit: The Rampart Spear (named variant of the standard Spearman)**
Category: Infantry. Stonehelm's Pikeman variant is not a separate unit class — it is an institutional upgrade to the standard Spearman line that reflects Stonehelm's generational investment in fortress defense doctrine. The Rampart Spear carries a longer weapon than the standard Spear, is trained to brace against cavalry charges (reducing cavalry attack effectiveness significantly on contact, unlike standard Spearmen who reduce it only moderately), and can anchor a defensive line against multiple attackers simultaneously. They are noticeably slower than standard Spearmen and perform poorly in open pursuit — they are not meant to chase. They are meant to be the thing that cavalry breaks against. In a Stonehelm garrison, three Rampart Spears hold a wall that would require twice as many standard defenders.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Stonehelm players build toward stone infrastructure faster than any other house. Their first investment after basic military production is typically a fortification or watchtower network that gives them map vision and defensive chokepoint control. They expand slowly and deliberately, establishing defensible perimeters rather than soft-contact resource webs. Their early game can look passive to an opponent who doesn't understand the house — by the time conflict arrives, the Stonehelm player has often built a position that costs three times as much to attack as it would have cost to build.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Territorial Governance (wide defensive control of territory — Stonehelm accumulates governance points through fortified settlements that are expensive to contest)
- Military Conquest (Stonehelm's conquest is slow and methodical — they take a position, fortify it, move forward, take another position — but it is very difficult to reverse)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Moral. Stonehelm's culture is built around protection — of the population, the settlement, the bloodline. This has a natural virtue lean because the house's institutional identity frames duty as the highest obligation. Stonehelm players who go Cruel are often using their fortresses not to protect their people but to trap them, which the game's systems reflect in subtle population morale penalties.

**Flavor text:** Stonehelm does not win battles. Stonehelm wins sieges — the ones it fights and the ones it never has to. Their brown-haired lords are the least dramatic figures at any political table, which other houses consistently mistake for weakness until they try to take a Stonehelm fortification and discover that the cost calculation has been done already, the chokepoints have been chosen deliberately, and the Rampart Spears have been waiting in position since before the attack was decided. Playing Stonehelm is an exercise in patience and spatial thinking. You are always asking: where do I want the fight to happen, and what do I need to build to make the fight happen there instead of anywhere else? When the answer is ready, Stonehelm is nearly impossible to dislodge. Other houses get larger. Stonehelm gets deeper. The difference only becomes clear at the moment of contact.

## House Westland

**Hair color:** Vivid red (from Westgrave lineage)

**Strategic archetype:** Military aggressor — the frontier house, built for speed, pressure, and the psychological weight of an army that arrives before it was expected

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Westland frontier militias are irregulars who grew up in contested territory and fight offensively by instinct)
- Swordsmen: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Westland swords are trained in aggressive open-field fighting, not defensive formations)
- Spearmen: 6/4 (more offensive, less defensive — Westland spears are used as a shock weapon, not a wall; they press forward rather than holding)
- Hunters: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Westland hunters are often the first combatants in a conflict, not just scouts)
- Bowmen: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Westland bowmen are trained to advance under fire, not hold position)

**Unique unit: The Outrider**
Category: Cavalry / Ranged hybrid. The Outrider is a light mounted archer who predates Westland's formal cavalry infrastructure — a scout and skirmisher who learned to fight on horseback in the frontier years before anyone built stables. They require minimal iron investment compared to full cavalry (they are lightly armored and their horses are unshod by choice, for silence), arrive earlier in the tech progression than standard cavalry, and are extremely fast. They cannot stand in direct combat against armored infantry. They harass, kite, threaten supply lines, and intercept retreating units. The Outrider is the reason Westland pressures the map faster than any other house.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Westland players attack something. Not always the strongest target, but something, because early pressure is the house's core strategic philosophy. The Outriders come online faster than any other cavalry equivalent, and they immediately begin disrupting opponent expansion plans. A Westland player in the first fifteen minutes is either attacking a neutral settlement before anyone else reaches it or threatening an opponent's resource lines. They do not build defensively. Defense, for Westland, is a temporary condition while preparing the next offensive.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Military Conquest (the most direct and reinforced path — Westland's entire stat profile and unique unit point to aggressive territory acquisition)
- Territorial Governance (Westland acquires territory through force, which means governance accumulates as a byproduct of conquest rather than as a deliberate strategy)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Cruel leaning. Frontier culture does not reward restraint — it rewards decisiveness. Westland players who optimize for aggression find that the most efficient choices are consistently coded as Cruel by the game's Conviction system. Burning a village to deny it to an opponent is a Westland instinct. This makes Westland naturally one of the more Conviction-costly houses to play, which creates an interesting pressure over long matches.

**Flavor text:** The vivid red hair of a Westland soldier is visible across a battlefield, which Westland commanders use as a psychological tool rather than trying to hide it. They want to be seen. They want the opponent to know they're already there, already moving, already deciding. Westland's Westgrave lineage comes from the border territories where the law was always three days' ride away and the only reliable truth was what you could hold with your own hands. That cultural inheritance shapes how Westland plays at every level: faster decisions, more offensive posture, genuine discomfort with waiting. Playing Westland is the most demanding and most viscerally satisfying experience the game offers for players who think in attacks rather than positions. The Outriders are on the map before your opponent has finished building their second structure. The question is not whether Westland pressures early — it is whether the pressure translates to long-term advantage or burns the house out by midgame. That is the Westland problem. It is a very interesting problem.

## House Hartvale

**Hair color:** Orange (from Hartborne lineage)

**Strategic archetype:** Population engine — the house that treats human capital as its primary resource and builds military and economic strength through demographic depth rather than specialist quality

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5 (neutral, but Hartvale produces Militia faster and more cheaply than any other house)
- Swordsmen: 5/5
- Spearmen: 5/5
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Hartvale bowmen are positioned as community protectors, trained to cover retreats and guard population centers)

**Unique unit: The Verdant Warden**
Category: Support / Infantry hybrid. A frontier steward who combines agricultural expertise with light military capability — someone who manages Hartvale's rural resource zones and can defend them when threatened. The Verdant Warden cannot be garrisoned in military buildings — they belong to population and economic structures. They increase food and water output in the zone they occupy, speed up population recovery after battles, and can fight at approximately 60% of a standard infantry unit's effectiveness. They are not a combat specialist but they are never unarmed. In Born of Sacrifice terms, Verdant Wardens represent the house's institutional investment in keeping the population itself — not just the army — capable of contributing to defense.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Hartvale players build population infrastructure before military infrastructure. Granaries, wells, and settlement expansion happen first, military production second. The result is a house that enters the midgame with more population than anyone else — which translates, through the Born of Sacrifice system, into a larger army generation cycle. Hartvale's armies at Level 1 are not better than opponents' — they are more numerous and replaced faster. This population surplus is Hartvale's foundational advantage and its primary resource to exploit.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Economic Dominance (population depth drives food surplus, which drives trade capacity and economic throughput)
- Dynastic Prestige (Hartvale's large population base means more Bloodline Members can be fielded and advanced, accumulating prestige through sheer volume of dynastic activity)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Moral. Hartvale's culture treats the population as its charge — the house is organized around the idea that the rulers exist to sustain the people, not the reverse. This creates a strong institutional pull toward Moral Conviction decisions. Hartvale players who go Cruel are systematically undermining their own population base, which the game's systems penalize in subtle but compounding ways.

**Flavor text:** Hartvale's orange-haired lords are the least martial-looking leadership in any room they enter, which is almost certainly why they keep winning conflicts they had no business winning. The house's Hartborne lineage comes from the river-valley agricultural communities where the measure of a great lord was how many people ate well, not how many enemies died quickly. Playing Hartvale feels slow at first — you are building granaries and wells when other houses are building barracks — and then suddenly, around the middle of the first iron era, you look at your population counter and understand that you have been compounding the whole time. Hartvale armies are not elite. They are continuous. Every generation of soldiers that cycles back into population through the Born of Sacrifice system comes back faster than any other house's equivalent cycle. The Verdant Wardens are the mechanical expression of this philosophy: even the people tending the fields are ready to defend them. Hartvale's opponents often win every individual engagement and still find themselves losing, because the thing they are fighting is not an army. It is a civilization.

## House Whitehall

**Hair color:** Neutral brown

**Strategic archetype:** Adaptive generalist — the house with no dominant path, no obvious weakness, and the most room for player expression; the house that wins through reads on the opponent rather than execution of a fixed plan

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5
- Swordsmen: 5/5
- Spearmen: 5/5
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 5/5

All Whitehall Level 1 units are precisely at the neutral baseline. This is intentional and meaningful — see flavor text.

**Unique unit: The Liaison Officer**
Category: Support. A military diplomat who specializes in inter-house intelligence — a figure trained to read opposing army compositions and relay tactical assessments in real time. Mechanically, the Liaison Officer reveals a wider radius of opponent unit stat information than the normal fog-of-war allows (not positions, but unit type compositions in engaged or recently-engaged formations), and provides a small morale bonus when Whitehall units are fighting a type of enemy formation they have previously engaged (representing adaptive learning across the match). The Liaison Officer is fragile, non-combatant, and high priority target for opponents who understand what they're facing.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Whitehall players do not have a default opening. They read the map, read their opponents, and make a decision in the first five minutes about which strategic lane to pursue for the match. This adaptive capacity is the house's defining characteristic. A Whitehall player who commits to an economic opening looks like Goldgrave. A Whitehall player who commits to military pressure looks like Westland but without the inherent stat advantages. The difference is that Whitehall can switch lanes at lower cost than any other house — their infrastructure is not optimized for one path in a way that creates stranded assets if the path changes.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- All five victory conditions are viable for Whitehall. The house has no dominant path and no closed path. This is the point.

**Conviction natural tendency:** Neutral. Whitehall has no institutional conviction gravity. Players who want to express a strong moral or cruel identity will find the house accommodating rather than resistant, but it provides no pull in either direction. Whitehall is the purest expression of player agency in the game's conviction system.

**Flavor text:** Whitehall is the house other houses underestimate and then study. Their neutral brown hair and absence of obvious iconographic drama make them easy to overlook in the pre-match assessment — no axe symbolism, no forge culture, no sacred lineage mythology — and this is precisely the advantage. Whitehall lords learned long ago that being underestimated at the start of a negotiation is worth more than arriving with obvious credentials. The house's perfectly neutral baseline statistics are not a development placeholder. They are a philosophical statement: Whitehall does not pre-commit to a shape. It becomes the shape the situation requires. Playing Whitehall is the highest-ceiling experience in the game for experienced players because every decision is genuinely open — there are no stat incentives pushing you toward cavalry or away from faith investment, no cultural grain to fight or follow. The Liaison Officer exists not because Whitehall needs intelligence to compensate for weakness, but because a house built on adaptability runs better with good information than anyone else. When Whitehall wins, other houses are always slightly unsure exactly how it happened.

## House Oldcrest

**Hair color:** Black/gray/pepper

**Strategic archetype:** Dynastic anchor — the oldest lineage, the house most invested in bloodline depth, prestige accumulation, and the political weight of institutional age

**Level 1 unit stat deviations from 5/5 baseline:**
- Militia: 5/5
- Swordsmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — Oldcrest household swords are trained as protectors of lineage before they are trained as soldiers)
- Spearmen: 5/6 (slightly more defensive — same institutional priority)
- Hunters: 5/5
- Bowmen: 6/5 (slightly more offensive — Oldcrest bowmen are the one unit type that trends offensive, reflecting a tradition of ranged precision as a noble skill)

**Unique unit: The Oathkeeper**
Category: Infantry / Support hybrid. A veteran soldier bound to the Oldcrest bloodline by a named oath — not a mercenary, not a conscript, but a personal retainer whose combat capability is enhanced by proximity to Bloodline Members. An Oathkeeper fighting in a unit commanded by or adjacent to an Oldcrest Bloodline Member gains a significant combat bonus (representing the oath activation — they fight as though their soul depends on it, because institutionally it does). Away from Bloodline Members, they fight at standard levels. This creates a powerful mechanic around Bloodline Member positioning — Oldcrest's hero-equivalent anchors are literally making the army around them stronger, and opponents must choose between targeting Bloodline Members to deactivate the bonus or targeting the Oathkeepers directly.

**Opening game tendency (first 15 minutes):** Oldcrest players invest in Bloodline Member development earlier and more heavily than any other house. Their first advanced building is often the structure that enables Bloodline Member experience accumulation (whatever this system is called at the prototype stage). Their standing army is typically slightly smaller than opponents in the early game because resources are going to Bloodline Member infrastructure. By the midgame, when Oldcrest Bloodline Members are meaningfully more advanced than other houses' equivalents, the compound advantage of the Oathkeeper bond begins to pay off.

**Strongest victory paths:**
- Dynastic Prestige (the most direct and reinforced path — Oldcrest accumulates prestige through bloodline depth faster than any other house)
- Faith Divine Right (Oldcrest's institutional age gives them a legitimacy claim within faith systems that other houses cannot manufacture)

**Conviction natural tendency:** Moral leaning. Oldcrest's dynastic culture treats the bloodline as a sacred trust — the current generation holds the lineage in custody for all future generations. This frames political and military decisions in terms of what the lineage can sustain, which creates a natural pull toward measured, sustainable choices coded as Moral by the game. Oldcrest players who go Cruel are making choices their institutional identity frames as betrayals of the lineage — the house's systems reflect this in bloodline reputation mechanics.

**Flavor text:** Oldcrest does not need to tell you it is old. The black-gray-pepper hair of its lords is the biological record of a lineage that has been producing leaders since before most other houses were farming the same plot of ground consistently. There is a weight to Oldcrest that other houses try to manufacture with ceremony and that Oldcrest simply has. Playing Oldcrest is a slow accumulation of compound advantages that are individually small and collectively decisive. The Oathkeepers are the most mechanically intimate unit in the game — they are literally measuring how important your Bloodline Members are by how much better they fight near them. A skilled Oldcrest player positions Bloodline Members with military precision, not just as hero anchors but as force multipliers for every Oathkeeper in range. When Oldcrest wins the Dynastic Prestige condition, opponents often feel like they have lost to time itself rather than to a player. Which is precisely how Oldcrest has always understood victory.

# SECTION B: LEVEL 2 UNIT ROSTER — FULL DESIGN

**Context:** Level 2 represents the iron transition era. Faith is not yet locked in — these are universal units available to all nine houses. All nine houses apply their stat deviations to these units just as they do to Level 1 units. Animal husbandry infrastructure is required for cavalry units. Iron is the critical new resource introduced at this tier. These units are designed as natural predecessors to the faith-specific Level 3 units already established in canon.

**PROPOSED / CREATIVE BRANCH — NOT YET CANON**

---

## Unit 1: Iron Guard

**Category:** Infantry
**Iron cost:** Medium (iron-reinforced armor and weapons require significant smelting investment)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 6/6

**Role:** The Iron Guard is the backbone of any Level 2 army — a professional heavy infantryman who has transitioned from the improvised arms of the survival era to proper iron equipment. They are versatile front-line fighters who hold a formation while the more specialized units around them do their work. They are not the most offensive unit at Level 2 nor the most defensive — they are the unit you put in the center because it will still be standing when the flanks are resolved.

**Upgrade path:** Iron Guard can advance toward the Iron Legionary (Old Light / The Order, light doctrine) or the Warden-Enforcer (Old Light / The Order, dark doctrine) at Level 3 — both faith paths represent a deepening of the institutional soldier identity, one toward disciplined honor, the other toward enforced order.

**Tactical note:** Excellent against standard infantry and holds well against cavalry charge. Poor at pursuing retreating units (heavy armor) and less effective against large numbers of light skirmishers who refuse to engage directly.

---

## Unit 2: Mounted Scout

**Category:** Cavalry
**Iron cost:** Low (the Mounted Scout is lightly armored and rides an unshod horse; speed over protection)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 5/4

**Role:** The Mounted Scout is the first cavalry unit available at Level 2 — a fast, fragile rider whose value is intelligence and harassment rather than combat. They can cross the map in half the time of any infantry unit, reveal large areas of fog-of-war, intercept supply movements, and chase down fleeing units. They are not built to engage formed infantry. They are built to make the opponent uncertain about where the next threat is coming from.

**Upgrade path:** The Mounted Scout is the general predecessor to all Level 3 cavalry. Faith divergence determines which cavalry line they advance into — Blood Rider (Blood Dominion, dark doctrine), Bear Rider (The Wild, dark doctrine), or Thornrider (The Wild, light doctrine). The scout who commits to a faith tradition becomes something with much sharper edges.

**Tactical note:** Best used in pairs or small groups for harassment and map control. Catastrophically fragile when caught in a pincer by infantry — a Mounted Scout surrounded by Spearmen is a dead Mounted Scout. Never send them in first against a formed line.

## Unit 3: Ironclad Rider

**Category:** Cavalry
**Iron cost:** High (full iron plate on horse and rider is the most iron-intensive unit at Level 2)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 7/6

**Role:** The Ironclad Rider is the heavy cavalry unit of the iron transition — armored, powerful, and deliberately paced. Where the Mounted Scout is speed and uncertainty, the Ironclad Rider is impact. They are built to break infantry lines through direct charge, to crush light units under weight, and to serve as the centerpiece of an offensive cavalry formation. They require animal husbandry infrastructure at a higher tier than the Mounted Scout — proper stabling, ironshoe infrastructure, and trained horse handlers — which means they are a midgame investment, not an opening move.

**Upgrade path:** The Ironclad Rider advances toward the Blood Rider (Blood Dominion, dark doctrine) — the faith path that takes the cavalry warrior's power and channels it through covenant obligation. An Ironclad Rider who chooses another faith path will find their equivalent in whatever mounted warrior form that faith produces, though Blood Dominion is the most direct thematic continuation of the Ironclad identity.

**Tactical note:** Devastating against light infantry and ranged units in the open. Extremely vulnerable to Rampart Spears (Stonehelm unique), chokepoints, and terrain that breaks the charge momentum. Slowest cavalry unit in the game. Once committed to a charge, the rider has limited ability to redirect.

## Unit 4: Crossbowman

**Category:** Ranged
**Iron cost:** Medium (iron bolts and iron reinforcement of the bow mechanism)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 6/5

**Role:** The Crossbowman replaces the intuitive craft of the Level 1 Bowman with mechanical precision — a ranged unit that trades rate of fire for armor penetration and consistent damage at medium range. Where Bowmen can volley quickly and are best in numbers, the Crossbowman is a specialist who can punch through shields and light armor that Bowmen cannot reliably threaten. They are slower to load than Bowmen but each shot carries more consequence. Against armored targets at Level 2, they are the premier ranged solution.

**Upgrade path:** Crossbowmen can advance toward the Flame Warden (Old Light, light doctrine) — the faith path that takes precision and discipline and converts it into something ceremonially devastating — or toward the Inquisitor (Old Light, dark doctrine), where the mechanical precision of the weapon becomes an instrument of something more purposeful and darker.

**Tactical note:** Excellent against armored infantry and static formations. Poor against fast-moving cavalry who can close the distance before reloading. Needs infantry protection. Should never be positioned where cavalry can reach them without going through a defensive line first.

## Unit 5: Siege Engineer

**Category:** Support
**Iron cost:** High (iron fittings on siege equipment)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 3/4

**Role:** The Siege Engineer is not a fighter — they are a builder and a breaker. They construct and operate the basic siege equipment that becomes critical once Stonehelm fortifications and other defensive structures become factors in a match. At Level 2, the siege equipment available through the Engineer is modest — rams, basic bolt-throwers, reinforced ladders — but they represent the first systematic answer to the fortification game that the defensive houses are playing. Without a Siege Engineer, taking a fortified Stonehelm position is a matter of attrition and sacrifice. With one, it becomes a solvable problem.

**Upgrade path:** The Siege Engineer's path forward is less about faith divergence and more about which aspects of their utility get amplified. Directionally, the engineering tradition feeds into the heavier siege capabilities of The Order's doctrine, where systematic application of force is a governing philosophy.

**Tactical note:** Highest-priority target for defenders in any siege scenario. Must be protected by infantry at all times. Catastrophically bad in open-field combat. The Siege Engineer is what you bring when you know you're attacking something built by Stonehelm.

## Unit 6: Covenant Aspirant

**Category:** Support
**Iron cost:** None (their value is not material)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 4/5

**Role:** The Covenant Aspirant is a pre-faith unit — a figure in the religious infrastructure who is working toward commitment to a faith tradition but has not yet crossed the threshold into doctrine. They function as a faith accelerant: their presence in an army or settlement increases the rate at which Conviction is generated and the rate at which faith infrastructure reaches Level 3 unlock. They can perform minor rites that provide small morale bonuses to nearby units. They are not yet the Covenant Priest that Blood Dominion will eventually produce — they are what comes before. All faith paths flow through an aspirant phase.

**Upgrade path:** The Covenant Aspirant advances directly into whichever faith tradition the player commits to at Level 3. Blood Dominion light doctrine produces the Covenant Priest from this predecessor. The specific path depends on player faith choice, not unit type.

**Tactical note:** Keep them behind the line, near your highest-morale units. Their effect on Conviction accumulation is most valuable in long engagements where the Conviction cost of aggressive decisions would otherwise compound. Against opponents who know what they are, they are always targeted first — protect them accordingly.

## Unit 7: Forest Tracker

**Category:** Ranged / Support hybrid
**Iron cost:** None (they reject iron-age trappings by inclination)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 5/5

**Role:** The Forest Tracker is the Level 2 evolution of the Level 1 Hunter — not a Bowman's upgrade but a wilderness specialist who has deepened their relationship with terrain rather than adopting iron infrastructure. They move through forest, marsh, and rough terrain at full speed without penalty (other units take movement penalties in these zones), can set ambushes that give the first strike advantage to units they are leading, and reduce the fog-of-war in natural terrain significantly. They are moderate fighters but excellent force multipliers for armies that want to use terrain actively rather than simply march through it.

**Upgrade path:** The Forest Tracker advances toward the Thornrider (The Wild, light doctrine) and precedes the Bear Rider (The Wild, dark doctrine) thematically — both Level 3 units represent a full commitment to the wild faith tradition that the Forest Tracker is approaching. The Tracker who chooses The Wild and takes the light path becomes someone who rides and commands the land's defenses. The dark path produces something more predatory.

**Tactical note:** Dramatically more valuable on maps with significant terrain complexity. In open-field matches, they are merely competent. In forested or river-valley maps, a Forest Tracker leading an ambush can swing an engagement entirely. Pair them with Mounted Scouts for a harassment doctrine that keeps opponents permanently uncertain about where the threat is.

## Unit 8: Armored Spearman

**Category:** Infantry
**Iron cost:** Medium (iron-tipped spears and partial iron armor)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 5/7

**Role:** The Armored Spearman is the direct evolution of the Level 1 Spearman into the iron era — heavier, more durable, and specifically trained to anchor defensive lines against cavalry charges. Where the Level 1 Spearman reduces cavalry effectiveness, the Armored Spearman nearly nullifies it in a braced formation. They are slower than their Level 1 predecessor and less useful in offensive operations, but in a defensive line they are the most iron-efficient unit available for holding ground against cavalry-heavy opponents. Stonehelm's Rampart Spear variant is drawn from this base unit.

**Upgrade path:** The Armored Spearman feeds into the Warden-Enforcer (The Order, dark doctrine) — the faith path that takes the disciplined defensive line and converts it into something with punitive intent — and to a lesser degree toward the Iron Legionary (The Order, light doctrine), whose formation tactics descend from the Armored Spearman's defensive discipline.

**Tactical note:** Essential against cavalry-heavy opponents. Poor in offensive roles — do not use them to press a line, use them to hold one. Vulnerable to ranged harassment if not protected by their own ranged units or positioned behind terrain. Excellent in chokepoints.

---

## Unit 9: Warband Captain

**Category:** Infantry / Support
**Iron cost:** Low (personal iron equipment; the value is not material but organizational)
**Baseline Off/Def:** 6/5

**Role:** The Warband Captain is a battlefield leader below the level of a Bloodline Member — a professional soldier who has demonstrated enough competence and authority to coordinate units in combat. They do not function as a Bloodline Member substitute, but they provide a leadership aura that increases the combat coordination of units within a moderate radius (reducing friendly unit response time when orders change, slightly increasing attack consistency). In large engagements where Bloodline Members cannot be everywhere, Warband Captains fill leadership gaps. They are also the units most likely to generate candidates for eventual Bloodline integration — the prestige path for exceptional Captains is one of the rarer ways a bloodline can absorb new blood.

**Upgrade path:** The Warband Captain's upgrade path depends on faith commitment — they can advance toward leadership roles within any of the four faith traditions at Level 3, serving as the organizational backbone of whatever doctrine the house adopts.

**Tactical note:** Most valuable in mid-to-large engagements where the player is managing multiple unit groups simultaneously. In small skirmishes, their leadership bonus is underutilized. They fight reasonably well but should not be used as primary assault units — their value is in the units around them, not in their individual combat performance.

## TRUEBORN

**Before The Frost:**
The Trueborn were not one house among many. They were the house. In the Age Before, the word "Trueborn" was not a family name but a title — to be Trueborn was to be recognized by blood and law as the legitimate ruler of the whole of the known world. Every other house existed within a structure they defined and administered. The Trueborn did not merely govern; they were the logic of governance itself. Their courts set the calendar, ratified marriages, settled territorial disputes, determined who was noble and who was not. Other houses built armies; the Trueborn built the system those armies answered to.

This made them simultaneously irreplaceable and deeply resented. You cannot rebel against rain. You can only endure it or leave.

**During The Frost:**
The Great Frost did not merely humble the Trueborn. It annihilated them. They lost more people, more infrastructure, more institutional memory than any other house — because they had more of all three things to lose. The sprawling capital, the archive cities, the courts, the roads they maintained — all of it collapsed in the first forty years. The silver-haired lines that had produced governors and judges and administrators for a thousand years died in refugee columns, in failed harvests, in the wars that erupted when the system that had kept the peace vanished overnight.

The Trueborn survived by doing something they had never had to do before: choosing neutrality. The last bloodline strong enough to hold the name retreated to a single fortified city — the one surviving remnant of what they had been — and declared that they would not take sides in the wars that others were fighting to survive. They would administer. They would arbitrate. They would be the one place in the world where agreements could be witnessed and honored. They survived because every faction, however desperate, understood that having a neutral party was more valuable than having one more enemy.

They lost their empire in the Frost. What they gained was something more durable: a myth. The Trueborn City became the idea that civilization had not entirely died.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Trueborn occupy a position of enormous symbolic power and military fragility. Their city is a monument to what was, staffed by descendants of administrators who have not governed anything larger than their walls in three generations. They hold neutrality like a religion — publicly. Privately, they watch every other house with the careful attention of people who believe, with total certainty, that they should be running all of this.

They want the sovereignty seat. Of course they do. It is, by their understanding of the world, theirs by right of blood and history. But they are not fools. They know that marching out to claim it would break the neutrality myth that keeps them protected, and they would be destroyed. So they play a longer game. They observe. They position. They wait for the moment when the other houses, exhausted by their wars, turn to them and ask for the Trueborn to restore order.

They are afraid they have waited too long. They are afraid some other house will claim the seat before the invitation comes. They are afraid that eighty years of neutrality has made their bloodline soft.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Trueborn story is about stewardship. They did not lose the empire — they preserved it. They held the seed of civilization through the long winter so it could bloom again. When they rule once more, it will not be conquest. It will be restoration. They are not a faction; they are the return of something that was temporarily interrupted. This is not self-deception entirely. It is also a warning to anyone paying attention. The Trueborn do not think they are trying to win. They think they are trying to fix a mistake.

**Their wound:**
The Trueborn City contains, in a sealed vault beneath the old court, the genealogical record of every noble line in the world before the Frost. They know who everyone was. They remember who was above whom. They remember the promises that were made and broken. They carry the entire social order of the dead world in their archives, and no one else can read it. This is their wound: the knowledge of what was taken, itemized and complete, with no means yet to reclaim it.

## HIGHBORNE

**Before The Frost:**
The Highborne were the Trueborn's most trusted intermediaries in matters of faith and doctrine. In the Age Before, when the Trueborn administered law and territory, the Highborne administered meaning. They staffed the temples, trained the clergy, interpreted scripture, and sat on the councils that decided which doctrines were orthodox and which were heresy. They were not priests themselves — they were the institution that produced priests. The distinction matters. They never entirely believed. They understood belief as a tool of social cohesion, and they were exceptionally good at wielding it.

This gave them a unique relationship with the Trueborn: close enough to be trusted, far enough to be useful. They held no armies, because they did not need armies. Their power was the power of legitimacy — the ability to declare that a coronation was valid, that a marriage was sanctioned, that a war was just. They were the endorsement that made other powers legible.

**During The Frost:**
When the Trueborn system collapsed, the Highborne faced a crisis that had no precedent in their experience: people needed meaning without the institutions that had previously supplied it. The Frost was not merely physical. It was epistemic. The gods the Highborne had so carefully administered suddenly needed to be real, not merely useful, because nothing else was left.

The Highborne adapted. They split. One faction doubled down on institutional authority — trying to maintain the old hierarchies of doctrine even in the absence of the empire that had given those hierarchies force. Another faction went the other direction entirely, stripping back to the rawer, older faith traditions that the official doctrine had always partially suppressed, finding that in a world of dying people, unmediated access to something sacred was more valuable than refined theology. Both factions survived the Frost. They reconverged, changed, at the other end. The Highborne who emerge into the Reclamation are people who understand, in their bones, that faith is both a system and a fire — and that you have to manage both.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Highborne are the most religiously sophisticated house in the world. They can speak fluently in the language of any of the four covenants. They can frame any political goal as a matter of doctrine. They understand the leverage that faith gives — over soldiers, over the common people, over other houses that need the legitimacy stamp the Highborne can offer.

What they want is the sovereignty seat, yes — but more precisely, they want to shape whoever sits on it. They have a theory of governance that has been refined over centuries: the sovereign rules the physical world; the faith shapes what the sovereign believes about the physical world; therefore, whoever controls doctrine controls the sovereign. They have not abandoned this theory just because the empire collapsed. They are rebuilding it.

They are afraid of the Wild covenant, which answers to no human institution, and of the Blood Dominion, which has its own ideas about what faith demands. They are afraid of a world that becomes purely material — a world where the question of who has the biggest army is the only question.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Highborne believe they are the stewards of civilization's interior. Anyone can build walls. Anyone can forge weapons. The Highborne maintain something harder to rebuild once it is lost: the sense that any of this means anything. They frame this as service, and they are not wrong. They also use it as leverage, and they are not wrong about that either. Their self-narrative is essentially true and essentially convenient, which makes it more dangerous than a lie.

**Their wound:**
During the Frost, a generation of Highborne clergy made a doctrinal decision that still haunts them — they formally endorsed a series of resource allocations by the nascent leaders of the Reclamation that resulted in the deaths of a significant number of commoners who were deemed less essential to survival. They called it necessary. They wrote it down in language designed to make it sound inevitable. The document survives. They know it survives. They do not know who else knows. They have been managing this secret, and the guilt underneath it, for three generations.

## IRONMARK

**Before The Frost:**
The Ironmark were the Trueborn Empire's manufacturing engine. They did not build beautiful things. They built things that worked. Bridges, weapons, siege equipment, the ironwork that held the great public buildings together — all of it passed through Ironmark hands and Ironmark forges. They were respected the way infrastructure is respected: absolutely necessary, largely invisible, rarely thanked.

Their relationship to the Trueborn was transactional and clear. The Trueborn provided protection and contracts; the Ironmark provided product. They were not close. They were not hostile. They understood each other. The Ironmark had no interest in governance, no patience for the rituals of court, and no love for the Highborne's theological machinery. They believed in things you could hit with a hammer and find out whether they worked.

**During The Frost:**
The Frost revealed the iron deposits — and the Ironmark were the only house that knew what to do with them. When the world collapsed and everyone else was fighting over food and shelter, the Ironmark recognized that the geological violence the Frost had caused was exposing something useful. They went into the earth. They dug. They built underground workshops and blast furnaces and tunnel systems that became, by accident, some of the most defensible positions in the world.

The axe became their symbol during this period — not because of war, but because of work. The axe was the tool that cut timber for the tunnels, shaped the ore, cleared the frozen earth. By the time the Frost lifted, the Ironmark had built something no one else had: industrial capacity. They came up from the ground with more iron than anyone and a workforce that had been conditioned, over 170 years, to value productivity above everything else.

They lost warmth. There is no other word for it. The underground generations were hard people who had survived by being useful. Sentiment was a luxury they had buried with the first generation.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Ironmark are the most economically potent house in matters of war materiel, and they know it. Every other house needs weapons. Every other house needs fortification. The Ironmark supply these things — not as a favor, but as a negotiation. They trade in iron and obligation in equal measure.

What they want is independence. The Age Before made them servants of the Trueborn system. The Frost made them survivors. The Reclamation has made them powerful in their own right, and they have no interest in returning to a world where they are merely the empire's forge. Whether they want the sovereignty seat is genuinely uncertain — they might want to own the seat's supplier rather than sit in it. A sovereign who depends on Ironmark iron is a sovereign who listens to Ironmark opinions.

They are afraid of scarcity — specifically, of the deposits running dry before the Reclamation is complete. The iron veins that made them powerful are finite. This knowledge drives them harder than any other house understands.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Ironmark story is simple, which is part of its power: they built what everyone else used. Every victory anyone won in the last two hundred years had Ironmark iron in it somewhere. The world owes them. Not gratitude — they are not sentimental about gratitude. But acknowledgment. The world should know whose hands made it.

**Their wound:**
During the Frost, the Ironmark made a deliberate decision to stop manufacturing weapons for the surface-side wars and focus entirely on underground survival infrastructure. In the short term, this was rational. In the long term, it allowed a war between two neighboring populations to continue far longer than it would have otherwise, because the side with access to Ironmark weapons eventually withdrew supply, and the resulting prolonged conflict killed tens of thousands of people who might have survived a faster resolution. The Ironmark did not cause this war. They also did not stop it when they could have. This is not something they discuss.

## GOLDGRAVE

**Before The Frost:**
The Goldgrave were the Trueborn Empire's merchant class elevated to nobility. They did not start as a great house — they earned the designation through three generations of financial dominance so complete that the Trueborn had no practical choice but to formalize what already existed. By the late Age Before, the Goldgrave controlled the trade routes, the credit networks, and the currency valuation mechanisms that made the empire function at the scale it did.

Other houses found this arrangement offensive. The Trueborn needed it to work. The Goldgrave understood that resentment was simply the price of being indispensable.

Their relationship with every other house was financial before it was anything else. They had allies where they had investment. They had enemies where the debts had gone bad. They were not cynical about this — they genuinely believed that trade was the highest form of civilization, that two houses doing business together were less likely to go to war than two houses doing nothing together, that wealth was peace by another name.

**During The Frost:**
The Goldgrave nearly did not survive. Their power was relational — they existed as the nexus of a network, and when the network collapsed, the nexus was nothing. The first forty years of the Frost were catastrophic for them. The trade routes became death marches. The credit networks became meaningless. The currency they had spent generations managing became fuel for fires.

They survived by pivoting completely. A faction of the Goldgrave recognized that trade in material goods was impossible, but trade in information was not. They became the Frost's most sophisticated information brokers — selling intelligence about who had food, where water sources were, which routes were passable, which factions were worth allying with. They rebuilt their network on the only currency that still worked in a starving world: knowing things other people did not know.

By the time the Frost lifted, the Goldgrave had rebuilt their network — smaller, harder, more information-dense. They emerged with fewer people and more institutional memory about the surviving world than anyone else.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Goldgrave are the wealthiest house in liquid terms — they move faster than anyone because they understand markets, and markets are recovering faster than anyone expected. They have investment in almost every other house's infrastructure, by design. They prefer this position. A debtor is an ally with better leverage.

What they want is stability. Specifically, the kind of stability in which trade can continue, debts can be honored, and the Goldgrave's financial architecture remains the skeleton beneath the political flesh. They do not particularly care who sits on the sovereignty seat, provided that person understands which house manages the treasury.

They are afraid of war — not because they are cowards, but because war destroys the infrastructure of commerce and nobody wins the kind of war they can see coming if the houses cannot find an arrangement. They are also afraid, in a way they do not entirely admit, that the information they sell is becoming a liability. Some of what they know about each house, if revealed to the wrong person, would start fires they cannot put out.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Goldgrave story is that they are the reason civilization exists at all. Not the Trueborn, who governed — governing is just administration. Not the Ironmark, who built — building is just labor. The Goldgrave connected things. Without connections, the best governing in the world produces nothing. Without trade, even the greatest forge starves. They are the circulatory system. You can live without a lot of things. You cannot live without blood moving.

**Their wound:**
During the Frost, a senior Goldgrave patriarch made a decision to withhold information about a safe food cache from a neighboring population in exchange for their land deed, which he needed for a future trade route. The population did not starve — they found another source — but the decision was made. It was discovered, eventually, by one of his own descendants, who has been carrying the knowledge of what their family is capable of for thirty years. This descendant is alive at match start. What they do with this knowledge is one of the most volatile facts in the political landscape.

## STONEHELM

**Before The Frost:**
The Stonehelm were the Trueborn Empire's garrison commanders. Not generals — the empire's generals were Trueborn or Trueborn-appointed. The Stonehelm were the people who actually held the walls. They designed fortifications, managed the logistics of long-term territorial defense, and understood that a war won in the field can still be lost if the roads behind you are not protected.

In the hierarchy of the Age Before, they were well-respected and somewhat overlooked. They did not produce spectacular victories; they prevented spectacular defeats. This is the kind of contribution that is difficult to celebrate and easy to take for granted. The Stonehelm were used to this. They did not seem to mind. Their pride was in being the house that had never let a position fall.

**During The Frost:**
The Great Frost was, functionally, a siege of the entire world. And the Stonehelm were the house that had been training for sieges for a thousand years. They survived better than almost anyone, and they survived in a way that maintained their identity: they built fortified positions, organized survivor communities within those fortified positions, and defended them with a discipline that made them among the most defensible populations alive.

They lost mobility. They held ground but gave up the ability to move freely. By the end of the Frost, the Stonehelm-held positions were islands — incredibly defensible, somewhat isolated, not connected to each other by anything resembling the communication network they had maintained before.

What the Frost took from them was their sense of belonging to something larger. They were fortress commanders without an empire to command for. They had never defined themselves by the Trueborn, exactly, but they had defined themselves as part of a structure. That structure was gone. They held their walls and wondered what they were defending.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Stonehelm enter the Reclamation as the house that has the most secure territory and the least clarity about what to do with it. Their fortifications are excellent. Their defensive doctrine is refined. They have spent eighty years of Reclamation improving positions they held through the Frost, and what they have built is genuinely formidable.

What they want is recognition that defense has value — that the house that has never lost a wall deserves a seat in whatever new order emerges, even if they did not produce the generals who won the campaigns that made the Reclamation possible. They want the sovereignty seat in the sense that they want their work acknowledged. Whether they want to govern, they are less certain about.

They are afraid of being bypassed — of a world where the great game moves fast and leaves slow, careful, defensive powers behind. They are afraid that what they built will be seen as the starting point for someone else's ambition rather than the culmination of their own.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Stonehelm story is that everything that survived the Frost survived because someone held the walls. The flashy victories of the Reclamation were only possible because the Stonehelm gave the other houses something to return to. They are the foundation. The rest is architecture. Foundations do not get monuments built to them. Foundations are the reason monuments are still standing.

**Their wound:**
During the Frost, one of the Stonehelm's most critical fortress positions was temporarily opened to a population of refugees, over the objection of the garrison commander, by a young member of the bloodline who could not justify the mathematics of leaving them outside. The fortress became overcrowded, disease spread, and losses were significant. The bloodline member was right that the refugees deserved entry. The garrison commander was right that the consequences were severe. Neither position has been fully resolved in the Stonehelm's institutional memory. They have been arguing about it, in the coded language of doctrine, ever since. It shaped a deep split between the house's humanitarian instinct and its survival logic.

## WESTLAND

**Before The Frost:**
The Westland were the frontier line of the Trueborn Empire — the house that held the edges where the empire thinned out and the wilderness began. They were administrators of the margins, soldiers of the boundary, and they held a relationship to the empire that was simultaneously its most exposed position and its most independent one. The center of the empire rarely paid attention to the frontier. The frontier had to handle itself.

This produced a culture of self-reliance that the other houses sometimes found abrasive and the Trueborn sometimes found inconvenient. The Westland did not wait for authorization from the capital to handle problems at the border. By the time authorization could arrive, the problem was solved. This was pragmatism. The capital occasionally read it as insubordination. The distinction was never resolved.

They came from the Westgrave lineage — the older, more deeply frontier-rooted line — and the vivid red of their hair became associated with the fire and urgency of the edge. They were proud of the association.

**During The Frost:**
The frontier collapsed first and hardest. The Westland's position, which had always been their strength, became a catastrophic liability when the Frost arrived and the wilderness they had been holding back became even more hostile than before. They lost more people in the first decade than almost any other house. They survived not through any sophisticated strategy but through sheer aggression — they fought for territory, for resources, for survival, in a way that stripped away every non-essential thing and left behind the hardest core.

What they lost was subtlety. What they gained was ferocity. The Westland that emerge from the Frost are not the house that managed the empire's frontier. They are the house that the frontier made.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Westland are aggressive by nature and by history. They push. When uncertain, they expand. When threatened, they counterattack before the threat fully materializes. This has made them one of the most militarily active houses in the early Reclamation, and also one of the most difficult to negotiate with, because they operate on instincts shaped by 170 years of winning through forward momentum.

What they want is space. More than the sovereignty seat, they want room to expand without hitting another house's wall. The sovereignty seat would give them that — a supreme claim would mean no more border disputes — but they would honestly take unchallenged dominion over a large territory over governance of a contested world. If forced to choose, they would probably take the seat. But forced choices irritate them.

They are afraid of encirclement. The Westland's strategic nightmare is a world in which every direction is blocked — where the diplomacy of other houses has created a cage around them. They watch the Goldgrave's economic web and the Trueborn's political positioning with more anxiety than they show.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Westland story is that everyone else had the luxury of thinking. The frontier never allowed that. Every generation of Westland has been shaped by the reality that hesitation kills, that the question you deliberate over is the question you answer too late, that the house that moves first sets the terms for everyone else. They do not frame this as aggression. They frame it as clarity. They see more slowly-moving houses as people who have been protected from consequences long enough to forget what consequences feel like.

**Their wound:**
During the Frost, a Westland commander made a decision that effectively redirected a significant surviving population into the territory of a neighboring group by force, displacing that group in a way that caused them considerable suffering. It was a decision that saved the Westland's core population and destroyed someone else's stability. The Westland do not discuss this as a wound because the house's dominant narrative has successfully framed it as a hard but necessary choice. But there are descendants of the displaced group alive in the Reclamation, and they have not forgotten. The wound is real. The Westland just does not know they carry it.

## HARTVALE

**Before The Frost:**
The Hartvale were the Trueborn Empire's population centers — the house that managed the great agricultural regions, the grain stores, the labor pools, the river-valley settlements that fed the empire and produced its workforce. In the hierarchy of the Age Before, they were considered somewhat beneath the military and administrative houses, because what they did was not glamorous. They grew food. They had children. They organized labor. The empire ran on them and preferred not to think about it.

The Hartvale were not unaware of this dynamic. They had been the backbone for long enough to understand what a backbone is worth when it breaks. They did not cultivate resentment exactly — resentment is a luxury for people who can afford to focus on something other than the harvest — but they cultivated a kind of patient certainty that eventually, the world would understand what they had always known about themselves.

**During The Frost:**
The Frost hit the Hartvale differently than most houses. They were agricultural people, and agriculture died. But they understood food systems in ways the other houses did not, and this knowledge — of root vegetables that survive frost, of early spring indicators, of how to manage seed stocks through multiple failed seasons — kept them alive when purely martial or industrial houses were starving.

They lost scale. The great agricultural regions of the Age Before were gone. What survived was a deep knowledge of small-scale, resilient food production that the Hartvale rebuilt the Reclamation around. They came out of the Frost smaller in absolute population than expected — because they were scrupulously honest about what their land could support, which meant controlled births during the worst decades — but denser in agricultural know-how than anyone.

The orange hair of the Hartborne line became associated, in folk culture, with harvest and with stubbornness in equal measure. Both reputations are accurate.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Hartvale are the population engine of the Reclamation. Their territory grows people — the combination of agricultural expertise and a specific cultural emphasis on family size and settlement has made their regions the fastest-recovering in terms of raw population. This is their primary strategic asset and their primary vulnerability. Population without protection is food for someone else's army.

What they want is safety and recognition. They want a world in which the people who actually sustain civilization are treated as partners rather than resources. The sovereignty seat would get them this recognition, but they are more interested in structural acknowledgment — in a world order that cannot treat them as expendable — than in the symbolic authority of the seat itself.

They are afraid of being harvested. Not literally. Politically. They know that powerful houses look at Hartvale population centers as recruitment grounds and tax bases and buffer zones. They have spent the Reclamation building defenses and alliances that make them more expensive to exploit than to partner with. They are not sure it is working.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Hartvale story is about roots, literally and figuratively. The houses that survived the Frost by fighting won battles. The Hartvale survived by planting things. The battle winners need to eat. The things planted are still growing. Every generation of Hartvale is larger than the last; every generation of battle-hungry houses is smaller. The patient house wins by outlasting everyone else. This is not a story that produces great warriors, but the Hartvale are not sure they want great warriors. They want grandchildren.

**Their wound:**
During the worst decades of the Frost, a Hartvale council made the decision to stop feeding a refugee population that had settled on their margins, on the grounds that the additional mouths would collapse the entire food system. They were correct in their calculations. The refugees died. The Hartvale survived. Three generations later, the Hartvale have convinced themselves that this was mercy — better a clean ending than a slow starvation of everyone. The truth, which a few of their historians know, is that it was not quick and it was not clean. The wound is buried deep under the Hartvale's patient, nurturing self-image, and it has been festering there for 150 years.

## WHITEHALL

**Before The Frost:**
The Whitehall occupied a strange position in the Trueborn Empire — they were everywhere. Not as governors or commanders, but as the empire's administrative middle layer. They staffed the bureaucracies, ran the census offices, managed the correspondence networks, adjudicated the disputes that were too small for the great courts and too large to be ignored. They were generalists in a world of specialists, and this was, for a long time, considered a limitation.

In the Age Before, the Whitehall had no defining character — no military tradition like the Stonehelm, no faith tradition like the Highborne, no trade tradition like the Goldgrave. They were capable of many things at a modest level of excellence. Other houses dismissed them, politely, as functionaries. The Whitehall absorbed this characterization without apparent distress and continued to function.

**During The Frost:**
The Frost proved, definitively, that the houses with single points of failure failed. The military houses that could not adapt. The agricultural houses whose specific knowledge did not transfer to new conditions. The trade houses whose networks collapsed. The Whitehall's lack of a single identity was, in the most brutal sense, adaptive. They could do whatever the situation required, at whatever level of competence the situation demanded, and move on to the next requirement.

They are the house about which the Frost produced the fewest dramatic stories, which is itself a story. They did not have a heroic founding myth of underground survival like the Ironmark, or a dramatic pivot like the Goldgrave, or a terrible choice like the Hartvale. They adapted, incrementally, continuously, and emerged from the Frost approximately as they had entered it: balanced, capable, unremarkable, and alive.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Whitehall's stated position — "five out of five, across the board" — is philosophically genuine and strategically impenetrable. They have no obvious weakness to target. They have no obvious strength to ally with. They can do everything at a level that makes them useful without making them indispensable, which is either a strength or a limitation depending on the situation.

What they want is influence without exposure. They want to be in the room where decisions are made, contributing to the logic of every outcome, without being on any side. They are, in this way, a secular version of what the Trueborn tried to be — neutral, essential, impossible to exclude.

They are afraid of being forced to choose. Their neutrality is not indifference; it is doctrine. They have theories about why choosing a side is a long-term losing strategy, and these theories are sophisticated and probably correct. The fear is that the war becomes hot enough that neutrality is no longer tenable — that every house demands an ally-or-enemy declaration and the Whitehall's careful positioning becomes a liability on all sides.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Whitehall story is about completeness. Every other house has a specialty that defines them and limits them. The Whitehall has no limits because they have no specialty. They are the whole where others are the parts. This is not arrogance — they say it matter-of-factly. It is simply a description of the system. The question "what are the Whitehall good at?" is answered: everything. The question "what are they exceptionally good at?" does not have a satisfying answer, and this is, they argue, the point.

**Their wound:**
The Whitehall's wound is the wound of having been perpetually underestimated for so long that they have internalized the underestimation. Somewhere in their collective identity is a deep uncertainty: Are they genuinely complete, or have they simply found a philosophy that makes the lack of a defining character sound like a virtue? The external world thinks of this as the Whitehall's secret insecurity. The Whitehall thinks of it as their philosophical question. It is both.

## OLDCREST

**Before The Frost:**
The Oldcrest were old before the Trueborn. This is not a metaphor. They predate the Trueborn Empire's formal structure — a house that was already old and settled when the Trueborn were still consolidating, and that was incorporated into the empire's framework through a combination of political negotiation and the quiet acknowledgment that some things are easier to work with than around.

In the Age Before, the Oldcrest were the empire's institutional memory. They kept records of records. They maintained lineages. They were the house you consulted when there was a precedent question — not because they governed precedent, but because they had been watching things long enough to know what happened the last time a situation like this arose. They were treated with a kind of respectful wariness that old, settled things tend to produce. The Trueborn acknowledged them as peers. Everyone else treated them as something slightly outside the normal hierarchy.

**During The Frost:**
The Oldcrest survived the Frost through an almost biological commitment to bloodline continuity. They made decisions during the collapse that other houses would have found monstrous — selective marriage, isolation of the most genetically robust lines, calculated ruthlessness about who received resources and who did not — all in service of the single principle that the Oldcrest bloodline must be maintained. They were correct that this strategy worked. They also lost something they have not publicly acknowledged: the warmth that had once characterized their culture. The Oldcrest before the Frost had been scholars and philosophers. The Oldcrest after the Frost were calculators.

Their black and gray hair, threaded through with the pepper of long lineages, became associated in folk culture with age and with patience and with the slightly unsettling sense that Oldcrest nobles had seen something like this before.

**At Match Start (Year 80 of Reclamation):**
The Oldcrest's bloodline depth gives them compound advantages that no other house can match in the long term. Marriages that were carefully arranged three generations ago produce results now. Traits cultivated across decades manifest in the current generation as concentrations of ability that look, to outside observers, almost supernatural.

What they want is the future they have been building toward for three hundred years. The sovereignty seat is not a destination for the Oldcrest — it is a scheduled event. They have been moving toward it since before the Frost, and the Frost was, in their historiography, a delay rather than a disruption. They are patient in the way that things with long time horizons are patient: they see moves other houses cannot see because other houses are not looking three generations ahead.

They are afraid of extinction — specifically, of a rapid military or political reversal that destroys in a decade what they have built across centuries. Their long-game orientation makes them vulnerable to fast, violent disruptions that their planning did not account for. They have plans for almost everything. They do not have plans for chaos.

**What they tell themselves:**
The Oldcrest story is about time. Every other house is playing in the present. The Oldcrest are playing in the future. Every marriage is an investment. Every alliance is a planting. Every treaty is a seed. The sovereignty seat will go to the house that deserves it — not by strength or faith, but by having made the right choices before anyone understood that choices were being made. This is not arrogance. It is mathematics. The Oldcrest have been doing the math longer than anyone else.

**Their wound:**
The Oldcrest's wound is moral and unresolved. The decisions made during the Frost — the calculations about who was worth saving — were made by people who believed they were serving the house. The current generation knows the history. Some of them believe the math was right. Some of them carry a guilt that has no clean outlet because the decisions produced exactly the outcome they were designed to produce. The Oldcrest bloodline is strong and capable because someone, generations ago, was willing to make terrible choices. Living with that is the work of every Oldcrest heir who has ever thought seriously about where they came from.

# SECTION B: THE RIVALRY MAP

---

## MAJOR RIVALRIES

---

**TRUEBORN ↔ HIGHBORNE: The Question of Legitimacy**

*Historical basis:* For the entire Age Before, the Trueborn and Highborne operated in a relationship of mutual dependency that neither house has ever forgiven the other for needing. The Trueborn could not effectively govern without the Highborne's ability to sanctify their rule and manage the doctrinal machinery that made obedience feel like righteousness rather than compulsion. The Highborne could not effectively operate without the Trueborn's imperial protection and the institutional infrastructure that gave faith authority. They needed each other. They resented needing each other. Over centuries, this manifested as a slow, refined competition — each house subtly working to reduce the other's indispensability while maintaining the partnership publicly.

*Current tension at match start:* The Frost changed the power balance. The Highborne emerged with genuine faith authority that they rebuilt from the ground up, no longer dependent on the Trueborn system to give it force. The Trueborn emerged with symbolic legitimacy but no empire to legitimize. They now look at each other across the ruins of the arrangement they used to share, and both houses see an opponent where they used to see a partner. The Highborne's position is that the new world's legitimacy comes through faith, not bloodline — that the Trueborn's claim to supremacy is an inheritance from a dead world. The Trueborn's position is that doctrine is downstream of sovereignty, and whoever holds the seat writes the doctrine whether the Highborne like it or not.

*How it typically plays out:* In a match, Trueborn and Highborne spend the early game maneuvering diplomatically, projecting neutrality or cooperation, while working against each other's longer-term positioning. One will typically try to lock in a faith alliance with the other covenants before the other can. Overt war between them is rare and late — they understand each other too well to be surprised by the other's moves, and there is a cold-war logic to their competition that tends to favor information and alliance over armies.

*The breaking point:* If the Highborne publicly declare a doctrinal position that the Trueborn cannot rule legitimately — that their bloodline's claim is void — or if the Trueborn use their institutional records to expose a historical Highborne transgression as a political weapon, the cold war becomes hot and does not cool.

---

**IRONMARK ↔ GOLDGRAVE: What Power Actually Means**

*Historical basis:* In the Age Before, the Ironmark made things and the Goldgrave sold them. This was an arrangement that should have been complementary and was, instead, a perpetual source of friction. The Goldgrave's margins on Ironmark product were, by Ironmark accounting, a form of parasitism. The Ironmark's inability to manage the distribution and financial complexity of trade was, by Goldgrave accounting, a problem that justified the margin. Both were right. Neither would concede it. They needed each other and built the relationship on that need, which is the least stable possible foundation.

*Current tension at match start:* The Frost broke the old arrangement and forced both houses to rebuild. The Ironmark came out with production capacity. The Goldgrave came out with a network. They are, again, in a position of interdependence — and the terms of that interdependence are being renegotiated constantly. The Goldgrave currently holds credit over several Ironmark operations that the Ironmark financed through Goldgrave loans during the hardest Frost years. The Ironmark views these loans as extortion at the moment of maximum vulnerability. The Goldgrave views them as fair exchange. The debt is the relationship.

*How it typically plays out:* In the early match, Ironmark and Goldgrave are economically entangled enough that full hostility is self-destructive for both. They operate in an uncomfortable partnership with persistent tension. The Goldgrave tries to use economic leverage to shape Ironmark decisions; the Ironmark tries to find alternative revenue streams that reduce Goldgrave dependency. The relationship breaks decisively in the mid-game, when one house makes a move the other cannot accept — usually a Goldgrave political alliance that threatens Ironmark independence, or an Ironmark military expansion that undercuts the trade routes Goldgrave depends on.

*The breaking point:* The Goldgrave calling in the Frost-era debts in full and immediately, or the Ironmark supplying weapons to a Goldgrave enemy as a political statement.

---

**WESTLAND ↔ STONEHELM: The Theology of Movement**

*Historical basis:* In the Age Before, the Westland held the frontier and the Stonehelm defended the interior. In theory, they were complementary — the frontier force and the garrison force, two parts of the same defensive system. In practice, they disagreed fundamentally about the nature of defense. The Westland believed that defense means forward pressure — you defend by pushing the threat away before it reaches your walls. The Stonehelm believed that defense means fortification — you defend by making your position too costly to take. These were not abstract disagreements. They produced real disputes over resource allocation, strategic priority, and command authority that the Trueborn empire had to arbitrate repeatedly. The Trueborn usually sided with the Stonehelm, because the Stonehelm produced defensible infrastructure that the empire needed regardless of who was right philosophically. The Westland felt this as a recurring betrayal.

*Current tension at match start:* In the Reclamation, the Westland's aggressive expansion has repeatedly produced conflicts along the borders of Stonehelm-held territory. The Westland expands; the expansion creates conflicts; those conflicts sometimes produce refugee flows and instability that Stonehelm fortifications have to absorb. The Stonehelm's position is that the Westland creates problems and leaves other people to contain them. The Westland's position is that the Stonehelm's careful, defended, patient approach is passivity dressed up as strategy.

*How it typically plays out:* Westland and Stonehelm begin at low-level hostility that grows with proximity. The Westland's natural expansion instinct eventually drives them into territory that Stonehelm considers a buffer zone. This produces a crisis — usually a military skirmish rather than a full war — that forces a diplomatic resolution. If the resolution fails, it becomes one of the match's defining conflicts. If it succeeds, the two houses are capable of a functional alliance based on Westland offense and Stonehelm defense, which is formidably effective and deeply uncomfortable for both parties.

*The breaking point:* The Westland fortifying a position that the Stonehelm considers a natural defensive boundary, or the Stonehelm signing a defensive treaty with a Westland enemy that closes a frontier the Westland was counting on.

---

**OLDCREST ↔ EVERYONE: The Patience Tax**

*Historical basis:* The Oldcrest's long-game orientation has, over centuries, produced an accumulated record of being correct about things that other houses were wrong about. They predicted the economic crisis that preceded the Trueborn Empire's late-period instability. They warned about the agricultural vulnerabilities that made the Frost's food collapse so severe. They were not listened to, because they presented their conclusions as observations rather than urgings — the Oldcrest do not lobby. They note, record, and wait.

This history has made the Oldcrest deeply resented in ways that are difficult to articulate, because no one can point to a specific Oldcrest betrayal or aggression. They are resented for being right in a way that implies everyone else was wrong — for making every generation of other houses feel as though they are being watched and found lacking.

*Current tension at match start:* The Oldcrest's compound bloodline advantages are visible enough now that other houses understand what they are seeing. A house whose elders were children during the Frost is approaching its third generation of carefully managed bloodline investment, and the results are becoming apparent. The reaction from most other houses is a combination of unease and competitive anxiety. The Ironmark finds the Oldcrest approach calculating to the point of inhumanity. The Hartvale respect the family commitment but distrust the calculation behind it. The Trueborn see the Oldcrest as the only house that genuinely threatens their long-term claim.

*How it typically plays out:* The Oldcrest are rarely the target of early aggression — they do nothing provocative, they maintain correct relationships, and attacking them feels disproportionate. But by the mid-game, other houses begin to recognize that the Oldcrest's long-game position has quietly strengthened, and the question of whether to disrupt it before it becomes unassailable becomes urgent. The match often features one or two houses forming a short-term anti-Oldcrest coalition to interrupt the compound advantage accumulation, which the Oldcrest anticipated and prepared for.

*The breaking point:* The Oldcrest making a move that reveals how far ahead they have been planning — when another house realizes that an event from three generations ago was Oldcrest orchestration — and the revelation that they have been outmaneuvered across decades rather than moments.

---

## NATURAL ALLIANCES

---

**HARTVALE ↔ STONEHELM: The Gardener and the Wall**

*Historical basis:* The Hartvale produce people and food. The Stonehelm protect positions. In the Age Before, they operated in complementary regions of the empire — Hartvale agricultural centers often sat behind Stonehelm-defended perimeters. The relationship was functional and not particularly warm, but it was clean: each house provided what the other needed without competing for the same resources.

*Current bond at match start:* The Reclamation has deepened the complementarity. Hartvale population density makes Stonehelm fortifications worth defending; Stonehelm fortifications make Hartvale settlements safe enough to grow. Neither house wants the other's territory or role. This is rarer than it sounds — most of the natural alliances in the world have underlying tensions from competing interests. Hartvale and Stonehelm have genuinely parallel interests.

*How it typically plays out:* A formal or informal early alliance that holds through the mid-game. The Hartvale provide the population base and agricultural support for Stonehelm garrisons; the Stonehelm provide the defensive infrastructure that allows Hartvale settlements to expand without military exposure. The alliance produces a large, stable, slow-growing block that is difficult to dislodge. Its weakness is that neither house is aggressive, which means they tend to fall behind in the zero-sum race for territory if more expansionist houses are allowed to expand unchecked.

*The tension underneath:* The Stonehelm occasionally treats Hartvale settlement populations as additional mouths in a garrison rather than civilians worth protecting on their own terms. When the Stonehelm's defensive calculus produces decisions that sacrifice Hartvale settlements for strategic position, the relationship strains.

---

**WHITEHALL ↔ GOLDGRAVE: The Art of Not Committing**

*Historical basis:* In the Age Before, the Whitehall's administrative middle layer and the Goldgrave's trade network were the two systems through which the empire actually functioned day to day. They had worked together for so long that they had, in certain regions, become difficult to distinguish. Whitehall administrators managed the records that Goldgrave trade required. Goldgrave wealth funded the infrastructure that Whitehall administration needed. They were, in a specific and limited way, the empire's connective tissue, and they recognized each other as such.

*Current bond at match start:* Both houses are philosophically committed to neutrality and flexibility. The Goldgrave needs stable political environments for trade; the Whitehall provides administrative services that stabilize political environments. More practically, both houses have reasons to avoid being dragged into the great conflicts that other houses are building toward, and they have historically found each other useful for diplomatic triangulation — using the appearance of Goldgrave-Whitehall coordination to communicate messages to other houses without either house being formally committed to anything.

*How it typically plays out:* An economic and diplomatic alliance that functions as a third-party moderating force in the early game. Neither house is powerful enough militarily to be a major factor alone; together, they represent enough economic and administrative leverage that larger houses consult them before making moves that would disrupt the general order. The alliance tends to hold until one house needs to make a commitment — usually the Goldgrave being offered a deal too good to refuse by a major power — that the Whitehall cannot endorse.

---

**HIGHBORNE ↔ HARTVALE: The Faith That Feeds**

*Historical basis:* The Highborne administered faith; the Hartvale were the most consistent practitioners of it. Not the most sophisticated — that distinction goes to the Highborne themselves — but the most genuine. The agricultural rhythms of Hartvale life were organized around faith observances in ways that, by the late Age Before, had become inseparable from the agricultural practice itself. Planting times were faith times. Harvest times were faith times. The Hartvale did not think of this as religious observation; they thought of it as how the year works.

*Current bond at match start:* The Highborne recognize that the Hartvale's population and territorial growth is making them one of the most significant faith constituencies in the Reclamation world. Influencing Hartvale doctrine is influencing the faith of a growing portion of the population. For the Hartvale, the Highborne offer doctrinal coherence and the sense of participating in something larger than regional tradition. The alliance is genuine on both sides, which makes it more durable than most.

*How it typically plays out:* A faith-political alliance that produces coalition strength in the mid-game. The Hartvale provide a large and devout population that amplifies Highborne faith authority; the Highborne provide doctrinal support that amplifies Hartvale conviction scores and gives their territory cultural coherence. The combination creates pressure on other houses to either join the faith bloc or explicitly position against it, which structures the political landscape around a faith axis the Highborne designed.

---

## COMPLICATED RELATIONSHIPS

---

**TRUEBORN ↔ WHITEHALL: Neutrality Recognizes Neutrality**

Both houses are committed to political neutrality, and they respect this about each other with something that has never quite become trust. The Trueborn's neutrality is strategic — they are waiting for an invitation to rule. The Whitehall's neutrality is philosophical — they genuinely do not believe committing to a side serves their long-term interests. These are different things, and both houses know it.

The complication is that the Trueborn's archives contain genealogical records that affect Whitehall lineage claims. This is not a threat the Trueborn have made explicit, but both houses know it is there. The Whitehall manages the relationship carefully, maintaining the appearance of cordiality while working to ensure that the records question never becomes pressing. The Trueborn maintains the appearance of indifference while noting, in their private councils, that Whitehall neutrality has made them an unreliable variable in the sovereignty calculation.

In most matches, they orbit each other cautiously. Their relationship is the most likely in the political landscape to be resolved either by a permanent formal alignment or by a sudden, decisive break.

---

**IRONMARK ↔ OLDCREST: The Hammer and the Clock**

The Ironmark find the Oldcrest's long-game orientation philosophically irritating in a way they cannot entirely articulate. The Ironmark believe in things you can hold, measure, and hit. The Oldcrest believe in processes that unfold across generations. These worldviews are not compatible, and they produce a relationship that is neither alliance nor rivalry but something more like mutual incomprehension.

At match start, the Ironmark has Oldcrest investments in some of their operations that predate the Frost — the Oldcrest made very long-term capital commitments in the Age Before that are now producing returns in ways neither house expected. The Ironmark receives benefits from Oldcrest seed money without having asked for it, which they find uncomfortable. The Oldcrest receives returns from the investment without needing to do anything, which they find appropriate.

This relationship is most likely to become a rivalry if the Ironmark's expansion and the Oldcrest's long-term positioning produce overlapping territorial interests. It is also possible, in a match where an aggressive third house threatens both, that they find unexpected common cause — the Ironmark's production capacity paired with the Oldcrest's generational knowledge would be formidable.

---

**WESTLAND ↔ HARTVALE: The Aggressive Cousin**

The Westland and Hartvale share the Hartborne line — the Hartvale through direct descent, the Westland through the Westgrave branch that crossed with Hartborne several generations back. This bloodline connection produces a complicated relationship that is neither family warmth nor pure competition.

The Westland's aggressive expansion has, in the Reclamation, occasionally produced movements into territory that Hartvale communities depend on. This has been managed — it has not produced war — but the management has required the Hartvale to make political concessions that still rankle. The Westland, for their part, finds the Hartvale's patient, population-focused approach incomprehensible in a world where moving first is the only real advantage. They think of the Hartvale as good people who will be gobbled up by less gentle powers if they do not start moving faster.

In a match, this relationship is highly situational. If both houses are under pressure from a common threat, the bloodline connection and complementary strengths produce one of the more effective emergency alliances available. If both houses are expanding normally, their interests eventually conflict, and the conflict is sharpened by the sense, on both sides, that this is not supposed to happen between relatives.

---

## TRUEBORN'S RELATIONSHIP WITH ALL HOUSES

The Trueborn City's official position is that it maintains cordial, neutral relationships with all founding houses. The Trueborn's actual position is that they view every other house through the lens of the sovereignty question — each house is either an obstacle, a potential vehicle, a threat, or a useful piece in an arrangement they are still constructing.

**Trueborn ↔ Highborne:** Formal rivalry beneath a surface of shared civilizational commitment. The Trueborn need the Highborne to eventually endorse their restoration; the Highborne need the Trueborn's symbolic legitimacy to anchor their doctrinal authority. Both houses understand that only one of them can define the terms of the new world's founding claim, and neither will yield that definition voluntarily. Starting state: Neutral with deep structural tension.

**Trueborn ↔ Ironmark:** Correct but cool. The Trueborn have nothing the Ironmark need that the Ironmark cannot obtain elsewhere. The Ironmark have nothing the Trueborn want in the short term. They do not interfere with each other. The Trueborn have noted, in their private records, that a restored empire would need Ironmark production, and that the Ironmark's independence orientation would make them a difficult subject house. This is a long-term problem they are managing by not accelerating it. Starting state: Neutral.

**Trueborn ↔ Goldgrave:** Mutually wary commercial relationship. The Goldgrave's information network is the most comprehensive in the world — more comprehensive than the Trueborn's own intelligence, which is a situation the Trueborn find intolerable and are working to correct. The Goldgrave knows the Trueborn is working to correct it. Both houses maintain a polite fiction of good relations while competing in the intelligence space. The Goldgrave's position is that they would finance a Trueborn restoration if the terms were right; the Trueborn's position is that restoration is not something that gets financed, it is something that gets recognized. Starting state: Cordial with strategic friction.

**Trueborn ↔ Stonehelm:** Genuine, if limited, respect. The Stonehelm held positions during the Frost that, in the Trueborn's reading of the records, protected surviving populations that would otherwise have died. The Trueborn acknowledge this in the coded language of formal correspondence. The Stonehelm received a specific Trueborn commendation during the Reclamation that they keep in their great hall. It means something to them. This makes the Stonehelm the house the Trueborn can reach most easily if they need to move from words to action. Starting state: Cordial.

**Trueborn ↔ Westland:** Cold. The Westland's aggressive expansion orientation runs directly against the Trueborn's vision of a restored, organized world. The Trueborn view the Westland as an argument against the idea that civilization has truly recovered — as people shaped by the frontier in ways that make them unsuitable for governance. The Westland view the Trueborn as relics whose claim is sentimental rather than functional. They do not trade this view publicly. They trade it, continuously, through intermediaries. Starting state: Neutral with mutual contempt just below the surface.

**Trueborn ↔ Hartvale:** Paternal and slightly condescending warmth that the Hartvale have learned to receive without showing their response to it. The Trueborn regard the Hartvale as exactly the kind of people a restored empire would be built to serve — growing, faithful, stable — and treat them accordingly, with the specific energy of a former ruler looking at a population they intend to govern again. The Hartvale are not fooled by this. They accept the warmth, bank the goodwill, and make no commitments. Starting state: Cordial.

**Trueborn ↔ Whitehall:** Described above. Cautious mutual respect between houses that share a commitment to not committing. Starting state: Neutral.

**Trueborn ↔ Oldcrest:** The most sophisticated bilateral relationship in the political landscape. The Trueborn and Oldcrest are the two houses with the deepest historical roots, and they recognize each other as such. Their relationship is that of very old institutions that have reached the same fork in the road and are determining, with great care, whether to travel it together or in opposition. The Trueborn's legitimacy claim is genealogical. The Oldcrest's compound advantage is genealogical. Both houses live in the logic of bloodline, and they share an understanding of what that means that no other house has. This produces genuine respect and genuine strategic concern in equal measure. Starting state: Neutral with deep mutual watchfulness.

# SECTION C: THE STARTING POLITICAL LANDSCAPE

Year 80 of the Reclamation. The world has been putting itself back together for three generations now, and it is starting to look like something — not what it was, but something. Roads are being maintained again. Markets are operating in the larger settlement clusters. A child born today can expect to see a world that her grandparents would not have recognized as possible. This is the most hopeful the world has been since the Frost began, and it is also, for exactly that reason, the most dangerous.

The houses survived the Frost in part because the Frost made certain kinds of ambition impossible. You cannot fight a war for sovereignty when you are fighting a winter for food. The collapse flattened the hierarchy and forced every house into a mode of pure survival that was, in its way, equalizing. Now the equalization is over. The hierarchy is beginning to reassert itself, and the houses that positioned themselves well during the Reclamation's early decades are arriving at Year 80 with advantages they intend to press. The Oldcrest have been making marriages. The Highborne have been planting doctrine. The Goldgrave have been extending credit. The Ironmark have been producing at a rate that no house can match. The Westland have been moving. Everyone has been moving.

The political map at Year 80 is not yet fixed, which is the most precise way to describe it. The Trueborn City stands as the one point of formal stability — recognized, tacitly, by every house as something close to sacred, which is why no one has tested that recognition. The Hartvale and Stonehelm have formed the most visible early bloc — an agricultural-defensive combination that is large, stable, and growing. The Highborne are in quiet conversation with several of the minor tribal communities that the four covenants have been incorporating, building a faith network that cuts across house lines. The Ironmark have just completed an expansion of their primary forge complex that has doubled their productive capacity and made every other house quietly recalculate how much they need Ironmark goodwill.

Underneath all of this, the thing that every court knows and no one says publicly: the question of who rules is now, for the first time since the Frost, actually answerable. The world has recovered enough that it can support a claim. It has recovered enough that fighting over the claim would not be apocalyptic. It has recovered enough that a house willing to push toward the sovereignty seat would find real opposition rather than exhausted retreat. The match is beginning because the conditions for the match are finally in place — not because anyone chose the moment, but because the moment arrived.

# SECTION D: BLOODLINE MEMBER PERSONALITY TRAITS

Bloodline members are not statistics. They are people — the kind of people you remember when the match is over, not because of their numbers, but because of what they did and why. The trait system exists to make bloodline members legible as individuals and to create the specific pleasure of watching a character's personality shape outcomes across a long game.

Each trait operates on three levels: how the member behaves internally (event choices), how the world responds to them, and what they leave behind when they die.

---

## MARTIAL TRAITS

**Fierce**
A member who has learned that the first strike usually decides the engagement. Fierce members push for aggressive action in military events — they advocate for forward attack over waiting, for decisive force over measured response. They are difficult to restrain once they have decided something needs to happen. In the Keep, they are respected by soldiers and regarded cautiously by diplomats. Their presence on a campaign reduces unit hesitation and improves early-engagement outcomes. They die the way they live — in front. Conviction push: toward Cruel if their fierceness involves disregard for suffering; neutral if it is purely tactical.

**Cautious**
A member who calculates before committing. Cautious members pull events toward defensive, conservative, information-gathering choices. They are more likely to recommend waiting, fortifying, and understanding the terrain before moving. In the Keep, they are trusted in council but sometimes regarded as timid by more aggressive bloodline members. Their campaigns have lower catastrophic loss rates and lower spectacular victory rates. They are the reason a dynasty survives a war it probably should have lost, and also the reason a dynasty fails to capitalize on an advantage it definitely should have pressed. Conviction neutral; pulls slightly toward Moral when the caution prevents unnecessary deaths.

**Brilliant**
A member with a battlefield mind that operates faster than events. Brilliant commanders see three steps ahead in combat situations, and events frequently give them opportunities to demonstrate it — option trees with correct choices that are opaque unless this trait is present. They are rare. The world knows when a Brilliant commander is in the field. Enemy houses respond with escalation or avoidance rather than standard engagement. Their death is a strategic event, not merely a personal loss. Conviction neutral; the brilliance is tactical, not moral.

**Reckless**
A member for whom the concept of acceptable losses includes themselves. Reckless members consistently choose high-risk, high-reward options. They win impossible victories and create unrecoverable situations with approximately equal frequency. The Keep finds them exciting and exhausting. Their campaigns are the ones that other bloodline members tell stories about, regardless of outcome. Conviction push: toward Cruel, because recklessness with other people's lives is not far from cruelty.

**Stalwart**
A member who does not yield. Stalwart members are not necessarily the best strategists or the most aggressive fighters — they are the ones who hold when every other option is retreat. In siege events, they are the difference between survival and capitulation. In long campaigns, their presence sustains unit morale past the point where it would otherwise collapse. In the Keep, they are pillars — the member everyone looks to when things are bad. Conviction push: toward Moral, because holding is also protecting.

---

## POLITICAL TRAITS

**Cunning**
A member for whom every relationship is a lever. Cunning members understand that what people say they want and what they actually want are different things, and they are very good at working with both simultaneously. Diplomatic events in their presence frequently have options that are only visible through this trait — paths that achieve the house's goal without the other party realizing what happened until it is done. The world finds them effective and unsettling. Other dynasties prefer not to negotiate with them directly if they can avoid it. Conviction push: toward Cruel when cunning becomes manipulation that costs others; neutral when it is merely sophisticated.

**Honorable**
A member for whom the word given is the word kept, at personal cost if necessary. Honorable members simplify diplomatic events by removing deception options — they will not take them, and they make the presence of deception options in other parties visible in ways that other traits cannot. Other dynasties respond to them with a specific kind of trust that takes years to build with other traits. They are expensive to play correctly, because honoring commitments sometimes means absorbing costs that a Cunning member would have avoided. Conviction push: firmly toward Moral.

**Ambitious**
A member who wants more than their current position provides. Ambitious members push events toward choices that increase power, prestige, and positioning — sometimes in ways that create short-term instability for long-term gain. In the Keep, they are useful and slightly dangerous; they are always looking at the arrangement and calculating whether it could be better for them. They are excellent at identifying political opportunities that other members miss. Their ambition is not necessarily self-interested at the expense of the house — it can manifest as ambition for the house itself. Conviction neutral; can push in either direction depending on what the ambition costs others.

**Loyal**
A member whose commitment to the dynasty is the defining fact of their character. Loyal members will not betray the house under any pressure — which is valuable and limiting in equal measure. Events that offer options based on personal gain are invisible to Loyal members; they do not consider them. In the Keep, they are the bedrock around which other members' complicated personalities operate. Other dynasties understand that reaching a Loyal member through bribery or threat is not worth attempting. Conviction push: toward Moral, because loyalty at its best is a form of love.

---

## SOCIAL TRAITS

**Charismatic**
A member whose presence changes the room. Charismatic members improve relations with minor factions, commons populations, and other dynasties simply by existing in proximity. Their diplomatic events produce better outcomes; their administrative presence improves population morale without additional investment. The world responds to them — they accumulate followers, allies, and admirers through the ordinary operations of a campaign that other members would never produce. What makes them complicated: they are more persuasive than they are necessarily correct. They can lead people confidently in the wrong direction, and the followers follow anyway. Conviction neutral.

**Cold**
A member who does not warm to people and whose affect communicates this clearly. Cold members are not disliked, exactly — they are respected and carefully kept at a distance. They perform exceptionally well in events where emotional distance is an asset: resource allocation decisions, strategic sacrifices, negotiations where the other party's emotional leverage needs to be neutralized. Their administrative output is efficient and impersonal. The population they govern is safe and provided for and does not love them. When they die, people are more relieved than grieved, which is not the same as unmourned. Conviction neutral.

**Beloved**
A member who has earned something more than respect. Beloved members are rare events — they require specific combinations of action and circumstance over time, and they cannot simply be declared. A Beloved member produces loyalty effects in both the population and the military that extend beyond normal ranges. When they die, the impact on the house is significant — not just mechanically, but narratively. Other dynasties and minor factions respond to their death in ways that shape the political landscape. The world notices when someone beloved is gone. Conviction push: toward Moral, because the conditions that produce a Beloved member typically involve consistent care for others over time.

**Feared**
A member who has made the world understand that crossing them has a specific, predictable cost. Feared members suppress external aggression — enemies calculate the cost of provoking them and frequently decide the cost is too high. In the Keep, they create a specific social environment: deference, caution, and a distinct absence of casual challenge. This is effective and isolating. They produce compliance, not loyalty. Minor factions in their territory behave, and do not love. When they die, some of what was being suppressed by their presence resurfaces immediately. Conviction push: toward Cruel, though a Feared member can maintain Moral conviction if their fear comes from consequences that were just rather than excessive.

---

## FAITH TRAITS

**Devout**
A member for whom faith is not a tool but a reality. Devout members perform all faith-related events with amplified outcomes — they gain conviction from faith acts that other members receive in smaller amounts, and their presence at covenant ceremonies produces extended effects. In the Keep, they are a source of stability for the population and a signal to faith-aligned minor factions that the dynasty takes covenant obligations seriously. Their faith is genuine, which makes it valuable and inflexible in equal measure. Conviction push: toward Moral through Moral-covenant practice; can push toward Cruel through Cruel-covenant practice with specific intensity.

**Apostate**
A member who has examined the faith of their house and rejected it — not from indifference, but from a considered position. Apostates are not disrespectful; they simply do not participate. This trait creates friction with faith-dependent covenant relationships while producing advantages in events where religious independence is useful: negotiations with secular powers, populations that are suspicious of faith-based claims, resource decisions that faith constraints would otherwise limit. In the Keep, they are a theological fact that the household has to manage. Conviction: does not interact with conviction in the same way as other faith traits; their choices come from other sources.

**Seeker**
A member who is in active inquiry about the nature of faith without having settled on conclusions. Seekers create unusual diplomatic event options — their openness to multiple covenant traditions can build relationships with faith communities that the house is not formally aligned with, because Seekers are not perceived as threatening. Over time, a Seeker may settle — emerging as Devout or Apostate based on what they encounter — or may remain in productive uncertainty for their entire life. Conviction neutral; Seekers produce the most interesting personal trajectories in the chronicle.

**Zealot**
A member for whom faith is the only lens through which the world is legible. Zealots produce maximum conviction effects for their covenant alignment and minimum flexibility in events that require compromise with competing faith traditions. They are excellent instruments for faith-based expansion and deeply counterproductive instruments for multi-faith diplomacy. In the Keep, they are polarizing — beloved by the faithful, alienating to everyone else. The world knows what a Zealot will do before they do it, which is both their power and their limitation. Conviction push: maximum intensity in whichever direction their covenant aligns.

---

## PERSONAL TRAITS

**Proud**
A member who cannot separate their sense of worth from the house's standing in the world. Proud members perform exceptionally well when the house is ascendant and poorly when the house is diminished — not through mechanical adjustment but through event behavior. When the house is strong, Proud members advocate for bold moves that increase standing. When the house is weak, Proud members resist accommodations that feel like admissions of diminishment. They are the source of the most expensive mistakes dynasties make, and also of the most unexpectedly courageous stands. Conviction neutral.

**Humble**
A member who has a realistic sense of what they are, what the house is, and what the situation demands. Humble members provide the clearest analysis of events because they are not filtering the situation through ego. They recommend the option that serves the goal, not the option that makes them look impressive. In the Keep, they are trusted in council but rarely celebrated in story. The world does not write songs about Humble members during their lifetimes. Sometimes it does afterward. Conviction push: toward Moral.

**Vengeful**
A member who does not let injuries pass. Vengeful members track grievances across decades of game time — if an event produced an outcome that cost them or their house something significant, the trait activates in future related events, producing options oriented toward redress. They are effective in conflicts where personal motivation amplifies performance and counterproductive in situations where the past needs to be set aside. Other dynasties learn, quickly, that wronging a Vengeful member creates a permanent entry in a ledger that will eventually be settled. Conviction neutral; can push toward Cruel when vengeance exceeds the original injury.

**Merciful**
A member who, when they have power over an outcome, bends it toward the least possible harm. Merciful members produce specific event outcomes that other traits do not access — the option to release a captive that other members would execute, to absorb a loss rather than inflict a worse one on someone else, to find the resolution that costs the house something real in exchange for not costing another party more. They are expensive to play well. They are also the members who produce the most durable alliances, because the world remembers being spared. Conviction push: firmly toward Moral; can produce extraordinary Moral conviction scores with consistent practice.

**Melancholic**
A member who carries something heavier than the current situation explains. Melancholic members have encountered loss, failure, or disillusionment in a way that has not resolved and may never resolve. They see clearly — sometimes more clearly than optimistic members — but they see the costs as well as the possibilities. Their performance in events is uneven: extraordinary in situations that demand endurance and seeing things as they are, weak in situations that demand forward enthusiasm. In the Keep, they are the member that other bloodline members understand but do not know how to help. Their chronicle entries are the ones that read like elegies. Conviction neutral; Melancholic members often produce the most interesting faith trajectories.

**Wrathful**
A member whose anger is always close to the surface and occasionally escapes it. Wrathful members are not uniformly destructive — their anger produces genuine intensity and can accomplish things that cooler members cannot. But events involving provocation or insult activate the trait in ways that produce options skewed toward escalation. A Wrathful member will win a confrontation that a Cautious member would have avoided, and cause a confrontation that a Diplomatic member would have resolved. Managing them is part of the game. Their death, if they die in anger, tends to leave unresolved situations behind them. Conviction push: toward Cruel when the wrath is disproportionate; neutral when it is appropriate response to genuine harm.

# SECTION E: LEGENDARY MEMBERS — WHAT MAKES A HERO

The world is full of capable people. Legends are different. A legend is what happens when a capable person is put under extraordinary pressure and does something that the world cannot stop talking about. The conditions matter. The person matters. Both have to be true simultaneously. This is why legends are rare — because the conditions and the person rarely coincide, and because many capable people face extraordinary conditions and do not rise to them, and because many people who would have risen to extraordinary conditions never encounter them.

---

## CONDITIONS THAT PRODUCE LEGENDARY STATUS

A bloodline member may move toward legendary status through specific combinations of event and response:

**Decisive military action against overwhelming odds.** Not every victory produces a legend. Winning when expected to lose, in a way that the world witnesses and cannot rationalize away, is one of the paths. The member must survive the event, and the victory must be significant enough that other dynasties register it.

**Extreme conviction, sustained across a long arc.** A member who accumulates conviction — in either direction — past a threshold of sustained commitment becomes legible to the world as something more than a functionary. This is not about conviction score alone; it is about consistent choice, over time, in a single direction. The world reads consistency as character, and character as legend.

**Surviving what should have killed them.** Near-death events that trigger a specific resolution — where the member survives through exceptional circumstance, whether through their own action or the action of the house — can accelerate legendary status if they are followed by a public return. The world changes its assessment of someone who has faced extinction and come back.

**A single act of extraordinary sacrifice or refusal.** Not all legends are built over time. Some are defined by one moment: the decision to release a captured enemy who could have been executed, the choice to remain behind and face what the army was retreating from, the refusal to recant under conditions where recanting would have been the rational choice. These moments are not predictable — they emerge from events — but they produce the sharpest legend profiles.

**Bloodline member age combined with maintained capacity.** A member who reaches advanced age while remaining active — who governed, fought, or served through multiple match-defining events — accumulates legendary status through duration. The world recognizes, eventually, that they have been watching the same person across decades, and the recognition reshapes how they understand that person.

---

## HOW THE WORLD KNOWS

Legendary status is not invisible. When a bloodline member achieves it:

- Other dynasties begin including their name in diplomatic correspondence in a specific register — acknowledging them as an individual rather than a role
- Minor factions in their region respond to their physical presence differently than to the presence of standard bloodline members
- Events in their territory generate a small modifier on population morale that reflects the symbolic value of their existence
- Chronicles begin accumulating entries about them — not just records but narrative fragments that will survive into the post-match historical report
- Enemy military commanders introduce hesitation modifiers before engaging forces they lead personally

The legend is not declared. It is recognized. This is the distinction. The world starts talking about them before the system formally acknowledges the status.

---

## WHAT STANDARD MEMBERS CANNOT DO

Legendary members access a tier of events and options that standard members do not:

- **The Stand** — an event option in which a legendary member chooses to hold a position personally when retreat is the tactically correct choice. The outcomes range from extraordinary to fatal. Standard members do not receive this option.
- **The Parley** — a legendary member's reputation is sufficient to request a direct meeting with an opposing dynasty's leader, bypassing the normal diplomatic event tree. The parley produces a single session of direct negotiation with outcomes not accessible through standard channels.
- **The Declaration** — a legendary member's public statement on a matter of faith, war, or sovereignty carries amplified conviction weight. They can shift population conviction scores in a way that standard members cannot.
- **The Name** — when a legendary member dies, the house may name a structure, a unit type, or a territory in their honor. Named assets carry a small but permanent modifier that reflects the legend's association.

---

## WHEN A LEGENDARY MEMBER DIES

The world does not absorb the death of a legend the way it absorbs the death of a standard bloodline member. When a legendary member dies:

- Dynasties that had a relationship with them receive a notification that is framed narratively, not mechanically — the death is announced in the language the world uses for significant events
- Minor factions in their territory experience a morale event that reflects the specific nature of the legend — a Feared legend leaves behind a period of testing, a Beloved legend leaves behind a period of grief
- The opposing dynasties who benefited from their death celebrate differently than they celebrate standard victories — some display discomfort alongside the victory, because the world's opinion of the act matters more when the act involves a legend
- A chronicle entry is written automatically and cannot be suppressed — the legend becomes history

---

## FIVE LEGENDARY MEMBER ARCHETYPES

---

**The Iron Mother**
*Story archetype: The dynasty holder*

Her husband died in the third year of a siege that should have ended the house. The siege was political — two houses had agreed to let the dynasty starve rather than rescue it. She negotiated from inside the walls, simultaneously managing the rationing of a population that was two months from collapse and conducting correspondence with every minor faction in the region that might have any reason to complicate the besiegers' supply lines. She was not charming. She was precise. By the month the house was supposed to fall, one of the besieging parties had a problem requiring their army's attention elsewhere. The siege ended. The dynasty survived.

She governed for thirty more years after that. She raised five children, all of whom emerged as functioning members of the dynasty — one as a general, one as an administrator, two as capable political operators, and one as a Seeker who became one of the covenant's most sophisticated interpreters. She died in her study, working, at the age of seventy-three.

When she died, the house held a structure she had built during the siege as the administrative core of the dynasty's territory. They named it the Iron Hall. The modifier persists.

*Mechanical expression:* An Iron Mother-type legendary member who has held governance through a crisis event accumulates an extended keep-stability modifier and gains access to parley events with dynasties that besieged the house unsuccessfully.

---

**The Sword That Remembered**
*Story archetype: The returning warrior*

He was taken captive during a Westland campaign — not a prisoner of high value, just a young cavalry officer who happened to be in the wrong position during a rout. He spent four years in a Westland garrison, not as a political prisoner but as labor. He learned the Westland's military doctrine from the inside. He watched how they moved, how they supplied, how they thought about terrain. He did not escape; he was eventually part of a diplomatic exchange so minor that neither house's leadership particularly noticed.

He returned to his dynasty and spent three years preparing a tactical brief that was, in hindsight, the most accurate intelligence the dynasty ever produced. When the conflict with the Westland came — as it eventually did — he commanded the response with an understanding of his opponent's doctrine that produced outcomes neither side had predicted. The Westland never determined how the dynasty had known what it knew.

He was never a charismatic figure. He was never beloved. He was feared, specifically, by the Westland — by name, in their internal documents. That is a different kind of legend.

*Mechanical expression:* The Returning Warrior-type generates a specific event category: Intelligence Arc, a series of events that produce precise tactical information about an opponent based on time spent in their territory. Standard members can gather intelligence. The Returning Warrior type converts raw intelligence into doctrine-specific advantage.

---

**The Voice of the Covenant**
*Story archetype: The faith architect*

She was born Highborne but raised in a disputed region where three covenant traditions overlapped and competed. By the time she was an adult, she could speak fluently — not merely transactionally, but genuinely — in the theological language of all four covenants. She was not an Apostate; she was something harder to categorize. She believed, but she believed in the architecture of belief rather than in any specific confession.

She spent forty years as a diplomatic intermediary in faith conflicts, brokering arrangements that should not have been possible between covenants that regarded each other as competitors for the same population of souls. What she understood that the clerics on all sides did not was that the conflicts were rarely about doctrine and almost always about territory, resources, and pride dressed up in doctrinal language. She addressed the territory and the resources while giving everyone enough face-saving theological framing to accept the outcome.

When she died, representatives from all four covenants attended her rites. They did not agree on what form the rites should take. They stood there together anyway.

*Mechanical expression:* The Voice of the Covenant-type accesses multi-covenant events that are normally locked behind full commitment to a single covenant. They can broker covenant truces in events where standard members would have to choose sides.

---

**The Last Ironmark**
*Story archetype: The dynasty's end*

This is a tragic archetype — the legendary member who becomes legendary through what they lose rather than what they win. The Last Ironmark type is a bloodline member who, after the death or departure of all other dynasty members, governs alone. The world sees a figure holding what should have already fallen. The world watches.

The specific archetype played out in an early Reclamation Ironmark dynasty that had been reduced through a decade of hard wars to a single surviving member — an older man who had outlived his children and his brothers. He governed his remaining territory alone for eleven years, refusing overtures from three different houses who offered integration that would have required ceding the Ironmark name. He produced no heirs. He spent those eleven years ensuring that the forge complex his family had built would outlast him — improving the infrastructure, training workers who would serve whoever came after, documenting the industrial processes in a form that any literate governor could use.

When he died, the three houses that had offered integration argued over who received the territory. The arrangement they eventually reached produced one of the Reclamation's more stable economic arrangements. He had, in some sense, managed this outcome from his grave.

*Mechanical expression:* A Last-type legendary member who governs without other bloodline members generates a unique event series: the Legacy Acts — decisions that have no short-term benefit but shape the post-dynasty landscape. The player cannot benefit directly from Legacy Acts; they are the game acknowledging that some things outlast the person.

---

**The King Who Waited**
*Story archetype: The long game vindicated*

A Trueborn member who was born into the Trueborn City's patient neutrality and spent his entire adult life maintaining it — watching houses expand, contract, form alliances, break them, while he said nothing, moved nothing, committed to nothing. He was regarded by other dynasties, collectively, as inactive. Harmless. A caretaker of a historical artifact.

In the match's late game, when the major houses were exhausted by the conflicts that he had watched from a careful distance, he made one move. A single alliance offer, timed to a moment when the receiving house had no other options, in language that made the offer look like generosity rather than strategy. The terms, examined carefully, were favorable enough to accept and structured enough to produce a specific outcome three years later that he had calculated for two decades.

He never declared for the sovereignty seat. He was declared for it, by a coalition that had come to understand, too late, that the game he was playing was longer than any of their games, and that they had been inside it without knowing.

*Mechanical expression:* The Patient-type legendary member generates a specific class of diplomatic event unavailable until the late game: the Long Offer — a proposal structured across multiple events that rewards patience with a final outcome that could not have been achieved through direct action. The player must resist the temptation to accelerate it.

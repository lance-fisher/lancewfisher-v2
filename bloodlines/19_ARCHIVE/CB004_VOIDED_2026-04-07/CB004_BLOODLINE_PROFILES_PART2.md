# HOUSE STONEHELM

---

## Identity Statement

Stonehelm is the endurance house — the bloodline that wins by refusing to fall, that converts patience and positional depth into military outcomes, and that treats every engagement as a test of who can sustain loss longest and still hold the line.

---

## Color System

**Primary color:** Stone grey — a medium-value, desaturated grey with warm undertones, like granite quarried from a southern ridge. Not silver (that belongs to Trueborn), not charcoal (that reads too close to Ironmark's forge-ash). This is the color of worked stone, of walls built to last, of cut blocks laid with mortar by people who expected the structure to outlive them.

**Accent color:** Quarry amber — a deep, warm amber-gold that reads like the light of a torch in a stone corridor. This accent appears on heraldic borders, on the chevrons of senior soldiers, and on the banded edging of their banners. It is the color of the lantern light that kept Stonehelm's enclosed communities alive during the Frost years — warmth held inside walls, preserved, not spent.

**Visual design rationale:** The primary/accent pairing was designed to avoid two failure modes. The first failure mode is reading as Ironmark — black-haired forgemasters and grey stone are adjacent in the mind, so Ironmark was anchored to near-black and forge-orange while Stonehelm was pushed toward the lighter, more geological end of the grey spectrum. The second failure mode is reading as inert or boring — grey alone signals passivity. The quarry amber accent solves this. A Stonehelm banner in the field reads as a warm light behind cold stone, which is exactly the psychological signal the house intends: we are not aggressive, but we are not empty. Come through our walls and find out what we kept alive inside them.

---

## Symbol System

**Dominant symbol:** The Arch. A single rounded stone arch, symmetrical, with a keystone rendered as a slightly larger, distinctly differentiated block at the crown. The arch is the engineering object that Stonehelm perfected before the Frost — the structural form that distributes force to its foundations rather than concentrating it at a single point. It is both literally what they built and philosophically what they are: a structure that holds because every part bears its share of the load.

**Controlled variations:**

1. **Full arch with keystone (formal military standard):** The complete arch rendered in clean line, keystone highlighted in quarry amber on military standards and regimental banners. Used in formal contexts: battle formations, alliance seals, royal correspondence.

2. **Arch silhouette (territorial markers):** A simplified, single-stroke arch used to mark Stonehelm-controlled territory on maps, roads, and boundary stones. No internal detail — just the outline. Meant to be read from distance and cut quickly into stone by field engineers.

3. **The keystone alone (command insignia):** Just the keystone block, rendered as a square slightly broader than it is tall, used on officer insignia and command banners. The keystone is the stone that locks the arch — remove it and everything falls. This reads to Stonehelm soldiers as: here is the piece that cannot be allowed to break.

4. **The double arch (alliance and treaty symbols):** Two arches sharing a common foundation block, their keystones touching or nearly touching at center. Used when Stonehelm enters formal alliance with another house. The double arch suggests mutual reinforcement — two independent structures that hold each other up. Appears on alliance documents, joint garrison flags, and diplomatic gifts.

5. **The broken arch (mourning and sacrifice markers):** An arch with one side partially collapsed — stones fallen, the crown no longer meeting — rendered in stone grey without amber accent. Used exclusively on memorial stones for fallen soldiers and on the door lintels of houses where a line has ended. Stonehelm has a cultural tradition of marking loss with structural metaphor. A broken arch at a gate says: something was held here that no longer can be. The grief is architectural.

**Context rules:** The dominant arch is never rendered as decorative filigree or ornamental flourish — Stonehelm has no patience for ornamentation. The symbol appears as a structural object, not an aesthetic one. When reproduced on armor, it is raised relief, not painted. When reproduced on cloth, it is woven, not embroidered with colored thread. The quarry amber appears only on formal military and governmental applications — it is not a fashion color. Soldiers do not add amber trim to personal kit.

## Cultural Origin Summary

**Pre-Frost:** Stonehelm's identity before the Frost arrived was defined by function, not politics. While Trueborn administered the world's social structures and Ironmark produced its weapons, Stonehelm cut and placed the stone that those structures sat on. Roads. Bridges. Fortified walls. Aqueducts. The physical substrate of civilization was Stonehelm's domain. Their lords were not generals or priests or merchants — they were master builders, engineering directors, overseers of projects that took decades and outlasted the people who began them. Stonehelm culture before the Frost was notable for its time horizon. Where other houses thought in seasons or reigns, Stonehelm thought in generations. A road built now would be used by children not yet born. A wall laid now would protect a city that did not yet know it needed protecting. This long temporal orientation was not sentiment — it was engineering discipline. Stone that was not placed correctly would fail at the worst moment, and the consequences would be measured in lives. Stonehelm's pre-Frost reputation was not glamorous. They did not appear in the great sagas as heroes or villains. They appeared in the backgrounds of things — the walls that held when sieges came, the bridges that let armies cross rivers that would otherwise have stopped campaigns, the cisterns that kept cities alive through dry years. Unglamorous, structural, essential.

**Frost era:** When the Frost came, the houses that depended on open-field agriculture and distributed trade networks collapsed fastest. Stonehelm did not. Their construction knowledge turned directly into survival knowledge in a way that no other house's specialization could match. A house that knows how to build walls, seal gaps against cold, design enclosed growing spaces, and manage heat retention inside stone structures had exactly the skills that the Frost demanded. Stonehelm went inward. Literally. They sealed communities inside stone enclosures — not palaces, not fortresses, but functional survival structures, maximally efficient at keeping warmth and food supply inside and cold and chaos outside. The brown-haired Stonehelm lines survived the Frost years in conditions that were grim but not fatal because their leaders made engineering decisions that prioritized survival over comfort or social convention. Communities that might otherwise have scattered or collapsed stayed organized because the physical infrastructure held them together. Food production moved inside. Water systems were redirected into covered channels. Every structure that could be reinforced was reinforced. Stonehelm lost people during the Frost — every house did — but they lost fewer, proportionally, than almost any house that survived. And they emerged from it without the kind of institutional fracturing that broke Hartvale's population foundations and scattered Westland's frontier communities into disconnected bands. They came through intact.

**Post-Frost:** Stonehelm's post-Frost identity is the direct product of what they learned during it. Depth beats breadth. Endurance beats aggression. Infrastructure built before it is needed saves lives when it is needed. Their lords in the Age of Reclamation look like they have spent their lives in quarries and construction yards rather than receiving halls — brown-haired, physically solid, often marked with the particular calluses and posture of people who lift heavy things and think about structural problems all day. Their political instinct is defensive and deliberate. They do not expand quickly because they do not trust land they cannot defend properly. Every new territory Stonehelm absorbs is a commitment — they will build on it, fortify it, connect it to the rest of their network with road and wall, and they will not release it. Taking Stonehelm territory has always cost more than it appeared to be worth, because the fortifications are real and the defenders behind them have been told since childhood that holding is not a failure to advance, it is a victory in itself. Post-Frost Stonehelm does not celebrate conquest. It celebrates the construction project completed, the wall that held, the battle that the other side decided not to start because the fortifications were too thorough to make it worthwhile.

## Societal Advantage

**Fortified Ground:** Stonehelm units fighting in or adjacent to constructed fortifications receive a permanent defensive bonus that scales with the age of the fortification. A freshly built palisade provides a minor bonus. A stone wall built in the early game and still standing in the late game has accumulated a structural quality rating — Stonehelm engineers have been maintaining and improving it continuously — and provides a substantially larger defensive bonus than any other house could derive from the same investment. This means Stonehelm's early infrastructure decisions compound over time. A player who builds a stone garrison point in the first twenty minutes and holds it through midgame arrives at the late game with a defensive anchor that is functionally disproportionate to its original cost.

**Timing:** This advantage is minimal in the first fifteen minutes (construction is not yet complete), becomes meaningful in the midgame (held positions begin compounding), and is decisive in the late game if the positions have been maintained. It rewards patience and positional commitment over opportunistic expansion.

---

## Required Disadvantage

**Slow Ground:** Stonehelm armies move more slowly than any other house's equivalently composed force — not because they are weak, but because they carry more. Stonehelm soldiers bring tools: picks, stone-cutting implements, bracing materials, extra provisions for sustained operations. They are prepared to stay. This preparation costs movement speed. A Stonehelm army that commits to a defensive line is exceptional in that role. A Stonehelm army trying to pursue a retreating enemy, respond to a flank attack on distant territory, or move quickly to reinforce a surprise engagement is structurally incapable of doing so at the speed the situation demands. Westland will always arrive first. Even Ironmark, which is not a fast house, can cross the same ground more quickly.

This is not an adjustable limitation. It is architectural. Stonehelm soldiers are not slow because of poor training — they are slow because of how they are equipped and what they are expected to do when they arrive. A Stonehelm player who over-extends territory, tries to fight on multiple fronts simultaneously, or commits to an aggressive opening campaign will discover that their army cannot be in two places at once and cannot get to the place it needs to be quickly enough.

## Level 1 Unit Values

All deviations are from the 5/5 (Offense/Defense) baseline.

**Militia: 5/6**
Stonehelm militia are construction workers, quarry laborers, and stone-cutters in military service. They are physically powerful and trained to hold a position — to brace, absorb, and endure rather than press forward. Slightly defensive. No offensive deviation because Stonehelm militia are not taught to initiate; they are taught to resist.

**Swordsmen: 5/6**
Stonehelm swordsmen are trained in close-quarters defense inside confined stone spaces — narrow corridors, gate approaches, fortification interiors. This environment rewards tight defensive technique over aggressive footwork. Slightly better at absorbing attacks than delivering them.

**Spearmen: 5/6**
The Stonehelm spear formation is a wall, not a thrust. Their spears are braced against the ground and angled upward — a formation designed to stop cavalry and absorb charges rather than advance on an enemy. Slightly more defensive; among the best formations in the game for absorbing frontal assault.

**Hunters: 5/5**
Stonehelm hunters are surveyors and engineers as much as scouts — they are mapping terrain for construction potential, not racing for map control. Precisely neutral. No offensive advantage from aggression, no defensive advantage from caution. They are thorough and unhurried, which makes them useful but not exceptional in pure scouting speed.

**Bowmen: 5/6**
Stonehelm bowmen are fortification archers — trained to shoot from elevated positions, through arrow slits, and along wall parapets. They are exceptional in fixed defensive positions. They are not exceptional in open field or advancing situations. Slightly defensive, and this deviation becomes dramatically more pronounced when fighting from inside Stonehelm fortifications (see Fortified Ground advantage).

## Unique Unit: The Rampart Spear

**Category:** Infantry (upgraded Spearman line — this is an institutional evolution, not an independent unit type)

**Stat baseline:** 5/8

**Physical description:** The Rampart Spear carries a weapon that is meaningfully longer and heavier than a standard spear — not a pike in the strict military sense, but extended enough to create a reach advantage at formation range. The weapon is braced differently: a ground spike at the base allows the Rampart Spear to plant the weapon and use body weight and ground contact as a stabilizing system rather than relying solely on upper body strength. This changes the unit's role from "spear carrier" to "living fortification."

**Special abilities:**

*Cavalry Counter (passive):* When a cavalry unit charges a formation that contains Rampart Spears, the effectiveness of that charge is significantly reduced. The extended weapon reach means cavalry cannot close to contact range before taking casualties from the formation, and the braced ground spike means the spear does not deflect or give way under impact the way a hand-held spear would. Cavalry attacking a Rampart Spear line take significantly higher casualties than cavalry attacking any other Level 1 or Level 2 infantry. This is not a special activation — it is always active when the unit is in formation. Cavalry players who do not scout for Rampart Spears before committing a charge will lose far more than they expected.

*Anchor Point (active, cooldown):* A formation of Rampart Spears can declare an Anchor Point, planting their weapons and locking their position in place. In Anchor state, their defense value increases further — they are now a physical obstacle as well as a fighting unit. The tradeoff is complete immobility for the duration of the anchor: they cannot reposition, pursue, or respond to flanking until they disengage. An anchored Rampart Spear formation holding a narrow passage is among the most defensively efficient units in the game. An anchored Rampart Spear formation that gets outflanked is a unit that cannot escape.

**Weaknesses:** The Rampart Spear is significantly slower than standard Spearmen even before anchoring. They cannot pursue. In an open-field engagement where an enemy infantry force manages to get around their flanks, they cannot turn fast enough to respond adequately. Ranged units targeting the rear or flanks of a Rampart Spear formation will find that the orientation of the unit's weapons provides almost no protection from unexpected directions. They are a positional unit, not a responsive one. Played incorrectly — deployed in open terrain without support, or used in a mobile engagement role — they will underperform standard Spearmen.

**Squad interaction:** Rampart Spears interact with Stonehelm's squad trait (see below). When their squad has taken casualties, the remaining members automatically tighten formation and their Anchor state defense value increases proportionally. A Rampart Spear squad at 60% strength in Anchor state is nearly as defensively effective as it was at full strength. This makes attritional assault against them geometrically frustrating: the more you whittle them down, the harder the survivors hold.

---

## Squad Trait: Compression

When a Stonehelm squad takes casualties, the surviving members automatically compress their formation — they close gaps, tighten spacing, and effectively remove the visual and physical signature of their losses. This is not morale resilience (Stonehelm soldiers feel grief and exhaustion the same as any other house's soldiers). It is trained behavior: the response to loss in Stonehelm's military culture is not rage and it is not retreat. It is tightening.

**Mechanical effect:** Stonehelm squads that have taken casualties up to 50% do not suffer the standard degradation in defensive effectiveness that applies to all other houses' squads at equivalent strength. A Stonehelm squad at 60% strength defends as effectively as most houses' squads at 80% strength. Below 50% the degradation begins, but slowly — a Stonehelm squad at 40% still holds meaningfully. Only below 25% do Stonehelm squads begin to lose formation coherence at the standard rate.

**Counterplay:** The compression trait can be broken by sustained area-effect attacks rather than targeted attrition — if the gaps being closed are replaced by simultaneous casualties across the formation, the compression behavior cannot keep pace. Siege weaponry, grouped bowmen volleys, and Ironmark's Axemen (who hit broad target zones) are more effective against Stonehelm squads than single-combat attrition.

**Design note:** This trait is deliberately the mirror of Ironmark's Ferocity Under Loss. Ironmark squads that take casualties become more dangerous in attack. Stonehelm squads that take casualties become more durable in defense. Both reward commitment to a position under fire. They play completely differently: Ironmark escalates, Stonehelm endures.

## Strategic Identity

**Early game (first 20 minutes):** Stonehelm players who understand their house begin fortification immediately. The first gold does not go to military production — it goes to a garrison post, a wall segment, a tower that will still be standing in the midgame. This is the correct choice even though it feels slow. Stonehelm's early military is smaller than their opponents' but positioned correctly: holding land they can defend rather than reaching for land they cannot. The instinct to overextend is the early Stonehelm mistake. A Stonehelm player who chases early territory gains and tries to match Westland's speed or Ironmark's production numbers has broken their own strategic logic. The correct early game is deliberate, fortified, and slightly smaller than opponents. This will be uncomfortable.

**Mid game:** The compound interest of fortification begins to pay out. Positions built in the first twenty minutes now provide the Fortified Ground bonus at meaningful scale. Stonehelm's Rampart Spears come online and change the calculus for any opponent trying to use cavalry. The Stonehelm player who reaches the midgame with their core territory intact and their key fortifications holding has converted their slow early investment into a position that most opponents cannot crack cheaply. The midgame is where Stonehelm's identity becomes legible to opponents — they begin to understand that taking Stonehelm territory will cost them more than they expected and probably more than it is worth. Political dynamics shift. Other houses begin routing their expansion around Stonehelm rather than through it. This is a kind of dominance even if it looks like passivity from the outside.

**Late game:** A Stonehelm player who has protected their core territory through the midgame arrives at the late game with a set of deeply fortified, high-value defensive positions that produce resources and sustain armies indefinitely. The endgame question for Stonehelm is whether they can translate positional dominance into active victory paths. The fortifications are assets, not just defenses — they hold territory that generates the economic base for victory conditions that require sustained accumulation. Stonehelm's late game weakness is that they have spent the match optimizing for not losing. Winning requires eventually doing something. The players who navigate this well use their fortified positions as the foundation for a deliberate, methodical campaign that their opponents cannot match in endurance. The players who never solve it hold the most defensible territory in the game and lose on points.

---

## Strongest Victory Paths

**1. Territorial Governance:** Stonehelm's fortification compound bonus means their held territory generates reliable, sustained resource production at higher efficiency than the same territory would under any other house. A player who wins Territorial Governance with Stonehelm does not win by having the most territory — they win by having territory that produces more per tile than anyone else's, sustained across a full-length match. The Fortified Ground advantage is directly a Territorial Governance engine.

**2. Economic Dominance:** Stone construction has trade value. Stonehelm's engineering capabilities, when not entirely devoted to military fortification, translate into export of construction expertise and physical materials that generates gold accumulation. Their fortified settlements have lower maintenance costs per unit of production than equivalently sized Hartvale or Goldgrave settlements (they are built to last), which means their economic output is more sustainable across late-game attrition. Economic Dominance via Stonehelm is not flashy — it is the accumulated surplus of a civilization that wastes very little.

---

## Conviction Natural Tendency

**Direction:** Moral, with deliberate pragmatism.

Stonehelm's cultural memory is built around a specific lesson: cruelty during the Frost years was a luxury nobody could afford. Communities that held together survived. Communities that fragmented under the pressure of desperate leadership did not. The Stonehelm lords who successfully led their people through the Frost years tended to be the ones who maintained trust and cooperation inside the walls, who distributed resources fairly, and who built the social cohesion that made the physical infrastructure worth defending. This is not idealism — it is engineering. A population that believes its leadership deserves trust works harder, holds positions longer, and endures more before breaking. Stonehelm's moral tendency is functional, not sentimental.

This creates a specific conviction profile: Stonehelm players who run high-virtue playstyles get measurable benefits in unit endurance, population cooperation, and the effectiveness of Compression under stress. Stonehelm players who run cruel playstyles are not crippled — the house can do it — but they are working against an institutional grain that compounds over time. The cruelty costs show up in morale slow-burn, in the effectiveness of Compression under stress, and eventually in the loyalty calculus of fortification defenders. The walls hold better when the people inside them believe they are holding for something worth holding.

## Flavor Text

There is a quality of patience in a Stonehelm lord that other houses mistake for slowness. They stand at the edge of a contested valley and they do not do what you expect them to do. They do not advance. They do not make demands. They look at the terrain and they imagine, with the quiet specificity of people who have been trained from childhood to see structural potential in landscape, where a wall would go. Where an archer could stand with elevation advantage. Where a road would come in from the northwest and how it would need to be defended if someone tried to cut it in the third winter. They are doing all of this before anyone has said a word about the valley's ownership.

Fighting Stonehelm feels like fighting architecture. You know, before you reach their lines, that every position you approach has been considered from the side you are coming from. The killing ground was designed for you. The arrow slits face the angle you will be standing at. The Rampart Spears are braced against the exact kind of charge you were about to order. Stonehelm does not win by being clever in the moment. They win by having already been clever three weeks ago, when nobody was watching and everyone else was doing something more dramatic.

The brown-haired soldiers who walk out of Stonehelm keeps look like the men and women they are: people who carry heavy things and build with them. They are not elegant. They do not perform martial pageantry. They move with the practical, slightly deliberate gait of people who are conserving energy for the work ahead. When they take their positions they do not look heroic. They look installed. This is, to Stonehelm, the higher compliment. Heroism is temporary. A properly installed fortification outlasts everyone.

Other houses tell their children stories about great battles. Stonehelm tells their children stories about sieges that never happened — about the enemy lord who looked at the walls and rode away, because the walls were too thorough to assault profitably. The lesson is the same in both cases: the house that was prepared is the house that survived. The difference is that Stonehelm's version of the story has fewer corpses.

# HOUSE WESTLAND

---

## Identity Statement

Westland is the pressure house — the bloodline that wins by arriving before opponents are ready, by creating psychological and physical disruption ahead of the main engagement, and by converting the momentum of aggression into territorial and military outcomes before any other house can establish defensive footing.

---

## Color System

**Primary color:** Frontier crimson — a deep, saturated red that is neither the bloodred of battle standards nor the ceremonial scarlet of imperial houses. This is the color of the vivid red hair of the Westgrave lineage, adapted into cloth and paint: rich, assertive, impossible to miss at distance. Where Ironmark's forge-orange is industrial heat and Highborne's gold is institutional prestige, Westland's crimson is personal declaration. We are here. We see you. We are coming.

**Accent color:** Dusty ochre — a dry, sun-bleached yellow that reads like frontier soil baked hard in summer. This accent does not complement the crimson so much as it grounds it: the red says we are aggressive, the ochre says we have always been this way, this land made us, we have walked it too long to be impressed by anything you built on better ground. Together they read as heat — the color combination of a horizon at midday when the air is dry and the grass is yellow and the sky is washed out and you have been riding since before dawn.

**Visual design rationale:** Westland's color system is designed to be visible first and symbolic second. On a battlefield with nine distinct houses visible in formation, Westland's crimson and ochre is the combination that reads as aggressive at distance. This is intentional. Westland's psychological strategy includes the visual announcement of their presence — their banners are meant to be seen before their arrows arrive. Other houses' symbols carry genealogical meaning. Westland's colors carry one message: we are already here.

---

## Symbol System

**Dominant symbol:** The Riding Hawk. A hawk in mid-dive — not perched, not circling, specifically the moment of committed descent when the bird has identified its target and is past the point of pulling back. Rendered at a three-quarter angle, wings partially folded, head forward and down, talons forward for impact. The specificity of the dive posture is intentional: this is not a symbol of watching or patrolling. It is a symbol of a decision already made and currently in execution.

**Controlled variations:**

1. **Full hawk in dive (military standard):** The complete Riding Hawk, wings and talons rendered in full detail against a crimson ground, on regimental banners and campaign standards. The ochre appears as the border of the banner and in the hawk's eye — one point of heat in the center of the image. Used in formal military contexts.

2. **Wing silhouette (cavalry insignia):** The distinctive silhouette of the hawk's partially folded wings, reduced to clean geometry. Used on cavalry pennants and the saddle marks of Westland's Outriders. Simplified for speed of recognition: in a fast-moving engagement, the wing shape is identifiable before the full symbol resolves.

3. **The strike mark (personal combat insignia):** Just the talons — four forward-reaching claws rendered as clean downward strokes. Applied to blades, armor plates, and the interior of shields. Personal rather than regimental: a Westland soldier's combat identity, not their unit identity. It reads as a small, private declaration of intent.

4. **The circling hawk (garrison and watch symbols):** The hawk in a relaxed circle, wings spread wide, head level — the posture before the dive, when the bird is watching. Used to mark Westland watch posts, patrol routes, and garrison positions. Contextually reads as: we are observing this territory. The contrast with the dive posture is understood. A circling hawk means we have not struck yet. A diving hawk means we have.

5. **The paired hawks (alliance symbol):** Two hawks in parallel dive, side by side, slightly overlapping wing lines. Used when Westland enters military alliance. The symbol communicates joint aggression — two houses committed to the same strike rather than one watching the other. Westland does not use an arch or a clasped hand for alliance. They use a second hawk. Alliances are, to Westland, about coordinated action, not about mutual stability.

**Context rules:** The Riding Hawk is never rendered in a static or resting posture in military contexts. A perched hawk is used only on Westland grave markers — the bird at rest is the bird that has finished its work. Using a perched hawk on a military standard is considered deeply inauspicious in Westland culture, associated with death and the end of engagement. Visitors to Westland courts who gift objects bearing resting bird imagery are doing so without understanding what it means, and Westland lords notice.

---

## Cultural Origin Summary

**Pre-Frost:** Westland's origin is the frontier. Not frontier in the metaphorical sense of ambition and challenge — literally the physical edge of what the civilized world had mapped and administered. The Westgrave lineage established itself in contested territory where the mechanisms of political authority existed in theory but not in practice. Courts and administrators made decisions about frontier land that took months to arrive and often contradicted the reality on the ground by the time they got there. The people who actually lived on that land — who defended it, hunted it, built on it, and buried their dead in it — developed governance systems that could not wait for distant approval. They were not lawless. They had law. But it was law that derived from capability and presence, not from hereditary authorization. The Westgrave lords before the Frost were people who had always known that legitimacy came from doing, not from being. Their red-haired bloodline carried the physical distinctiveness of isolation — generations on a frontier that did not receive the same influx of population and bloodline mixing that the core territories did. They were already visible. Already recognizable as a distinct people. Before the Frost, they were the house on the edge of things. Fast, self-sufficient, occasionally alarming to their neighbors in the center. Not important enough to eliminate, too capable to absorb. The Westgrave lords of the pre-Frost era had a specific reputation: they arrived where they were needed before anyone had formally invited them, handled the situation according to their own judgment, and expected to be thanked for it later. Other houses found this behavior somewhere between useful and insufferable, depending on whether they needed something handled.

**Frost era:** The Frost vindicated the frontier. Where houses built on institutional depth and centralized infrastructure watched their systems collapse in sequence — trade routes that failed made supply chains that failed made political authority that failed — the Westgrave lineage's decentralized, self-sufficient communities scattered and survived individually. There was no single point of failure to cascade. When the central networks fell, Westland's communities were already operating without them. They did not have to adapt because they had never fully relied on the things that were breaking. The Frost years for Westland were not an organizational crisis — they were an extended version of the conditions they had always operated in. Cold, isolated, self-reliant, forced to act on local judgment without waiting for distant authority to validate the decision. The vivid red hair of the Westgrave bloodline became a survival signal during the Frost: other scattered communities learned that a red-haired figure arriving at your settlement meant someone was coming who knew how to keep things alive. The Westgrave community leaders became regional anchors during the worst years — not because they had formal authority but because they had proven competence and a reputation for arriving before you asked.

**Post-Frost:** When the Frost receded and the Age of Reclamation began, Westland consolidated faster than anyone expected. The scattered communities that had survived the Frost independently came back together around the Westgrave identity with the aggressive instinct intact and with a generation of experience in rapid self-organization. Post-Frost Westland is not a house that learned to be fast — it is a house that has always been fast and now has the scale and infrastructure to express that speed at military and political level. Their lords in the Age of Reclamation look like their ancestry: vivid red hair visible across distances that would require effort to see any other house's identifying features. They use this deliberately. Westland soldiers are told since childhood that being seen is not a vulnerability — it is a statement. You can see us because we want you to know we are coming. We are not hiding. We do not need to. Their aggressive instinct was not moderated by the Frost. If anything, it was confirmed. Speed had been the survival strategy. Speed would be the expansion strategy.

---

## Societal Advantage

**Frontier Tempo:** Westland armies complete all movement actions — march, advance, withdrawal, and repositioning — at a base speed modifier that is higher than any other house. This applies at all stages of the game. Westland's Outriders (unique unit) appear on the map earlier in the game than standard cavalry would become available, and at the standard cavalry availability point Westland can field a second generation of Outriders while other houses are still on their first. The practical effect is a consistent window where Westland arrives somewhere before opponents expect them — on a contested resource node, at a flank angle, at a settlement before the settlement has finished building its garrison. This window is widest in the early game, where the speed differential is most pronounced, and narrows as other houses complete fortification and supply chain infrastructure that compensates for Westland's positional advantage.

**Timing:** Critical in the first 25 minutes. Meaningful through the midgame. Requires increasingly deliberate management in the late game when opponents have established the infrastructure to compensate.

---

## Required Disadvantage

**No Depth:** Westland's entire strategic identity is built around the speed of arrival and the quality of first contact. What it is not built around is the sustained grinding attrition that follows. Westland units at extended engagement — fighting for the third hour, resupplied three times, rotating through the same contested position day after day — begin to degrade faster than any other house's equivalently trained units. This is not a morale failure in the simple sense. It is structural. Westland's logistics systems, command culture, and unit training are optimized for rapid decisive action. They have never developed the deep institutional infrastructure for sustained static warfare that Stonehelm built during the Frost or that Ironmark's production culture creates automatically. A Westland army trying to conduct a siege against a fortified Stonehelm position is the worst strategic matchup in the game — not because Westland soldiers are weaker, but because they are using a tool in the application it was never designed for. Extended sieges require patience, supply depth, and the willingness to grind. Westland has none of these things institutionally. Players who push Westland into long static engagements are removing the house's core advantage while fighting in a mode where their disadvantage is most exposed.

---

## Level 1 Unit Values

All deviations are from the 5/5 (Offense/Defense) baseline.

**Militia: 6/5**
Westland militia are the descendants of frontier communities that treated preparedness for violence as a basic civic skill rather than a professional specialization. They are slightly more effective at first engagement — hitting harder in the initial exchange — but not trained for sustained holding actions.

**Swordsmen: 6/5**
Westland swordsmen are trained in the aggressive cut-and-close style that frontier fighting rewards: the objective is to end the fight quickly, not to endure it. Slightly better in offense, standard in defense. Their training is optimized for the first thirty seconds of an engagement.

**Spearmen: 6/4**
The most extreme deviation in Westland's roster. Their spearmen are explicitly not trained as a wall — they are trained as a shock weapon. The Westland spear formation advances into an enemy position with the intention of breaking it rather than holding the line. They are terrible in a sustained defensive role: a Westland spear formation ordered to brace and hold against repeated cavalry charges will do so worse than almost any other house's spearmen. They exist to punch forward and break something. Do not deploy them as a wall.

**Hunters: 6/5**
Westland hunters are the most aggressive scouts in the game. They do not merely map territory — they contest it, harass enemy scouts, and establish forward positions before armies follow. Slightly more offensive, carrying the frontier tradition of treating reconnaissance as inherently an act of claim.

**Bowmen: 6/5**
Westland bowmen are trained to advance and fire — to harass retreating or repositioning enemies, to put pressure on supply lines at range, to be part of the forward momentum of the army rather than the rear anchor. Slightly more offensive.

## Unique Unit: The Outrider

**Category:** Light cavalry (mounted archer)

**Stat baseline:** 6/3

**Physical description:** The Outrider is a light cavalry archer on a fast, lightly armored horse — no plate, no heavy shield, carrying a short composite bow and a single sidearm blade. They are equipped for speed and harassment, not for direct combat. The rider and horse are selected for endurance at pace: Outriders cover ground quickly not just in sprints but across sustained distances, which is what makes them effective at supply line interdiction rather than only battlefield skirmishing.

**Special abilities:**

*Early Deployment (passive):* Outriders become available significantly earlier than standard cavalry. While other houses are still working toward the resource thresholds that unlock mounted units, Westland can field Outriders. This timing advantage compounds the Frontier Tempo societal benefit — not only does Westland move faster, it has cavalry-level harassment capability before any other house is logistically ready to respond to cavalry.

*Supply Line Pressure (active):* An Outrider unit assigned to a Supply Line Pressure action begins targeting the logistical chain of an opponent rather than engaging their army directly. This reduces the opponent's supply efficiency to armies in the targeted theater — not catastrophically in a single application, but persistently, and it stacks across multiple Outrider units. An opponent who ignores three Outrider squads operating on their supply lines for twenty minutes will find their frontline armies gradually undermined.

*Harassment (passive):* Outriders in motion take meaningfully reduced casualties from infantry counterattack — a unit of Outriders that fires and repositions before the enemy can close to contact range is exposing itself to far fewer defensive attacks than a unit that stands and trades. This is a passive benefit of the mobility inherent in the unit type, but Westland's Outriders have it more pronounced than standard cavalry archers.

**Weaknesses:** Outriders cannot stand in direct combat against armored infantry under any circumstances. Their 6/3 stat baseline is stark: they are extremely dangerous at range and in motion and essentially incapable of surviving close contact with anything that wears metal. A cornered Outrider unit that cannot disengage before infantry reaches it will be destroyed quickly. Experienced opponents who can close off the retreat angles and force an Outrider unit into direct contact will eliminate it efficiently. Stonehelm's Rampart Spear formations are particularly devastating against Outrider charges because the extended reach neutralizes the speed advantage before the Outrider can break away.

**Squad interaction:** Outrider squads use Westland's Compression Countermeasure (see below). When at full strength, they move faster and fire faster. Partial squads are not simply weaker — they are genuinely more vulnerable than the numbers suggest, because the coordinated harassment pattern that makes them effective requires multiple riders maintaining complementary angles. A depleted Outrider squad that has lost a third of its members is not operating at two-thirds efficiency — it is operating below that because the coordination structure has fractured.

---

## Squad Trait: Pressure from Strength

Westland squads at full or near-full strength (above 80% of initial members) operate with a bonus to movement speed and first-strike initiative — they are faster on approach and their initial attack in an engagement lands before the enemy's counterattack fully resolves. This represents the coordinated momentum of a fresh, organized unit moving with practiced intent.

**Mechanical effect:** At above 80% strength, Westland squads receive a movement speed bonus and a first-strike timing bonus. The first strike bonus means that in the opening exchange of a melee engagement, Westland strikes first — before the opponent's attack registers. Against equivalent units, this regularly produces an early casualty imbalance in Westland's favor before the sustained exchange begins. It is the mechanical expression of the frontier combat philosophy: arrive fast, hit first, create the advantage before the enemy has time to establish a defensive posture.

**Decay:** Below 80% strength, both bonuses begin to decrease. Below 60%, both bonuses are gone. Below 50%, the squad is performing at standard parameters. There is no rage or compression effect as in Ironmark or Stonehelm — a depleted Westland squad is just a depleted squad. It has lost its specific advantage and gained nothing to compensate. Westland's strategic logic pushes hard against using depleted squads in new engagements: pull them, resupply them, send them back at full strength.

**Design note:** This trait incentivizes the commitment of fresh forces. A Westland player who rotates their fresh squads forward and rests depleted ones is using the house correctly. A Westland player who grinds the same squads through repeated engagements is not only losing fighters — they are losing the speed and first-strike advantage that made those fighters dangerous in the first place.

---

## Strategic Identity

**Early game (first 20 minutes):** Westland's correct opening is pure aggression within a specific targeting priority. The first objective is map control — not territory possession in the settled, fortification-backed sense that Stonehelm pursues, but information and position. Westland's Hunters are more offensively capable than anyone else's at this stage and can contest scouts, deny reconnaissance, and establish forward positions before the opposing house understands what is happening. The second objective is economic disruption: Outriders hitting the early resource collection of an opponent before that opponent has any cavalry to respond with. Early Outrider pressure does not need to destroy anything — it needs to slow the opponent's economic build by forcing them to respond to a cavalry threat they are not yet equipped to counter. Westland's early game is designed to create a time debt in opponents: they are spending actions on Westland's harassment rather than on their own development.

**Mid game:** The speed advantage narrows as other houses complete defensive infrastructure and cavalry becomes available to everyone. Westland's midgame is about converting the time debt they created in the opening into territorial and positional outcomes before the window closes. This is the hardest period to play correctly as Westland — the opening aggression instinct needs to shift into something more strategic. The players who navigate this well use the accumulated early position to commit to a specific victory path and begin building toward it with the same speed they used in the opening. The players who just continue pressing forward without converting their gains into something durable find themselves in the late game with plenty of territory and no depth behind it.

**Late game:** Westland's late game is fundamentally a race. They need a specific victory condition to become decisive before Stonehelm's fortification depth, Ironmark's production compound, or Hartvale's population engine accumulates enough to overcome the speed advantage. A Westland player who is still fighting a grinding war in the late game has lost — not yet on paper, but in the strategic logic of the matchup. The late game is where Westland's Required Disadvantage (No Depth) becomes most costly. The solution is to not arrive at the late game in a war of attrition. Win earlier, or win via the specific paths that reward territorial and military momentum rather than sustained presence.

---

## Strongest Victory Paths

**1. Military Conquest:** Westland's arrival speed and Outrider harassment capability make them the most natural Military Conquest house. They can close victory conditions through territorial and military dominance before opponents consolidate the kind of defensive infrastructure that makes conquest expensive. Early military pressure that creates an opponent's early defeat is Westland's cleanest path.

**2. Territorial Governance (aggressive variant):** Westland can pursue Territorial Governance not through the Stonehelm model of deeply fortified, slowly accumulating production, but through the rapid acquisition of large quantities of territory in the early-to-mid transition. If Westland can hold land by holding opponents too off-balance to retake it — rather than by fortifying it — they can accumulate the territorial threshold needed for Governance victory through military presence rather than defensive investment. This is a high-variance path: if the opponent stabilizes and begins reclaiming, Westland does not have the defensive infrastructure to hold territory the way Stonehelm does.

---

## Conviction Natural Tendency

**Direction:** Neutral, with a historical lean toward Cruel.

Westland's cultural memory does not carry the moral architecture that Highborne or Trueborn developed. The frontier does not produce the institutional structures that codify virtue as strategic investment. What it produces is pragmatism: the decision that keeps your community alive is the correct decision, assessed on that basis alone. Pre-Frost and during the Frost, the Westgrave lords made decisions about resource allocation, community acceptance, and territorial defense that were judged by outcome, not by doctrine.

This creates a house whose players can run either direction with fewer natural penalties than most houses, because Westland's cultural memory is genuinely neutral in moral terms — but whose optimization pressures tend to push toward Cruel, because the fastest and most efficient expressions of Westland's military strategy involve actions the game codes as Cruel: burning supply lines, pressuring civilian populations, using speed to prevent opponents from organizing resistance. These things are not wrong in Westland's cultural framework. They are effective. The question the conviction system asks is whether effectiveness is the only value in play, and Westland's cultural tradition answers: usually yes.

---

## Flavor Text

A Westland lord's red hair is the first thing you see. This is not accident. Westland riders on the horizon are recognizable before you can make out their numbers, before you can assess their kit, before you can do any of the things a reasonable military mind should do when an unknown force appears in your peripheral vision. They want you to see them. They want you to feel the moment of recognition and then spend the next three minutes in the uncomfortable calculus of whether what you are doing is worth continuing to do when Westland is already approaching from the northeast.

The psychological pressure of Westland's early presence is part of the strategy. You know they move faster than you. You know their Outriders are already in places you were planning to go and haven't gone yet. You know that the supply convoy you sent two hours ago may have already been met. There is a specific kind of combat stress that Westland inflicts before the first engagement: the sense that you are behind, that they arrived somewhere you needed to be, that you are responding to them rather than acting independently. Other houses lose battles to Westland. Good players lose the initiative to Westland even in games they ultimately win.

In the Age of Reclamation, Westland lords at diplomatic councils are the people who have already left before the conclusion. They read faster than the room, decide faster than the room, and treat the deliberate pace of political negotiation as a kind of time they are not getting back. This makes them effective and genuinely difficult to be around. The frontier produced a people who experience caution as a kind of loss — every moment spent assessing is a moment the other house is using to get somewhere first.

The red-haired soldiers who ride out of Westland keeps have a quality about them that is distinct from Ironmark's purposeful severity or Stonehelm's patient solidity. They look alive in a specific way — alert, slightly restless, oriented toward whatever is happening over the next ridge rather than fully present in the current position. This is not anxiety. This is a people whose cultural memory tells them that the most important things happen to whoever arrives first.

# HOUSE HARTVALE

---

## Identity Statement

Hartvale is the people house — the bloodline that treats population as the primary strategic resource, that wins by fielding more bodies than any opponent can answer, and that absorbs losses and demographic setbacks with a resilience built from having already survived the worst population catastrophe of the known world.

---

## Color System

**Primary color:** Harvest gold — a warm, medium-deep yellow that reads like cut grain in early autumn. Not the bright coin-gold of Goldgrave (which is economic, precise, commercial) and not the darker institutional gold of Highborne (which is ceremonial and hierarchical). Hartvale's gold is organic, agricultural, the color of the thing that feeds people. It is warm where Goldgrave's gold is cool, domestic where Highborne's gold is elevated.

**Accent color:** Hearthstone red — a deep, warm brick-red that reads as the inside of a kiln or the side of a house fire that has been burning since before you arrived. Where Westland's crimson is aggressive declaration, Hartvale's red is the warmth of a maintained interior — the fire that was tended through the Frost years, the hearth around which the surviving community organized. It reads as domestic rather than martial, which is exactly the right signal for a house whose power derives from community rather than individual capability.

**Visual design rationale:** The harvest gold and hearthstone red combination is the warmest palette in the nine-house color system. It is intentionally welcoming — the color combination of a place where people live and eat and organize their lives. This is strategic, not incidental. Hartvale's expansion strategy involves absorbing neutral settlements and integrating new populations, and the visual signal of their approach is deliberately non-threatening. The color combination says: we feed people and we keep them warm. Other houses' color systems communicate power or identity or prestige. Hartvale's communicates capacity to sustain life.

---

## Symbol System

**Dominant symbol:** The Hearthfire. A stylized flame rendered in clean, geometric form — not the wild irregular flame of an open fire, but the contained, measured flame of a hearth that someone has been tending carefully. The flame rises from a base that reads as a foundation stone, with two lateral extensions that suggest the arms of a hearth enclosure. It is the fire that did not go out during the Frost years. That is the entire cultural content of this symbol, and Hartvale carries that content explicitly.

**Controlled variations:**

1. **Full hearthfire with foundation stone (formal military standard):** The complete symbol — flame, lateral arms, and foundation stone rendered in harvest gold on hearthstone red field. Used on formal military banners, alliance documents, and governmental seals. The foundation stone is always present in formal applications because it represents the community that contains and maintains the fire.

2. **The flame without foundation (campaign standard):** The flame portion alone, without the base and arms, rendered as a stylized three-prong shape. Used on campaign banners during active military operations where the full symbol is too detailed for fast recognition at distance. Faster to produce and read than the full symbol.

3. **The paired hearthstone (family and community markers):** Two flames side by side, slightly leaning toward each other, rendered in reduced scale. Used to mark communities that have been formally integrated into Hartvale's population structure — not conquered, integrated. There is a cultural distinction in Hartvale between a settlement that was taken militarily and a settlement that was brought in. The paired flame appears on communities that came willingly or were absorbed through the Hartvale model of population management. A single flame marks communities that were taken by force.

4. **The cold hearth (mourning symbol):** A flame outline without interior fill — just the shape, not the light. Used on memorial stones and in the specific cultural ceremony Hartvale observes for mass population loss. The cold hearth was the specific visual language developed during the Frost years for communities that did not survive: you placed the cold hearth stone where a settlement had been and where the people who lived there were gone. Hartvale has more cold hearth stones in their historical territory than any other house, and they do not remove them.

5. **The hearthline (territorial boundary markers):** The flame symbol reduced to a single vertical line with two small horizontal strokes at the base — almost runic in simplification. Used on boundary markers, road signs, and supply chain nodes. Readable from horseback. Means: Hartvale maintains this.

**Context rules:** The hearthfire is never rendered as a weapon or weapon-adjacent symbol. Hartvale has a specific cultural prohibition against combining their fire symbol with martial imagery — no flames on blades, no flaming weapon motifs, no fire-and-sword combinations. The fire means life-sustaining, not destructive. Soldiers wear the symbol on the interior of their armor or on personal items, not prominently displayed in combat contexts. In combat, Hartvale's soldiers carry unit-identification colors rather than the symbol itself.

---

## Cultural Origin Summary

**Pre-Frost:** Hartvale's pre-Frost identity was built around scale. Where Ironmark built high-quality specialist capability and Stonehelm built durable infrastructure, Hartvale built population. Their territory in the pre-Frost era was among the most densely populated in the known world because they were exceptionally good at the systems that produce and sustain large numbers of people: agricultural optimization, community integration, the management of diverse populations in close proximity without the social fracturing that population density tends to produce at scale. The Hartborne lords before the Frost were administrators and community managers as much as military leaders — their most valuable skills were the ones that kept large populations fed, organized, and cooperating. This made them politically significant far beyond what their military capability alone would have suggested. Population is the foundation of every other kind of power: armies, economic output, agricultural production, and the political weight of having more people who depend on you than anyone else has. Hartvale understood this before the Frost as an operating principle. Other houses understood it in theory. Hartvale lived it institutionally. Their pre-Frost reputation was not for martial glamour or religious authority but for the specific, unglamorous competence of running the largest communities in the world without them falling apart.

**Frost era:** The Frost destroyed Hartvale more than it destroyed any house that survived. Population-dense communities that depend on optimized agricultural systems have a catastrophic vulnerability: when the agricultural system fails, the population can die faster than sparse communities because there are more people competing for resources that have suddenly become insufficient. Hartvale's entire pre-Frost advantage — the dense, well-integrated communities that had produced their power — became a structural liability in the first Frost winter. They starved faster than houses that had always operated with less. Disease spread through their communities in patterns that sparse frontier settlements like Westland never experienced. The social cohesion that Hartvale had built with such skill under normal conditions fractured under the weight of desperate resource competition. Communities that had been models of integration collapsed into factional violence. The orange-haired Hartborne lords who survived the Frost years emerged from them with a specific kind of grief that the other surviving houses do not fully share: they had been the most populous house in the world, and the Frost had nearly finished them. They did not simply suffer the Frost. They were specifically, architecturally targeted by the Frost in ways that their pre-Frost strengths had made them vulnerable to.

**Post-Frost:** Hartvale's post-Frost identity is driven by two simultaneous imperatives that never resolve into a single clear priority. The first imperative is recovery: they lost the most, proportionally, and every Hartborne lord in the Age of Reclamation is operating with a permanent background awareness of how many they lost and how far the recovery has come. The second imperative is prevention: they will never be that vulnerable again. Their population-management philosophy in the Age of Reclamation is built around redundancy, diversification, and an integration model that does not concentrate people so densely that a single agricultural failure can cascade. They still build large communities — that is still the Hartvale identity, the thing they know how to do better than anyone — but they build them differently, with supply chain redundancy and community network design that the pre-Frost version of Hartvale never thought was necessary. Their orange-haired lords have an almost uncomfortable focus on demographic data: birth rates, community size, integration pace, resource consumption per person. This is not coldness. It is the opposite of coldness. It is the obsession of people who lost enormous numbers of their bloodline and will not stop until the numbers come back.

---

## Societal Advantage

**Population Engine:** Hartvale produces population (the underlying resource that generates militia, laborers, and eventually all military units) at a higher rate per settled community than any other house. At equivalent economic development levels, a Hartvale settlement will have a larger underlying population than an equivalent Ironmark, Stonehelm, or Westland settlement. This underlying population advantage translates into three specific mechanical expressions:

First, Hartvale produces Militia faster and more cheaply than any other house — not better Militia, but more of them, sooner, for fewer resources. In the early game this means Hartvale can field a numerically larger force for the same investment than any opponent.

Second, Hartvale's Bowmen are produced at a lower resource cost per unit than any other house's Bowmen — not because they are cheaper to train but because their population base supports a larger pool of candidates and their logistics system moves them into service faster. At equivalent investment, Hartvale fields more Bowmen.

Third, Hartvale can integrate neutral settlements — converting them to population contributors — at a faster pace than any other house. Where Trueborn converts through diplomacy and Westland converts through threat or conquest, Hartvale absorbs through population management: they arrive at a neutral settlement with the Hearthmasters and begin integrating the local population into Hartvale's supply and production network. This is slower than Westland's military takeover and faster than Trueborn's diplomatic process. The integrated settlement produces population for Hartvale's engine.

**Timing:** Present at game start, significant by midgame when production volume begins to compound, decisive in late game if unchecked.

---

## Required Disadvantage

**Quality Ceiling:** Hartvale's individual units are not exceptional. Their 5/5 baseline across the board is not a starting point that gets pushed upward through house bonuses — it is where Hartvale stays. Where Ironmark's Swordsmen hit harder because of better steel, and Stonehelm's Spearmen hold better because of trained defensive formations, and Westland's Militia hits faster because of frontier conditioning, Hartvale's equivalent units are precisely average because Hartvale's population-management system produces soldiers at scale, not at peak quality. A Hartvale Swordsman is exactly as good as the baseline says they are. This is a genuine limitation in single-unit engagements where quality matters more than quantity — in a one-on-one fight between a Hartvale Swordsman and an Ironmark Swordsman, the Ironmark unit wins at a rate that reflects the underlying stat differential. Hartvale answers this with more Swordsmen, which is a correct answer in many situations and an insufficient answer in situations where quantity cannot compensate for quality: key chokepoints, high-value elimination targets, elite unit engagements in the late game.

---

## Level 1 Unit Values

All deviations are from the 5/5 (Offense/Defense) baseline.

**Militia: 5/5**
Precisely neutral, but Hartvale produces Militia at higher volume and lower cost than any other house. The unit itself is not better — it is simply available in larger numbers sooner. In a head-to-head quality comparison, a Hartvale Militia and any other house's Militia perform identically. Hartvale's advantage is numerical.

**Swordsmen: 5/5**
Precisely neutral. No deviation. Hartvale Swordsmen are as good as the game's average soldier, no better, no worse. Their advantage in the Swordsmen line is logistics: Hartvale can maintain larger Swordsmen forces in the field for longer because their supply chain (centered on the Hearthmasters) is more efficient.

**Spearmen: 5/5**
Precisely neutral. Hartvale spear formations are reliable and unremarkable. They will not break early and they will not press hard. They exist in larger numbers than opponents can easily match.

**Hunters: 5/5 with speed modifier**
Hartvale hunters are neutral in combat but move faster than any other house's hunters. They are not scouts in the martial-aggression sense that Westland hunters are — they are population surveyors, community assessors, and advance integration teams. They move quickly because identifying and reaching neutral settlements before opponents is part of Hartvale's integration strategy. They cannot contest Westland's aggressive scouts in direct engagement, but they cover the map faster than anyone expects a neutral-stat unit to cover it.

**Bowmen: 5/5**
Precisely neutral, but Hartvale fields more Bowmen per equivalent resource investment than any other house. A Hartvale Bowmen formation is not better than a Stonehelm Bowmen formation at an individual archer level. There are simply more of them.

---

## Unique Unit: The Hearth Masters

**Category:** Support (non-combat — the most consequential non-combat unit in the game)

**Stat baseline:** 2/2 (they cannot fight and should not try)

**Physical description:** The Hearth Masters are not a military unit in any meaningful sense. They are community administrators, supply chain engineers, and population managers who have been embedded with the army to do for military operations what they do for civilian settlements: make large numbers of people function together over sustained periods. A Hearthmasters unit in the field looks like a logistics convoy commanded by very competent civilians wearing Hartvale insignia. They carry records. They assess, they count, they organize. They have a small armed escort and no meaningful combat capability of their own.

**Special abilities:**

*Supply Line Extension (passive):* Armies operating with Hearthmasters present can sustain operations farther from their base settlements than any other house's armies without Hearthmasters. The standard supply radius — the distance from a base at which an army begins taking attrition from non-combat supply failure — is significantly extended for any Hartvale army with Hearthmasters attached. This is how Hartvale conducts extended campaigns: not by being harder in combat but by being more capable of staying in the field.

*Attrition Resistance (passive):* Non-combat casualties — losses from disease, exposure, hunger, and supply failure — are dramatically reduced for Hartvale armies in the field when Hearthmasters are present. These invisible losses compound over time in a long campaign and become decisive in the late game. Hartvale armies with Hearthmasters arrive at month three of a campaign with more people than they left with, relative to an equivalent army from another house that has been bleeding 1-2% of strength per week to non-combat causes.

*Settlement Integration (active):* When positioned adjacent to a neutral settlement, Hearthmasters can begin an Integration action. This is a time-limited process (not instant) that, on completion, adds the settlement's population to Hartvale's production engine and begins generating the supply bonuses the settlement can provide. Faster than military takeover (because it doesn't require securing the settlement militarily before integration begins) and slower than Trueborn's diplomatic conversion (which has unique bonuses not available to Hearthmasters). Unique to Hartvale.

**Weaknesses:** Hearthmasters cannot fight. Their 2/2 stat baseline is not a typo — they are soft targets. Any enemy unit that reaches a Hearthmasters column will eliminate it quickly. The penalty for losing a Hearthmasters unit is severe: armies that were operating on extended supply lines suddenly return to standard supply radius parameters, active Integration actions are cancelled, and the attrition resistance disappears immediately. Armies in the field that suddenly lose their Hearthmasters suffer an immediate morale hit on top of the logistics regression. Protecting the Hearthmasters is one of the central tactical challenges of playing Hartvale correctly. Experienced opponents who understand Hartvale will prioritize Hearthmasters elimination above almost any other tactical objective.

**Squad interaction:** See Hartvale's squad trait below.

---

## Squad Trait: Resupply

Hartvale squads in the field, when not in active combat, receive a slow trickle of squad member replacements drawn from the local population pool when operating near Hartvale-aligned settlements or Hearthmasters. This is not health regeneration — it is literal replacement of fallen squad members with new ones drawn from Hartvale's surplus population.

**Mechanical effect:** Over time, between engagements, Hartvale squads trend back toward full strength at a rate that is not available to other houses. A squad that fought to 70% strength and then had twenty minutes of non-combat time near a Hartvale settlement or Hearthmasters will approach 85-90% strength before the next engagement. This compounds over a long campaign: Hartvale squads enter the late game with higher average strength than any other house's equivalently deployed squads because they have been slowly resupplying throughout.

**Limits and counterplay:** The Resupply trait requires proximity to a population source. Squads operating in deep enemy territory without Hearthmasters or nearby Hartvale settlements cannot resupply. The trait provides no in-combat benefit — it operates exclusively between engagements. An opponent who can keep Hartvale squads in constant continuous engagement, never giving them the rest windows that enable resupply, can deny the trait its effect. Westland's harassment strategy is directly designed to do this: never let Hartvale armies rest, keep them under constant attritional pressure, prevent the resupply windows. This is a core component of the Westland versus Hartvale matchup.

**Design note:** The Resupply trait reflects Hartvale's population-depth advantage in the most direct way possible. Other houses bleed out over a long campaign. Hartvale continuously replaces what it loses from the vast surplus of human capital it has spent the game building. Playing against Hartvale in the late game has a specific quality: the enemy army that was at 65% strength two hours ago is somehow back at 80% strength now, and you never saw a resupply convoy come through.

---

## Strategic Identity

**Early game (first 20 minutes):** Hartvale's opening is demographic. While other houses are building military production and fortification infrastructure, Hartvale is building population capacity — the thing that will generate every other resource over time. Their first buildings are not barracks or walls but community centers, agricultural optimizations, and the infrastructure that accelerates population growth in existing settlements. This feels slow in the first ten minutes because it is slow in the first ten minutes. Hartvale's Hunters are moving fast to identify neutral settlements for integration, and Hearthmasters are following close behind them to begin that integration before opponents can contest it. The Militia they field in the opening is cheap and available in quantity, which lets them hold contested zones numerically without the quality of military investment other houses are making. The early game goal is not military dominance. It is demographic head start.

**Mid game:** The population engine begins to generate compound returns. Hartvale's numerical advantage in cheap units starts to create real pressure because numbers that an opponent expected to be manageable are now too large to manage at the same time as other strategic objectives. The Hearthmasters are enabling military operations at ranges other houses cannot sustain, which means Hartvale's midgame expansion is larger than the territory immediately surrounding their starting position. The integrated neutral settlements are producing population that feeds into the army. The Bowmen formations, which were slightly oversized in the opening, are now significantly oversized relative to what any other house can field at equivalent investment. A Hartvale player who reaches the midgame with a functioning Hearthmasters network and a large integrated territory has what the game designers call the Population Cascade: too many people to slow down.

**Late game:** Hartvale's late game is a war of attrition that Hartvale wins by not losing at the same rate. Their squads are slowly filling back to strength between engagements. Their armies are still being resupplied by a population engine that has been running for the full match. Their Hearthmasters are maintaining supply chains that allow operations far from their core territory. The late-game Hartvale army is not the best individual-unit army in the game — an Ironmark production army with maximum steel infrastructure fielding fully upgraded Axemen will beat a Hartvale equivalent unit-for-unit. But Hartvale is not fighting unit-for-unit. They are fighting force-for-force, and their force keeps being there, larger than it should logistically be, long after the opponent expected it to have deteriorated.

---

## Strongest Victory Paths

**1. Territorial Governance:** Hartvale accumulates territory through integration rather than conquest, which means their territorial gains come with immediate population production attached. Integrated settlements are not simply controlled land — they are active contributors to the population engine. Territorial Governance victory for Hartvale is the cleanest expression of their strategic identity: they hold more territory, it is all producing for them, and the accumulated production advantage is how they win.

**2. Economic Dominance:** Population is the foundation of economic output. Hartvale's large integrated population produces more agricultural surplus, more labor, and more trade goods than any equivalent territory under any other house. Their Hearthmasters, when not deployed in military logistics roles, function as economic administrators who optimize settlement production. A Hartvale player who pursues Economic Dominance is treating the population engine as an economic engine, which it also is. The late-game Hartvale economy has more going into it than anyone else's.

---

## Conviction Natural Tendency

**Direction:** Moral, with practical grief.

Hartvale's conviction tendency is the most morally weighted in the game's roster, but it is weighted differently from Highborne's institutional virtue. Highborne believes in virtue as a form of divine legitimacy. Hartvale believes in virtue because they remember what cruelty during a crisis cost them. The communities that fragmented into desperate factional violence during the Frost years lost more people than the communities that maintained cooperation and distributed resources fairly, even when fair distribution meant everyone had less. The Hartborne lords who survived the Frost and rebuilt in its aftermath had spent years watching the calculus of cruelty play out in real time: short-term extraction caused long-term collapse, and the people who paid for that collapse were always the people at the bottom of the hierarchy, which was also the people Hartvale depended on to produce everything.

Hartvale's moral tendency is therefore not idealistic. It is deeply practical and deeply personal. Playing cruel with Hartvale is not just fighting the cultural grain — it is mechanically undermining the population integration system that is the house's entire source of power. Neutral settlements integrate faster when they believe Hartvale treats its people well. Integrated populations produce more when they are not afraid. The Resupply squad trait is partially a function of population willingness to send their people into service for a lord they trust. Run a cruel Hartvale and you are degrading the integration rate, reducing population cooperation bonuses, and slowing resupply — all of which reduce the Population Engine to something closer to what other houses can match. The house works at full capacity only when the people who are its primary resource believe they are valued by it.

## Flavor Text

The orange-haired lords of Hartvale count things. This is the observation every other house eventually makes about them, usually phrased with some mix of incomprehension and faint condescension: they count people. They count births and deaths and community sizes and integration rates and the number of years it takes a resettled population to begin producing surplus. They carry the numbers of the Frost with them the way other houses carry battle honors — not as something to be proud of but as something that must never be allowed to happen again. Forty percent. That is the number that appears in the oldest Hartborne family documents, in the oral records that predate the written ones, in the specific way that Hartvale lords speak about loss. They lost forty percent of their people. Not territory. Not political authority. People. The underlying resource. The thing everything else was built from.

An army commanded by a Hartvale lord does not look like what you expect when you first encounter it. It is larger than it should be for the territory that produced it. It is sustained in the field longer than logistics should permit. You fight it for two months and it is no smaller than it was. You eliminate a squad and the next time you see that unit it has somehow refilled. The Hearthmasters move through Hartvale lines with the organized efficiency of people who have spent their professional lives thinking about how many people it takes to maintain how much capacity for how long. Fighting Hartvale is not fighting an army. It is fighting a civilization on campaign.

Their soldiers are not individually impressive. A Hartvale swordsman is exactly as dangerous as a swordsman should be — no more. What makes a Hartvale soldier psychologically distinctive is the company. There are always more of them than you expected. There were more last time, too. And in the settlement two days' march behind the front line, the orange-haired administrator who arrived with the Hearthmasters two weeks ago has already set up the systems that will produce another cohort for the spring. The Hartvale soldier knows that if they fall, someone will take their place. Not as consolation — as operational reality. Hartvale's military culture is built on the understanding that the individual soldier is valued, the loss is genuinely grieved, and the next person is already coming. These things are all simultaneously true and they do not contradict each other.

To fight Hartvale is to play a different kind of game than the one you thought you were playing. You came to win battles. They came to outlast them.

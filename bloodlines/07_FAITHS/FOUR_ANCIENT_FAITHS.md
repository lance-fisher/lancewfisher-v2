# Bloodlines — The Four Ancient Faiths

The four ancient faiths that predate the founding houses and shape the spiritual landscape of the game world. Each faith offers a distinct worldview, unique mechanics, and a different relationship to the act of sacrifice.

### Content Added 2026-03-15

## The Old Light (Covenant of the First Flame)

A faith centered on protection, mercy, divine guardianship, and the defense of innocents. Also described as centered around enlightenment, unity, order, societal cohesion, disciplined governance, and defensive stability.

The Old Light emphasizes the preservation of life, communal welfare, and the belief that divine fire illuminates the path to righteous civilization. Followers seek to protect the weak and maintain societal order through compassionate governance.

---

## The Blood Dominion (The Red Covenant)

A faith centered on conquest, sacrifice, domination, and the belief that strength demonstrates divine favor. Darker in tone, this covenant carries moral cost and conviction consequences but provides strong military benefits.

The Blood Dominion holds that power is the truest measure of divine approval. Sacrifice, whether symbolic or literal, demonstrates commitment to the covenant. Followers gain military advantages but risk ideological isolation and conviction consequences.

---

## The Order (Covenant of the Sacred Mandate)

A faith built around law, doctrine, institutional authority, and structured governance under divine mandate. Emphasizes authority, rigid doctrine, hierarchy, and administrative control.

The Order sees governance itself as a sacred act. Laws are divine instructions. Institutional structures are the physical expression of heavenly will. Followers gain administrative and governance advantages but are bound by doctrinal rigidity.

---

## The Wild (Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots)

A primal faith rooted in nature mysticism, ancient spirits, and the balance of the natural world. Emphasizes adaptive survival, environmental strength, and ancient rooted power.

The Wild predates all other covenants. Its followers believe in the sentient power of the earth, forests, and waters. Strength comes from harmony with the natural world. Followers gain environmental and survival advantages but may struggle with industrialized expansion.

---

## Faith Mechanics Overview

Faith is governed by three interacting components:
1. **Covenant** — the religion the dynasty follows (one of the four above)
2. **Faith Intensity** — the degree to which the population actively practices the faith
3. **Faith Alignment** — a sliding scale representing how the covenant is practiced along a light vs dark spectrum

**Faith Alignment Example:**
A dynasty may follow the Blood Dominion but renounce human sacrifice and instead conduct symbolic or animal offerings (lighter interpretation). Another dynasty may practice brutal blood rites and sacrificial rituals (darker interpretation). Both remain within the same covenant but differ in alignment. Large alignment differences between dynasties of the same covenant can lead to internal religious conflict and schisms.

**Faith Intensity:**
- Must be maintained through upkeep (not a passive buff)
- Costs resources
- Scales with population participation
- Higher intensity unlocks stronger abilities
- As faith grows stronger it can radicalize

**Faith Actions:**
Rituals, offerings, temple construction, priesthood expansion, religious festivals, sacrificial rites. Faith actions consume resources and may also consume captured units or population.

**Spiritual Manifestations (at extreme intensity):**
Angels, demons, cursed armies, healing, resurrection, plague removal, nature forces. World-level pressure from extreme dark behavior.

**Faith Buildings:**
- Shrine — small religious structure increasing faith intensity
- Temple — major religious structure enabling powerful rituals
- Grand Sanctuary — late-game structure tied to faith victory

## THE OLD LIGHT — Covenant of the First Flame

### Theological Identity

The Old Light holds that something made the world with intention, and that intention was good. The fire of creation still burns in every living person, every family, every community that chooses to protect rather than prey. The Frost was not punishment — it was abandonment, the consequence of a world that forgot what it was made to protect. The Reclamation is not recovery — it is the chance to return to what the world should have been.

The Old Light is not pacifist. It is morally serious. There is a significant difference. A dynasty that shelters behind the Old Light's reputation for mercy while doing nothing to earn that reputation is practicing hypocrisy, and the covenant is specifically aware of hypocrisy.

### Faith Building Progression

**Shrine of the First Flame:** The foundation. The First Flame Shrine requires a small ongoing candle and oil offering (minor gold and food cost) and generates low faith intensity. Available from Level 1. The flame is kept burning at all times — if the settlement loses power or is sacked, the flame may be extinguished (temporary faith intensity collapse in that territory until re-lit).

**Temple of the Covenant:** Mid-game structure. Requires sustained Shrine presence in the territory for a minimum of two game cycles before construction begins. The Temple requires a Flame Keeper — a Bloodline Member or appointed specialist who resides in the Temple and conducts regular services. Flame Keeper quality affects the faith intensity the Temple generates. A Flame Keeper who is also a Faith Prodigy generates significantly more intensity.

**Grand Sanctuary — The Hall of First Light:** Late-game, Level 4 minimum. A physical expression of the dynasty's commitment to the covenant. The Hall of First Light requires:
- An active Temple in the territory
- A Flame Keeper of at least moderate experience
- A substantial gold investment (the Hall is visually and architecturally significant)
- A one-time population contribution (workers who become permanent caretakers — consumed from the population pool)

### Ritual System

**Ritual of Sanctuary:** Performed at Shrine level. Designates a territory as under Old Light protection. For a period, civilian populations in the territory are immune to deliberate civilian targeting by enemy forces (game mechanic: enemy units that target civilian structures in a Sanctuary territory during this ritual's active period incur conviction penalties on the attacking dynasty's side — their own soldiers recognize what they're doing). Cost: moderate gold.

**The Healing Vigil:** Performed at Temple level. After a battle in or adjacent to the territory, the Temple can conduct a Healing Vigil that restores morale to all surviving units more quickly than natural recovery. Cost: moderate food and gold. Duration: one game cycle. Has a cooldown before it can be repeated.

**The Flame Procession:** A public faith event that routes through the dynasty's territories, increasing faith intensity in every territory it passes through. Conducted by a Flame Keeper accompanied by a Militia or ceremonial guard unit. Cost: moderate gold over the procession's duration, mild food. Effect: faith intensity boost in each territory it visits, and a population loyalty bonus that persists for several cycles.

**The Great Blessing:** Performed at Grand Sanctuary level. Once per Level of play. A mass ceremony that provides the most significant morale and faith intensity boost the Old Light can generate short of a divine manifestation. Requires: Flame Keeper at senior level, significant gold and food, attendance of one Bloodline Member. Effect: all allied units receive a morale boost that persists for one full game cycle; faith intensity across all territories with Shrine or higher rises; population loyalty increases dynasty-wide.

**Dark Doctrine — The Inquisition:** A formal investigation and suppression campaign conducted by an Inquisitor unit in a territory. Targets heterodox faith expressions within the Old Light or outright rival faith practice. Gradually reduces enemy faith intensity in occupied territories and generates intelligence about covert faith networks. Cost: Inquisitor unit deployment, gold. Conviction cost: moderate Cruel shift — suppression is not a neutral act, and the population knows it.

**Dark Doctrine — The Purge:** Escalated from an Inquisition. When the Inquisition identifies and eliminates a heterodox population, the Purge formally removes them from the territory — through execution, exile, or forced conversion. Generates significant faith intensity gain but major conviction shift toward Cruel. Triggers Heresy event risk if the purged population has sympathizers within the dynasty.

### Conviction-Faith Interactions

**Light Doctrine + Moral Conviction:** The full expression. Maximum faith intensity possible. Population loyalty compound bonus. The Flame Keeper generates extra intensity passively. Divine manifestation events become possible at sustained peak.

**Divine Manifestation (Light + Moral):** At sustained peak intensity with Moral conviction, a manifestation event may trigger. For the Old Light: a wave of healing affecting all units in a radius of the Grand Sanctuary; a plague removal from any territory currently suffering; a morale restoration event that affects even routing units within the covenant's territory.

**Dark Doctrine + Cruel Conviction:** The Inquisition machine at full power. Suppression becomes efficient and merciless. The Inquisitor units gain effectiveness. Enemy faith intensity drops faster. The Trueborn coalition formation probability rises sharply — the world can see what is happening.

## THE BLOOD DOMINION — The Red Covenant

### Theological Identity

Power is the truest measure of divine approval. The universe does not reward the humble — it rewards the strong, the willing, and the committed. Sacrifice is not merely a ritual. It is a demonstration of understanding: that the world requires something real from those who want something real in return. The Blood Dominion does not ask for faith. It asks for proof.

The most dangerous Blood Dominion player is not the one who kills most eagerly. They are the one who knows exactly when sacrifice advances power and when it merely invites premature retaliation. The covenant rewards calculation as much as it rewards commitment.

### Faith Building Progression

**Blood Altar:** The foundation. A stone altar with permanent markings in Blood Dominion iconography. The Blood Altar requires a regular offering — which at Shrine level means livestock from the Ranch. The offering consumes one unit of Ranch output per cycle. In exchange, it generates faith intensity and a minor combat morale bonus to any unit that witnesses the ritual.

**Temple of the Red Covenant:** Mid-game. Requires sustained Blood Altar presence and a Covenant Priest assignment. The Temple enables larger rituals with larger effects — and begins requiring human sacrifice for the most significant ones in dark doctrine. Light doctrine Temple rituals substitute elaborate animal sacrifice and celestial timing.

**Grand Sanctuary — The Altar of Ascension:** Late-game, Level 4 minimum. The Altar of Ascension is the most physically imposing faith structure in the game. It requires:
- Active Temple and Blood Altar in the territory
- A Covenant Priest of senior level
- Large gold investment
- One-time population contribution consumed as permanent ritual attendants
- Dark Doctrine requirement: at least one great sacrifice (Bloodline Member or equivalent) conducted at the Temple before the Grand Sanctuary is complete

### Ritual System

**The Offering:** Performed at Blood Altar level. A livestock sacrifice consumed from Ranch production. Generates moderate faith intensity, a minor morale bonus to all units in the territory for one cycle. Cost: one Ranch production unit. Available from Level 1.

**The War Offering:** Performed before a military engagement. Prisoners of war (or captured units) are sacrificed at the Blood Altar to invoke martial favor. Cost: the lives of the sacrificed prisoners (loses the ransom value). Effect: the army that witnesses the ritual gains an attack bonus for the engagement. The morale bonus scales with the quality and number of those sacrificed.

**Dark Doctrine — The Blood Rite:** Performed at Temple level. Requires human participants drawn from the population (willing participants during high faith intensity; conscripted during lower intensity). The Blood Rite generates the highest faith intensity output of any Blood Dominion ritual short of a Grand Rite. Cost: population (consumed), gold, time. Effect: major faith intensity spike; combat bonus to all units in the territory for two cycles; conviction shift toward Cruel proportional to scale.

**Dark Doctrine — The Grand Rite:** Performed at Grand Sanctuary level. The Great Offering described in the Covenant Test. Consumes significant population, a Covenant Priest's full focus for multiple game cycles, and substantial gold and resources. Effect: the most powerful faith intensity surge in the game; unlocks access to dark manifestation events; major conviction shift toward Cruel.

**Dark Doctrine — Bloodline Sacrifice:** The most extreme Blood Dominion ritual. A Bloodline Member volunteers or is designated for sacrifice. The sacrifice produces The Sacrificed unit (the Level 5 apex unit) and a permanent faith intensity escalation that cannot be achieved any other way. Effect: The Sacrificed unit is created; dynasty-wide faith intensity increases permanently; the Bloodline Member's name is carried by The Sacrificed for the rest of the match.

**Light Doctrine — The Celestial Offering:** Performed at Temple level. Timed to celestial events (solstice, eclipse, significant astronomical moments — tracked in-game). An elaborate animal sacrifice performed with great ceremony. Cost: significant livestock, gold, time. Effect: large faith intensity boost, population morale boost, minor combat bonus. The timing component means this ritual requires planning — the player must prepare and be ready when the celestial window opens.

**Light Doctrine — The Covenant Vigil:** A multi-cycle ritual where a Covenant Priest maintains continuous vigil at the Blood Altar, performing minor daily offerings. Cost: sustained gold and livestock over the duration. Effect: sustained faith intensity maintenance at above-normal levels throughout the vigil period; slight combat bonus to all units.

### Conviction-Faith Interactions

**Dark Doctrine + Cruel Conviction:** Maximum power amplification. The sacrificial system operates at peak efficiency. Enemy morale penalties are maximized. Unbeliever sacrifice becomes available at extreme threshold. Dark manifestation is possible.

**Dark Manifestation (Dark + Cruel):** At sustained peak dark doctrine intensity with Cruel conviction: a summoning event. Blood Dominion dark manifestation is the raising of a cursed force — an army of the dead, not literally but practically. Enemy units within range suffer a morale collapse event. The summoned presence is temporary (one battle, one engagement) but represents one of the most powerful single combat interventions in the game.

**Light Doctrine + Moral Conviction:** The principled Blood Dominion dynasty — rare, respected, and genuinely powerful. Maximum Celestial Offering effect. Population participates willingly in rituals. No unbeliever sacrifice available. The combination produces the "harsh but principled" civilization type that other dynasties don't know how to categorize.

## THE ORDER — Covenant of the Sacred Mandate

### Theological Identity

Governance is holy. Law is divine instruction. The act of creating and maintaining a just, structured, functioning civilization is the most sacred act a human being can perform. The Order does not separate church and state because in Order theology, there is no meaningful distinction. Both are expressions of the same sacred mandate to organize the world into something that works.

The Order does not define what a just law is — it defines the framework within which just laws must operate. This is both its strength (adaptable to any civilizational form) and its danger (a tyrannical governance structure executed with perfect Order doctrine is still Order). What the doctrine insists upon is legitimacy, structure, and accountability — whether the result is gentle or harsh.

### Faith Building Progression

**The Mandate Pillar:** The Shrine equivalent. A physical marker in the territory declaring the Sacred Mandate's authority. Unlike other faiths' shrines, the Mandate Pillar is administrative as well as religious — it generates a minor governance bonus (settlement administration quality in the territory improves slightly) alongside faith intensity. Requires no ongoing offering — the Order's faith is maintained through governance actions rather than ritual offerings. However, governance failures (low loyalty, unpaid taxes, army morale collapse) reduce the Pillar's faith intensity generation.

**Temple of the Mandate:** Mid-game. Requires a Mandate Pillar and an Adjudicator — an appointed specialist who functions as judge, administrator, and faith leader simultaneously. The Adjudicator's decisions can be positive or negative for the population depending on their quality, creating a governance micro-system within faith management.

**Grand Sanctuary — The Court of the Sacred Mandate:** Late-game, Level 4 minimum. The most institutionally complex faith structure. The Court requires:
- Active Temple and Pillar in the territory
- An Adjudicator of senior level
- A formal governance declaration (the dynasty must have established and maintained a legal code for at least two Level periods)
- Large gold and stone investment
- A written Charter — a specific game object representing the dynasty's formal doctrinal commitment to the Mandate

### Ritual System

**The Adjudication:** Performed at any level. A formal public judgment on a specific matter — a dispute, a crime, a governance question. The quality of the judgment (measured by whether the outcome aligns with what the population perceives as fair) determines the faith intensity generated. Poor judgments generate negative faith intensity from the Pillar for one cycle.

**The Charter Proclamation:** A major public event at Temple level. The dynasty formally declares its governing principles to its population. Cost: substantial gold (the ceremony), a Bloodline Member's presence. Effect: significant faith intensity boost, major population loyalty increase in the territory, the Charter becomes a tracked game object that future Adjudication events reference.

**The Mandatory Census:** Light Doctrine ritual at Temple level. A formal population census and registration — every person in the territory is counted, registered, and assessed. Cost: time and gold. Effect: gold income increases (better taxation accuracy), recruitment capacity improves (better population knowledge), faith intensity boost from the act of governance itself.

**The Conversion Court:** Dark Doctrine ritual at Temple level. A formal judicial proceeding against practitioners of other faiths within the territory. The outcome forces conversion, exile, or — at extreme dark doctrine — execution. Cost: gold, time, Adjudicator focus, significant conviction shift toward Cruel. Effect: faith intensity boost, enemy faith intensity in the territory reduced, population loyalty risk (if the converted/exiled were beloved).

**The Grand Proclamation:** Grand Sanctuary level. A world-facing declaration of The Order's legitimacy claim. The dynasty formally asserts divine mandate over a specific territory or region — a claim beyond what they currently hold. Cost: maximum gold, Adjudicator at peak capacity, a Bloodline Member present. Effect: a claim is filed at the Trueborn City; the claimed territory's population begins hearing about the claim; if the claim goes unchallenged diplomatically for one cycle, the territory's loyalty shifts slightly toward the Order dynasty.

**Dark Doctrine — The Mandate Decree:** An executive order with religious authority. The dynasty declares a specific behavior illegal and enforces it throughout their territories with maximum institutional force. Cost: gold for enforcement, Warden-Enforcer units for practical enforcement. Effect: the decree is enforced, generating compliance from the population but resentment proportional to how controlling the decree is perceived. Used correctly, it generates order and faith intensity. Misused, it breeds the Heresy event.

### Conviction-Faith Interactions

**Light Doctrine + Moral Conviction:** The Order at its best — governance that is legitimate, structured, and genuinely serving the population. Maximum Adjudication effect. The Charter generates higher loyalty than for Neutral or Cruel dynasties. Administrative bonuses are compounded.

**Dark Doctrine + Cruel Conviction:** The Order as an enforcement machine. Maximum compliance. No dissent. No voluntary loyalty — only coerced obedience. Warden-Enforcer units are maximally effective. The Court of the Sacred Mandate is a place of genuine fear. Highly stable until the fear breaks — and when it breaks, it breaks completely.

**Divine Manifestation:** The Order's manifestation is institutional rather than supernatural — at sustained peak faith intensity with appropriate conviction, the dynasty's governance systems operate at superhuman efficiency. Tax collection is perfect. Desertion is eliminated. Every Adjudication generates positive outcomes. The world's populations perceive the dynasty's governance as touched by something greater than human competence.

## THE WILD — Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots

### Theological Identity

The world is alive. The forest is not a collection of trees — it is an entity with memory and intention. The rivers remember where they have been. The animals are not lesser creatures — they are partners who have simply not been asked to participate. The Wild does not worship nature. It recognizes that it is part of nature, and that the distance most humans have put between themselves and the living world is the source of most human suffering.

The Frost? The Wild understands the Frost differently from every other covenant. The Frost was not a punishment or a failure. The Frost was the world breathing out after a long period of human excess. The world is breathing in now. The Wild is the covenant that knows how to breathe with it.

### Faith Building Progression

**The Grove Marker:** The Shrine equivalent. Not a built structure — a living tree or stone formation marked with Wild covenant symbols and maintained by a community ritual. The Grove Marker is the only faith structure in the game that can grow over time — as faith intensity in the territory increases, the Marker develops into a larger, more established grove organically. Requires no ongoing resource offering — it requires regular community ritual attendance, which consumes population time rather than material resources. If the territory's population is neglected or forced, the Grove Marker weakens.

**Grove of the Elder Root:** The Temple equivalent. Requires a developed Grove Marker and a Grove Keeper — a specialist who lives within the grove, maintains its spiritual health, and conducts the major rituals. A Grove Keeper who is also a Bloodline Member (devoted as a faith practitioner) generates significantly more intensity. The Grove has a physical presence as architecture — a clearing within ancient trees, marked stones, the bones and relics of ritual animals. It reads as ancient even when newly established because the Wild's aesthetic is that it looks like it has always been there.

**Grand Sanctuary — The Ancient Thorned:** Late-game, Level 4 minimum. Not a building. A transformation — the territory's forest itself becomes the sanctuary. The Thorned Ancient requires:
- Grove of the Elder Root with at least two full Level periods of history
- Grove Keeper of senior level
- A Forest Sovereign unit (the Level 4 faith unit) assigned to the territory
- No Grand Trade Exchange or Grand War Foundry in the territory (the Wild's Grand Sanctuary cannot share a territory with the most industrial structures)
- A Wildfire event (see Dark Doctrine rituals) or a major successful defense of the territory by Wild doctrine forces

### Ritual System

**The Blood of the Grove:** Light Doctrine. A ritual conducted at the Grove Marker where the community makes small blood offerings — pricked fingers, marked skin — directly to the root system of the primary grove tree. Cost: minor food (feast following the ritual), population time. Effect: faith intensity boost, the Grove's health improves, slight population loyalty increase as the community feels connected to the land.

**The Hunt:** Available at Grove level. A formal ritual hunt conducted in forest territory. The Grove Keeper designates a specific hunt, the community participates, and the kill is returned to the Grove for ritual purposes. Cost: Hunters or Bowmen time (a military unit participates instead of being available for combat that cycle). Effect: faith intensity boost, the participating units gain experience, food supply gains from the hunt's product.

**The Binding:** A territorial ritual at Grove level. The Grove Keeper conducts a ritual that formally binds a territory to the Wild covenant — marks its rivers, trees, and key landscape features with covenant symbols and performs a ceremony of recognition. Cost: grove time, gold. Effect: the territory's resource production adjusts toward Wild affinity (forests produce more wood; rivers produce more water; the general resource profile shifts toward what the Wild covenant values). The Binding cannot be undone while the Grove is active.

**The Wild Call:** Light Doctrine at Grove level. A ritual that calls animals in the region to the Grove. Animals that respond become permanent residents of the grove territory — wolves, deer, bears. They are not units. They are a population mechanic: their presence increases the territory's food production (hunting) and its faith intensity. At high intensity, they begin to follow Wild units on campaign (see The Hunt Escort mechanic).

**Dark Doctrine — The Wildfire:** A deliberate environmental disruption ritual. The Wild's dark doctrine recognizes that nature can destroy as well as sustain. A Wildfire ritual conducted in enemy territory uses the animals and natural forces of the region to create a controlled disaster — a fire in a forest, a river flood, a disease event in a cattle herd. Cost: Grove Keeper focus for one full cycle, significant gold, a Wild unit presence in or adjacent to the target territory. Effect: the targeted territory's resource production collapses for one to two cycles; enemy armies in the territory take attrition; the territory's population morale drops. Conviction shift toward Cruel if civilian populations are harmed.

**Dark Doctrine — The Primal Rite:** Conducted at the Thorned Ancient level. An ancient ceremony where the covenant fully releases the boundary between the human and animal worlds. For one engagement, all Wild units in the Grand Sanctuary's territory fight alongside the full animal presence of the grove — wolves attack flanks automatically, bears cannot be routed, the forest itself impedes enemy movement. Cost: Grand Sanctuary ritual focus for one full cycle, large food sacrifice, the Grove Keeper's full capacity. Effect: the described combat bonuses for one major engagement, then a recovery period where the territory's faith intensity temporarily drops (the ritual takes something out of the grove).

### Conviction-Faith Interactions

**Light Doctrine + Moral Conviction:** Maximum harmony. The Wild in its purest expression. Animal partners are at their most responsive. The Wild Call generates more animals with greater variety. The Grove grows toward Thorned Ancient status more quickly. The forest itself seems more alive in this dynasty's territory — enemy scouts report unusual difficulty navigating it.

**Light Doctrine + Moral Conviction — Divine Manifestation:** At peak intensity: a nature event. A wild forest expands to cover terrain that was not forest before (mechanically: adjacent non-forest territory develops light forest properties within range of the Grand Sanctuary). Animal partners emerge in force — the forest appears to move. This is not subtlety — it is a declaration that the Wild is paying attention to this territory.

**Dark Doctrine + Cruel Conviction:** The Wild as consuming force. Wildfire is at maximum devastation. Primal Rite is at maximum power. The Bear Rider intimidation aura reaches its greatest range. The world outside this dynasty's territory feels like it is being watched by something that does not intend to be kind.

## Faith Intensity Maintenance — Full System

### Generation Sources

Faith intensity is generated by:
- Active faith buildings in territory (Shrine/Grove Marker / Pillar / Blood Altar)
- Ritual performance (each ritual type above provides intensity per cycle while active)
- Sacred Ground bonus (dramatic amplification when faith building is on Sacred Ground)
- Population participation (high loyalty populations engage more deeply with faith, generating more intensity)
- Bloodline Members in faith roles (a Bloodline Member as Flame Keeper, Covenant Priest, Grove Keeper, or Adjudicator generates significantly more than an appointed specialist)
- Doctrine consistency (a dynasty whose actions consistently align with their doctrine path generates a passive intensity bonus)

### Decay Sources

Faith intensity decays when:
- Faith buildings are destroyed or abandoned
- The territory is occupied by an enemy
- The population's loyalty drops below a threshold (disengaged populations stop participating)
- Governance actions contradict the faith doctrine (a cruel act by an Old Light dynasty, or a merciful act in a context where Blood Dominion expects sacrifice, can reduce intensity)
- Time passes without ritual reinforcement (faith intensity has a natural decay rate that ritual performance must exceed to sustain)

### Intensity Thresholds

Faith intensity operates in five tiers:

| Tier | Label | Effect Summary |
|------|-------|----------------|
| 0-20% | Latent | Faith exists in name only; no mechanical bonuses; population considers themselves nominally of the faith |
| 20-40% | Active | Basic ritual access; minor morale and loyalty bonuses; faith units available |
| 40-60% | Devout | Full ritual access; faith-influenced units available; faith spreading to adjacent territories |
| 60-80% | Fervent | Faith-specific elite units available; major combat and governance bonuses; spiritual manifestation probability rises |
| 80-100% | Apex | All faith mechanics fully unlocked; manifestation events become probable; the Covenant Test triggers; Level 5 faith apex units become available |

### Faith Investment and Strategic Cost

Maintaining faith intensity above the Devout threshold requires sustained resource investment. This investment competes with military and economic priorities. A dynasty that reaches Fervent intensity across multiple territories is spending resources that could have gone to cavalry or iron production. This is the fundamental design tension of the faith system: the most powerful faith mechanics are available only to dynasties that have committed significantly — and commitment has real cost.

A dynasty that neglects faith during military expansion and then attempts to build faith intensity rapidly when it needs it will find the process slower and more expensive than a dynasty that maintained consistent faith investment throughout.

### Player Manual Covenant Expansion (Added 2026-03-15)

## The Old Light — Expanded Gameplay Identity

Based on praying to the old gods, moral and protective. Emphasizes protecting children and the innocent, acting with integrity, and not enslaving people. Late game can result in holy armies, blessed warriors, including Christ-like anointed characters capable of resurrection, healing, and removing plague or disease from people. Includes temples, unity, prayer, and sacred orders. Not dependent on culture integration requirements. Can be protective and moral without being obligated to integrate cultures. It is about stewardship and protection.

Not a passive covenant. Late-game access to formidable sacred military power. Strongest when paired with disciplined governance and real military competence. Not a covenant of surrender. It is a covenant of morally serious force. Mercy without strength is fragile, and strength without integrity would betray the covenant itself.

## The Blood Dominion — Expanded Gameplay Identity

Leans into enslavement, conquest, and sacrifice. Believes in the power of war and conquering and enslaving and sometimes sacrificing people. Inspired by brutal sacrificial civilizations while still within the medieval theme. Late game can include summoning a demon or hell-equivalent manifestation, raising a cursed army, and similar catastrophic expressions. Conquest of tribes may result in no prisoners in fanatical states, only total murder. Can include blood rites and plague-like effects.

Major downsides: isolation, trade refusal from others, and forced conflict dynamics. Strongest when played with disciplined brutality, not indiscriminate madness. The most dangerous Blood Dominion ruler is not the one who kills most eagerly. It is the one who knows exactly when terror advances power and when terror invites premature retaliation.

## The Order — Expanded Gameplay Identity

A structured doctrine and institutional belief. Emphasizes authority, law, sacred mandate, and enforced order. Can create powerful institutional systems, courts, and conversion infrastructure. Not good or evil in simple terms, but represents doctrinal authority and structured conversion pressure. Can serve as a Vatican-style seat of belief and conversion system in late game.

Power comes from building structures of obedience and meaning that outlast individual engagements. Well suited to dynasties that want to make the world feel administratively and spiritually claimed. Danger: can become rigid or coercive without actual strength and competence. A dead bureaucracy with holy language is still dead. Must be lived as forceful structure, not empty procedure.

## The Wild — Expanded Gameplay Identity

Focuses on mysticism and nature. Calls on gods of nature and powerful mysticism. Late game can recruit animals as an uncontrolled guerrilla element, such as packs of aggressive wolves and bears. Includes weather and natural disasters. Also has resurrection and healing elements in a nature-based way. Includes primal rites, groves, and spiritual connection to the land.

Not a narrow "druid" theme. Can become a terrifying strategic identity. Packs of aggressive wolves and bears, weather events, natural disasters, nature-based healing and resurrection make The Wild a covenant of asymmetry and living pressure. Especially powerful in certain terrains and in dynasties willing to let conflict become less orderly. Where The Order says the world must be structured, The Wild says the land itself will answer. Best Wild dynasties understand how to align people, land, and bloodline with something older and more dangerous than institutions.

## THE OLD LIGHT — Covenant of the First Flame: Full Design

### Core Theology

The Old Light does not hold that the divine is watching from a distance. It holds that the divine is the act of witnessing itself — that the sacred is the capacity to see clearly, to hold what has been seen in memory, and to be changed by it. The flame is not a symbol of warmth. It is a symbol of inescapable illumination. You cannot be in the presence of fire and hide. Everything becomes visible: the wound, the rot, the beauty, the face of the person dying in front of you.

The Great Frost, in Old Light theology, was not wrath. It was the consequence of collective forgetting — a civilization that had stopped looking at itself, that had allowed comfortable ignorance to calcify into organized blindness, until the world had no choice but to extinguish a light that had already gone dark on its own. The Frost did not punish. It completed.

This faith demands two things that pull against each other in ways the dynasty must resolve through its choices. First: it demands that the living hold the dead in active memory — not as sentiment but as obligation. The names of the fallen are not ceremonial. They are a form of debt, and a form of witness, and the reckoning they represent is real. To follow Old Light and let your people die unnamed, unremembered, used as raw material with no acknowledgment, is not merely political failure. It is theological betrayal.

Second: it demands that what the flame illuminates be faced. The light is not a mercy that looks away. When it finds corruption — in a population, a bloodline, a doctrine, a civilization — it does not grieve and move on. Something that has been seen by the light has been called to account, and the account must be settled. Light doctrine settles accounts through mercy and witness. Dark doctrine settles them through fire.

This distinction — the same illuminating flame, two different readings of what illumination obligates — is what separates the Remembrance Compact from the Purging Flame. Both paths are sincere. Both carry the same texts. One has concluded that witness requires living with the weight of what you have seen. The other has concluded that witness requires acting on it without exception. The most dangerous Old Light player, on either path, is the one who has stopped questioning their own reading. The faith does not protect dynasties from certainty. It only guarantees that certainty has consequences.

What separates the Old Light from every other covenant is its relationship to loss. The Blood Dominion treats loss as fuel. The Order treats it as a governance failure to be institutionally managed. The Wild treats it as the world's indifference expressed locally. The Old Light treats it as a wound that must be held in the record permanently, because a civilization that forgets its losses is a civilization that has lost the ability to grieve correctly, and a civilization that cannot grieve correctly will eventually treat loss as an abstraction — and then it will create losses without limit.

The Old Light is not pacifist. It is morally serious. There is a significant difference. A dynasty that shelters behind the Old Light's reputation for mercy while doing nothing to earn that reputation is practicing hypocrisy, and the covenant is specifically aware of hypocrisy. The flame illuminates everything, including the dynasty holding it.

### Light Doctrine — The Remembrance Compact

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

A dynasty walking the Remembrance Compact has accepted that the light's obligation runs in both directions. They are witnessed by the same divine attention they invoke when they light the flame for their dead. This produces dynasties of unusual moral seriousness, and unusual moral vulnerability. They cannot commit atrocities anonymously. They cannot let their populations die in uncounted anonymity and still claim the flame. Every death that passes through their civilization enters the record and will be there when judgment comes.

The Remembrance Compact demands that the ruler bleed alongside their people — not symbolically, but structurally. The faith's rituals are designed so that the most powerful effects require the dynastic member's personal participation in an act of witness, of formal acknowledgment of cost. The covenant cannot be maintained by gold alone. It requires the dynasty to keep looking at what it has done.

**What a Remembrance Compact civilization looks like:**

The Remembrance Compact civilization is governed by an unusual combination of genuine accountability and martial capability. Its courts try cases seriously, including cases against Bloodline Members. Its dead have names in public records that are maintained by the temple structure and accessible to any member of the population. Its armies are loyal in the specific way that people are loyal to an institution they have watched pay its debts — because they have watched the dynasty bleed alongside them in the rituals, they know the covenant is not rhetorical.

This civilization does not look gentle. It looks careful. Its governors ask questions before they act, because the Compact's mechanics make the consequences of unconsidered action compound across cycles in ways that a more casual dynasty would not track. Its military is not the most aggressive. It is the most persistent — Compact Bond ensures that units who have lost people fight on, and the Vigil of Reckoning turns military losses into morale reinforcement rather than morale collapse. Armies fighting under the Remembrance Compact are fighting for something that has already paid them.

Its military fights because the dynasty has formally and repeatedly demonstrated that it regards the lives of its soldiers as valuable and their deaths as matters of covenant significance. That is not an abstraction in this world. That is the difference between an army that routes and an army that holds.

### Dark Doctrine — The Purging Flame

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

The Purging Flame path has arrived at a conclusion that follows logically from the same theology the Remembrance Compact holds — and that is what makes it genuinely dangerous rather than simply cruel. If the light sees everything, and if what it sees has moral weight, and if moral weight demands response, then the faith that only witnesses without acting is not mercy but cowardice at scale. The Purging Flame dynasty has decided that the light's nature is not simply to record but to judge, and that judgment without consequence is not judgment at all but observation.

Heresy is not error. It is contagion. Apostasy is not disagreement. It is the seed of the Frost planted in a warm civilization, and every generation that mercy permits to grow is a generation closer to the cold returning.

This doctrine produces dynasties of profound internal coherence. There is no ambiguity in a Purging Flame court — you hold to the light as the Inquisitor defines it, or you are the problem being solved. This is not cynicism. It is completely sincere. A Purging Flame dynasty believes it is merciful in the most important sense: it is protecting the many clean from contamination by the few corrupt. The horror of this doctrine is not that it is hypocritical. It is that it is entirely earnest, and that earnestness makes it capable of industrialized atrocity with a clean institutional conscience.

**What a Purging Flame civilization looks like:**

The Purging Flame civilization is clean in the way that things are clean when everything that might carry contamination has been removed. Its streets have records. Its shrines glow with maintained flame. Its population is extraordinarily compliant — because the alternative has been made visible, repeatedly, in the pyres and the public processings of the Examinations. The population does not experience itself as terrorized. It experiences itself as purified, and there is a genuine social warmth within the Compact's population, a genuine sense of shared identity, because they are the ones who have been affirmed.

Its military fights with the specific confidence of soldiers who have been told they are fighting contagion rather than people. This produces high initial morale and willingness to commit atrocities that other doctrine paths would generate Conviction costs for. The Purge Templar fighting under the Consuming Witness does not feel like a murderer. They feel like medicine.

The Purging Flame's governance style is that of a state whose categories are theological — people are either Clean or Marked, and the institution exists to maintain the ratio of one to the other at acceptable levels. This is highly stable until the examination apparatus becomes too eager and begins marking populations that the civil infrastructure cannot afford to lose. A Purging Flame dynasty's greatest internal threat is not rebellion. It is the moment the Inquisitor's hunger for findings outpaces the dynasty's capacity to absorb the consequences.

## THE BLOOD DOMINION — The Red Covenant: Full Design

### Core Theology

The Blood Dominion knows something that other faiths spend generations trying to avoid knowing: the bond between people, between a dynasty and its population, between the living and the dead, is not maintained by sentiment. It is maintained by cost. Things that cost nothing mean nothing. The covenant between a ruler and those they rule, between a soldier and the cause they die for, between a civilization and its own survival, is not sealed by rhetoric or law or memory. It is sealed by blood. The shedding of it, the sharing of it, the willingness to be cut open in service of something larger than the individual body — this is the mechanism through which the sacred bond activates. Not metaphorically. The blood is the covenant. The covenant is the blood.

The theology comes from a specific reading of the world's near-ending. The Great Frost did not arrive because civilization forgot something, as Old Light holds. It arrived because civilization had stopped paying. The bonds between people had become cheap — maintained by habit and convenience rather than genuine cost — and cheap bonds do not hold when the cold comes. The Frost was the consequence of a world full of people who had promised things they had not paid for. The Blood Dominion's resolution is absolute: pay. Pay first, pay visibly, pay in a currency that cannot be counterfeited.

And if the world demands payment before it will hold together, the question the dynasty must answer is: whose blood? Who pays the covenant's cost? The light path and the dark path give different answers. The theology is identical. A dynasty reading the Red Covenant's foundational texts toward the light doctrine concludes that the ruler bleeds first — that the covenant's power runs upward from the strong to the weak, that sacrifice is a leader's obligation before it is a population's. A dynasty reading the same texts toward the dark doctrine concludes that the strong exist to extract and the weak exist to provide — that the hierarchy of sacrifice is fixed by the hierarchy of power, and that the one who holds the altar is not the one who goes on it.

Both readings are internally coherent. Both have produced historical civilizations. Both can be held by dynasties who are genuinely sincere in their theology and not simply rationalizing cruelty or sentimentality. The most dangerous Blood Dominion player, on either path, is not the one who kills most eagerly. They are the one who knows exactly when sacrifice advances power and when it merely invites premature retaliation. The covenant rewards calculation as much as it rewards commitment.

The Blood Dominion's relationship to death is not romantic. It is transactional in a way that the faith's practitioners would not describe as cold but that observers from other covenants consistently experience as alien. The dead are not mourned in the Old Light sense. They are not absorbed into the law's institutional memory as the Order does. They are not returned to the world-cycle as the Wild holds. They are entered into the covenant as payment received. Their sacrifice was the price. The price was accepted. The bond holds.

### Light Doctrine — The Covenant Bond

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

A dynasty walking the Covenant Bond path has accepted that the ruler bleeds first. The most sacred act within this doctrine is the ruler's willingness to pay before demanding payment, to demonstrate through visible personal cost that the covenant is real and mutual. This produces dynasties of unusual unit loyalty because the population understands, in visceral terms, that their sacrifice is shared rather than extracted. When a Covenant Priest opens their own palm before a battle and bleeds into the ground of the territory they are defending, the soldiers watching are not receiving inspiration. They are receiving a theological statement about what the covenant obligates the strong to do. They believe it because they have watched it happen.

The vulnerability of this doctrine is proportional to its strength. A Covenant Bond dynasty that takes heavy losses has not merely lost soldiers. The covenant is strained — the shared sacrifice structure has absorbed more than it was designed to absorb in one period, and if the losses are not formally acknowledged through the proper rites, the bond between the survivors and the ruling dynasty begins to attenuate. The covenant promise was mutual. The survivors paid their part. If the dynasty does not pay its part in return, the bond weakens. This makes Blood Dominion light doctrine strong in sustained defensive war but vulnerable to prolonged attrition.

**Rituals:**

**The Opening of the Vein**
- Building required: Blood-Altar (Tier 1) or higher
- Who participates: One Bloodline Member as primary officiant, who undergoes the cutting; population representatives from 3 settlements who witness it
- What is consumed: 25 Provisions, 15 Rare Materials (ritual vessel and cutting instruments — specific maintained tools, not improvised), 10 Gold. The Bloodline Member takes a formal Wound: not a health reduction but a narrative status lasting 6 cycles that prevents them from leading cavalry charges or initiating direct combat.
- Duration: 1 cycle.
- What happens to participants: The 3 settlements whose representatives witnessed the rite generate faith intensity at +18% for 8 cycles. Population happiness in those settlements is +12% for the same period. The Wound the Member carries is not concealed — it is publicly known.
- Mechanical effect: Faith intensity +6%. Those settlements' populations are 25% less likely to experience loyalty drift for 15 cycles. The covenant has been paid for personally.
- Interruption: If the Altar is attacked during the rite, the covenant vessel is desecrated. The Wound status activates without the population benefit. Faith intensity -3%.

**The Bloodline Renewal**
- Building required: Covenant Hall (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Covenant Priest as officiant; all Bloodline Members present in the territory who choose to attend (non-mandatory, but Members who decline are noted and may face social consequences); population representatives from every active settlement
- What is consumed: 50 Provisions, 30 Gold, 25 Rare Materials, 20 Labor. Each participating Bloodline Member sheds blood into a shared vessel — a small cut, formal, witnessed. The vessel is then carried through each represented settlement and the blood is given to the earth of each one.
- Duration: 3 cycles of ceremony preparation plus 1 cycle of the formal rite.
- What happens to participants: All participating Bloodline Members gain +12 to their base Conviction modifier for 25 cycles. Members who decline to participate gain a Covenant Debt marker that makes them slightly vulnerable to internal political challenges.
- Mechanical effect: All population settlements reset loyalty drift to neutral — accumulated grievances are formally absorbed by the ceremony. Faith intensity +10%.

**The Binding of the Dead**
- Building required: Covenant Hall (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Covenant Priest officiant; surviving members of depleted units (required); family members of the dead if accessible
- What is consumed: 35 Provisions, 25 Rare Materials (binding cords made from materials contributed by the families of the dead, representing the continued physical connection of the living to the fallen), 20 Gold. Livestock are slaughtered at the Altar's secondary station — their blood joins the ceremony.
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: Surviving members of fully decimated formations who undergo the Binding persist as Covenant Remnants — units at half strength, full morale, zero upkeep for 12 cycles. They are not disbanded. The dead entered the covenant. The living continue it.
- Mechanical effect: All units who died in the preceding 10 cycles are entered into the covenant. Each entered soldier generates +9% morale on surviving units from the same formation for 18 cycles. Faith intensity +7%.
- Interruption: If the Covenant Hall is raided during this rite, the dead remain unbound — their deaths weigh on the survivors as unresolved debt. Morale penalty -15% for 10 cycles.

**What a Covenant Bond civilization looks like:**

The Covenant Bond civilization carries its sacrifices visibly. Its leadership is marked — by the formal Wound status that attends every major ritual, by the Covenant Scars that senior members wear publicly, by the understood knowledge among the population that the people at the top have bled. Its armies are bonded in a way that armies maintained through pay and compulsion are not, because the covenant has been formally and repeatedly demonstrated to flow in both directions. The population does not experience itself as maintained. It experiences itself as held.

Its governance is not gentle. The Covenant Bond dynasty does not apologize for requiring sacrifice from its people. It requires sacrifice from itself first and in full visibility, which changes the character of what it asks. An army that has watched the Covenant Priest take a formal Wound before a battle will not route easily because routing means abandoning someone who has already paid in advance for their presence there.

Its military fights with the specific character of soldiers who know that their deaths will be entered into the covenant rather than anonymized. The dead under the Covenant Bond are not statistics. They are entries in a record that generates continuing returns for the people they left behind. This is the Blood Dominion's answer to the Old Light's Compact Record — not memory for its own sake, but memory as ongoing fuel for the living.

### Dark Doctrine — The Blood Rite

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

The Blood Rite path has absorbed the Covenant Bond's theology fully. It agrees that the covenant must be paid, that cost is the mechanism through which the bond becomes real, that sacrifice is not cruelty but stewardship. It has drawn a different conclusion about who pays. The weak exist within the covenant as material. The strong exist within it as architects. Asking the weak to opt out of their contribution is like asking the earth to decline to accept the grain that's sown in it — the question is categorically wrong.

The covenant is not a negotiation between equals. It is a hierarchy of purpose, and the Blood Rite priest who stands before the altar and performs the Tithe is not committing violence. They are completing the covenant's structure.

This doctrine does not hide from itself. Its rituals are performed publicly. Its priests do not dissemble about what they are doing. What makes the Blood Rite path genuinely disturbing is not that it is secretly ashamed of itself — it is that it is completely at peace with itself, and that peace is indistinguishable from certainty, and certainty at scale looks identical to institutions that have made atrocity orderly and gave it a title and a calendar.

**Rituals:**

**The Tithe of the Weak**
- Building required: Blood-Altar (Tier 1) or higher, Blood Rider unit present as enforcer
- Who participates: Blood Rider oversees; low-loyalty population of the designated settlement provide the material; Blood-Altar clergy conduct the rite
- What is consumed: 30 Labor (an abstraction representing a specific number of individuals from the settlement's lowest-loyalty tier), 15 Gold, 12 Rare Materials. The settlement's population count decreases.
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The Blood Rider unit gains a Tithe-Mark for 8 cycles. The remaining population's loyalty paradoxically stabilizes post-Tithe: the doctrine has told them the Tithe selected those who were weakest and least faithful. The survivors understand themselves as having been evaluated and retained.
- Mechanical effect: Faith intensity in the settlement +10%. All military units stationed in the settlement gain +18% combat morale for 12 cycles. A Blood Reserve token is generated for permanent unit enhancement. Neighboring factions with Old Light or Order faith above Devout generate a Horror event if they have line of sight to the settlement. Faith intensity +8%.

**The Blood Convocation**
- Building required: Covenant Hall (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Senior clergy officiate; captive enemy soldiers, enslaved populations, or conscripted civilians constitute the ritual material. A Bloodline Member must be present for the Convocation to reach maximum scale.
- What is consumed: 55 Provisions, 35 Rare Materials, 25 Gold, 25 Labor. The scale of the Convocation determines its output.
- Duration: 4 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member present carries the Convocation Mark for 20 cycles — increasing their authority within the faction. Up to 5 units can be permanently enhanced with Blood Reserve tokens from a single Convocation.
- Mechanical effect: Blood Reserve tokens generated equal to scale of participation. All Blood Dominion units in the territory gain +22% combat morale for 18 cycles. The Convocation is visible to neighboring factions and triggers Horror responses up to 10 hexes away. Faith intensity +12%.

**The Wound's Hunger**
- Building required: The Wound (Tier 3)
- Who participates: The Wound's clergy; captive enemies as material; a Bloodline Member must personally deliver the first sacrifice to initiate the rite at full scale

- Duration: 3 cycles to establish, active for up to 12 cycles while captives are delivered.
- What happens to participants: Each sacrifice delivered generates +5% health for all Blood Dominion units on the field (distributed equally), +3% faith intensity, one Blood Reserve token. If more than 20 captives are sacrificed in a single activation, the rite escalates into Grand Feeding — all Blood Dominion units deal +28% damage and Bloodbound units temporarily gain their full apex combat package.
- Mechanical effect: The faith is fed in real time. Scale is limited only by the dynasty's captive supply. Faith intensity +16% base, +5% per 5 captives above 20.
- Interruption: If The Wound is damaged during the Hunger, half effects. If destroyed during the Hunger, a catastrophic faith backlash — -22% faith intensity and region-wide panic.

**What a Blood Rite civilization looks like:**

The Blood Rite civilization is ordered, not chaotic. Its records are meticulous. Its rituals are conducted on schedule by trained clergy following established procedures. The Tithes are calendared. The Convocations are announced in advance. The administrative machinery of the Blood Rite dynasty is not scrambling — it is efficient, because it has removed all moral ambiguity from its resource management. People are resources. Resources are managed. The records are clean.

Its population is compliant in the specific way of populations that have watched the covenant's consequences and made a rational calculation about which side of the altar they prefer to be on. The loyalty metrics are stable. The internal rebellion probability is low. What the Blood Rite civilization cannot produce is the genuine bond of the Covenant Bond path — its population is not held by shared sacrifice. It is held by the understood cost of not being held.

Its military fights as the instrument of a civilization that has decided that war is a resource extraction mechanism. Prisoners are the covenant's supply. Conquest is the mechanism for generating new Tithes. The Blood Rider and Bloodbound units have been formally invested by the covenant and fight with the absolute confidence of people who know the structure they serve is metaphysically correct. They are not cruel people. They are people doing a job that their theology has resolved as necessary.

### The Covenant Test — Blood Dominion

**Trigger:** Any dynasty following the Blood Dominion reaches Level 4 faith progression (Fervent intensity sustained for 10+ cycles with an active Covenant Hall or higher).

**What it demands:**

Light doctrine (Covenant Bond): The covenant demands the Covenant Scar — a formal ritual in which a Bloodline Member undergoes the permanent scarring at The Wound (even if The Wound is not yet built, a temporary sacred space at the Covenant Hall suffices for the Test). ## THE ORDER — Covenant of the Sacred Mandate: Full Design

### Core Theology

The Order does not hold that the divine speaks through fire, or through blood, or through the forest's indifferent growth. It holds that the divine speaks through structure. Through the fact that law exists, that hierarchy can be made to function, that human beings are capable of building institutions that outlast any individual and that constrain even those with the power to ignore them.

In a world that nearly ended from chaos — from the collapse of every system that had organized people's lives — The Order offers the most specific answer to why The Great Frost came: because there was no law that held. Civilization failed not because it forgot the dead or refused to pay, but because it had no mechanism capable of making anyone accountable to anything larger than their immediate appetite. Every person with power acted on that power without institutional constraint. Every bond was personal and therefore breakable. Every structure was sustained by the goodwill of whoever was strongest at the moment, which meant every structure was one bad season from dissolution. The Frost was the outcome of a world without binding law. The Mandate's answer is permanent, comprehensive, enforced law — not as convenience but as theology. Governance is not administration. It is the closest act to the sacred that living people can perform.

The Order asks its followers to believe something difficult: that the law matters more than the person. That an institution built correctly is more valuable than any individual within it, including the individual who built it. The light doctrine says this means the mandate exists to protect the governed — the law binds the powerful more than the weak, because the powerful have the most capacity to do harm unchecked. Every court, every trial, every formal proclamation under the Iron Compact exists to demonstrate that the dynasty is not above the law it administers. The dark doctrine says this means the mandate exists to enforce the hierarchy — the law defines who is governed and who governs, and compliance is the only expression of faith the institution requires. Both paths use the same texts. Both paths have enforced them at scale. The civilizations they produce are recognizably related and mutually incomprehensible.

The Order's relationship to The Great Frost is the most institutional of any covenant's response. Where the Old Light holds that the Frost was a moral failure, and the Blood Dominion holds that it was a failure of payment, and the Wild holds that it was the world asserting itself, The Order holds that the Frost was an administrative failure. The systems that should have held did not hold. The mandate was never properly written and enforced. The solution is to write it correctly and enforce it comprehensively, and to ensure that the Mandate outlasts every individual dynasty, ruler, and generation that passes through it. The institution must persist when the people within it do not. That is the whole point.

### Light Doctrine — The Iron Compact

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

A dynasty walking the Iron Compact path has accepted that the law they invoke is the law they live under. The Mandate cannot be claimed as protection for the population while being used as a tool of extraction for the dynasty — the faith has no mechanism for this kind of hypocrisy that does not produce measurable structural consequences. The Iron Compact produces dynasties with unusual institutional stability because their populations experience governance as something other than organized exploitation. The law is the same for everyone, including the ruling bloodline, and the faith's rituals are specifically designed to enforce this through public ceremony that is genuinely binding rather than ceremonially hollow.

The vulnerability of this doctrine is bureaucratic scale. The Iron Compact operates through formal process — trials, declarations, oaths with legal weight, institutions that must be maintained. These require resources, personnel, and time that a crisis may not allow. An Iron Compact dynasty under rapid military pressure will find its governance machinery straining because the doctrine does not permit the summary shortcuts that crisis seems to demand. The dark path's appeal in moments of emergency is that it offers efficiency. The Iron Compact's answer is that efficiency purchased at the cost of the Mandate is not efficiency but dissolution of the thing that makes the dynasty worth defending.

**Rituals:**

**The Proclamation of Mandate**
- Building required: Lawpost (Tier 1) or higher
- Who participates: Senior law official as officiant; all Bloodline Members in the territory must attend (non-attendance formally recorded and carries minor political consequence); population representatives from each settlement witness and acknowledge
- What is consumed: 20 Gold, 15 Rare Materials (formal documentation — legal instruments, not decorative objects), 20 Labor.
- Duration: 1 cycle for the public ceremony, but requires 2 cycles of preparation.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member who formally declares the Proclamation carries the Mandate Seal for 15 cycles — all governance actions they take within this period are 18% more effective. Population representatives return to their settlements with the Mandate Acknowledged status, reducing governance grievance accumulation by 20% for 10 cycles.
- Mechanical effect: All population settlements in the territory receive formal acknowledgment that the dynasty's authority derives from the Mandate rather than from force alone. Loyalty stability in all settlements increases for 12 cycles. Faith intensity +5%.

**The Trial of Account**
- Building required: Hall of Mandate (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Judge (a Bloodline Member or appointed specialist), defendant (a specific individual — a military commander, a settlement administrator, a Member of the bloodline who has taken actions that generated Conviction debt), witnesses from affected populations
- What is consumed: 30 Gold, 25 Rare Materials, 25 Labor. If the defendant is a Bloodline Member, their current administrative output is halted for the duration of the trial.
- Duration: 3 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The defendant, if found responsible, receives a formal Accounting — a permanent record carrying +15% Conviction accountability for their future decisions. If acquitted, they carry the Cleared Record status — +10% effectiveness to all their actions for 15 cycles.
- Mechanical effect: The Trial formally resolves a specific governance grievance. Any outstanding population grievance tied to the defendant's administration is cleared upon the verdict. Faith intensity +7%. A dynasty that consistently uses the Trial to protect powerful members from accountability will find that its population develops cynicism — repeated acquittals of Bloodline Members while lower officials are convicted generates a Mandate Credibility erosion that compounds.
- Interruption: If the Hall of Mandate is attacked during the Trial, the proceedings are suspended. This generates a Mandate Violated event — faith intensity -5% and a governance crisis in the affected settlement.

**The Oath of Iron**
- Building required: Hall of Mandate (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: All Bloodline Members who will be bound by the oath — minimum 3, maximum the full bloodline; Mandate Officiant presides; population witnesses are required for the oath to carry its full legal weight
- What is consumed: 40 Gold, 30 Rare Materials, 20 Provisions. Each participating Member formally binds themselves to a specific commitment — typically a policy regarding taxation, military service, or governance practice.
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: Members who take the oath carry the Iron Oath status for 30 cycles. While this status is active, they cannot take actions that contradict the oath's terms without generating a Mandate Breach event — which carries severe Conviction penalties and activates the faith's breach response. The oath is real. It constrains.
- Mechanical effect: Population trust in the dynasty increases significantly across all settlements covered by the oath's terms. Faith intensity +9%. The oath creates a formal accountability mechanism that functions even when political pressure would otherwise allow the dynasty to renege.
- Interruption: Cannot be interrupted. Oaths are not interrupted — they are kept or broken.

**What an Iron Compact civilization looks like:**

The Iron Compact civilization is slow and durable. Its courts hear cases. Its proclamations have specific terms. Its administrative officials can be tried. This produces a civilization that is harder to destabilize than most, because populations governed by genuine rule of law are less susceptible to the propaganda of competing factions that claim the dynasty is merely powerful and therefore arbitrary. The law has been formally and repeatedly demonstrated to operate on the dynasty itself.

Its governance style is meritocratic within the faith's framework — appointments go to qualified administrators, trials are conducted by judges rather than simply by the most powerful person in the room, and the Trial of Account exists specifically to prevent the ruling family from becoming untouchable. This is not democracy. It is legitimate authority, and the distinction carries real mechanical weight.

Its military fights with the specific confidence of soldiers who know they serve an institution rather than a person. When the Bloodline Member commanding them dies, an Iron Compact army does not rout — it reorganizes under the next authority in the formal chain because the structure exists independently of any individual holding it. This is The Order's response to the vulnerability every other covenant shares: the dynasty's military is not contingent on specific people.

### Dark Doctrine — The Iron Fist

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

The Iron Fist path takes the Iron Compact's theology and removes its condition. The Iron Compact holds that law binds the powerful as much as the weak, because the powerful have the most capacity for harm. The Iron Fist holds that this is sentimentality dressed as jurisprudence. The Mandate exists because some people are capable of enforcing order and some people require it to be enforced on them, and the law's function is not to balance these groups but to institutionalize their relationship. The dynasty is the Mandate. The Mandate is the dynasty. Compliance is the only legitimate expression of faith available to those who are not in the dynasty, because they are not in the position of interpreting the law — they are the population the law governs.

This doctrine produces the most administratively efficient civilizations in the game. The Iron Fist's institutional machinery does not slow down for consultation or formal process — it decides and enforces. Its population compliance rates are extremely high because non-compliance has been made formally illegal and the enforcement apparatus is comprehensive. What it produces in exchange is a civilization whose stability is entirely top-down: the population is compliant, not loyal, and the moment the dynasty demonstrates any weakness, the entire compliance architecture becomes available for capture by whoever can project authority over it.

**Rituals:**

**The Mandate Assertion**
- Building required: Lawpost (Tier 1) or higher
- Who participates: Warden-Enforcer units as the enforcement arm; law officials as formal declarants; the settlement's population as the audience — attendance is not optional
- What is consumed: 25 Gold, 20 Rare Materials, 20 Labor
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: Warden-Enforcer units gain the Authority Mark for 8 cycles. The settlement's population enters a Compliance State — reduced loyalty metric, replaced by a Compliance metric that drops only when enforcement visibly fails rather than drifting based on grievances.
- Mechanical effect: Faith intensity in the settlement +7%. Compliance replaces loyalty for 20 cycles. Rebellion probability is extremely low during this period. However, if any enforcement action fails publicly in this settlement, the Compliance metric drops catastrophically rather than gradually.

**The Formal Suppression**
- Building required: Hall of Mandate (Tier 2) or higher, Warden-Enforcer unit present
- Who participates: Warden-Enforcer as primary enforcement agent; law officials as documentation apparatus; the targeted population as material
- What is consumed: 30 Gold, 25 Rare Materials, 30 Labor. The targeted population segment — those designated as Non-Compliant — are formally processed: fined, relocated, imprisoned, or eliminated depending on the dynasty's Conviction level.
- Duration: 3 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The Warden-Enforcer conducting the suppression gains +15% combat effectiveness for 10 cycles. The settlement's remaining population experiences a Compliance Reset — all compliance metrics return to baseline and the memory of the suppression functions as a deterrent for 20 cycles.
- Mechanical effect: All non-Order faith activity in the targeted settlement is eliminated for 20 cycles. Enemy faith conversion pressure is zeroed out for the same period. Faith intensity +9%. Adjacent settlements with line of sight generate a Compliance Response — some adopt compliance preemptively to avoid being the next target.

**The Iron Judgment**
- Building required: The Iron Spire (Tier 3)
- Who participates: The Iron Spire's full institutional apparatus; Warden-Enforcer and Dominion Arbiter units as enforcement; a Bloodline Member must personally deliver the Judgment; the defendant territory or population has no procedural role
- What is consumed: 70 Gold, 60 Rare Materials, 50 Labor, 35 Faith Currency.
- Duration: 1 cycle for delivery, 4 cycles of preparation
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member who delivers the Judgment carries the Arbiter's Mark permanently for this match. All enforcement actions they subsequently order carry +25% institutional weight — enforcement success rates are higher and compliance responses from adjacent settlements are more immediate.
- Mechanical effect: A specific territory or enemy dynasty is formally placed under Iron Judgment — a permanent designation. All Order units fighting a dynasty under Iron Judgment gain +25% combat morale for 25 cycles. Neutral factions experience -15% trade willingness toward the judged entity. The Iron Spire generates passive intelligence on the judged entity's military movements. Faith intensity +15%.

**What an Iron Fist civilization looks like:**

The Iron Fist civilization is the most legible governance on the map. Its laws are public. Its enforcement is documented. Its institutional apparatus functions in the visible, formal way of an organization that has nothing to hide because it has decided that what it is doing is not wrong — it is simply the Mandate being properly applied to people who require it. There is no secret cruelty in a fully realized Iron Fist dynasty. Everything is done through official channels with official documentation and official clergy conducting official ceremonies.

Its population's compliance metrics are exceptional. Its rebellion probability is the lowest in the game as long as the dynasty continues to project unambiguous authority. What it cannot produce is the kind of self-sustaining popular investment that light doctrine creates — the population does not love the Mandate. They recognize it as the most powerful structure in their vicinity and align accordingly. The moment it appears vulnerable, the alignment recalibrates.

Its military is the most institutionally organized in the game. Commands flow through clear hierarchy. Warden-Enforcer units function as institutional anchors that prevent tactical chaos. The Dominion Arbiter can issue binding decisions in the field that carry the full institutional weight of the Iron Spire. An Iron Fist army does not need morale in the way other armies do — it needs compliance, and compliance is what the institutional machinery reliably produces.

## THE WILD — Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots: Full Design

### Core Theology

The Wild does not hold that the divine is memory, or covenant, or law. It holds that the divine is the fact of the world — the indifferent, total, prior-to-everything fact of existence before human beings arrived and began naming things and building structures and deciding that their arrangements were important.

The Great Frost was not punishment, not consequence, not abandonment. It was the world asserting what it always was: larger than what had been built inside it, indifferent to the buildings, patient in the way that things are patient when they do not die. The civilizations that fell to the Frost were not being punished. They were being reminded. The Wild's theology is built on this reminder, and it is the most ancient faith in the game precisely because it requires the least from the world to remain true. The forest does not need to believe in The Wild for The Wild's theology to be correct.

What makes the Wild distinct from every other covenant is that it is not making a claim about what the divine wants from human beings. The Old Light says the divine wants witness. The Blood Dominion says it wants payment. The Order says it wants compliance and structure. The Wild says that framing the question in terms of what the divine wants from humanity is the category error that produced The Great Frost. The divine is not watching and wanting. The divine is happening, continuously, in every living thing, and it will continue happening with or without the dynasty, the civilization, or the civilization's theology. The Wild is the only faith that does not require the world to care about human beings in order to be true.

This produces two very different temperaments at the theology's edges. The Living Compact holds that if the world is alive and indifferent, then the appropriate human response is to participate honestly — to take what is taken, give what is given back, and accept that your civilization is a temporary arrangement that the world tolerates. This is not despair. It is clarity. A dynasty aligned with a living world is protected by what it has aligned with, and what it has aligned with is the oldest and most durable thing on the map. The Consuming Wild holds that if the world is alive and indifferent, and if indifference means the strong consume the weak as a matter of ecological fact, then predation is not cruelty but accuracy. Winter kills the lame deer. The flood does not spare the poorly positioned settlement. A civilization that replicates this mechanism is not being cruel. It is being honest about what the world is.

Both paths begin from the same theological premise. The world is alive. The world does not care about you specifically. The question is whether you respond to that fact with reverence or with appetite. The Living Compact chooses reverence. The Consuming Wild chooses appetite. And the world, being indifferent, accommodates both equally.

The Wild's relationship to the other covenants is the most asymmetric. The Old Light and The Wild have the deepest theological incompatibility in the game — one holds that the dead persist in memory with moral weight, the other holds that death returns things to a cycle and that grief is a human conceit the forest does not share. The Blood Dominion and the Consuming Wild have surprising structural compatibility at the level of their darkest shared premise. The Order and The Wild are mutual refutations: The Order holds that civilization is humanity's highest achievement, and The Wild holds that civilization is a temporary arrangement the world tolerates conditionally.

### Light Doctrine — The Living Compact

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

A dynasty walking the Living Compact path has accepted that the world is a partner rather than a resource. Its territories are managed the way a person manages a relationship — you take, and you give back, and the accounting is not purely financial. The faith's rituals are structured as exchanges: you release something into the natural world, and the world responds. You take something from what it provides, and you acknowledge the taking formally. You mark territory not with walls but with living things — planted groves, maintained waterways, designated resting territories for animals.

These acts are not conservation in a modern ecological sense. They are covenant-keeping in the oldest sense: the dynasty has made an agreement with the ground it stands on, and the ground is aware of the agreement.

This doctrine's strategic vulnerability is its geography-dependence. The Living Compact's strongest bonuses are generated within territories the dynasty has genuinely maintained over time — territories with active Grove Markers, Spirit Lodges at full capacity, Thornrider units that have been stationed in the territory long enough to know its patterns. A Living Compact dynasty that has been in a territory for 20 cycles fights very differently there than a dynasty that arrived 5 cycles ago. The faith rewards depth of presence over breadth of conquest.

**Rituals:**

**The Grove-Binding**
- Building required: Grove Marker (Tier 1) or higher
- Who participates: Thornrider unit as primary officiant; any Bloodline Member with the Naturalist trait if available; the participating population provides labor and materials

The Bloodline Member who participated gains the Grove-Bound trait for 15 cycles — immune to Conviction penalties from natural events in this territory.
- Mechanical effect: The territory gains a Living Compact designation — all Wild faith intensity generation increases 18% for as long as the Grove is maintained. Territory defense bonuses increase 12%. Faith intensity +6%.

**The Release**
- Building required: Spirit Lodge (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Spirit Lodge elder as officiant; a Bloodline Member who will conduct the release; a healthy animal from the dynasty's best breeding stock released into the wild unmarked and unretained. The loss of a prime animal is a real resource cost.
- What is consumed: 30 Provisions, 20 Rare Materials (sacred markings applied to the released animal, made from natural materials).
- Duration: 1 cycle.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member who performs the release gains the Released One trait for 15 cycles — +20% to any Conviction check involving restraint. It reinforces the capacity for the action most difficult within a strategy game's logic: not taking when taking would profit.

**The Thornrider's Claim**
- Building required: Spirit Lodge (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Thornrider unit leads; the dynasty's mounted cavalry must be present and formally brought through the territory; the route covers every major terrain feature

Enemy units entering the territory trigger a detection event 2 cycles earlier than normal. Territory defense bonuses increase an additional 15%. Faith intensity +9%.

**What a Living Compact civilization looks like:**

The Living Compact civilization is old-looking regardless of how recently it was built. Its groves feel established. Its markings on the landscape look like they grew rather than were placed. Its architecture is minimal because the faith's buildings are living things — the Grove Marker is a tree, the Spirit Lodge is a clearing with ancient stones, the First Grove is the forest becoming something that recognizes the dynasty standing within it.

Its governance style is the least institutional of any covenant's — there are no courts, no formal trials, no bureaucratic machinery. Governance happens through the Thornrider's knowledge of the territory, the Spirit Lodge elder's understanding of what the land needs, and the dynasty's accumulated relationship with the populations that live within the Wild's claimed territories. These populations tend to be stable because they are in a covenant that has demonstrably improved the land they live on, and improving the land they live on is the most direct possible argument for political legitimacy.

Its military does not project the way other covenant militaries do. It does not advance cleanly across open terrain with the Order's formation discipline or the Blood Dominion's brutal momentum. It fights best where the dynasty has spent the most time. In a Living Compact dynasty's home territory, at Fervent or higher intensity, the military effectively has the entire terrain fighting alongside it — enemies lose visibility, lose movement, lose defensive bonuses they were counting on, while Wild units move freely and the animals that have been in covenant with the dynasty for 20+ cycles emerge from the woods as something between witness and participant.

### Dark Doctrine — The Consuming Wild

**The doctrine's theological logic:**

The Consuming Wild has heard the Living Compact's theology and decided that half of it is sentiment. The world is indifferent. The Wild's theology is built on that truth. The Consuming Wild path takes that truth to its complete form: if the world is indifferent, and if indifference is the law, and if winter kills the weak and strengthens the herd, then mercy is not a virtue — it is a refusal to participate honestly in what the world is. The forest does not preserve the lame deer. The flood does not spare the poorly positioned settlement. The strong consume the weak because that is the mechanism through which the living world maintains itself, and a civilization that replicates this mechanism is not cruel. It is accurate.

This doctrine produces dynasties of genuine military ferocity — the Consuming Wild is the most aggressive faith path in the game in its territory control mechanics, because the faith frames expansion and predation as theologically correct rather than as moral compromises. What it produces in exchange for this ferocity is a civilization with no mechanism for the kind of internal solidarity that other faiths generate. The Consuming Wild population is not bonded by covenant, or law, or shared memory. It is bonded by strength. The moment the dynasty shows weakness, the binding unravels.

**Rituals:**

**The Culling**
- Building required: Grove Marker (Tier 1) or higher, Bear Rider unit present
- Who participates: Bear Rider oversees; the designated population — the dynasty's weakest settlement, determined by lowest productivity and loyalty metrics — contributes its least productive members as the ritual material; no ceremony beyond the act itself
- What is consumed: The designated individuals. The settlement's population decreases. 15 Rare Materials, 20 Gold.
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The Bear Rider unit carries the Culling Mark for 10 cycles — all combat effectiveness +18%. The remaining population in the culled settlement enters a Survival Hierarchy state: loyalty is replaced by a dominance compliance metric that holds as long as the dynasty is winning.
- Mechanical effect: Food production improves 8% in the territory as resource pressure decreases. Faith intensity +7%. All military units in the territory gain +12% combat morale for 10 cycles.

**The Pack Rite**
- Building required: Spirit Lodge (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Senior Consuming Wild priest; all combat units currently stationed in the territory; the prey — captive enemy soldiers or designated local population as material; Bear Rider or Bonebreaker unit leads the rite
- What is consumed: 35 Provisions, 25 Rare Materials (predator markings applied to participating units), 20 Gold. The prey is consumed within the rite — not symbolically.
- Duration: 2 cycles.
- What happens to participants: All participating combat units gain the Pack-Marked status for 12 cycles — +15% damage, +10% movement speed, ability to coordinate attacks without movement penalty.
- Mechanical effect: Faith intensity +10%. All Pack-Marked units function as a coordinated formation even when geographically dispersed — they share a morale pool during Pack-Marked duration. Resistant to morale manipulation but vulnerable to a single concentrated morale collapse. If the pack breaks, all Pack-Marked units rout simultaneously.

**The Apex Feast**
- Building required: The First Grove (Tier 3)
- Who participates: Full Consuming Wild clergy; all apex predator units (Bear Riders, Bonebreakers) present in the territory; a mass of captives serves as the ritual's material; a Bloodline Member must preside
- What is consumed: 75 Provisions, 55 Rare Materials, 45 Gold, 35 Faith Currency. And the captives. The Apex Feast is the Consuming Wild's most definitive ritual statement.
- Duration: 4 cycles, during which the territory generates Horror events visible to all neighboring factions.
- What happens to participants: All Bonebreaker and Bear Rider units in the territory gain the Feast-Blessed status for 25 cycles — +20% all combat stats, immunity to morale reduction, health regeneration 5% per cycle during combat. The Bloodline Member who presides carries the Apex Mark permanently for this match.
- Mechanical effect: The territory's faith intensity reaches maximum possible value for 15 cycles. All Wild units across the entire dynasty gain +10% combat effectiveness for 15 cycles. Horror events reach 16 hexes, affecting all neighbors simultaneously. Faith intensity +20%.

**What a Consuming Wild civilization looks like:**

The Consuming Wild civilization does not look like civilization in the way that any other covenant produces civilization. Its settlements are functional, not ornamental. Its markers in the landscape are predator markers — territory declarations made in the language of what hunts rather than what builds. Its population lives in a specific kind of clarity: they know what the dynasty is, they know what it requires, and they have made the calculation that being within it is preferable to being outside it. This is a population in a genuinely functioning predator compact, not a corrupted one. The compact is correct on its own terms.

Its governance style is hierarchy enforced by demonstrated capability. A Consuming Wild ruling dynasty is not obeyed because it has formal authority or because it has demonstrated moral legitimacy. It is obeyed because it is stronger than anything the population could align with instead. This is extremely stable in periods of expansion and military success and extremely brittle the moment the dynasty loses a major engagement.

Its military is the most directly ferocious in the game. The Culling and Pack Rite produce combat units with no hesitation because the faith has resolved all hesitation as theological error. The Bonebreaker hunts the weakest unit in any opposing force because the covenant has made it theologically correct to target weakness. The Bear Rider is the thing that should never be in the same engagement as your most vulnerable units. An army facing the Consuming Wild at Fervent or higher intensity is facing a civilization that has made it a religious obligation to find and destroy everything fragile about the opposing force. The Cold settling over the territory is not a weapon the dynasty deployed. It is winter recognizing something that shares its nature.

### The Covenant Test — The Wild

**Trigger:** Any dynasty following The Wild reaches Level 4 faith progression (Fervent intensity sustained for 10+ cycles with an active Spirit Lodge or higher).

**What it demands:**

Light doctrine (Living Compact): The covenant demands The First Grove ceremony — a 5-cycle event during which the dynasty cannot initiate combat (defensive response is permitted; new offensive actions are not). The dynasty must be willing to stop fighting long enough for the world to recognize what it has built. The First Grove ceremony requires that no military action be initiated, that the full clergy and Bloodline Members present in the territory participate, and that living materials from across all of the dynasty's territories be contributed. It is the covenant asking: are you willing to stop being a military actor long enough to be something else? For dynasties in the middle of active expansion, this timing demand is genuinely costly. That cost is the point.

Dark doctrine (Consuming Wild): The covenant demands the Apex Feast — conducted not against captured enemies but against a specific designated settlement from within the dynasty's own territory that has been identified as the weakest, the most dependent, the least capable of contributing to the covenant's predatory capacity. The covenant is testing whether the dynasty applies the Culling's logic to itself as well as to its enemies. The faith does not permit the dynasty to consume external weakness while protecting internal weakness. The Consuming Wild demands consistency.

**Failure (both paths):** If the Test is declined, the First Grove site (light) enters a Covenant Withdrawn state — all territory bonuses granted by Grove-Binding rituals are suspended for 15 cycles. The territory itself seems to recede from the dynasty's presence. If the Apex Feast is declined (dark), all Culling and Pack Rite bonuses currently active are revoked simultaneously, generating a dynasty-wide morale event. The covenant does not forgive an animal that hesitates at the kill. Faith intensity for either path collapses to Active tier. **Success:** Full access to The First Wild. Sustained Apex faith intensity tier unlocks.

### Apex Faith — Level 5: The First Wild

The armor is stripped and functional, and the mount is something older, something that agreed to carry a rider not through covenant but through recognition of equivalent predatory status. **What it feels like when The First Wild walks onto a battlefield:** Light doctrine — the Waking Green activates in all territories the dynasty has maintained, simultaneously. ### Cross-System Interactions — The Wild

**Conviction:** The Wild is the covenant with the loosest conviction requirements for mechanical effectiveness, but not the most forgiving of misalignment. The Living Compact genuinely peaks with Moral conviction — not because the faith is morally demanding in the Old Light sense, but because the Living Compact's rituals are designed around giving things back to the world, and a dynasty with Moral conviction has the psychological profile to perform the Release, to maintain the Grove Tithe, to decline to exploit resources that the covenant has specifically identified as belonging to the living world. A Living Compact dynasty at Cruel conviction finds the Thornrider's Claim producing less and the Grove-Binding failing to fully root, because the covenant recognizes that the dynasty's relationship with the land is extractive rather than reciprocal. The Consuming Wild at Cruel conviction is the most coherent combination in the game's theology — predation without hesitation, no conviction cost for the Culling, the Pack Rite generating maximum output.

**Dynasty:** The Wild is the faith most likely to produce Naturalist Bloodline Members — dynasty members who have developed a direct personal relationship with the territory through the Grove-Binding and Thornrider's Claim rituals. A dynasty with a Naturalist Member in the Thornrider unit's command has the most territory-specific military advantage available in the game. **Territory:** The Wild is the most terrain-specific faith in the game. The Living Compact generates its strongest effects in forest, coastal, and highland terrain — biomes with living complexity that the Grove-Binding can attach to. Desert and heavily urbanized terrain generate significantly reduced Wild faith intensity. The Consuming Wild is less terrain-specific because predation operates across all terrain types, but its strongest bonuses emerge in transitional terrain — forest edges, river crossings, mountain passes — the spaces where the ecological hierarchy is most active and where the act of controlling movement is most decisive. Both Wild paths share the core territorial dynamic: the longer the dynasty has been in a territory, the more the territory participates in its military and economic activity.

## SECTION A: What Faith Actually Is in Bloodlines

The player who chooses a covenant at the start of a match is not selecting a faction bonus. They are committing their civilization to an answer. A specific, non-negotiable answer to the question that every civilization in this world has been forced to answer by the memory of The Great Frost: why does power exist, and what is it for?

Each of the four covenants is a complete theology — a total account of the world's nature, the cause of its suffering, the mechanism of its redemption or endurance, and the obligations it places on those who hold power within it. None of them are naive. None of them are soft. All four emerged from the wreckage of a world that nearly ended, and all four carry in their doctrine the memory of that near-ending. They differ on what it meant, what caused it, and what the living owe the dead and each other. These are not abstract questions. In this world, the answers you build your civilization around determine what your armies are willing to do, who your population believes they are, what kinds of leaders your bloodline produces, and whether your dynasty can survive being tested.

What separates light doctrine from dark doctrine within any given covenant is not goodness versus evil. That framing is too simple for what these faiths actually are. What separates them is where the doctrine places the cost. Light doctrine places cost on the dynasty — the ruler pays, leads through sacrifice, governs through obligation. Dark doctrine places cost on the governed, the conquered, the weak, the apostate. The theology is often identical. The same framework that makes Blood Dominion's light doctrine a system of shared obligation held together by mutual bloodshed can be read, by a different temperament with the same scriptures, as a justification for unlimited extraction from anyone too weak to object. The faith contains both readings. The civilization the player builds determines which reading wins.

## THE OLD LIGHT — Covenant of the First Flame

### What This Faith Is

The Old Light does not hold that the divine is watching from a distance. It holds that the divine is the act of witnessing itself — that the sacred is the capacity to see clearly, to hold what has been seen in memory, and to be changed by it. The flame is not a symbol of warmth. It is a symbol of inescapable illumination. You cannot be in the presence of fire and hide. Everything becomes visible: the wound, the rot, the beauty, the face of the person dying in front of you. The Great Frost, in Old Light theology, was not wrath. It was the consequence of collective forgetting — a civilization that had stopped looking at itself, that had allowed comfortable ignorance to calcify into organized blindness, until the world had no choice but to extinguish a light that had already gone dark on its own. The Frost did not punish. It completed.

This faith demands two things that pull against each other in ways the dynasty must resolve through its choices. First: it demands that the living hold the dead in active memory — not as sentiment but as obligation. The names of the fallen are not ceremonial. They are a form of debt, and a form of witness, and the reckoning they represent is real. To follow Old Light and let your people die unnamed, unremembered, used as raw material with no acknowledgment, is not merely political failure. It is theological betrayal. Second: it demands that what the flame illuminates be faced. The light is not a mercy that looks away. When it finds corruption — in a population, a bloodline, a doctrine, a civilization — it does not grieve and move on. Something that has been seen by the light has been called to account, and the account must be settled. Light doctrine settles accounts through mercy and witness. Dark doctrine settles them through fire.

### Light Doctrine — The Remembrance Compact

**The doctrine's logic:**

A dynasty walking the Remembrance Compact has accepted that the light's obligation runs in both directions. They are witnessed by the same divine attention they invoke when they light the flame for their dead. This produces dynasties of unusual moral seriousness, and unusual moral vulnerability. They cannot commit atrocities anonymously. They cannot let their populations die in uncounted anonymity and still claim the flame. Every death that passes through their civilization enters the record and will be there when judgment comes. This is not a comfortable doctrine to hold power under, which is precisely why dynasties that hold it tend to hold it ferociously — because the alternative is the dark path, and the dark path begins as relief from accountability and ends as an inquisition.

The Remembrance Compact demands that the ruler bleed alongside their people — not symbolically, but structurally. The faith's rituals are designed so that the most powerful effects require the dynastic member's personal participation in an act of witness, of formal acknowledgment of cost. The covenant cannot be maintained by gold alone. It requires the dynasty to keep looking at what it has done.

**Rituals:**

**The Naming of the Fallen**
- Building required: Wayshrine (Tier 1) or higher
- Who participates: One Bloodline Member as officiant; open to all units and population in the territory
- What is consumed: The ritual requires the physical burning of a record — the names of the dead inscribed on materials that will combust fully in the flame. 25 Timber (the pyre), 15 Provisions (funeral sustenance distributed to the attending population), 10 Rare Materials (inscription materials and the vessel for the ash). The ash is then interred at the base of the Wayshrine.
- Duration: 2 cycles. Cannot be interrupted without consequence.
- What happens to participants: The officiant Bloodline Member carries a Witness Mark for 10 cycles — a narrative status denoting that they have formally entered the dead into the record. Units who attended the Naming gain Compact Bond, a persistent morale floor that prevents their resolve from dropping below 40% for 15 cycles.
- Mechanical effect: All Bloodline Members who died in the preceding 10 cycles are entered into the Compact Record. Each entered member generates +3 Conviction stability per cycle for 20 cycles. Enemy propaganda targeting this population is halved in effectiveness — a population whose dead are named and remembered has an anchor identity that resists replacement. Faith intensity: +5%.
- Interruption: If the Wayshrine is attacked mid-rite, the pyre is scattered. The names are not entered. The population in the territory experiences a Grief Without Resolution event — faith intensity drops 4% and morale of all units in the territory drops 15% for 10 cycles. This is the worst outcome because the dead have been dishonored at the moment they were offered remembrance.

**The Vigil of Reckoning**
- Building required: Hall of Remembrance (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: A dedicated Flame Keeper plus a minimum of 5% of the territory's population, who stand vigil in shifts. At least one Bloodline Member must attend for one of the 5 cycles.
- What is consumed: 45 Timber, 35 Provisions (the vigil-keepers must be fed), 25 Rare Materials. The Flame Keeper who leads the vigil does not sleep for its duration. At the vigil's end they are physically depleted, entering a rest state that reduces their administrative output for 5 cycles.
- Duration: 5 continuous cycles. The flame cannot be allowed to go out.
- What happens to participants: The Flame Keeper emerges from the vigil with the Witness Deepened trait for 20 cycles — all faith intensity generation from this building is increased 20%. The Bloodline Member who attended gains the Compact Bound trait permanently for this match, meaning they are immune to faith corruption mechanics for the remainder of the game.
- Mechanical effect: All injured units in home territory recover morale and health at +40% rate for 10 cycles following the vigil's completion. Grief events from war deaths have their Conviction cost reduced 50% — the population has been given a formal structure for loss rather than being left to metabolize it silently. One Flame Warden unit is generated at no material cost upon completion.
- Interruption: If interrupted before cycle 3: no effect, resources lost, the attempted vigil becomes a failure that triggers a minor morale collapse in the participating population (they gathered in grief and were denied the completion of it). If interrupted between cycles 3 and 5: partial effect activates — healing bonuses apply for 5 cycles only, no Flame Warden generated.

**The Recitation of Witness**
- Building required: The Eternal Cathedral (Tier 3)
- Who participates: Senior Flame Keeper as primary officiant; the entire Cathedral clergy; one Bloodline Member must be present for the final cycle
- What is consumed: 60 Provisions, 45 Rare Materials (the illuminated scrolls — each one records an enemy faction's military actions against Old Light forces in detail), 30 Gold, 15 Faith Currency. The scrolls are read aloud in their entirety and then sealed in the Cathedral vaults. This is not performance. The reading is theological: the light has witnessed everything done here and will remember it.
- Duration: 1 cycle, but requires 3 cycles of preparation during which the scribes compile the scrolls.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member present for the final cycle gains The Recorded Name — their deeds from this match are specifically entered into the Cathedral record. If this Member dies in a subsequent battle, the Cathedral generates a passive manifestation-level morale effect for 3 cycles.
- Mechanical effect: All enemy factions bordering Old Light territory are subject to The Witnessing for 15 cycles. Enemy Bloodline Members who die fighting Old Light during this period are recorded, creating Diplomatic Weight tokens usable to propose non-aggression agreements at doubled acceptance probability. All Old Light units gain +7% combat morale for 15 cycles. Faith intensity: +10%.
- Interruption: The Recitation cannot be interrupted once begun — it is entirely internal. However, if the Cathedral is destroyed within 5 cycles of completion, all effects end and faith intensity drops 15%.

**The Rite of Ash Absorption**
- Building required: Hall of Remembrance (Tier 2) or higher
- Who participates: Flame Keeper officiant; the survivors of the defeated units; any Bloodline Member who chose to lead the defeated force
- What is consumed: 20 Timber, 25 Provisions, 15 Rare Materials. The weapons of units that were destroyed in the defeat are burned. The smoke is witness. The Bloodline Member who led the force and survived must formally speak the names of those lost under their command. If they were not present for the battle, the rite is unavailable.
- Duration: 1 cycle. Requires a minimum of 2 cycles without active combat in the ritual territory.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member who speaks the names carries the Weight of Command for 10 cycles — they cannot avoid Conviction accountability for the deaths under them, but this accountability is formal and acknowledged rather than unresolved. This prevents the worst of the Conviction debt from festering.
- Mechanical effect: Absorbs 40% of the incoming Conviction penalty from military defeat, distributing the damage across a 20-cycle decay period rather than hitting immediately. The population experiences the loss as tragedy rather than catastrophic humiliation. Surviving units from the defeated force who witnessed the rite gain +20% combat resolve for 5 cycles — they have been given meaning for what they survived. Faith intensity: +4%.

**Passive doctrine effects:**

- Latent (0-20%): No doctrine effects. The faith exists as social infrastructure only.
- Active (20-40%): Background grief recovery activates in home territory. Conviction losses from natural disaster, plague, and famine events are reduced 15% because the faith gives the population a cognitive framework for loss. The dead are being held by something. The living can continue.
- Devout (40-60%): Injured units in home territory recover 25% faster. Bloodline Members who survive battles in which other Members died gain the Witness trait permanently — +10% to all Conviction-affecting decisions for 20 cycles per surviving battle. The weight of what they saw becomes moral authority rather than psychological damage.
- Fervent (60-80%): All Remembrance rites cost 20% fewer resources. Enemy faith corruption and propaganda mechanics targeting Old Light populations operate at half conversion rate. The Compact's population has an identity anchor too specific to be replaced generically. A passive presence radiates from faith buildings: enemy units within 3 hexes suffer -7% morale — not from threat, but from the weight of being observed by something that remembers.
- Apex (80-100%): The Eternal Cathedral, if built, passively generates diplomatic memory records of all military actions taken against Old Light. These are visible as in-game intelligence and presentable to neutral factions as evidence. Additionally, all Old Light units gain the Remembered passive: when they die in combat, adjacent living units receive +12% combat morale for 3 cycles as the fallen enter the record in real time.
- Sustained Apex (10+ cycles): Dynasty of Witness activates. All Bloodline Members gain once-per-lifetime access to The Record — the full weight of the dynasty's documented history bearing on a single Conviction check or diplomatic action, overriding external pressure. Cannot be faked. Cannot be purchased.

**Spiritual manifestation — The Choir of the Witnessed (Fervent+):**

The Choir manifests when Old Light reaches Fervent intensity and at least one major engagement — 20 or more units, 10 or more dead — has occurred in home territory within the past 15 cycles. What arrives is not an army. It does not look like an angel and does not behave like a weapon. It appears as a slow upwelling of pale golden light from the Hall of Remembrance or the Eternal Cathedral — not fire, not lightning, but a warm diffuse illumination that seems to have no single source, accompanied by the sound of many voices speaking low and overlapping, distinct words unrecoverable, the register unmistakable: this is the sound of people being listed. They are being named. All of them.

Mechanically, the Choir freezes the current combat morale of every unit in the territory — own forces and enemy alike — at its present value. Routing units stop routing. Frenzied units stop overextending. Both sides fight at exactly the resolve they carried when the Choir appeared. This is the Remembrance Compact's vision of what the divine does with a battle: it holds it. It witnesses it. It does not intervene to make one side win. In a battle turning through morale collapse, this rescues a routing Old Light army from dissolution. Against an enemy winning through psychological pressure rather than attrition, it is infuriating.

Duration: 8 cycles. Ends if the Hall of Remembrance or Eternal Cathedral is destroyed, or faith intensity drops below Fervent. Cannot be attacked directly. Enemy dark doctrine rituals at Fervent or above can disrupt it over 3 cycles of sustained counter-ritual effort — the Choir is patient but not invulnerable.

**Doctrine-specific unit abilities:**

**Flame Warden (Level 3, Light Doctrine)**
The Flame Warden carries the torch of the Hall of Remembrance not as weapon but as standard — a live flame maintained through combat by deliberate effort. The Warden has chosen the hardest thing the doctrine asks: to hold the light steady while the battle moves around it.
- Vigil Aura: All units within 4 hexes gain +10% morale stability — a floor effect, not a ceiling boost. Morale cannot drop below 40% within this radius while the Warden stands.
- Last Rites: When any allied unit dies within 2 hexes, the Flame Warden may spend one action to perform a battlefield naming. This prevents the death from radiating the panic/grief propagation that normally ripples through nearby units when allies fall. The death is absorbed into the record rather than broadcasting as terror.
- Torch of Witness: Once per battle, the Flame Warden designates an enemy unit as Witnessed. That unit loses all stealth, ambush bonus, and flanking advantage for the remainder of the battle — they have been seen. They cannot become unseen.

**Sunblade Champion (Level 4, Light Doctrine)**
The Sunblade Champion is a warrior who has undergone the Vigil of Reckoning themselves and emerged from it. They fought before the ceremony. They fight differently after it.
- Purification Strike: Attacks are specifically effective against spiritually corrupted units — dark doctrine faith units at high intensity, entities manifested from dark doctrine rituals, units under active curse effects. +30% damage against these targets.
- Bearer of Remembrance: The Champion extends Vigil Aura at half effectiveness even without a Flame Warden present, serving as an anchor point for the doctrine's morale-stabilizing presence.
- The Unquenched: Once per engagement, when reduced to critical health, the Champion's torch gutters and holds — recovering 20% maximum health. Cannot trigger below 5% health. The name is stated on the unit card. Its meaning is not inspirational. It is theological: what the compact holds, it holds.

**Apex unit — The Unbroken (Light Doctrine expression):**
The Light Doctrine Unbroken is ash-grey. Lit from within. It carries both sword and torch, and both look as though they have been in use since longer ago than anyone can measure. It does not enter the battlefield with triumph. It enters the way a witness enters a room: with attention.

Stats profile: High durability, moderate offense. The Unbroken regenerates 5% health per cycle during combat while standing in Old Light territory. Its morale cannot break. It is immune to fear, curse, and corruption effects. The unit or formation that kills The Unbroken is marked Witnessed for the remainder of the match — visible to all factions, carrying -10% morale when facing any Old Light force they subsequently meet. The record remembers who extinguished the flame.

Unique ability — Keeper of the Record: Once per match, The Unbroken invokes the full weight of what the dynasty's flame has witnessed. Area of effect: 6 hexes. Allied units within radius gain the Remembered trait for 10 cycles. Enemy units within radius suffer -20% morale for 10 cycles — not from the warrior but from the presence of everything they have done being held, documented, and permanent.

The Choir of the Witnessed is active for the first 5 cycles of The Unbroken's presence. They arrive together. The feeling is not of a weapon being deployed. It is of the faith itself coming to see what its followers built.

### Dark Doctrine — The Purging Flame

**The doctrine's logic:**

The Purging Flame path has arrived at a conclusion that follows logically from the same theology the Remembrance Compact holds — and that is what makes it genuinely dangerous rather than simply cruel. If the light sees everything, and if what it sees has moral weight, and if moral weight demands response, then the faith that only witnesses without acting is not mercy but cowardice at scale. The Purging Flame dynasty has decided that the light's nature is not simply to record but to judge, and that judgment without consequence is not judgment at all but observation. Heresy is not error. It is contagion. Apostasy is not disagreement. It is the seed of the Frost planted in a warm civilization, and every generation that mercy permits to grow is a generation closer to the cold returning.

This doctrine produces dynasties of profound internal coherence. There is no ambiguity in a Purging Flame court — you hold to the light as the Inquisitor defines it, or you are the problem being solved. This is not cynicism. It is completely sincere. A Purging Flame dynasty believes it is merciful in the most important sense: it is protecting the many clean from contamination by the few corrupt. The horror of this doctrine is not that it is hypocritical. It is that it is entirely earnest, and that earnestness makes it capable of industrialized atrocity with a clean institutional conscience.

**Rituals:**

**The Inquisitor's Examination**
- Building required: Hall of Remembrance (Tier 2) or higher, with active Inquisitor unit present
- Who participates: The Inquisitor as primary examiner, assisted by hall clergy. The targeted settlement's population undergoes formal inquiry — questioning, observation of devotional practice, review of social relationships.
- What is consumed: 25 Gold (inquisitorial apparatus), 20 Rare Materials (instruments of examination and documentation), 25 Labor (the tribunal process is intensive and occupies significant human hours). The Inquisitor who conducts the examination cannot be redeployed for its duration.
- Duration: 3 cycles.
- What happens to participants: The Inquisitor carries Authority of Finding for 10 cycles following the examination, making subsequent examinations in this territory 20% faster. The targeted settlement's population does not recover full happiness until the finding is resolved — the examination itself is a form of controlled pressure applied to the social body.
- Mechanical effect: The settlement is classified Clean or Marked. Clean generates +5% faith intensity and temporary loyalty bonus — the population feels affirmed and surveilled simultaneously, which reinforces doctrinal compliance. Marked generates a mandatory choice: perform the Purging Rite (see below), exile the heretics (population reduction, the exiled scatter into neighboring territories carrying faith disruption pressure), or delay (the Marked status corrupts adjacent settlements at -3% faith intensity per cycle until resolved). Note on randomness: at extreme dark doctrine weight, the Examination will always return Marked. The instrument has become too eager. This is a mechanic and a warning.
- Faith intensity change: +5% if followed by Purging Rite within 5 cycles; +2% if Clean; -4% if delayed beyond 5 cycles

**The Purging Rite**
- Building required: Hall of Remembrance (Tier 2), must follow an Examination with a Marked result
- Who participates: Inquisitor as officiant; Purge Templar units if available as enforcers; the condemned population as material
- What is consumed: 15 Timber (the pyres — plural, at scale), 10 Rare Materials (restraints, official seals of the finding), 35 Labor (the rite is physically intensive and requires significant personnel). The population of the purged settlement is reduced. The reduction is not symbolic.
- Duration: 1 cycle.
- What happens to participants: Inquisitor unit gains +20% combat morale for 15 cycles — they have been affirmed in their function. Purge Templar units present gain the Cleansed Blade trait for 10 cycles, increasing their damage against heresy-designated targets by 15%.
- Mechanical effect: The Marked designation is eliminated. Faith intensity +8%. Population of the purged settlement suffers -30% happiness for 20 cycles but does not rebel unless dynasty Conviction is critically low — the remaining population has internalized the doctrine's account of what happened. Adjacent enemy populations with line of sight to the purge suffer -15% voluntary conversion likelihood toward Old Light. The purge is visible. It is meant to be visible.
- Interruption: Cannot be interrupted once begun.

**The Writ of Unclean Declaration**
- Building required: The Eternal Cathedral (Tier 3)
- Who participates: Senior clergy of the Cathedral conduct the formal theological declaration; a Bloodline Member must ratify it; it is distributed through all available diplomatic and trade channels without additional cost
- What is consumed: 45 Gold, 35 Rare Materials, 25 Faith Currency. The declaration is a permanent document. It cannot be retracted within the match.
- Duration: 2 cycles to draft and ratify; effect is permanent once issued.
- What happens to participants: The Bloodline Member who ratifies the Writ carries the Declarant status — they become the named theological authority for the designation and face double the Conviction consequences if the dynasty later violates the spirit of the declaration through accommodation or mercy to the declared population.
- Mechanical effect: Formally designates an entire enemy dynasty as Unclean — a theological classification with material consequences. ### Faith Spread — Old Light

Old Light spreads through loss. It is most compelling to populations that have experienced significant collective grief — war dead, famine, plague, mass displacement — who have not been given a framework for what they lost. Old Light offers that framework at the cost of nothing except sustained remembrance. The faith enters through the stories that travel with refugees and traders: that somewhere there is a civilization that names its dead, that holds them in the record, that does not let them dissolve into anonymity. This is not a small offer in a world that nearly ended.

Light doctrine Old Light is uniquely resistant to displacement once rooted because it attaches itself to local history. To convert away from Old Light is, literally, to abandon the names of your dead. The faith has made itself synonymous with collective memory, which means removing it feels like a second death for every ancestor it has claimed.

Dark doctrine Old Light spreads differently: it spreads through fear of the Inquisitor. Populations under occupation by a Purging Flame dynasty experience conversion not through attraction but through the systematic elimination of alternatives. The threat is not abstract. The pyres are visible.

### Conflict and Compatibility

**Old Light vs. Blood Dominion:** Theological first-principles incompatibility. Old Light honors the dead by remembering them. Blood Dominion honors them by using them as fuel. These frameworks cannot reconcile at the doctrinal level. In mixed populations, constant low-grade social tension even without political pressure. In war, atrocity rates between these two factions are elevated because each side interprets the other's ritual practice as active sacrilege, and sacrilege is a reason to escalate beyond what military necessity requires.

**Old Light vs. The Order:** The most navigable cross-covenant relationship. The Record and the Law are structurally compatible — both are permanent, both bind the present to the past, both hold people accountable to something larger than immediate interest. A light-doctrine Old Light dynasty and a light-doctrine Order dynasty can achieve stable coexistence with modest diplomatic investment. The fracture point emerges when an Order dark doctrine dynasty encounters an Old Light light doctrine dynasty: the Order says the Law is absolute and admits no exception; the Remembrance Compact says that exceptions for mercy and acknowledgment of cost are the whole point. This is not academic disagreement when applied to prisoners, apostates, and conquered populations.

**Old Light vs. The Wild:** The deepest theological difference in the game. Old Light holds that the dead persist in the record and that this persistence carries moral weight on the living. The Wild holds that death returns things to the cycle and that grief is a human conceit the forest does not share. Old Light finds this almost incomprehensible — a spiritual tradition that forgets its dead is not a faith, it is animal metabolism. Wild populations resist Old Light conversion because the Wild's theological frame has no slot for what Old Light is offering. However: both faiths make sacred claims about specific places. Common diplomatic language can sometimes be built around territory that has been made sacred by what it witnessed and what it nurtured — an imperfect but functional overlap in a game that requires finding language somewhere.

## THE BLOOD DOMINION — The Red Covenant

### What This Faith Is

The Blood Dominion knows something that other faiths spend generations trying to avoid knowing: the bond between people, between a dynasty and its population, between the living and the dead, is not maintained by sentiment. It is maintained by cost. Things that cost nothing mean nothing. The covenant between a ruler and those they rule, between a soldier and the cause they die for, between a civilization and its own survival, is not sealed by rhetoric or law or memory. It is sealed by blood. The shedding of it, the sharing of it, the willingness to be cut open in service of something larger than the individual body — this is the mechanism through which the sacred bond activates. Not metaphorically. The blood is the covenant. The covenant is the blood.

The theology comes from a specific reading of the world's near-ending. The Great Frost did not arrive because civilization forgot something, as Old Light holds. It arrived because civilization had stopped paying. The bonds between people had become cheap — maintained by habit and convenience rather than genuine cost — and cheap bonds do not hold when the cold comes. The Frost was the consequence of a world full of people who had promised things they had not paid for. The Blood Dominion's resolution: pay. Pay first, pay visibly, pay in a currency that cannot be counterfeited. And if the world demands payment before it will hold together, the question the dynasty must answer is: whose blood? Who pays the covenant's cost? The light path and the dark path give different answers. The theology is identical.

### Light Doctrine — The Covenant Bond

**The doctrine's logic:**

A dynasty walking the Covenant Bond path has accepted that the ruler bleeds first. The most sacred act within this doctrine is the ruler's willingness to pay before demanding payment, to demonstrate through visible personal cost that the covenant is real and mutual. This produces dynasties of unusual unit loyalty because the population understands, in visceral terms, that their sacrifice is shared rather than extracted. When a Covenant Priest opens their own palm before a battle and bleeds into the ground of the territory they are defending, the soldiers watching are not receiving inspiration. They are receiving a theological statement about what the covenant obligates the strong to do. They believe it because they have watched it happen.

The vulnerability of this doctrine is directly proportional to its strength. A Covenant Bond dynasty that takes heavy losses has not merely lost soldiers. The covenant is strained — the shared sacrifice structure has absorbed more than it was designed to absorb in one period, and if the losses are not formally acknowledged and entered into the covenant record through the proper rites, the bond between the survivors and the ruling dynasty begins to attenuate. The covenant promise was mutual. The survivors paid their part. If the dynasty does not pay its part in return, the bond weakens. This makes Blood Dominion light doctrine strong in sustained defensive war but vulnerable to prolonged attrition — the covenant maintenance cost scales with losses in a way that cannot be indefinitely deferred.

### Dark Doctrine — The Blood Rite

**The doctrine's logic:**

The Blood Rite path has absorbed the Covenant Bond's theology fully. It agrees that the covenant must be paid, that cost is the mechanism through which the bond becomes real, that sacrifice is not cruelty but stewardship. It has drawn a different conclusion about who pays. The weak exist within the covenant as material. The strong exist within it as architects. Asking the weak to opt out of their contribution is like asking the earth to decline to accept the grain that's sown in it — the question is categorically wrong. The covenant is not a negotiation between equals. It is a hierarchy of purpose, and the Blood Rite priest who stands before the altar and performs the Tithe is not committing violence. They are completing the covenant's structure.

This doctrine does not hide from itself. Its rituals are performed publicly. Its priests do not dissemble about what they are doing. What makes the Blood Rite path genuinely disturbing is not that it is secretly ashamed of itself — it is that it is completely at peace with itself, and that peace is indistinguishable from certainty, and certainty at scale looks identical to institutions that have made atrocity orderly and gave it a title and a calendar.

### Faith Spread — Blood Dominion

Blood Dominion spreads through visibility. It does not require trade routes or cultural prestige. It requires that what it does be seen. A neighboring population with line of sight to a Blood Convocation does not experience abstract conversion pressure. They experience a specific encounter with a civilization that has something their own does not: a willingness to pay costs that makes it capable of things theirs cannot match. Some convert because they respect the power. Some because they fear becoming its material. Some because the theology genuinely answers something: that suffering has purpose, that obligation runs both ways between the strong and the structure they are sustained by.

Blood Dominion also spreads most aggressively in the immediate aftermath of conquest. Occupied territories experience maximum conversion pressure within the first 10 cycles — before the population has developed conceptual language to resist it. The covenant's physical presence in the territory is felt before the theology can be articulated, and by the time the theology can be articulated, the population has already been changed by witnessing the rituals.

### Conflict and Compatibility

**Blood Dominion vs. Old Light:** Old Light honors the dead by holding them in memory. Blood Dominion honors them by making the living use them as fuel. These are not reconcilable frameworks. Mixed populations experience constant pressure. In war, atrocity rates between these factions are elevated because each side interprets the other's ritual practice as active desecration of the dead and is therefore willing to commit acts beyond what military necessity requires.

**Blood Dominion vs. The Order:** The most functional cross-covenant relationship Blood Dominion has. The Order understands that structure requires cost, that hierarchy has obligations, that law is not sentiment. Two dark doctrine dynasties — Blood Rite and Order dark path — can cooperate because each recognizes in the other a theology that does not flinch from what power costs. The friction emerges from their competing accounts of what governs the covenant's relationship with ordinary people: Blood Dominion says sacrifice, The Order says compliance with law. These produce different populations in different states of subjugation, and eventually the two frameworks make claims the other cannot accommodate.

**Blood Dominion vs. The Wild:** Surprising structural compatibility at the theological level — both faiths accept the premise that the strong consume the weak as a basic fact of existence. Dark doctrine Blood Dominion and dark doctrine Wild can cooperate with minimal friction around this shared premise. The incompatibility emerges in practice: Blood Dominion wants to sacrifice nature as material (livestock, land, the labor of people living in it), and the Wild's dark doctrine holds that nature defines the terms of all consumption. What the Wild's dark path considers natural predation, Blood Dominion's dark path considers covenant sustenance, and eventually these descriptions make competing claims about the same piece of ground.

## THE ORDER — Covenant of the Sacred Mandate

### What This Faith Is

The Order does not hold that the divine speaks through fire, or through blood, or through the forest's indifferent growth. It holds that the divine speaks through structure. Through the fact that law exists, that hierarchy can be made to function, that human beings are capable of building institutions that outlast any individual and that constrain even those with the power to ignore them. In a world that nearly ended from chaos — from the collapse of every system that had organized people's lives — The Order offers the most specific answer to why The Great Frost came: because there was no law that held. Civilization failed not because it forgot the dead or refused to pay, but because it had no mechanism capable of making anyone accountable to anything larger than their immediate appetite. The Frost was the outcome of a world without binding law. The Mandate's answer is permanent, comprehensive, enforced law — not as convenience but as theology. Governance is not administration. It is the closest act to the sacred that living people can perform.

The Order asks its followers to believe something difficult: that the law matters more than the person. That an institution built correctly is more valuable than any individual within it, including the individual who built it. The light doctrine says this means the mandate exists to protect the governed — the law binds the powerful more than the weak, because the powerful have the most capacity to do harm unchecked. The dark doctrine says this means the mandate exists to enforce the hierarchy — the law defines who is governed and who governs, and compliance is the only expression of faith the institution requires. Both paths use the same texts. Both paths have enforced them at scale. The civilizations they produce are recognizably related and mutually incomprehensible.

### Light Doctrine — The Iron Compact

**The doctrine's logic:**

A dynasty walking the Iron Compact path has accepted that the law they invoke is the law they live under. The Mandate cannot be claimed as protection for the population while being used as a tool of extraction for the dynasty — the faith has no mechanism for this kind of hypocrisy that does not produce measurable structural consequences. The Iron Compact produces dynasties with unusual institutional stability because their populations experience governance as something other than organized exploitation. The law is the same for everyone, including the ruling bloodline, and the faith's rituals are specifically designed to enforce this through public ceremony that is genuinely binding rather than ceremonially hollow.

The vulnerability of this doctrine is bureaucratic scale. The Iron Compact operates through formal process — trials, declarations, oaths with legal weight, institutions that must be maintained. These require resources, personnel, and time that a crisis may not allow. An Iron Compact dynasty under rapid military pressure will find its governance machinery straining because the doctrine does not permit the summary shortcuts that crisis seems to demand. The dark path's appeal in moments of emergency is that it offers efficiency — one person deciding, no process required. The Iron Compact's answer is that efficiency purchased at the cost of the Mandate is not efficiency but dissolution of the thing that makes the dynasty worth defending.

What it produces in exchange for this efficiency is a civilization whose stability is entirely top-down — the population is compliant, not loyal, and the moment the dynasty demonstrates any weakness, the entire compliance architecture becomes available for capture by whoever can project authority over it.

### Faith Spread — The Order

The Order spreads through adjacent populations observing institutional stability over time. It does not spread through conquest primarily — it spreads through the contrast between the dynasty's governance coherence and the disorder elsewhere. Populations experiencing factional conflict, governance failure, or arbitrary rule are most susceptible to Order conversion because the faith offers the most concrete answer to disorder: law, hierarchy, enforcement. Populations in neighboring territories who have watched an Order dynasty for 10 cycles and found it consistently more stable than their own governance begin converting not out of idealism but out of pragmatic calculation.

Dark doctrine Order spreads through a different mechanism: it spreads through the demonstration that non-compliance has visible, predictable costs. A Formal Suppression conducted in line of sight of neighboring territories generates conversion pressure in those territories not because the observers find the doctrine attractive but because the alternative to converting has been made observable.

### Conflict and Compatibility

**The Order vs. Old Light:** Structurally compatible until tested by specific circumstances. The Record and the Law are not incompatible as foundations for a civilization. The fracture emerges around the question of whether accountability processes are genuine or procedural — Old Light demands emotional and moral acknowledgment of cost, The Order demands formal procedural compliance. These produce very different governance cultures when mercy, exceptions, and the treatment of conquered populations become active questions.

**The Order vs. Blood Dominion:** The most functional dark-doctrine cross-covenant pairing. Both accept that power requires cost, that hierarchy has obligations, that law and sacrifice are mechanisms for maintaining what would otherwise dissolve. Cooperation is achievable between two dark-doctrine dynasties of these faiths because neither will try to moralize the other out of their methods. The conflict emerges when their frameworks make competing claims about the same territory: Blood Dominion wants to sacrifice it, The Order wants to govern it. These are not reconcilable at scale.

**The Order vs. The Wild:** The deepest structural incompatibility in the game, more fundamental even than Old Light vs. The Wild. The Order holds that civilization and its laws are the highest achievement available to human beings. The Wild holds that civilization is a temporary arrangement that the world tolerates conditionally and will eventually end. The Order's entire theology is a refutation of the Wild's, and vice versa. Two dynasties of these faiths cannot coexist at high faith intensity without one actively working to eliminate the other's cultural presence.

## THE WILD — Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots

### What This Faith Is

The Wild does not hold that the divine is memory, or covenant, or law. It holds that the divine is the fact of the world — the indifferent, total, prior-to-everything fact of existence before human beings arrived and began naming things and building structures and deciding that their arrangements were important. The Great Frost was not punishment, not consequence, not abandonment. It was the world asserting what it always was: larger than what had been built inside it, indifferent to the buildings, patient in the way that things are patient when they do not die. The civilizations that fell to the Frost were not being punished. They were being reminded. The Wild's theology is built on this reminder, and it is the most ancient faith in the game precisely because it requires the least from the world to remain true. The forest does not need to believe in The Wild for The Wild's theology to be correct.

The Wild makes an offer to its followers that no other faith makes: it does not promise that your civilization will survive. It does not promise that what you build will last. It promises that if you understand your relationship to the world correctly — if you acknowledge what you are and what the world is, if you take what the world provides without pretending you own it, if you contribute to the living system you exist within rather than simply extracting from it — then you will be part of something larger than your dynasty, and that something will outlast you even when you do not. The light doctrine builds toward a kind of sacred participation in the world's life. The dark doctrine concludes that participation is for the weak, that natural law is predation, and that winter is not cruel because cruelty requires intention and winter has none.

### Light Doctrine — The Living Compact

**The doctrine's logic:**

A dynasty walking the Living Compact path has accepted that the world is a partner rather than a resource. Its territories are managed the way a person manages a relationship — you take, and you give back, and the accounting is not purely financial. The faith's rituals are structured as exchanges: you release something into the natural world, and the world responds. You take something from what it provides, and you acknowledge the taking formally. You mark territory not with walls but with living things — planted groves, maintained waterways, designated resting territories for animals. These acts are not conservation in the modern ecological sense. They are covenant-keeping in the oldest sense: the dynasty has made an agreement with the ground it stands on, and the ground is aware of the agreement.

This doctrine's military and strategic vulnerability is its geography-dependence. The Living Compact's strongest bonuses are generated within territories the dynasty has genuinely maintained over time — territories with active Grove Markers, Spirit Lodges functioning at full capacity, Thornrider units that have been stationed in the territory long enough to know its patterns. A Living Compact dynasty that has been in a territory for 20 cycles fights very differently in that territory than a dynasty that arrived 5 cycles ago. This is intentional. The faith rewards depth of presence over breadth of conquest.

### Dark Doctrine — The Consuming Wild

**The doctrine's logic:**

The Consuming Wild has heard the Living Compact's theology and decided that half of it is sentiment. The world is indifferent. The Wild's theology is built on that truth. The Consuming Wild path takes that truth to its complete form: if the world is indifferent, and if indifference is the law, and if winter kills the weak and strengthens the herd, then mercy is not a virtue — it is a refusal to participate honestly in what the world is. The forest does not preserve the lame deer. The flood does not spare the poorly positioned. The strong consume the weak because that is the mechanism through which the living world maintains itself, and a civilization that replicates this mechanism is not cruel. It is accurate.

This doctrine produces dynasties of genuine military ferocity — the Consuming Wild is the most aggressive faith path in the game in its territory control mechanics, because the faith frames expansion and predation as theologically correct rather than as moral compromises. What it produces in exchange for this ferocity is a civilization with no mechanism for the kind of internal solidarity that other faiths generate. The Consuming Wild population is not bonded by covenant, or law, or shared memory. It is bonded by strength. The moment the dynasty shows weakness, the binding unravels.

It will hold as long as the dynasty is winning.

## Cross-Covenant Mechanics

### Faith Alliance

When two dynasties of the same faith form a formal Faith Alliance, the shared covenant activates effects beyond what either dynasty produces individually. These effects are calibrated to whether the allied dynasties share the same doctrine path or hold different paths within the same faith.

**Same-covenant, same-doctrine alignment:**
Both allied dynasties gain a 15% faith intensity bonus that does not require ritual or building investment — the presence of a fellow practitioner at the same depth of doctrine is itself spiritually reinforcing. All rituals conducted by either dynasty within 8 hexes of the border region between their territories cost 10% fewer resources. Spiritual manifestations of the same type can propagate across the border: if one dynasty's manifestation is active and the allied dynasty reaches Fervent, the manifestation expands into the allied territory for 3 cycles at 50% effect rather than requiring a separate manifestation trigger.

**Same-covenant, different-doctrine alignment:**
The Faith Alliance provides only the baseline diplomatic bonuses between allied dynasties. No spiritual reinforcement activates — a Remembrance Compact dynasty and a Purging Flame dynasty reading the same texts and reaching different conclusions are not spiritually unified, even if they are politically cooperative. Tension events are generated periodically (every 15 cycles) as the doctrine difference creates friction around specific policy decisions. If the tension events are left unresolved, the alliance weakens without formal dissolution — it degrades rather than breaks cleanly, which creates governance uncertainty.

**Holy War multiplier:**
Any Faith Alliance active during a jointly declared Holy War against a third party receives an additional +10% combat morale bonus for both allied armies in every engagement against the target dynasty, stacking with all existing faith bonuses. The shared enemy clarifies what the shared faith is for.

### Holy War

Holy War is a formal faith-state declaration, not simply a political conflict. When a dynasty declares Holy War, the following mechanics activate beyond the standard war state:

**Faith intensity generation:**
Every military engagement that occurs after a Holy War declaration generates passive faith intensity at 2% per cycle for the declaring dynasty, regardless of outcome. The act of fighting in the name of the covenant sustains it.

**Conviction interaction:**
Atrocities committed during Holy War generate 30% less Conviction penalty for the declaring dynasty — the faith framework absorbs some of the moral cost of warfare by declaring it sanctioned. This is not a removal of accountability. It is a delay. The suppressed Conviction debt accumulates and releases in a single event if the Holy War ends in a loss or a drawn outcome — the faith's promise of divine sanction was not honored, and the population's reckoning with what was done in its name arrives all at once.

**Unit behavior:**
All faith units of the declaring dynasty gain +15% combat morale during Holy War for the duration. Non-faith units gain +5%. Units of the targeted dynasty that share the declaring dynasty's covenant (same faith, fighting each other in a schismatic Holy War) suffer -15% morale — they are fighting people who hold the same texts and the same gods, and the theology cannot easily explain why.

**Population conversion:**
Territories conquered during an active Holy War experience 40% accelerated faith conversion toward the conquering dynasty's faith. The population has been told the conquest was sanctioned. Some believe it.

**Termination:**
Holy War ends with either the military conclusion of the conflict or a formal religious truce brokered by a neutral dynasty at Devout or higher faith intensity. A religious truce carries its own diplomatic weight — the broker gains significant neutral faction goodwill.

### Faith Conversion

When a dynasty conquers a territory, the local population's faith does not change overnight. Conversion is a mechanics model, not an instant switch. The method available to the conquering dynasty depends on its doctrine path.

**Light doctrine conversion:**
Available to all four faiths' light paths. Light doctrine conversion operates through presence, performance, and demonstrated benefit. Faith buildings generate passive conversion pressure proportional to their tier and the dynasty's faith intensity level. Rituals conducted at high visibility generate conversion events that offer the local population the choice to participate. Conversion through light doctrine is slow — typically 15-25 cycles to shift majority faith affiliation in a reluctant population — but it is stable. Populations converted through light doctrine methods are less likely to revert upon conquest by a different dynasty because the conversion produced genuine cultural change rather than surface compliance.

Specific methods by faith:
- Old Light light doctrine: Names the local dead in Old Light rites — the population's grief and memory are incorporated into the covenant. Highly effective in populations that have experienced recent losses.
- Blood Dominion light doctrine: Offers the Covenant Bond to local leaders — shared sacrifice rituals that invite participation rather than imposing it. Effective among military populations who respond to the offer of belonging through cost.
- Order light doctrine: Establishes legal infrastructure that benefits the local population — dispute resolution, property protection, formal rights. Effective in populations experiencing disorder or arbitrary rule.
- Wild light doctrine: Maintains and improves local natural resources — the living compact produces visible territory health that the local population can observe. Effective in agricultural and pastoral populations with strong land connection.

**Dark doctrine conversion:**
Available to all four faiths' dark paths. Dark doctrine conversion operates through suppression of the existing faith and systematic replacement. The timeline is faster — typically 8-12 cycles to achieve nominal compliance — but the conversion is brittle. A nominally converted population under dark doctrine is one crisis away from reversion if the suppressing dynasty shows weakness. Additionally, forcibly converted populations generate ongoing Conviction pressure on the ruling dynasty at 1% per cycle per converted settlement, representing the sustained moral cost of maintained suppression.

Specific methods by faith:
- Old Light dark doctrine: Inquisitor's Examination followed by Purging Rite. Eliminates alternative faith practice, generates compliance through the demonstrated cost of heterodoxy. Produces clean metrics with simmering resentment beneath.
- Blood Dominion dark doctrine: Tithe and Convocation conducted publicly in the converted territory. The local population understands that the covenant's demands apply to them now. Compliance follows from the demonstration.
- Order dark doctrine: Mandate Assertion followed by Formal Suppression. The law is declared, the non-compliant are processed, the remainder is governed. The cleanest institutional mechanism in the game.
- Wild dark doctrine: The Culling conducted in the new territory, followed by Territory Claim markers. The population's weakest members are removed and the remainder understands what the faith is. Fastest compliance generation in the game; shortest stability duration.

### Faith Intensity Decay — The Exact Model

Faith is not a resource that holds at whatever level it reaches. It requires continuous maintenance. These are the specific decay mechanics:

**Base decay rate by tier:**
- Latent (0-20%): No decay. The faith exists as ambient cultural presence and is self-sustaining at minimal intensity.
- Active (20-40%): 0.5% decay per cycle if no faith building is actively maintained. Maintaining a Tier 1 building at minimum upkeep prevents all decay at this tier.
- Devout (40-60%): 1% decay per cycle against base. Requires a Tier 1 building at full upkeep OR a Tier 2 building at minimum upkeep to prevent decay. At minimum upkeep, intensity stabilizes but cannot grow.
- Fervent (60-80%): 1.5% decay per cycle against base. Requires a Tier 2 building at full upkeep OR a Tier 3 building at minimum upkeep to prevent decay. Additionally, requires at least one ritual to have been completed within the past 10 cycles — without recent ritual activity, decay rate doubles to 3% per cycle.
- Apex (80-100%): 2% decay per cycle against base. Requires a Tier 3 building at full upkeep and at least one major ritual completed within the past 8 cycles. Additionally requires a Bloodline Member in an active faith role — if no Member is serving as a faith leader (light doctrine) or covenant officiant (dark doctrine), decay rate triples to 6% per cycle.

**Minimum maintenance investment by tier:**
- Tier 1 building minimum upkeep: 5 Gold per cycle, 3 Provisions per cycle
- Tier 2 building minimum upkeep: 12 Gold per cycle, 8 Provisions per cycle, 4 Rare Materials per cycle
- Tier 3 building minimum upkeep: 22 Gold per cycle, 15 Provisions per cycle, 8 Rare Materials per cycle

**Accelerated decay triggers:**
- Faith building destroyed: -15% faith intensity immediately plus doubled decay rate for 8 cycles in the affected territory
- Bloodline Member faith leader killed or removed from role: -8% intensity immediately plus +1% additional decay per cycle for 10 cycles
- Covenant ritual disrupted mid-execution: -3% to -8% faith intensity depending on ritual tier; see individual ritual entries
- Enemy dark doctrine manifestation active in territory: +50% decay rate for the duration of the manifestation
- Dynasty Conviction drops to Cruel while holding light doctrine path: +1% additional decay per cycle per Cruel tier increment below neutral — the faith and the conviction are misaligned, and the population begins to notice
- Dynasty Conviction rises to Moral while holding dark doctrine path: +1% additional decay per cycle per Moral tier increment above neutral — same misalignment in the opposite direction
- Territory under active military occupation by enemy force: Decay rate doubles for every cycle of continuous occupation
- Faith building upkeep defaulted for 3+ cycles: Faith intensity drops to the ceiling of the next lower tier and stabilizes there, requiring a major ritual to recover

## SECTION 2 — WORLD ORIGIN AND HISTORICAL FOUNDATIONS

ENTRY — THE GREAT FROST AND THE REBIRTH OF THE BLOODLINES

The Great Frost and the Rebirth of the Bloodlines Canonical World Origin and Design Doctrine

In the distant past of the world that would one day become the setting of Bloodlines, human civilization existed in a form far more structured and expansive than the fractured societies seen at the beginning of the game.

Kingdoms rose across continents. Cities grew into centers of trade, governance, and cultural life. Merchant networks carried goods across mountains and seas. Armies marched beneath royal banners, enforcing borders and projecting power. Diplomacy, religion, commerce, and war were organized through institutions that had endured for generations.

Families ruled kingdoms. Dynasties shaped the fate of nations. Bloodlines became synonymous with political identity, economic influence, and military strength.

For centuries, the world operated within this balance of power.

Then the climate began to change.

At first, the shift seemed minor. Winters arrived earlier than expected. Snow lingered longer into the spring. Harvest yields declined in certain regions but remained stable in others. Rulers and scholars debated the causes, but few believed the changes represented anything more than a temporary hardship.

Years passed, and the pattern worsened.

Winters grew longer. Summers shortened. Crops began to fail across wide regions of the world. Rivers froze earlier each year and thawed later. Trade caravans struggled to reach distant cities through snow-covered passes that had once been reliable routes.

The climate continued to deteriorate.

What had once been seasonal hardship slowly became something far more severe.

Within a generation, the world entered a catastrophic environmental collapse that later generations would remember as The Great Frost.

The Great Frost was not a single storm or a single winter. It was a prolonged era of relentless cold that lasted for decades.

Glaciers expanded across landscapes that had once been fertile farmland. Entire coastlines became locked in ice. Inland rivers froze so deeply that trade vessels could no longer move between cities. Crop failures spread from region to region until food shortages became the defining condition of life across the known world.

Famine followed.

Where famine spreads, order weakens.

Kingdoms struggled to feed their populations. Central governments began to fracture under the pressure of scarcity. Trade networks collapsed as merchants abandoned routes that had become too dangerous or impossible to traverse.

Cities that had once thrived as economic centers became desperate enclaves struggling to survive.

Migration began as populations fled failing regions in search of warmer climates or fertile ground. Many of these migrations ended in tragedy, as travelers encountered frozen terrain, impassable mountains, or rival groups competing for the same dwindling resources.

Political alliances dissolved. Military structures crumbled. Armies could no longer be maintained when food itself had become scarce.

The institutions that once defined civilization began to vanish.

Libraries burned for warmth. Palaces emptied as ruling families abandoned their capitals. Trade guilds disappeared along with the networks that sustained them.

The world that had once been defined by kingdoms and empires slowly disintegrated.

What replaced it was survival.

Small groups of people gathered in defensible locations where food and water could still be found. Mountain valleys, sheltered forests, and river basins became refuges for those fortunate enough to reach them.

The survivors reorganized themselves around the strongest remaining leaders. These leaders were often members of the old noble families whose authority had once been formalized through crowns and titles.

But the crowns had lost their meaning.

What remained were bloodlines.

The families that had once ruled nations now led tribes of survivors. Their authority was no longer derived from law or tradition. It was earned through the ability to protect their people and endure the unforgiving winters.

Over the course of decades, the remnants of human civilization transformed into a network of scattered dynasties, tribes, and fortified settlements.

These surviving groups developed new ways to endure the brutal climate.

Communities constructed underground storehouses to preserve food during the long winters. Defensive structures were built to protect against raids by rival groups competing for limited resources. Entire cultural traditions emerged around surviving the cold.

Religious interpretations of the catastrophe spread across the surviving populations.

Some believed the Frost was divine punishment for the arrogance of the old world. Others believed it was a trial designed to separate the strong from the weak. Still others saw the expanding ice as the will of nature reclaiming balance from humanity's excess.

Faith became central to survival, as belief systems helped communities endure the psychological and spiritual weight of living in a world that seemed permanently hostile.

Throughout the decades of freezing winters, warfare continued in a far more primitive and desperate form than the organized battles of the old kingdoms.

Conflicts were no longer fought for territory alone. They were fought for food stores, shelter, and survival itself.

During this time, many dynasties abandoned the identities they once held.

Families that had once been merchants took up arms as raiders. Military houses that had once commanded organized armies became protectors of agricultural enclaves. Religious authorities became war leaders when survival demanded strength.

The old world had defined families through institutions such as trade guilds, noble courts, and structured military hierarchies.

The Frost destroyed those institutions.

In their absence, dynasties became adaptable. They survived by becoming whatever their circumstances required them to be.

This period of hardship lasted for generations.

Children were born who had never seen a world without constant winter. Entire cultural memories of the old kingdoms faded into myth and legend.

Then, slowly, the climate began to change again.

The endless winters did not disappear overnight, but their severity began to weaken. Snow melted earlier in the year. Summers grew longer. Rivers thawed with greater reliability. Fertile soil emerged from beneath layers of ice that had buried it for decades.

The world began to thaw.

This period marked the end of the Great Frost and the beginning of what later historians would call The Age of Reclamation.

For the first time in generations, the surviving dynasties saw the possibility of rebuilding civilization.

However, the thaw did not restore the world to its former stability.

The climate remained volatile. Violent cold cycles occasionally returned without warning, plunging entire regions back into temporary deep freezes.

When these frost seasons arrive, the land becomes inhospitable to expansion and large-scale warfare.

Crops fail temporarily. Rivers freeze again. Movement across the land becomes dangerous or impossible.

Over time, the dynasties learned to prepare for these cycles. Communities constructed winter shelters and food reserves designed to sustain populations during the return of severe cold.

At the beginning of Bloodlines, the world exists in a fragile state between collapse and renewal.

Ruins of the old world lie scattered across landscapes that are only now emerging from beneath the ice. Ancient cities remain buried beneath glaciers. Forgotten roads and fortifications appear as the snow recedes.

The surviving dynasties stand at the threshold of a new era.

Each bloodline carries the memory of what it once represented in the ancient world.

But the decades of survival during the Great Frost changed them.

The identities that once defined the dynasties were shaped by institutions that no longer exist.

The Frost forced every family to adapt in order to survive.

Because of this history, the dynasties that emerge into the Age of Reclamation are not bound by their past.

They remember who they were.

But memory does not dictate destiny.

In Bloodlines, a bloodline represents heritage, culture, and historical identity, but it does not restrict the strategic direction a player may choose.

A dynasty that was once known for commerce may become a war machine.

A war-born house may evolve into diplomacy and political control.

The player defines what the bloodline becomes.

Bloodlines provide identity.

They do not impose destiny.

## SECTION 5 — CORE RTS MECHANICS

---

ENTRY — WARCRAFT 3-ALIGNED RTS MODEL AND CORE GAMEPLAY DIRECTION

SECTION 1: CORE GAMEPLAY DIRECTION (WARCRAFT 3-ALIGNED RTS MODEL)

The game BLOODLINES is explicitly aligned to a gameplay and presentation philosophy closely modeled after the RTS structure of Warcraft 3.

This alignment is intentional and foundational.

The following characteristics define this direction:

* Lower unit count compared to large-scale RTS games
* Increased importance of individual units
* Emphasis on micro-management and positioning
* Combat engagements that are deliberate and meaningful
* Strong battlefield readability at all times
* Clean UI layout that supports layered systems
* Elite unit presence (Bloodline Members) as central gameplay anchors

This direction is chosen to:

* Prevent system dilution caused by large-scale unit chaos
* Preserve clarity while introducing complex layered systems (Faith, Conviction, Bloodline)
* Allow Bloodline Members to remain visible, meaningful, and impactful
* Support longer-form matches without visual or cognitive overload

This direction is considered LOCKED unless explicitly overridden in a future session.

## SECTION 8 — CONVICTION SYSTEM

---

ENTRY — FAITH AND CONVICTION AS SEPARATED SYSTEMS

SECTION 3: FAITH AND CONVICTION (SEPARATED SYSTEMS)

Faith and Conviction are explicitly defined as independent systems.

This separation is a CORE DESIGN PRINCIPLE.

SUBSECTION 3.1: FAITH SYSTEM

Faith is defined by:

* Selection of one of four ancient faiths at the end of Level 1
* A measurable intensity level that must be maintained
* Resource costs tied to participation and scale
* Population involvement requirements
* Rituals and ceremonies tied to infrastructure

Faith evolves over time and impacts:

* Visual identity
* Gameplay effects
* Late-game divergence paths (Level 4)

SUBSECTION 3.2: FAITH DUALITY (DARK AND LIGHT PATHS)

Each faith contains both:

* Dark expression path
* Light expression path

Example (Blood Dominion):

Dark Path:
* Ritual sacrifice of enemies
* Blood ceremonies
* Dedicated altars
* Population consuming blood during ritual

Light Path:
* Animal sacrifice instead of human
* Structured ceremonial worship
* Resource trade-offs (food impact)
* Priest/priestess development

This duality applies to ALL faiths.

SUBSECTION 3.3: CONVICTION SYSTEM

Conviction is defined as:

* A behavioral morality spectrum
* Determined by player actions over time
* NOT tied to faith selection

Critical rule:

A player may:

* Follow a dark faith path and still maintain positive conviction
* Follow a light faith path and still maintain negative conviction

Faith and Conviction MUST NEVER be forced into alignment.

---

ENTRY — CONVICTION AXIS MODEL AND DESIGN OUTCOMES

CONVICTION SYSTEM — CORE PRINCIPLE

Faith and Conviction are separate systems. This separation is mandatory and foundational.

Faith represents belief. Conviction represents behavior. These systems must never be directly tied together.

AXIS MODEL

Civilizations are defined by two independent axes:

Faith Doctrine Axis:
* Light Doctrine
* Dark Doctrine

Conviction Axis:
* High Conviction
* Neutral Conviction
* Low Conviction

DESIGN RULE — NON DEPENDENCY

A civilization can follow a Dark Doctrine and maintain High Conviction.

A civilization can follow a Light Doctrine and have Low Conviction.

Faith does not determine morality. Conviction does not determine belief.

CONVICTION — DESIGN INTENT

Conviction measures:
* integrity
* consistency
* treatment of others
* exercise of power

It reflects how a ruler behaves, not what they believe.

EXAMPLE OUTCOMES

Dark Faith + High Conviction:
* Structured, disciplined, internally respected societies
* Harsh but principled

Light Faith + Low Conviction:
* Corrupt, hypocritical, unstable societies
* Externally righteous but internally broken

FINAL DESIGN STRUCTURE — THREE PILLARS

Every civilization in Bloodlines is defined by three pillars:

1. Dynasty — defines lineage and political identity
2. Faith — defines worldview and cultural systems
3. Conviction — defines moral character and leadership integrity

## SECTION 9 — FAITH SYSTEM

---

ENTRY — FAITH SYSTEM CORE STRUCTURE AND DOCTRINE PATH SYSTEM

FAITH SYSTEM — CORE STRUCTURE

Faith is a civilizational system representing worldview, belief structure, and societal philosophy.

Faith is not a simple religion mechanic. It defines how a civilization understands power, order, and existence.

Faith is chosen at the end of Level 1 progression. This moment represents the point where a civilization forms its ideological identity.

THE FOUR ANCIENT FAITHS

The Bloodlines world contains four primordial belief systems:

1. The Old Light — Covenant of the First Flame
2. Blood Dominion — The Red Covenant
3. The Order — Covenant of the Sacred Mandate
4. The Wild — Covenant of the Thorned Elder Roots

These are ancient systems that existed before the current dynasties. Players choose which faith their civilization adopts and develops.

FAITH INTENSITY AND MAINTENANCE

Faith is not static. Faith has intensity which increases or decreases based on:

* population participation
* ritual activity
* alignment with doctrine
* presence of structures

Faith requires ongoing resource investment. Costs scale with:

* population size
* number of religious structures
* depth of participation

Neglecting faith results in:

* loss of cohesion
* potential unrest
* weakened cultural identity

FAITH LEVEL 4 — APEX STATE

At Level 4 progression:

* A Grand Faith Structure is constructed.
* The civilization reaches a final ideological state.
* This transformation is permanent and irreversible.
* It unlocks powerful late-game mechanics tied to the chosen faith.

FAITH EXPANSION — DOCTRINE PATH SYSTEM

Faith includes a second layer called the Doctrine Path.

Each Faith contains two interpretations:

* Light Doctrine
* Dark Doctrine

These represent different expressions of the same belief system. They are not separate faiths, but divergent cultural implementations.

DOCTRINE PATH — DESIGN INTENT

The Doctrine Path system exists to:

* create diversity within the same faith
* introduce internal ideological conflict
* enable narrative depth
* avoid forcing rigid playstyles
* support interaction with Conviction

BLOOD DOMINION — DOCTRINE EXAMPLES

Dark Doctrine:
* Ritual sacrifice of enemies
* Elaborate blood ceremonies
* Construction of blood altars
* Population participation in blood rites
* Possible sacrifice of bloodline members

Ceremonies require:
* specific structures
* time to perform
* population involvement

These rituals can produce powerful effects.

Light Doctrine:
* Animal sacrifice instead of human sacrifice
* Ceremonial gatherings tied to celestial events
* Resource cost impacts such as reduced food supply
* Development of priesthood roles within the bloodline

Bloodline members may become priests or priestesses devoted to the faith. Large ritual structures may be constructed.

OTHER FAITHS — DOCTRINE APPLICATION

All four Faiths support Light and Dark interpretations. Each Faith contains its own variation of ritual behavior, population involvement, mechanical impact, and cultural identity.

The Old Light:
* Dark — inquisitions, purges, forced control
* Light — guidance, protection, structured belief

The Order:
* Dark — authoritarian control, surveillance, rigid enforcement
* Light — efficient governance, disciplined systems

The Wild:
* Dark — primal domination, ritual hunts, nature unleashed
* Light — harmony, sustainability, balance with the land

FAITH SPREAD AND GLOBAL INFLUENCE

Faith can spread across populations beyond a single civilization.

Methods of spread may include:
* cultural influence
* conquest
* missionary activity
* shared borders

If a single Faith becomes dominant globally, late-game religious conflict can occur.

Possible outcomes:
* holy wars
* doctrinal conflicts
* civilizational division

## SECTION 10 — BLOODLINE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

---

ENTRY — BLOODLINE MEMBERS AS CORE GAMEPLAY ENTITIES

SECTION 2: BLOODLINE MEMBERS (CORE GAMEPLAY ENTITIES)

Bloodline Members function as central units in gameplay, similar in structural importance to hero units in Warcraft 3, but with significantly expanded systemic depth and consequence.

They are defined as:

* Persistent entities across the match
* Limited to 20 active members (with overflow entering dormant state)
* Present in both battlefield and meta-layer systems

Bloodline Members can:

* Lead armies
* Participate directly in combat
* Be captured by opposing players
* Be married into other houses (diplomatic function)
* Be sacrificed (depending on faith path)
* Be assigned roles that influence gameplay systems

Role examples include:

* General (military leadership)
* Diplomat (political influence)
* Trader (economic influence)
* Religious figure (faith system influence)

They are NOT disposable.

They are:

* Dynasty anchors
* Identity carriers
* Long-term strategic assets
* Mechanically and emotionally significant

Their loss, capture, or sacrifice must have meaningful impact.

ENTRY — VISUAL IDENTITY SYSTEM AND DYNAMIC VISUAL EVOLUTION

SECTION 4: VISUAL IDENTITY SYSTEM (FOUNDATIONAL FRAMEWORK)

Each Bloodline is defined by a unified identity system consisting of:

* Hair color (primary identifier)
* Color palette
* Symbol family (not a single symbol)
* Architectural design language
* Material tone
* Conviction-based visual modifiers
* Faith-based visual modifiers

SUBSECTION 4.1: HAIR COLOR SYSTEM (LOCKED)

Each founding bloodline has a fixed hair color identity:

* Trueborn: Silver
* Highborne: Dark Gold (with highlights)
* Ironmark: Black
* Goldgrave: Bright Gold
* Stonehelm: Brown
* Westland: Vivid Red
* Hartvale: Orange
* Whitehall: Neutral Brown
* Oldcrest: Black / Gray / Pepper

Hair color serves as the fastest visual identifier of bloodline.

SUBSECTION 4.2: COLOR PALETTE SYSTEM

Each bloodline includes:

* Primary color
* Secondary color
* Accent color

These are applied across:

* UI highlights
* Unit accents
* Banners
* Selection indicators
* Ability visuals (subtle use)

SUBSECTION 4.3: SYMBOL FAMILY SYSTEM (LOCKED)

Each bloodline must have:

* A core defining symbol
* A family of symbol variations

Symbols must NOT be singular or static.

They must support variation across:

* UI
* Units
* Buildings
* Banners
* Rank indicators

Unused symbol variants may be assigned to lesser houses.

ENTRY — DYNAMIC VISUAL EVOLUTION

SECTION 5: DYNAMIC VISUAL EVOLUTION (FAITH + CONVICTION + BLOODLINE)

All visual representation in the game is influenced by three layers:

1. Bloodline (base identity)
2. Faith (overlay identity)
3. Conviction (tone modifier)

Dark Expression:
* Sharper architecture
* Dim lighting
* Fire elements
* Aggressive visual tone

Light Expression:
* Open structures
* Natural lighting
* Clean geometry
* Balanced visual tone

SUBSECTION 5.2: FAITH VISUAL MODIFICATION

Faith introduces:
* Unique buildings
* Ritual structures
* Environmental props
* Ceremony-specific layouts

Example:

Blood Dominion:
* Dark: blood altars, ritual execution spaces
* Light: ceremonial structures, organized worship areas

ENTRY — CHARACTER VISUAL STYLE

SECTION 8: CHARACTER VISUAL STYLE (REALISM DIRECTION)

Characters must adhere to:

* Realistic proportions
* Natural anatomy
* Non-cartoon presentation

Explicitly rejected:
* Oversized limbs
* Stylized exaggeration
* Cartoon-like proportions

Accepted:
* Grounded realism
* Historically inspired clothing
* Material-based design (leather, steel, cloth)
* Subtle facial variation
* Visible age progression

SUBSECTION 8.1: BLOODLINE MEMBER READABILITY

Despite realism, Bloodline Members must remain identifiable via:

* Hair color (primary identifier)
* Garment design (role + bloodline palette)
* Symbol accents
* Subtle silhouette differentiation

They must be:
* Instantly recognizable
* Visually distinct
* Non-cartoonish

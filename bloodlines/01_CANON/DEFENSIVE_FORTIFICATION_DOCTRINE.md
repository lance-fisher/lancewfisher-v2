# Bloodlines — Defensive Fortification Doctrine

**Status:** SETTLED CANON
**Locked on:** 2026-04-14
**Authority:** Lance W. Fisher direction, session of 2026-04-14
**Precedence:** Canonical strategic pillar. Not optional flavor. Binds the design bible, combat systems, stronghold systems, AI planning, siege logic, economy tradeoffs, match flow, and Unity implementation plans.

---

## Purpose

Bloodlines must treat defensive fortification as a major and fully viable strategic path, especially around primary keeps, bloodline seats, and high-value dynastic strongholds. The design goal is not for defense to be cosmetic, symbolic, or merely delaying. The design goal is for meaningful investment into fortification to create real security, real deterrence, and real military difficulty for the attacker. A player who makes significant and deliberate investment into defending their realm, especially the main keep where the head of the bloodline resides, must feel that the investment matters and cannot be casually erased by repeated wave attacks, suicide assaults, or late-game mass rushing.

A heavily fortified bloodline seat must not behave like a normal structure with a larger health pool. It must function as the center of an integrated defensive network. The keep and its surrounding systems should form a layered fortress complex that is difficult to breach quickly and costly to assault recklessly.

## Doctrine Pillars (Verbatim)

### 1. Fortification must be layered, not singular.

A defended stronghold should be capable of having meaningful outer defenses, inner defenses, and final defensive cores. The fall of one defensive ring must not automatically render the entire base tactically meaningless. Breaching the perimeter should be an achievement, not the whole battle.

### 2. Defensive systems must support one another.

Walls, gates, towers, defensive emplacements, garrisons, chokepoints, kill zones, signal systems, reserve mustering points, and inner fallback positions must function as a connected defensive ecosystem rather than isolated health-bar objects.

### 3. Deep investment in defense must create real defensive leverage.

A player who commits heavily to fortification should gain more than time. That investment should meaningfully increase the attacker's losses, slow assault tempo, increase siege requirements, create higher operational risk, and strengthen the defender's ability to stabilize and recover the position.

### 4. Bloodline keeps and capital seats must be especially resilient if properly fortified.

A primary keep housing key bloodline figures, succession lines, or dynastic command should be among the hardest targets in the game when fully developed. The attacker should need a real siege plan, not just repeated unit spam.

### 5. Wave-spam and suicide erosion must not be a dominant answer to fortification.

The game must not allow heavily defended strongholds to be reliably broken by mindless repetition alone. Failed assaults should matter. Repeated reckless attacks should result in real military waste, tempo loss, morale or cohesion penalties where appropriate, and increased defender advantage rather than automatically guaranteeing eventual structural collapse.

### 6. Siege should be an earned military operation.

To break elite fortifications, attackers should often require real preparation such as siege engines, engineers, logistics, supply continuity, scouting, breach planning, elite units, faith powers, sabotage, coordinated multi-front timing, isolation of the target, or similar serious commitments. Assaulting a major fortress should feel like a campaign objective, not a casual click action.

### 7. Late-game defense must remain relevant.

Do not allow offense to scale upward so completely that defensive investment becomes meaningless in the late game. Bloodlines must preserve the viability of fortress-centered strategy all the way through longer and larger matches. High-tier defensive development should unlock stronger and more specialized forms of fortification, recovery, and layered resistance.

### 8. Defensive commitment should have tradeoffs, but the reward must be real.

A player who heavily invests in fortification may accept opportunity costs in army size, expansion speed, economy tempo, or other areas. That is acceptable and desirable. But once that investment is made, the resulting fortress must provide authentic strategic value and not merely superficial delay.

### 9. Different settlement classes should have different defensive expectations.

Border settlements, military forts, trade towns, regional strongholds, and primary dynastic keeps should not all defend the same way or reach the same defensive ceiling. Major bloodline seats and intentionally developed fortress-citadels should represent the highest expression of defensive power in the realm.

### 10. The player should feel the difference between an unfortified realm and a fortified one.

A neglected seat of power should be vulnerable. A disciplined, well-fortified capital should command respect.

## Interpretive Depth (Canonical Elaboration)

This section expands each pillar into explicit design implications that every future session, canon edit, and implementation milestone must honor.

### Pillar 1 implications — Layered fortification

The defensive architecture of a fortified settlement is at least three concentric layers:

- **Outer works** — first contact surface. Fieldworks, moats, palisades, abatis, earthen berms, outer walls, forward towers, gatehouses with killing bays. The outer works exist to extract a cost from the assault before any inner structure is threatened. Their fall is expected in a serious siege. Their fall does not determine the fate of the keep.
- **Inner ring** — the main fortified enceinte. Primary curtain walls, flanking towers, main gatehouses, machicolations, murder holes, shielded firing platforms, inner garrison barracks, granaries, cisterns, reserve mustering yards. The inner ring is the main military problem for an attacker. Breaching it is a campaign achievement.
- **Final defensive core** — the keep proper, the bloodline seat, the throne hall, the inner sanctum. Kept small, deeply stocked, designed to resist even a broken inner ring for a meaningful period. This is where the head of bloodline retreats. Its fall is the fall of the house.

A defender who invests only in one layer has built a shell, not a fortress. A defender who develops all three has built a real fortress in the canonical Bloodlines sense.

### Pillar 2 implications — Connected defensive ecosystem

Every defensive component must have non-trivial relationships with the others. Examples of required connective tissue:

- **Walls ↔ Towers**: Walls do not work alone. Wall sections must be covered by towers that can range the approach and flank anyone scaling or breaching the section.
- **Gates ↔ Killing bays**: A gatehouse channels the attacker into a known corridor. That corridor must be overlooked by firing positions capable of devastating a bottled assault.
- **Garrison ↔ Reserve mustering yards**: A garrison is not a single blob. It is a rotating force drawn from reserve yards that resupply fresh units to contested sections and retire damaged ones for triage and recovery.
- **Signal systems ↔ Inner fallback**: Warning infrastructure (watchfire chains, bell towers, horn relays, optional faith-powered wards) must alert inner sections in time to reposition reserves and commit commanders before the outer works fall silent.
- **Chokepoints ↔ Kill zones**: Defensive terrain must force approach through planned fire corridors. Where the attacker cannot be stopped, they must at minimum be channeled.
- **Inner fallback positions ↔ Final core**: Even after a breach, retreating defenders must have structured fallback lines (inner street barricades, redoubts, tower-anchored secondary walls) rather than immediately collapsing to the core.
- **Supply ↔ All of the above**: Fortification without supply continuity starves. Granaries, cisterns, arsenals, and blood reservoirs (for houses that require them) must be designed as part of the defensive ecosystem, not as separate economy buildings with no defensive tie.

A fortified stronghold is one integrated artifact. Cutting any of these connections weakens the whole.

### Pillar 3 implications — Defensive leverage

Investment in defense produces gameplay-relevant advantages beyond raw hit points. Canonical leverage forms:

- **Attritional cost multiplier**: Attackers assaulting a developed fortification take losses materially greater than the losses they inflict, proportional to the depth of fortification.
- **Tempo drag**: A developed fortress slows the attacker's broader campaign. Time spent besieging is time not spent expanding elsewhere.
- **Siege requirement escalation**: The more developed the fortification, the more siege preparation is required before assault is viable. Attacking an elite fortress with line infantry alone is futile.
- **Operational risk imposition**: Laying siege to a developed fortification exposes the besieger to counter-operations: sorties, relief armies, coalition intervention, faith counter-powers, population unrest in the besieger's own realm.
- **Defender recovery**: A defender who repulses an assault recovers materially faster than a defender who was overwhelmed. Morale swings toward the defender. Reinforcements arrive. Fortifications damaged in the repulsed assault can be rebuilt under cover of continued occupation.

### Pillar 4 implications — Bloodline keeps as apex defensive structures

Primary dynastic keeps are the highest-priority fortified structures in the realm. They must embody the apex of defensive engineering:

- **Bloodline presence bonus**: The presence of the head of bloodline, the heir designate, or committed defensive specialists at the keep confers real defensive bonuses (garrison cohesion, morale, reserve availability, loyalty surge).
- **Succession-aware defense**: A keep holding an intact succession line behaves differently from one emptied of bloodline. Political will to resist is a measurable defensive factor.
- **Dynastic command aura**: A commander-class bloodline member in the keep amplifies the defensive ecosystem's coordination (faster reserves, tighter flanking, stronger resolve).
- **Symbolic weight**: Destroying a developed primary keep is a kingdom-ending event. It is canonically the hardest target in the game when fully developed.

### Pillar 5 implications — No wave-spam collapse

The simulation must refuse to reward repeated reckless assaults as a path to structural collapse. Canonical consequences for wave-spam:

- **Assault failure penalty**: A failed assault against an intact fortified position inflicts real losses on the attacker, not chip damage traded for certain eventual success.
- **Attrition asymmetry**: Losses scale faster for the attacker than the defender in fortified contests. The defender's reserve-and-triage system, garrison cycling, and supply continuity hold up under repeated assault; the attacker's supply lines, replacement cost, and morale do not.
- **Morale and cohesion penalties**: Units that have survived a repulsed assault suffer real combat degradation on subsequent assaults until they reset. Newly arrived units absorb the first contact penalty each time.
- **Assault tempo limits**: Repeated assaults on the same fortification within a short window suffer compounding penalties. The attacker must regroup, refit, and re-coordinate — which takes real match time and resources.
- **Defender recovery window**: Between failed assaults, the defender gains repair windows, reserve rotation, and potentially reinforcement arrival.

Mindless repetition is a losing strategy against a developed fortress. This is a hard design rule.

### Pillar 6 implications — Earned siege

Breaking elite fortifications requires real military operations. Canonical siege requirements are plural — any serious fortress will demand multiple of these:

- **Siege engines**: Rams, siege towers, trebuchets, mantlets, bombards (faction-appropriate). Siege engines require build time, resources, escort, and movement logistics.
- **Engineer / sapper specialists**: Units capable of mining walls, raising earthworks, constructing siege lines, or directing siege engine fire.
- **Logistics and supply continuity**: Siege camps, supply wagons, foraging rights, rear-area security. A siege that runs out of food or loses its supply line collapses.
- **Scouting and intelligence**: The attacker must learn the fortress's weaknesses — weak sections, garrison patterns, reserve locations, faith and bloodline presence, supply status.
- **Breach planning**: A coordinated siege identifies a target section, prepares the approach, times the assault with supporting fires, and commits reserves to exploit a breach before the defender can seal it.
- **Elite units**: First-wave breaching requires shock troops capable of surviving the teeth of the defense long enough to open a corridor. Line infantry alone will not do.
- **Faith powers**: Covenant-sanctioned siege operations, doctrine-specific curses, holy breaches, divine interventions (at Fervent/Apex intensity) that alter the defensive equation.
- **Sabotage**: Covert operations that weaken fortifications before or during siege — gate-opening, fire-raising inside the walls, poisoning supplies, turning garrison defectors.
- **Coordinated multi-front timing**: Drawing the defender's reserves to the wrong section by staging diversions, feints, or simultaneous multi-gate assaults.
- **Isolation**: Cutting the fortress off from relief — blockading roads, intercepting reinforcements, destroying nearby supply depots.

Assaulting a major fortress is a campaign-scale military operation. The game must reinforce that framing.

### Pillar 7 implications — Late-game defensive relevance

Fortification must scale. Canonical late-game fortification expressions:

- **Apex fortification tiers**: Grand wall projects, citadel keeps, shielded inner cores, reinforced gatehouses, hardened towers, specialized garrison barracks, faith-warded inner sanctums.
- **Faith-integrated fortifications**: High-intensity faith buildings inside the fortress (apex covenant structures, doctrine-path wards) confer defensive bonuses and can negate or contest attacker faith powers.
- **Specialized garrison classes**: Late-game unlocks include defensive specialist units that are only effective in fortified contexts (wallbreakers, guard captains, tower artillerists, cellar guards, faith-warden units).
- **Recovery systems**: Developed fortresses recover damage faster than basic fortifications. Advanced supply and repair infrastructure reduces downtime after a repulsed assault.
- **Layered resistance mechanisms**: Multi-layered wards, backup supply caches, secondary reserve yards, hidden passages (house-specific), sally ports. Each additional layer adds to the attacker's campaign cost.

No amount of late-game offensive scale should render a truly developed fortress-citadel trivially reducible. The defender's apex matches the attacker's apex.

### Pillar 8 implications — Tradeoffs and real reward

Fortification has real opportunity cost. Canonical tradeoffs:

- **Stone, iron, and wood commitments** that could have gone into armies instead.
- **Population commitments** to garrison, engineering, and signal-keeping rather than field armies or productive labor.
- **Commander commitments** to defensive governance rather than offensive warfare.
- **Economy tempo** reduction because fortification infrastructure competes with trade and recruitment infrastructure.
- **Expansion speed** reduction because a fortress-centered strategy consolidates rather than expands.
- **Faith building flexibility** reduction because fortress-aligned covenant structures may crowd out more mobile faith investments.

These tradeoffs are desirable and balanced. They are not paid without a return. Once the investment is made, the resulting fortification must deliver authentic strategic value.

### Pillar 9 implications — Settlement class hierarchy

Defensive ceilings differ by settlement class. Canonical class hierarchy (ascending defensive potential):

- **Border settlement**: Minimal fortification potential. Stockade, watch towers, local militia levy. Meant to warn, delay, and retreat. Loss is expected under serious assault.
- **Military fort**: Purpose-built defensive structure. Strong walls for its scale, a rotating garrison, supply for medium-term siege, engineer assignment. Designed to hold against raids and modest sieges. Can be reduced by developed siege operations.
- **Trade town**: Moderate fortification with an economic core. Gates, walls, civic guard, reserve mustering for civilian defense. Primary asset is the economic infrastructure; defensive investment is proportional.
- **Regional stronghold**: Major fortified settlement. Layered defenses, inner ring, dedicated garrison, significant supply capacity, tower networks, signal chains. A real siege requirement to reduce.
- **Primary dynastic keep**: The bloodline seat. Full layered fortification, apex garrison, commander presence, faith infrastructure, succession line, symbolic weight. Among the hardest targets in the realm.
- **Fortress-citadel (developed)**: A primary keep whose owner has actively invested in the fortress path. Apex-tier fortifications, every defensive leverage form maximally active. Canonically the hardest target in the game.

Not every settlement needs to defend the same way. This hierarchy lets builders choose how deeply to invest by class.

### Pillar 10 implications — The felt difference

The gameplay experience must communicate the difference between an unfortified realm and a fortified one in ways the player cannot miss:

- **Visual**: A fortified keep looks different. Walls, towers, banners, garrisons visible, supply trains moving, signal fires lit when threatened.
- **Auditory** (when audio lands): Horns, bells, drums, faith chants, the ambient sounds of a place that is ready.
- **Behavioral**: NPCs, population, and AI actors treat a fortified realm with more caution than a neglected one.
- **Numerical**: Measurable resistance, measurable attacker cost, measurable recovery — visible in combat logs, debug overlays, and post-engagement reports.
- **Narrative**: Political weight. A fortified keep commands respect. A neglected one invites opportunism.

## Implications for the Seven Core Systems

- **Territory System** — Defensive investment becomes a primary territorial attribute. Control points and provinces must carry fortification-tier metadata, layered defense state, and differential defensive ceilings by class. Territorial control math incorporates defensive leverage.
- **Population System** — Garrison and engineer populations are distinct commitment categories with their own maintenance costs and replacement pipelines. Fortified settlements support and are supported by dedicated labor pools.
- **Resource System** — Fortification-tier construction consumes stone, wood, and iron at scale. Apex fortification pulls on specialized resources (potentially inclusive of blood for Ironmark, sacred materials for faith-warded sections, captured engineers for specialty works).
- **Dynastic System** — The primary keep is the canonical bloodline seat. Dynasty members in the keep confer defensive bonuses. Capture of a developed keep, with its succession line intact, is catastrophic for the defender. This doctrine explicitly reinforces the Dynasty Consequence Cascade implemented 2026-04-14 (capture, succession, interregnum).
- **Faith System** — Faith buildings inside fortifications provide defensive bonuses proportional to intensity and doctrine alignment. Apex-tier faith structures in a keep function as defensive instruments, not merely spiritual ones. Covenant-specific fortification styles are canonical.
- **Conviction System** — Defensive commitment correlates with stewardship and oathkeeping (the keep is held, the realm is guarded, the oath to the bloodline is kept). Treacherous gate-opening sabotage correlates with desecration. Siege massacres (failed or successful) correlate with ruthlessness.
- **Born of Sacrifice System** — Defensive specialist elite units (wall-warden veterans, tower-masters, garrison captains) can be produced through the Born of Sacrifice pipeline with defensive-aligned consecrations. A keep under threat can conduct defense-consecration ceremonies.

## Implications for Match Flow

- **Founding (Stage 1)**: Initial defensive posture (basic walls, watchtowers) begins during early consolidation. Keep selection and siting determine later fortification ceiling.
- **Expansion and Identity (Stage 2)**: Strategic decision to invest in fortress path or offensive path. Regional strongholds are developed.
- **Encounter / Establishment / Neutral City (Stage 3)**: Trade and diplomatic architecture around fortifications. Signal chains extend, reserve networks form.
- **War and Turning of Tides (Stage 4)**: Siege operations begin. Fortified realms face real sieges; unfortified realms face fast collapse. Defensive investment pays out most visibly here.
- **Final Convergence (Stage 5)**: Apex fortifications are targeted by apex sieges. The final convergence of the match often plays out as a siege of the primary keep.

## Implications for AI Planning

- **Defensive AI**: AI must be able to choose the fortress path, develop layered defenses, station commanders for defensive leverage, and repel sieges. Defensive AI must understand garrison cycling, reserve commitment, and supply continuity.
- **Offensive AI**: AI attacking a developed fortress must prepare a siege operation. Attacking with line infantry alone must register as a losing plan. AI should stage siege engines, scout, starve supply lines, and commit elites for breach assaults.
- **Mixed AI**: AI balancing both sides of a fortification contest must reason about assault tempo limits, failure penalties, and the multi-front coordination required for a successful siege.
- **Hostile AI rejection of wave-spam**: AI behavior trees and decision heuristics must treat repeated reckless assault as a losing branch and prefer earned siege operations. The simulation rewards this choice.

## Implications for Siege Logic

See the companion file `04_SYSTEMS/SIEGE_SYSTEM.md` (new) for the attacker-side design. Key canonical positions:

- Siege units are a class unto themselves, with build cost, escort requirement, and operational limits.
- Engineers are a specialist population with distinct recruitment and maintenance.
- Supply continuity is tracked for besieging forces as well as besieged settlements.
- Breach mechanics resolve wall segments individually, not monolithically.
- Failed assault penalties are explicit simulation effects.

## Implications for Economy Tradeoffs

- **Fortification infrastructure** competes with offensive infrastructure for stone, iron, and wood.
- **Garrison upkeep** competes with field army upkeep for population and food.
- **Commander commitment** to defensive governance competes with commander commitment to offensive operations.
- **Fortress path dynasties** accept slower map expansion in exchange for a highly defensible core.
- **Offensive path dynasties** accept greater vulnerability at home in exchange for faster expansion and bigger armies.

Both paths are canonically viable. Neither is a default. The game must not balance in a way that makes one path strictly dominant.

## Canonical Non-Negotiables (From This Doctrine)

1. A developed fortress-citadel is canonically the hardest target in the game.
2. Wave-spam and suicide erosion are explicitly non-viable as the dominant answer to elite fortifications.
3. Siege is an earned military operation requiring plural commitments (engines, engineers, logistics, scouting, elites, faith, sabotage, isolation, multi-front timing).
4. Failed assaults impose real costs on the attacker. Repeated reckless assault is a losing strategy.
5. Late-game offense does not invalidate developed late-game defense. Apex matches apex.
6. Defensive commitment produces real strategic reward, not merely superficial delay.
7. Settlement classes have distinct and meaningful defensive ceilings.
8. The primary dynastic keep is the apex defensive structure, integrating bloodline and succession considerations into the fortification equation.
9. Fortified realms feel different — visually, mechanically, narratively — from neglected ones.
10. This doctrine is a canonical strategic pillar, not optional flavor.
